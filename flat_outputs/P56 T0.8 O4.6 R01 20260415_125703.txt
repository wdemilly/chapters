The ledger did not balance, and I had known it would not balance before I opened it.

I sat at the kitchen table with the book flat under both hands and the stub of a tallow candle guttering beside me, and I made myself look at the column again. Forty-one pounds, six shillings, and eightpence to the good, which sounded like a comfortable sum until you set it against the quarter's rent coming due the first week of December. Six weeks. Six weeks of full coaches and fair weather and no disasters, and I might scrape through with enough left to keep the cellar stocked through Christmas. Six weeks of anything less, and the numbers would eat me alive.

I closed the ledger and pressed my palms flat on its cover, as if the figures might rearrange themselves through force of will. They would not. They never did. But every morning I checked, because the day I stopped checking was the day I lost the place, and I would not lose this place.

The kitchen was cold. The fire I had built twenty minutes ago was still finding its courage, licking at the kindling without much conviction. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to kneel at the hearth, rearranging the wood to give the flame a better draught. The trick was patience -- small sticks first, then the split oak, then the heavier logs only when the heat was steady enough to take them. My mother had taught me this when I was nine, standing behind me with her hand on my shoulder, correcting my grip on the poker. She had not been a woman who wasted fuel, or words, or time.

I wanted -- just for a moment, in the grey dark of a late October morning with no one awake to see it -- to sit down and not stand up again. To let someone else build the fires, tally the accounts, count the candles and the coal. Not forever. Just for one morning.

I stood up and wiped my hands on my apron. The taproom fire would not build itself.

By the time I had the taproom grate cleared and the fire drawing properly, the bar stock checked and yesterday's accounts reconciled against the takings in the cashbox, the windows were showing the first dirty grey of dawn. The glass was rimed at the edges -- the cold had settled in overnight and meant to stay. I ran my thumb along the sill and felt the damp where the putty had cracked. Another thing that needed attention. I added it to the list I kept in my head, which was longer than any list I had ever committed to paper and considerably less likely to be lost.

I checked the cellar hatch. Locked. The dining room: chairs pushed in, table wiped, the good candlesticks put away. The front door: bolted. I unbolted it. The White Hound was open for business, whether business intended to arrive or not.

Out the back, Jem was already in the stable yard. I could see him through the kitchen window, his one hand working a bridle onto its hook with the practised economy of a man who had long since stopped thinking about what he could not do and concentrated entirely on what he could. He did not look up. He did not need to. The morning had its order, and we both knew our parts in it.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins half-set in her reddish hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar and took her place behind it without a word, running her cloth along the counter in the same pattern she had used every morning for seven years. I did not greet her. She did not greet me. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual -- the London coach was late more often than it was punctual, and I had stopped expecting otherwise around the same time I stopped expecting the weather to improve my mood. But when it finally rattled into the yard at half past nine, it was running short as well. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them looked as though the road had personally offended them.

The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the air of someone preparing to lodge a formal complaint with God. She had a trunk, and the trunk had a split hinge, and she had opinions about both the hinge and the road and the driver and the state of English coaching generally. The second passenger, a thin man in a brown coat, disappeared into the dining room without speaking to anyone, which was the most sensible thing I had witnessed all morning.

I directed Jem to the trunk before the woman could draw breath for her next salvo. He appeared at her elbow -- he had a way of materialising exactly where he was needed, as if the stable yard had its own system of silent bells -- and took the handle with his good hand, steadying the split hinge against his chest. The woman opened her mouth, looked at his pinned sleeve, and closed it again.

"The dining room is through on your left," I told her. "Breakfast is laid. The hinge can be mended by noon if you'd like to leave the trunk with us."

She blinked. She had been preparing for an argument, and the absence of one seemed to unsettle her more than the argument itself would have done. "Well," she said. "I suppose that would be acceptable."

She went. The coachman climbed down from the box, stamping his boots against the cold, and crossed the yard to where I stood.

"Road north of Hatfield is a disgrace," he said, without preamble. "Ruts deep enough to break an axle. Someone ought to speak to the turnpike trust."

"Someone has," I said. "Twice. They've promised repairs before Christmas."

"Christmas." He spat to one side. "I'll have lost a wheel by then."

"Take the lower fork past the mill. The surface holds better on that stretch, and it only adds ten minutes." I was already turning back toward the kitchen. "I'll have your ale sent out."

He nodded once, satisfied or at least silenced, and went to check his horses. The yard cleared. The passengers settled. Jem carried the trunk inside. Nan had watched the whole business from behind the bar with one eye on the door, her cloth moving in its steady rhythm. I noted it. Said nothing.

The chandler arrived at eleven with his cart and his monthly delivery and his newest price.

"Fourpence ha'penny the dozen," he said, setting the first crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table as though he were presenting a gift.

I did not pick up a candle. I did not need to inspect them; his tallow was reliable, which was the only reason I continued to buy from him. "It was fourpence last month."

"Tallow's gone up. The renderers in Hitchin have raised their price."

"The renderers in Hitchin are charging you threepence farthing a pound, same as they've charged you since August. I buy from the same market, Mr. Fells."

He had the grace to look uncomfortable, though not the grace to retract the price. "There's the cost of carting to consider."

"Your cart, your horse, your road. The same cart, horse, and road you had last month at fourpence."

"I could take them elsewhere. The Bull at Welwyn has been asking--"

"The Bull at Welwyn buys thirty dozen a quarter. I buy sixty. You'll not find a better volume on this stretch of road, and we both know it."

A silence. Mr. Fells looked at his candles, then at me, then at his candles again. I waited. I had learned from my mother that the person who spoke next in a negotiation was the person who had already lost it.

"Fourpence farthing," he said.

"Fourpence. Same as last month. And I'll take an extra dozen at the same rate, which I suspect will more than cover your trouble with the renderers."

He considered this. He was not a bad man, Mr. Fells. He was a man with a margin to protect, same as me, and we had been doing this particular dance long enough that we both knew the steps. He nodded.

"Fourpence. And the extra dozen."

"Thank you, Mr. Fells. Nan will show you where to stack them."

He took his crate and went. I stood in the kitchen with the smell of tallow still hanging in the air and allowed myself one small, private moment of satisfaction. It was not much. Fourpence was not a fortune. But it was the price I had set, and it was the price I had got, and on a morning when the ledger would not balance, that counted for something.

The satisfaction lasted as far as the next thought, which arrived -- as it always did -- without invitation. Fourpence a dozen, sixty dozen a quarter, the rent due in six weeks, the coal to buy, the stable roof to mend. All of it added against the same immovable fact: the lease was in my name only so long as I remained unmarried. The moment I took a husband, everything -- the inn, the income, the right to stand in my own kitchen and argue with a chandler over halfpennies -- became his. Not shared. His.

I had refused two proposals on the strength of that arithmetic. I did not regret either refusal. But I noticed, in the flat practical way I noticed everything, that the arithmetic left no room for anything else.

I picked up the ledger and went back to work.

The soldier came around the back, as the sign asked.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked -- young, thin, standing outside the door with the particular stillness of a man who was deciding whether he was welcome enough to announce himself. His coat was too large for him and had been too large for him for some time. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather separating from the sole on the left foot so that I could see a stripe of grey stocking underneath. He was perhaps twenty, though the war had a way of making men look older than they were, and younger, both at once.

He raised his hand. He hesitated. He knocked.

I opened the door.

He was shorter than I had expected from the window -- barely my own height, with fair hair cropped close and a face that would have been unremarkable if it had not been so carefully emptied of expression. He looked at me. He did not ask for anything. He waited.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

A pause. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the fire. "I'll bring you something."

He understood. He sat where I told him without argument, his hands flat on his knees, his back very straight. I cut bread -- the end of yesterday's loaf, still sound -- and ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since morning. I set both in front of him with a spoon and a cup of small beer, and I went back to my work at the table.

He ate carefully. Not slowly -- he was hungry, that was plain -- but with the deliberate attention of someone who had learned not to waste food and did not intend to start. When the bread was gone and the broth finished, he set the spoon down and looked at me.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, as though he felt this needed justification: "I've people in Huntingdon."

I nodded. He stood. He touched his forehead in something that was not quite a salute and not quite a bow, and he went.

After the door closed, I stood at the window and watched him cross the yard and pass through the gate onto the road. His left boot flapped with every step. He was somebody's son, and he was walking north in boots that would not last another twenty miles, and there was nothing else I could do for him.

I wiped the bowl and the spoon and put them away.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary grind of a coaching inn between rushes. Two men in the taproom got loud over something I could not be bothered to identify -- a wager, or an insult, or the particular species of grievance that breeds in men who have been sitting too long with too much ale.

I walked to their table. "Gentlemen. You'll settle this outside or you'll settle it nowhere, but you will not settle it in my taproom."

They settled it nowhere. Nan, watching from behind the bar, did not need to move.

The coach had gone. The guests were settled or departed. Jem was outside, Nan at the bar, and I was in the kitchen with the accounts and the evening's menu and the steady, unremarkable work of keeping a place running that did not care whether I was tired or discouraged or lonely. The inn required provisioning. The inn required clean linen and sound fires and someone to lock the doors at night. The inn did not ask how I felt about any of it, which was, on the whole, a mercy.

Evening came down early, the way it does in late October, as though the day had lost interest in itself. I moved through the closing routine alone -- a thing I had done so many times that my body could have managed it without my mind's assistance, and on some nights very nearly did.

Taproom fire raked down to coals. Cellar hatch locked. Bar tallied -- Nan had already done it, her figures neat and precise in the margins of the daily sheet. Kitchen closed: pots scoured, surfaces wiped, the morning's bread dough set to prove by the banked fire. I checked the dining room. I checked the front door. I checked the bolt and turned the key.

My hands at the end of the day were what they always were: reddened from cold water, roughened from work, the nails cut short because long nails caught on everything and I had no patience for catching. There was a small cut on my right index finger -- the edge of Mr. Fells's delivery note, which had been sharp enough to draw blood, though I had not noticed at the time. I noticed it now, a thin line of dried red across the pad of my finger. A hazard of the profession. My mother's hands had looked the same.

I crossed the yard on the last circuit. The stable was quiet -- Jem would have settled the horses. The sky was clear and hard with stars, and the cold had a bite to it that promised frost by morning. On the second floor, a light burned behind Bridget's window. I could see her shape there, sitting in her chair, watching the yard as she always watched the yard. I did not look long. She was there. She was watching. That was enough.

I went back inside and locked the kitchen door behind me. The bolt slid home with a sound I knew as well as my own breathing -- the particular scrape and click of iron in its housing, the sound that meant the day was over and the inn was mine again, and I was alone in it.

I was alone in it.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

I had not seen it when I came in -- or perhaps I had seen it and not registered it, the way one fails to register a wasp on a windowsill until it moves. Nan must have put it there. The direction was written in a hand I did not recognise, but the seal I knew. Dark red wax, pressed with a signet I had seen on documents and correspondence for as long as I could remember. Stainforth.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The paper was good -- heavier than anything I would buy for myself, cream-coloured, with a faint watermark visible when I held it to the candlelight. The seal was unbroken. It had been waiting for me.

I broke it.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though for different reasons. I read it standing, by the light of the kitchen candle, and I read it twice to be certain I had understood.

He had received an offer for the White Hound property -- the building, the land, the stabling, the yard. His nephew, Mr. George Stainforth, wished to take possession of the premises and convert them to use as a hotel suited to a better class of traveller. The current lease, held in my name, was due for renewal in the spring. Mr. Stainforth did not intend to renew it. He was, however, prepared to discuss terms for an orderly transition, and he proposed a meeting at his offices in Stevenage on the fourteenth of November. He trusted I would see the wisdom of a cooperative arrangement. He remained my obedient servant.

I set the letter down on the table. The candle guttered and steadied. The kitchen was very quiet -- the particular quiet of a room that has been used hard all day and is now holding its breath.

Six weeks. He had given me until the spring, but the meeting was in three weeks, and the message underneath the courtesy was plain enough: agree to go quietly, or be pushed.

I looked at the letter on the table. I looked at the ledger beside it. I looked at the kitchen -- my kitchen, my mother's kitchen, my grandmother's kitchen -- and I said, aloud, to no one:

"You'll have your meeting, Mr. Stainforth. And I will have my lease."
