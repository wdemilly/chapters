The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the smudge before it could ruin the figure beneath.

Six weeks. That was what the ledger said when I subtracted what we held from what we owed, and the ledger did not lie, however much I might have preferred it to be vague about the matter. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between solvency and catastrophe was exactly as thin as the paper under my thumb. I closed the book and set it on the shelf above the kitchen dresser, spine out, where I could reach it without thinking. I had been reaching for it without thinking since I was fourteen.

The kitchen fire needed building up. The coals from last night had gone to white ash with a faint orange heartbeat at the centre, and I knelt on the stone floor and fed it kindling, then a split log, then another, and blew until the flame caught and held. My knees ached when I stood. October had come in cold this year, and the windows were rimed at their edges with a lace of frost that would not burn off until midmorning. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to the taproom.

The taproom fire was dead. I rebuilt it from nothing, which took longer, and while it caught I checked the bar stock. Three casks of ale, one nearly spent. The gin was low. The brandy was adequate, but adequate was not comfortable, and comfortable was not what I could afford. I counted bottles, noted what was needed, and moved on to the accounts from last night -- two shillings and eightpence in the box, which matched what I had tallied before bed. Nothing missing. Nothing wasted. Nothing to spare.

I wanted -- and this was the thought I allowed myself once each morning, like a single spoonful of sugar in otherwise sensible tea -- I wanted one week where the numbers were not a calculation but a certainty. One week where I opened the ledger and felt something other than the low hum of arithmetic keeping disaster at arm's length. But wanting was not working, and the taproom fire had caught, so I wiped my hands on my apron and went out the back.

Jem was in the stable yard. He had the big bay's bridle over his arm -- the one arm he still possessed -- and was straightening the cheekpiece with his teeth and his hand in a single practised motion that looked impossible and was, to him, as ordinary as breathing. He did not look up when I crossed the yard. He did not need to. The tack was set right, the stalls mucked, the water trough filled. The morning required it, and Jem answered the morning the way he answered everything: without being asked.

I went back inside. Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins half-set in her reddish hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar and began wiping it down with a cloth she kept in the same spot every night. We had done this before. We had done this many hundreds of times. There was nothing to say about it, and so neither of us said anything.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual -- the London coach was late more often than it was punctual, and I had stopped expecting otherwise around the age of twenty -- but it was also running short. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both in foul temper. The first was a stout woman in a brown travelling cloak whose trunk had a split hinge, and she had opinions about this that she shared with me, with Nan, with the empty dining room, and with the general air of Hertfordshire. The second was a thin clergyman who wanted tea and silence, in that order, and was getting neither.

I directed Jem to the trunk before the woman finished her first sentence. He appeared at the coach door without being called -- he had a sense for luggage in distress -- and had the thing braced under his arm and halfway to the inn before she turned around to point at it. She blinked. Then she followed him inside, her complaints trailing off into a puzzled silence that was, I suspected, the first quiet moment of her journey.

The coachman climbed down and stretched his back with the slow grimace of a man who had been sitting on a hard bench since Islington.

"Road's gone to ruin north of Hatfield," he said, pulling off his gloves. "Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel. Someone ought to speak to the turnpike trust."

"Someone has," I said. "Twice. They've promised repairs before the frost sets in."

He gave me the look that men give when they suspect a woman is being optimistic. "And you believe them?"

"I believe them the same way I believe the coach will run on time. I don't, but I plan for it anyway. Will you eat, or are you pressing on?"

"Pressing on." He pulled his gloves back on. "If I've still got wheels by Baldock, I'll count it a victory."

I gave him two minutes because two minutes was what the schedule allowed, and then the coach was gone, the yard was clearing, and the stout woman was being shown upstairs by Nan, who had appeared at the dining room door with the efficiency of someone who had been watching the whole business through the glass.

The morning settled. The clergyman got his tea. The woman got her room. Jem took the trunk. The yard went quiet. Nan wiped down the bar with one eye on the front door, a habit so fixed I sometimes thought she did it in her sleep. I noticed. I said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past ten, as he did on the first Tuesday of every month, with his cart of tallow candles and his increasingly creative arithmetic.

"Tenpence ha'penny the dozen," he said, setting the first box on the kitchen table with the careful placement of a man presenting a gift.

"That's a ha'penny more than last month," I said. "Which was a ha'penny more than the month before."

"Tallow's dear, Miss Dale. The war's done but the prices haven't come home yet."

"Mr. Fenton, you buy your tallow from Jessop's in Hatfield at fourpence the pound, same as you have for three years. Jessop hasn't raised his price because Jessop has more tallow than he can sell, on account of the army not needing it anymore. You charge the Red Lion ninepence the dozen, and you charge the Swan ninepence the dozen, and you charge me tenpence ha'penny because you think I won't have checked."

He had the grace to look uncomfortable, which was not the same as having the grace to look ashamed.

"Ninepence ha'penny," I said.

"Miss Dale --"

"Ninepence ha'penny, or you can load your candles back on the cart and explain to your wife why you've lost the White Hound's custom over a penny a dozen."

A silence. He knew his margins. More to the point, he knew that I knew his margins, and that the road between here and his next customer was long enough to make the trip back unprofitable.

"Ninepence ha'penny," he said.

"Thank you, Mr. Fenton. Leave them by the dresser."

He left. The candles stayed. I stood in the kitchen with the smell of tallow and the sound of Fenton's cart rattling away down the lane, and I permitted myself one moment of satisfaction -- small, private, the kind that does not survive being spoken aloud. I was good at this. I was good at the thing that kept the roof over our heads. It was not nothing.

It was also not enough to change the number in the ledger, and I knew that, too. The rent, the suppliers, the chandler's creeping prices -- all of it pressed against the same immovable fact. If I married, the inn would cease to be mine to negotiate for. The lease, the accounts, the right to tell Mr. Fenton what his candles were worth -- all of it would transfer to a husband by operation of law, as neatly as if I had never existed. I had refused two proposals for precisely this reason, and I had not regretted either refusal, and I did not intend to acquire a third occasion for not regretting. The thought arrived, as it always did, with the flatness of settled arithmetic. I set it aside and went to check the dining room.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the sign on the road asked. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way of men who have been thin for long enough that the body has stopped complaining about it and simply rearranged itself around the absence. His coat was army issue, or had been once. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the left sole separating from the upper like a mouth trying to speak.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at something he is not certain he is permitted to want. His hand came up once, hesitated, dropped.

I opened the door before he could decide.

He startled. He was younger than I had thought -- the thinness aged him, but his face, beneath the dirt and the uncertain expression, was a boy's face.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

He considered lying. I watched him consider it -- the brief flicker of pride, the calculation of whether honesty would cost him more than it gained. "Not recent," he said.

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He understood. He sat. He did not ask for more than what was offered, and he did not pretend he did not need what was offered, and the plain dignity of that was the thing I noticed most.

I gave him bread and broth -- the end of yesterday's loaf, the last of the stockpot that I had been meaning to clear. He ate with the careful, deliberate pace of someone who has learned that eating too fast on an empty stomach is a mistake you only make once. He held the spoon the way a man holds a tool he is concentrating on, as though the act of eating required his full attention and he did not wish to do it badly.

"Thank you," he said when the bowl was empty. He said it to the bowl, not to me, which was how I knew he meant it.

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, after a pause that contained an entire geography of uncertainty: "There's work in the mills, I'm told."

I did not say that I had heard otherwise. I did not say anything about the war, or the roads, or the other men I had fed at this table who were headed north on the same rumour. "The road's better past Baldock," I said. "Keep to the turnpike."

He nodded. He stood. He thanked me again, and he went.

I closed the door. The kitchen was quiet. His bowl was on the table, the spoon laid neatly beside it, and I stood there looking at them for a moment longer than the task required. He had held the spoon with both hands, I realized. As if someone might take it from him. He was somebody's son, and his boots could not carry him to the end of the week.

I washed the bowl and put it away.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say, it passed in work. Two men in the taproom got loud over a disputed bill, voices rising in the particular key of men who have been sitting too long with too much ale and too little purpose. I crossed from the bar and set my hand flat on their table.

"Settle it or settle your accounts and go," I said. "I don't mind which."

They settled it. Nan watched from behind the bar and did not need to intervene.

The coach came and went. Rooms were turned over. The kitchen was scrubbed and set for the evening meal. Nan polished glasses. Jem reshod the grey mare. I sat in the kitchen between tasks with the ledger open, not because the numbers had changed but because the act of looking at them was a kind of discipline, a refusal to be surprised by what was coming.

By evening the last guests were settled, the fires checked, the bar tallied, the kitchen closed. I moved through the inn's final circuit alone -- taproom fire down to coals, cellar hatch locked, stable yard quiet. Jem would have seen to the horses; I could hear the soft shift and blow of them settling in their stalls. I crossed the yard toward the back door and glanced up, as I always did, at the second floor. A lit shape behind Bridget's window -- the faint glow of a candle and the outline of her watching. I did not look long. She was there. She was always there.

Inside, I locked the back door and set the bolt. My hands at the end of the day were what they always were: reddened from cold water and lye soap, roughened across the knuckles, a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught the skin. I noticed it without registering it as significant. It was not significant. It was the ordinary cost of a day spent handling paper and casks and other people's problems with my bare hands.

The inn was quiet in the way of a place that has been used hard and is now resting. I stood in the kitchen with the bolt set and the fire banked, and I let myself notice -- just for a moment, the way you let yourself notice a bruise only after the work is done -- that I was alone. That the quiet was not peace but absence, and that I had chosen it, and that the choosing did not always feel like victory.

Then I saw the letter.

It was on the kitchen table, half-hidden beneath the cloth I had used to wipe down the surface after supper. Someone -- Nan, most likely -- had left it there without comment. I picked it up and knew the hand at once. The direction was written in the careful, measured script of a man who did not waste ink on flourishes: *Miss D. Dale, The White Hound, Great North Road, Hertfordshire.* Stainforth's seal on the back, pressed deep into dark red wax.

I turned it over once.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth's letters were always brief; he was not a man who believed in persuading where he could simply inform. I read it standing up, by the light of the kitchen candle, and the words were exactly as precise and as final as the hand that had written them.

He had a buyer for the property. His nephew, Mr. Philip Stainforth, intended to take possession of the site and convert the premises to use as a gentleman's posting house. The current lease, held in my name by the courtesy of his late father's arrangement with my late mother, would not be renewed at the quarter. He was offering me the opportunity to discuss terms -- his word, *terms*, as though this were a negotiation rather than an eviction -- at a meeting to be arranged at his convenience before the first of December.

Six weeks. The same six weeks the ledger had given me this morning, only now they were not a margin. They were a deadline.

I set the letter down on the table. I smoothed the crease where I had gripped it too hard. The candle guttered in a draught from the chimney, and the kitchen was very quiet, and Stainforth's handwriting lay on the scrubbed wood like a sentence handed down from a bench.

I looked at it for a long moment.

"Well, Mr. Stainforth," I said to the empty room, "you had better bring your nephew, because he is going to need someone to explain to him why he has just made the worst purchase in Hertfordshire."
