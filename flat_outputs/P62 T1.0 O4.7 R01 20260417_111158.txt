Chapter One

The ledger said six weeks. I read the column twice, because the numbers had not improved since the last time I read them, and closed the book.

Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and a shortfall on it that I could close with a good coaching fortnight or widen with a bad one. The book went under the bar of the counting shelf, where it lived between a tin of sealing wax and a jar that had once held candied ginger and now held nothing at all. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists. The kitchen was cold in the way a kitchen is cold at five in the morning when a fire has been built up but not yet made up its mind to be useful.

I wanted, quite suddenly and with no warning, a cup of tea brought to me by someone else.

The want lasted as long as it took to cross the flagstones to the range. I prodded the fire, fed it, and set the kettle on the hob. Then I went through the rooms the same as every morning: taproom fire laid and caught, bar stock checked against the chalk on the slate, the brass on the doors polished where a guest's sleeve would rest. Last night's accounts tallied and squared. The night candles were burned down to their nubs and I pinched off the last of them and dropped the ends in the tin for rendering. Nothing wasted. Waste was a luxury that belonged to houses with servants and to widows with settlements, and I was neither.

At the back door I looked out at the yard. The puddles at the trough were rimed white at the edges and the cobbles had the dull gleam of a cold that had not decided whether to freeze or only to complain. Jem was by the tack room already, mending a breast strap. He had taught the stump to do the work of the other hand, and the pinned sleeve moved where it needed to move, and I did not watch him work, because he did not like to be watched.

Nan came through the kitchen door at ten to six with two pins in her mouth and her apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without looking at me. We had done this before. Many times.

The London coach came in at a quarter past eight, which was late, but the London coach was frequently late, and only a fool counted on it. Two passengers stepped down where there should have been four. One had a trunk with a split hinge. The other had a stiff neck and a pinched mouth, and it was plain from the set of her that she was going to have opinions.

"My trunk," she said, before her boots had met the cobbles, "is ruined."

"Jem," I said, and he was there, because he always was. "The hinge. Twine for now, and we'll see to it proper after breakfast."

"I cannot have my linen crushed."

"No, ma'am, you cannot. We'll lay it out in the small parlour and press what needs pressing. Nan will see to it." I did not wait for her to decide whether she was still angry. "Breakfast is in the dining room. There's a fire."

She opened her mouth. She closed it. She went to the fire.

The coachman had come down stiff-legged and was slapping his gloves against his thigh. I knew him. Dawkins, out of the Saracen's Head at Snow Hill, a sensible man with a wife in Islington and a weakness for strong cheese.

"Miss Dale."

"Mr. Dawkins."

"Road above Hatfield's gone to the devil. Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel. Somebody wants to tell the turnpike trust before they lose a coach in earnest."

"I'll write to Mr. Shipley this week."

"Appreciate it."

"Two minutes for your tea, then you're off. Nan's put it by the bar."

He touched his hat. "Bless you, Miss Dale."

"Mind the hill down to Welwyn. The frost is worse on the shady side."

He went in. Jem was already at the trunk with the twine. The yard cleared itself, as yards will when the people who are meant to leave leave, and I stood for a moment at the pump and let the cold wake my hands. Nan was wiping the bar, her eye on the door every second wipe. I saw it. I said nothing. There are prices that get paid for asking, and she was a grown woman of one-and-twenty.

Partlet the chandler came at eleven, as he always did on a Wednesday, which was his way of pretending he was not a little early. He set his box on the counter with more ceremony than was required by a box of tallow candles.

"Miss Dale."

"Mr. Partlet."

"I'm afraid the price is gone up again."

"Has it."

"Tallow's dear this year. You know how it is. The summer that wasn't. The beasts were lean in September and the renderers are squeezing every farthing."

"A penny a dozen, I suppose."

"Tuppence."

I took up the invoice and held it at an angle to the window, as though the light might correct an arithmetical error. It would not. The light did not care about Partlet's margins and neither, this morning, did I.

"Mr. Partlet. You pay Wakeling's in Ware one and three the stone for raw tallow. You pay the girls at Hoddesdon two shillings a week to dip, and you get a hundred and forty dozen out of them between Monday and Saturday. I can do the sum. So can you."

"Miss Dale, I never said---"

"You charge the Bull at Stevenage a ha'penny less a dozen than you charge me. You charge the Red Lion at Baldock the same as the Bull. There is no reason in the world for the White Hound to pay tuppence more than a house with half my standing order."

"Custom is custom."

"Custom is what we say it is, between the two of us, in this room. I'll take your candles at last quarter's price. You'll keep the standing order. Or you'll cart the box back to Ware and explain to Mrs. Partlet why you lost a house that takes twelve dozen a month."

He did me the courtesy of looking at the ceiling for a decent interval before he gave in.

"Last quarter's price."

"Thank you, Mr. Partlet."

"You've your mother's tongue on you."

"I'll take that as a compliment, though I don't believe you meant it as one."

He almost smiled. He almost did not. I watched the door close behind him and allowed myself one small, dry breath, which was as close to a celebration as the morning was going to get. I had done a thing I did well. I was permitted to notice.

I was not permitted to dwell. Between the chandler and the noon dinners I went through the spirit cupboard, counted bottles, and did the arithmetic I had been doing since I was twelve. The rent, the tallow, the short coach. Set against these, the only solvent I had ever been offered: a husband, and with a husband the lease would pass out of my hand the moment the curate closed the book. I had said no to Tom Ashby when I was nineteen and to Mr. Verney the surveyor when I was three-and-twenty, and I had said it the same way both times, which is to say plainly, and without apology, and I had not been sorry since. If I had ever wondered whether I might have wanted to say yes, I had wondered it in the dark, where such things are cheap.

A knock at the kitchen door, before I had heard the footsteps that should have come before it. I turned from the stockpot.

There was a young man outside, and he had not knocked at first. He had stood for some seconds in the yard, looking at my door, deciding whether he was welcome enough to knock. I had seen him through the window glass.

He was thin. He was perhaps twenty. His coat had been a decent coat, once, and his boots had been decent boots, and neither of them was decent now. The welt of the left boot had come away from the upper and showed the stocking beneath, and the stocking was not clean. He had a soldier's haircut grown out to a boy's.

He knocked. He was prepared for me to say no.

"When did you last eat?" I said.

He thought about lying. I watched him decide against it. "Day before yesterday, ma'am. A bit of bread at Welwyn."

"Kitchen, not taproom. You sit at that stool. You do not come through that door." I pointed at the inner door to the passage. "Do you understand me?"

"Yes, ma'am."

"Sit."

He sat. I gave him bread, and a bowl of the barley broth that was meant for the midday ordinary, and a cup of small beer. He held the spoon with both hands, as my father had the winter he was ill. He ate slowly at first, because he knew enough to know that quick eating after not eating was its own cruelty, and then he ate as a hungry boy eats, and I turned to the pot and stirred a pot that did not want stirring, so as not to watch him.

"Where are you bound?" I said, when he was most of the way down the bowl.

"North, ma'am. My mother's in Grantham."

"That's a fair way."

"Yes, ma'am."

"Finish. There's cheese."

He finished. He thanked me twice, the second time with an awkward bob that was almost a bow and almost a salute and was neither. I gave him the heel of the loaf wrapped in a scrap of muslin and told him to keep to the verges north of Baldock because the ruts were bad. He thanked me a third time and was gone.

I stood at the door a moment after it shut. His boots. His mother in Grantham. A boy who had sat at my kitchen stool and held a spoon with both hands because his hands did not trust themselves. Somebody had carried him as a baby, and somebody had kissed his head, and somebody was waiting in a house in Grantham with a candle in the window and nothing like enough bread.

I stirred the pot. The pot forgave me.

The afternoon came. The afternoon went. Two carters in the taproom got loud over a wager concerning a mare neither of them owned, and I set my palms on the bar and said, "Gentlemen. Not in my house." It was enough. They went back to their ale with the sheepish courtesy of men who knew they had been in the wrong and were grateful not to be made to admit it further. Nan caught my eye and returned to her glasses.

The inn was quiet. It was always quiet between coaches. I reset the rooms on the first floor, turned two mattresses, brought up clean linen, came down, went through the kitchen with the cook for the evening's mutton, and wrote a note to Mr. Shipley about the road above Hatfield. The note went in the box by the door for the morning post.

By nine I was locking up. The last guests had gone to bed or to the private parlour to drink themselves toward it. The taproom fire was down to coals; I raked them forward, banked the ash, set the guard. The cellar hatch took the big key and I heard the bolt drop in its seat. In the yard the horses had been settled. I did not need to check. Jem had been through.

My hands at the end of the day were red from cold water and from lye, and there was a cut on the pad of my right index finger where the edge of Partlet's invoice had taken me earlier, a thin clean slice that had not bothered to bleed properly. I put it to my mouth. That was all the thought I gave it. A paper cut from a chandler was the sort of wound I could afford.

The yard was still. The stable lanterns were out. I looked up, because I always did, at the second floor on the west side. There was a lit shape at Bridget's window. She was watching. She had seen me cross the yard, and she would see me cross it back, and she would say nothing to me in the morning about either, because Bridget was civilized, and because she knew better than most that a woman alone in a yard at nine at night does not require comment.

I shot the last bolt on the front door and set my forehead for a moment against the wood. It was cool. It was solid. It had been hung by my grandfather in 1770 and it had not yet failed in its office, and I was tired, and I was alone, and I let myself know it.

Then I went back through to the kitchen, because there was the letter.

It had been on the dresser since the afternoon post. I had seen it and walked past it and seen it and walked past it, and now there was nothing else in the house to do. The direction was in a hand I knew. The seal was Stainforth's: the bird with its wing out, pressed a little crooked, as it always was, as if even his wax would not quite behave.

I lit a fresh candle from the embers in the range. I sat down at the table. I picked the letter up and turned it once in my hand, because I had earned that much hesitation, and then I broke the seal.

*Miss Dale*, it began, and the rest was what I had known it would be from the moment I had seen the wing in the wax, and worse.

He wrote that he had given "long and sympathetic consideration" to the matter of the White Hound's lease, which came up for renewal at Lady Day. He wrote that he had, with regret, concluded that the present arrangement could not continue on its current terms. He wrote that he had received an offer from a party whom he did not name but who was prepared to take up the lease at a figure considerably in advance of what I presently paid, and to undertake certain improvements to the premises "consonant with the traveling public of a better stamp." He wrote that, in deference to my mother's memory and my own long tenure, he proposed a meeting at his house on the Friday fortnight, at which I might lay before him any case I wished to lay. He wrote that he would require my answer by the last post of that day, and that failing such answer he would consider the matter closed.

He wrote that he remained, with every good wish, my obedient servant.

I read it through once. I read it through again, because the first time I had read it the blood had gone out of my hands and I wanted to be sure.

He was offering me a meeting as a man offers a condemned woman a chaplain. He had a buyer. He had a price. He had a fortnight, and a deadline, and the civility to pretend that any of it was open to discussion. The lease had been my grandmother's and my mother's and mine, and Mr. Phineas Stainforth proposed to hand it over to a nephew, or a cousin, or some other well-mannered young man with a plan for a better class of traveller, because he had, at last, found a way to correct the irregularity of a woman.

The wax wing lay on the table where I had broken it. I looked at it for some time.

"A fortnight," I said, aloud, to the empty kitchen, and heard my own voice come out steadier than I had expected. "Very well, Mr. Stainforth. Let's see what you've sold that isn't yours to sell."

I folded the letter along its creases and put it in the pocket of my apron, because I did not yet want it on the dresser where anyone could find it, and I took up the candle, and I went to bed.
