The ink had bled through to the second page of the ledger, which meant the nib wanted replacing, which meant another ha'penny she'd rather not spend.

I pressed my thumb against the blot and watched it smear. Six weeks. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the numbers in the column beside my thumb were not going to rearrange themselves into a more agreeable sum no matter how long I sat staring at them in the dark. I knew this because I had been staring at them since half four, and they had remained stubbornly, uncharitably fixed.

I closed the ledger.

The kitchen was cold in the way that October kitchens are cold when the fire has been banked overnight and no one has yet had the decency to coax it back. I knelt at the grate and worked the bellows until the coals caught, then fed in kindling -- ash first, then a split of elm that had been drying since August. The elm was slow to take. I held the bellows steady and thought about nothing, which was a skill I had perfected over many early mornings and which served me better than prayer.

The fire found its temper. I hung the stockpot on the crane and swung it over the heat, then filled it from the pail I'd drawn last night. There would be broth by midday if I started the bones now. There would be broth by midday regardless, because there was always broth by midday. That was how the White Hound worked. That was how I worked.

I wanted -- and this was a thought I permitted myself only in the early hours, when no one was awake to see it cross my face -- I wanted, just once, for the numbers to come out right without my having to subtract something I needed. A new nib. A second candle in the hallway. The luxury of not counting.

The wanting passed. It always did. I wiped my hands on my apron and went to open the taproom.

The fire there took faster -- I'd laid it the night before with good dry wood, and the draught from the chimney was reliable if nothing else in this building was. I checked the bar stock: three casks of ale, one nearly spent; the gin bottle at a quarter; the brandy untouched because no one who stopped at the White Hound was paying brandy prices. I noted the ale in my head and moved on. Last night's accounts were already tallied -- I'd done them before bed, standing at this bar with a tallow stub guttering beside the cashbox. Tuppence over what I'd expected, which meant someone had paid for a second drink they hadn't ordered, or I'd miscounted. I preferred to believe I'd miscounted. Generosity from strangers made me uneasy.

The windows were rimed at their edges with the thin frost that comes before true cold sets in. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists, tucked the cuffs into my palms, and went out the back.

Jem was already in the stable yard. He had a bridle across his knee and was working at a buckle with his one hand and the practised brace of his stump, his back to me, his shoulders set in the particular way that meant he'd been up as long as I had and did not wish to discuss it. The horses were quiet. The yard was swept. I looked, confirmed what I already knew -- that Jem Bowker did not require instruction -- and went back inside.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar and took her place behind it without a word, running one hand along the wood to check that it was dry. We had done this before. We had done this perhaps two thousand times. The efficiency of it was its own language, and I was grateful for a language that required no pleasantries at this hour.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual -- the London coach was late the way rain was wet, which is to say predictably and to no one's particular surprise -- but it was running short as well. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them in moods that suggested the road from Hatfield had not improved since last I'd heard complaints about it. The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the compressed fury of someone who has been personally offended by every rut in Hertfordshire. The second was a thin man who said nothing and looked as though he intended to continue saying nothing for the foreseeable future. I could work with silence. Fury required management.

The woman's trunk had a split hinge, which she announced to me, to the yard, and to the empty air above Jem's head as though the hinge were a matter for Parliament.

"The latch gave way at Hatfield," she said. "Hatfield. I might have lost everything."

"Jem," I said. He was already moving. He took the trunk by its leather handle and bore it toward the door without comment, holding it at an angle that kept the split hinge from gaping further. The woman watched him go with the look of someone whose grievance has been acknowledged but not sufficiently lamented.

"Your room is the second on the left at the top of the stairs," I told her. "Breakfast is laid in the dining room. The trunk will be there before you are."

She opened her mouth -- to protest, or to add a footnote to her complaints -- but I had already turned to the coachman, who had climbed down from the box and was stamping his boots on the cobbles with the weary aggression of a man who has been sitting in cold wind since dawn.

"The road above Hatfield," he said.

"I know."

"It's worse than last month. There's a stretch past the Stanborough crossroads that'll break an axle before Christmas."

"You've reported it to the turnpike trust?"

He gave me a look that contained the whole of his opinion on turnpike trusts. "I'm reporting it to you, Miss Dale, because you're the one who'll have to explain to your next set of passengers why the coach is late again."

"Tell your passengers to write to the trust. I'll give them the address. It won't help, but it'll keep them occupied on the road and you'll have a quieter journey."

He almost smiled. Almost. "Right then."

"There's ale inside if you've ten minutes."

"I've five."

"Then you'd better drink fast."

He went. The passengers went -- upstairs, to the dining room, out of my immediate concern. Jem took the trunk. The yard cleared. I stood for a moment in the sudden quiet and watched Nan through the window. She was wiping down the bar with one hand, her eyes on the door, steady and ready and waiting for whoever came through it next. She caught my gaze and held it for half a breath -- not a question, not a reassurance, just the brief acknowledgement of two people who understood that the morning was now properly underway and there was nothing to do about it but continue.

The chandler arrived at eleven.

Mr. Fenton was a man of medium height, medium colouring, and an above-average talent for raising his prices in small enough increments that he imagined I would not notice. He came through the kitchen door with a crate of tallow candles balanced on one hip and set them on the table beside my ledger with the careful placement of a man presenting evidence.

"Morning, Miss Dale."

"Mr. Fenton. What's the price this month?"

"Fourpence ha'penny the dozen."

I looked at the candles. I looked at Mr. Fenton. The candles, to their credit, did not flinch.

"It was fourpence last month," I said. "And threepence three-farthings in April."

"Tallow's gone up."

"Tallow has gone up a farthing the pound since midsummer. I buy from the same suppliers you do, Mr. Fenton -- or rather, I buy from the people who sell to the people you buy from, and I pay attention to what they charge. Your margin at fourpence was comfortable. At fourpence ha'penny, it's handsome. I don't begrudge you a living, but I won't fund your retirement."

He shifted the crate an inch to the left, as though repositioning it might reposition the negotiation. "The Bull at Baldock pays fourpence ha'penny without complaint."

"The Bull at Baldock hasn't paid fourpence ha'penny since July, and I know this because Mrs. Coker told me so herself at the Stevenage market, where she also told me that she's considering a new chandler out of Welwyn. You may take that intelligence for what it's worth."

Fenton's mouth thinned. He looked at the crate. He looked at me. He performed a small, private arithmetic behind his eyes.

"Fourpence farthing," he said.

"Done."

He left. The candles stayed.

I stood in the kitchen with my hands on the edge of the table and allowed myself one moment -- just the one -- of satisfaction. It was a farthing a dozen. It was nothing. It was the difference between a ledger that balanced and one that didn't, multiplied across every week between now and quarter day, and I had earned it with knowledge and nerve and the particular advantage of being a woman whom other women talked to at market.

The thought followed the satisfaction the way a shadow follows a body in afternoon light: that none of this -- the negotiation, the ledger, the lease itself -- would survive my saying yes to any man who asked me. The law was plain on the subject. A wife's property was her husband's property. The White Hound, the income, the right to stand in my own kitchen and argue a chandler down by a farthing -- all of it would pass to whatever husband I was foolish enough to acquire. I had been asked twice. I had refused twice. The refusals had cost me more than the men who'd asked would ever understand, because the refusing was not the hard part. The hard part was knowing that I wanted to be wanted, and refusing anyway.

I picked up the crate of candles and carried them to the storeroom.

He came around the back, as the sign asked.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked -- a young man, thin in the particular way of people who have been thin for a long time and have stopped noticing it. His coat was too large for him, army issue by the cut, and his boots were coming apart where the sole met the upper. He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand raised and did not knock. He was looking at the door the way a dog looks at a threshold it has been chased from before -- measuring the risk, calculating whether the effort of asking was worth the probable refusal.

I opened the door before he could decide.

He startled. Up close, he was younger than I'd guessed from the window. Twenty, perhaps. Perhaps less. His face had the scrubbed, wind-raw look of someone who has been sleeping outdoors, and his eyes moved to my hands before they moved to my face -- checking, I thought, for a weapon, or for a gesture that meant go away.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He considered the question with more care than it warranted. "Not today, ma'am."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the fire.

He sat. I cut bread -- two thick slices from yesterday's loaf -- and ladled broth from the stockpot into a bowl. I set both in front of him with a spoon and said nothing further, because nothing further was required. He ate the way hungry people eat when they are trying not to appear hungry, which is to say slowly and with a studied indifference that fooled neither of us.

When the bowl was empty, he set down the spoon and placed his hands flat on the table, palms down, as though reporting for inspection.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, after a pause that contained the whole of his uncertainty: "Somewhere north."

I nodded. He stood, and I walked him to the door, and he went out into the yard and across it and through the gate without looking back. His boots left uneven prints in the frost that was already softening on the cobbles.

I closed the door. I stood with my hand on the latch and looked at the empty bowl on the table, the spoon laid neatly beside it. He had held the spoon the way my father used to hold a pen -- too tightly, as though someone might take it from him.

I washed the bowl and put it back on the shelf.

The afternoon passed the way afternoons pass at a coaching inn between rushes -- in small tasks that accumulate into hours without announcing themselves. I reset the rooms. I checked the kitchen stores against the evening's requirements. Nan polished glasses behind the bar with the unhurried precision of someone who understood that the next wave of custom would arrive whether the glasses were polished or not, but preferred to be ready.

Two men in the taproom got loud over something -- a disputed card hand, from what I could hear through the doorway, though the specifics hardly mattered. Disputes in the taproom were like weather: variable in form, predictable in pattern.

I walked in. They looked up.

"Settle it outside or settle it quietly. Those are your choices, and you have until I finish this sentence to pick one."

They picked quietly. Nan, behind the bar, did not look up from her glass.

The evening came on slowly, the way it does in late October -- not a dramatic darkening but a gradual withdrawal of light, as though the day were losing interest in itself. I moved through the closing routine with the ease of long practice. Taproom fire raked down to coals. Cellar hatch locked. Bar tallied and the cashbox put away. Kitchen cleaned, pots scoured, the stockpot set to the side of the grate where it would stay warm without boiling dry. I checked the stable yard from the back door -- quiet, the horses settled, Jem's lantern already dark in the room above the tack store.

I crossed the yard toward the inn's front door on my last circuit and looked up. The second floor, the window at the far end: a lit shape behind the glass. Bridget, in her chair, watching the yard as she watched it every night. I did not pause. She was there. She was always there.

I bolted the front door. I turned the key. I stood in the hallway with my back against the wood and my hands at my sides and felt the silence of the inn settle around me like water filling a basin. My hands were red from the cold water I'd used to scour the pots, and there was a thin cut on my right index finger where the edge of Fenton's invoice had caught the skin -- a paper cut, absurd, the smallest possible wound, and I had not noticed it until now.

I was alone. I noticed this the way I noticed the cut -- belatedly, without surprise, as a fact that had been true for some time and was simply making itself visible.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

It had not been there at supper. Someone -- Nan, probably, or Jem -- had brought it in from wherever the post had been left and set it beside the ledger without remark. I did not know how long it had been sitting there. Long enough for the seal to catch the lamplight when I came through the door, red wax with an impression I recognized before I read the direction.

Stainforth.

I picked it up. The paper was heavy, cream-coloured, the direction written in a clerk's careful hand. I turned it over once. The seal was intact, stamped with the Stainforth crest -- a stag's head, pretentious for a family that had been yeoman farmers two generations back, but Mr. Phineas Stainforth had aspirations, and aspirations required heraldry.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth's letters were always brief; he was not a man who wasted words when he could waste other people's time instead. I read it standing, by the light of the single candle I had carried from the hallway.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable interest for the property and grounds presently held under lease by yourself, including the coaching inn known as the White Hound. The prospective purchaser wishes to take possession at the earliest opportunity. I am prepared to discuss terms for the surrender of your lease at a meeting to be arranged at your convenience, provided such meeting takes place before the 30th of November next. I trust you will appreciate the generosity of this notice. Yours &c., P. Stainforth.*

I read it again. The thirtieth of November. Five weeks. He was giving me five weeks to agree to hand over everything my grandmother had built, everything my mother had kept, everything I had held together with my bare hands for two years -- five weeks, and he called it generous.

I set the letter down on the table beside the ledger, beside the blotted ink, beside the column of numbers that had refused all morning to become enough. The candle guttered in a draught from the window. The kitchen was very quiet.

I looked at the letter. I looked at the empty kitchen. I thought of Fenton and his fourpence ha'penny, of the soldier and his borrowed spoon, of the frost on the windows and the six weeks in the ledger that had just become five, and of Mr. Phineas Stainforth sitting in his study with his stag's-head seal and his considerable offers, waiting for a woman to do the sensible thing and surrender.

"You'll have your meeting, Mr. Stainforth," I said to the empty room. "And I will bring the lease, and the accounts, and every farthing I've fought for, and you will find that I am not my father and I am not for sale."

The candle steadied. I picked up the letter, folded it, and put it in my apron pocket, where it sat against my hip like a coal that had not yet decided whether to burn.
