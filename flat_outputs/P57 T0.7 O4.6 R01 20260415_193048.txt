The ink had dried wrong on the third column, and I was licking my thumb to smudge it out when I realized the number underneath was worse than the error above it.

Six weeks. That was what the ledger said, if I was generous with the arithmetic and unkind to myself about the coal. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and between now and then, every shilling would need to do the work of two. I shut the book. The leather cover was cracked along the spine where my mother's hands had opened it ten thousand times before mine, and the crack had deepened since October began, which meant I was pressing too hard or worrying too much or both.

The kitchen was cold. Not the sharp, dramatic cold of deep winter, but the mean, damp, creeping kind that October sends ahead like a scout -- the sort that gets into your knuckles first and then your disposition. I knelt at the hearth and built the fire up from last night's banked coals, feeding it kindling in careful stages because kindling cost money and patience did not. The flames caught. I held my hands near them longer than was strictly necessary, not because I was idle but because I was tired, and tired hands are clumsy hands, and clumsy hands break things.

I wanted, just once, to sit in this kitchen in the morning and feel something other than arithmetic.

That was a thought I could not afford, so I stood up and got on with it.

The taproom fire next. I carried the ash bucket through and swept the grate, laid the new fire, checked the draught. The flue had been pulling badly since September and I'd stuffed a rag into the gap where the brickwork had shifted, which was the kind of repair that solved nothing and prevented everything from getting worse, which was, if I thought about it, a fair summary of most of what I did. The bar stock I checked by touch and count -- four casks of ale, the gin three-quarters full, the brandy barely a third. I noted the brandy in my head. I would not be ordering more until the month turned.

The windows along the front were rimed at their edges, a thin crust of frost that caught the first grey light and held it. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and tucked the cuffs into my palms. The inn at this hour was mine in a way it was not at any other time -- no guests, no demands, no voices wanting things. Just the building and its sounds: the creak of old timber settling, the faint rattle of a shutter on the second floor that Jem had promised to fix and would fix when he got to it, which would be when he got to it and not a moment sooner.

I tallied last night's accounts standing at the bar with the candle guttering. Fourteen shillings, eightpence. Not enough and not disastrous. The coach yesterday had been short two passengers, and the ones who had come had drunk modestly and eaten less. I wrote the figure in the margin of the ledger page I'd carry to the kitchen later and closed the book again.

Out the back, the stable yard was already stirring. Jem was there -- I could see him through the window -- his one hand working a bridle straight on its hook, his pinned sleeve tucked neatly out of the way. He did not look up when I opened the door, and I did not greet him, because we had done this before, the two of us, hundreds of mornings, and the silence between us at this hour was not unfriendly. It was professional.

I checked the yard. The cobbles wanted sweeping. The water trough was full, which meant Jem had already been to the pump. The stable door hung true on its hinges -- he'd rehung it last week after the wind caught it -- and through the gap I could see the horses standing quiet in their stalls, their breath making clouds in the cold air. Everything in order. Everything accounted for.

Back inside, Nan came through the kitchen door with her pins half set and her apron already tied, which was Nan's way of telling the world she was ready for work even if her hair was not. She went to the bar without a word, took up the cloth, and began wiping down the counter. I watched her for three seconds -- the quick, efficient sweep of her hand, the way she checked the taps without being asked -- and then I went back to the kitchen, because the morning coach was due in an hour and there was bread to slice and a kettle to set and no one was going to do any of it by standing about admiring the staff.

The London coach came in forty minutes late, which was not unusual for late October, when the roads north of Hatfield turned to something between mud and malice. I heard it before I saw it -- the particular grinding clatter of a coach that has been fighting bad road for the last three miles -- and I was at the front door with my apron straight and my face arranged before it pulled into the yard.

Two passengers instead of four. The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended the step with the compressed fury of someone who has been personally offended by every mile since London. The second was a thin gentleman who looked as though he had been trying to sleep and had been thwarted by the stout woman's opinions, which she was still delivering at volume.

"The trunk," the woman said, pointing. "The hinge is split. I told the guard at Barnet and he did nothing."

I looked at the trunk. The hinge was indeed split -- brass, cheap, the kind that gives way if you pack too heavily or look at it wrong. "Jem," I said, not raising my voice, because Jem was already there, materializing from the stable yard the way he always did when there was luggage to manage, and he had the trunk off the coach and under his arm before the woman could draw breath for her next complaint.

"We'll have that seen to," I said. "If you'll follow me, I'll show you to the dining room. There's tea ready."

She blinked. Whatever she'd been preparing to say -- and she had clearly been preparing something extensive -- died on her lips. She followed me inside. The thin gentleman drifted after her like a leaf caught in her wake.

The coachman climbed down last. He was a man I knew by sight -- Harding, who ran the Tuesday and Friday route -- and he had the look of someone who wanted a word and did not want it overheard.

"The road's gone above Hatfield," he said, pulling his gloves off. "Proper gone. There's a stretch past the Lemsford turning where the ruts are two feet deep and the drainage ditch has given way. I near lost a wheel."

"How far past the turning?"

"Quarter mile. Maybe less. It's the same place it was bad last spring, only worse."

"I'll send word to the surveyor," I said, which was what I said every time, and which would accomplish exactly as much as it had accomplished every other time, which was nothing. But it was what I had. "You'll want something hot before you go on."

"Haven't time." He was already pulling his gloves back on. "Running late as it is."

"Then take this." I handed him the heel of bread and the wedge of cheese I'd wrapped in cloth and brought out with me, because Harding was always running late and never had time and would drive the next twelve miles on nothing if someone didn't put food in his hand. He took it with a nod that served for thanks, climbed back up, and was gone.

The yard cleared. Jem took the trunk inside. The thin gentleman had vanished upstairs. The stout woman was in the dining room, where I could hear her telling Nan about the state of the roads as though Nan had personally constructed them. Nan's voice came back, level and pleasant, and I did not need to intervene, because Nan could handle a disgruntled guest the way a river handles a stone -- she just flowed around it.

I stood in the empty yard for a moment and let the quiet settle. Then I went back inside, because quiet was a luxury and the chandler was due at ten.

He arrived at half past, which was his way of reminding me that his schedule was not governed by mine. Mr. Fenton was a soft man with hard prices -- round in the middle, mild in the face, and absolutely unyielding when it came to the cost of tallow.

"Sixpence ha'penny the dozen," he said, setting the crate of candles on the kitchen table as though he were presenting a gift.

"It was fivepence three months ago."

"Tallow's up. The whole market's up. I can show you the figures if you like."

"You could show me the figures," I said, "and I could show you what Jessop in Welwyn charges for the same candle, which is fivepence farthing, and then we could both pretend we don't know that you buy your tallow from the same renderer he does, at the same price, and that the difference between your figure and his is not the market but your margin."

Fenton's mild face did something complicated. "Jessop's candles are inferior."

"Jessop's candles burn. That is the primary requirement of a candle."

"Miss Dale--"

"Fivepence ha'penny," I said. "That's a farthing more than last quarter, which I am giving you because I am reasonable, not because I believe the market has moved. And I'll take four dozen now instead of three, which means you leave here with more money in your pocket than you expected, and I don't have to see you again until January."

He looked at me. I looked at him. The kitchen was very quiet.

"Fivepence ha'penny," he said.

"Four dozen."

He counted them out. I counted the coins. He left. The candles stayed.

I permitted myself, once the door was shut, a small and private satisfaction. It was not triumph -- triumph required a worthier opponent -- but it was competence, and competence was the currency I traded in, and today the exchange rate had been favorable.

The thought that followed was less welcome, but it came anyway, the way it always did when I'd been counting coins and calculating margins. Every farthing I'd saved, every negotiation I'd won, every careful economy that kept the White Hound open -- all of it rested on a lease that bore my name only because I had not given that name away. Two men had asked me to. Two decent, unremarkable men who would have been perfectly adequate husbands and who would have become, on the day I married either of them, the legal owner of everything I'd spent my life building. I had refused them both, and I did not regret it, and I did not think about it more than once a week, which was a lie, but it was a useful one.

I wiped down the table where the candle crate had been and went to check on the dining room.

The afternoon brought the quiet that always settled over the inn between coaches -- a held breath, the building resting. Nan polished glasses behind the bar. Jem was in the yard, doing whatever Jem did in the hours when no horses needed managing, which I suspected involved sitting on an upturned bucket and staring at the sky with the philosophical intensity of a man who has seen considerably worse skies and is grateful for a dull one. I reset the two upstairs rooms that had been used, changed the linen, checked the washstand ewers, and carried the slops down myself because there was no one else to do it and because the work did not diminish me. It was the work. It was always the work.

I was back in the kitchen, sleeves rolled to the elbow, scrubbing the stockpot, when I saw him through the window.

He'd come around the back, the way the signs asked. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way that means weeks, not days. His coat had been military once; you could see where the facing had been unpicked, leaving a ghost of brighter cloth beneath. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the sole of the left one separating from the upper so that it flapped slightly with each step, and I knew without looking further that his stockings would be worn through and his feet would be blistered and there would be nothing in his pockets worth mentioning.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. Just stood there, looking at the door the way a dog looks at a threshold it is not certain it is allowed to cross. He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to ask.

I dried my hands on my apron and opened the door.

He straightened. Up close he was older than I'd thought -- the thinness had made him look younger from a distance, but the lines around his eyes and the set of his mouth belonged to a man closer to five-and-twenty. His eyes were steady and careful, the eyes of someone who has learned to assess a situation before committing to it.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He considered this as though it were a question with more than one possible answer. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He came in and sat where I'd pointed, and he did not look around the kitchen with curiosity or gratitude or any of the other things people expected of hungry men who'd been let indoors. He simply sat, and waited, with the particular stillness of a man who has spent a long time learning not to take up more space than he has been offered.

I gave him bread, and broth from the pot that had been simmering since morning, and a heel of cheese that was harder than I'd have served a paying guest but softer than anything he'd had lately. He ate carefully. Not slowly -- there was real hunger in the way his hands moved -- but carefully, as though he remembered that eating too fast after going without was a mistake you only made once.

I did not watch him eat. I went back to the stockpot and scrubbed it, and when I heard the spoon set down on the bowl I turned around.

"Thank you, ma'am," he said. He stood. The courtesy was automatic, drilled into him somewhere that was not his mother's kitchen.

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, as though this were not specific enough, or perhaps too specific: "Wherever there's work."

I nodded. He nodded. He went out the way he'd come in, and I watched through the window as he crossed the yard and turned onto the road, the sole of his left boot flapping with every step.

I stood at the window longer than I needed to. His hands, when he'd held the spoon -- they'd been steady. No tremor, no shake. But he'd gripped it the way you grip something when you are concentrating very hard on not letting anyone see that your hands are not as steady as they look.

He was somebody's son. He was probably somebody's brother. And he was walking north into October with his boots falling apart, and there was nothing I could do about any of it except what I had done, which was feed him, which was not enough, which was what I had.

I went back to the stockpot.

In the taproom, late afternoon, two men who'd been nursing their ale since the coach arrived found something to disagree about -- I never learned what, and it did not matter, because the sound of raised voices in a taproom is its own species of problem regardless of the cause.

"Gentlemen," I said from the doorway, and I did not raise my voice, because I have never found that raising my voice improves a situation that has been caused by men raising theirs. "If you'd like to continue your conversation, you're welcome to do so in the yard. If you'd prefer to stay where it's warm, you'll do so quietly."

They stayed. They were quiet. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and looked away again, and the cloth in her hand resumed its circuit of the counter.

The rest of the afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say it passed in work, and the work was unspectacular, and I did it, and it was done. I checked the cellar stores. I reviewed the evening menu, which was generous language for deciding between mutton stew and mutton stew with turnips. I mended a tear in the curtain of room six with a needle and thread I kept in my apron pocket for exactly this purpose. I spoke to Jem about the shutter on the second floor, and he said he'd get to it, and I said I knew he would, and we both understood that this exchange had been conducted before and would be conducted again and that the shutter would be fixed at a time of Jem's choosing and not mine.

Evening came the way it always did -- not all at once but in stages, the light pulling back from the yard, the windows going dark, the candles (fivepence ha'penny the dozen, four dozen, and worth every farthing of the negotiation) doing their work. The last guests settled. The last fire checked. Nan tallied the bar and left the figures on the counter for me, her handwriting small and precise, and went upstairs. Jem would have seen to the horses by now. The stable yard was quiet.

I moved through the closing routine alone. Taproom fire raked down to coals. Cellar hatch locked -- I checked the padlock twice, because I always checked it twice, because my mother had always checked it twice, and some habits are inherited whether you choose them or not. Front door bolted. Back door bolted. The kitchen banked and swept.

My hands, when I looked at them in the last of the candlelight, were red from cold water and rough from the day's work, and there was a thin cut on my index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught me when I'd unfolded it. I hadn't felt it at the time. I rarely felt the small injuries until the day was done and there was nothing left to do but notice them.

I crossed the yard one last time. The air was sharp and still and smelled of woodsmoke and horse and the particular cold sweetness of late October, when the year is turning and knows it. The second floor -- Bridget's window -- showed a faint glow behind the glass, and I could see the shape of her there, sitting in her chair, watching the yard the way she watched it every night. I did not wave. She did not wave. She was there. I knew she was there. That was enough.

I went back inside and bolted the kitchen door and stood in the middle of the room, and the inn was quiet around me in the way of a place that has been used hard and is resting now, and I was alone in it.

I was alone.

I let myself know it for the space of one breath -- the specific weight of it, the silence that is not peaceful but merely empty -- and then I turned to the table to put out the last candle, and that was when I saw the letter.

It had been left on the table sometime during the day -- slipped under the kitchen door, perhaps, or handed to Nan, who had set it here and forgotten to mention it in the rush. The seal was red wax, pressed with an impression I recognized before I read the direction. Stainforth. His handwriting on the front, my name in his careful, clerk-taught script: Miss D. Dale, The White Hound, Great North Road.

I picked it up. I turned it over. The wax was thick and neatly applied, because Stainforth was a man who did everything neatly, including the things that were designed to ruin you.

I broke the seal.

The letter was short. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy served a different purpose. He had received an offer of interest -- those were his words, *an offer of interest* -- regarding the White Hound property and its lease. A gentleman of means wished to acquire the premises for conversion to a more suitable establishment. Stainforth believed this to be an opportunity of mutual benefit. He proposed a meeting at his offices in Stevenage on the fourteenth of November to discuss the terms under which the current lease might be surrendered. He trusted I would see the wisdom of an orderly transition. He remained, et cetera, et cetera, my obedient servant, which he was not and had never been.

The fourteenth of November. Three weeks. He was giving me three weeks to agree to hand over everything my grandmother had built, my mother had held, and I had kept alive with my bare hands for two years.

I set the letter down on the table. The candle flame trembled in a draught I could not feel.

The kitchen was silent. The inn was silent. Somewhere outside, an owl called once and did not call again.

I looked at the letter. I looked at the candle. I looked at my hands -- red, rough, cut, capable -- and I said, to no one, to the room, to the quiet and the dark and the man who thought three weeks was enough to take my life apart:

"You'll need a better offer than that, Mr. Stainforth."
