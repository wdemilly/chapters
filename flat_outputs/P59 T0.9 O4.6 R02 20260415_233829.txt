The ink had dried unevenly on the last column. I licked my thumb and pressed it to the figure, and the seven smeared into something that might have been a two, which would have been a kinder number but was not the truth. Six weeks and three days until the quarter's rent came due, and the ledger did not care that my thumb was cold.

I closed the book.

The kitchen fire had caught properly now, the coals I'd banked overnight giving up their heat to the fresh wood I'd laid across them at half past five. The room was still cold enough to see my breath, but the cold would retreat within the hour. It always did. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to fill the kettle.

The White Hound woke in a particular order, and I had learned that order from my mother, who had learned it from hers. Kitchen fire first. Then the kettle. Then the taproom hearth, which drew badly when the wind came from the north and drew beautifully when it came from any other direction, and today the wind was from the north. I knelt on the stone flags and coaxed the thing to life with a patience I did not feel, feeding it kindling in slivers until the draught caught and held.

I wanted one morning to come downstairs and find it already done.

That was a foolish thought, and I set it aside in the way I set aside all foolish thoughts, which was to stand up, brush the ash from my skirt, and find the next task. The bar stock was low on gin and adequate on everything else. I counted the bottles twice because I had miscounted on Tuesday and shorted a coach party by a measure of brandy, and a coach party shorted on brandy is a coach party with opinions. The second count matched the first. Good.

Last night's accounts were already tallied. I had done them before bed, by the stub of a candle I could not afford to burn so low, because the numbers were worse if I left them for morning. In the morning I had the energy to be frightened by them. At night I was merely tired, and tired was easier.

The windows were rimed at their edges, a fine tracery of frost that caught the first grey light. The year was nearly into November, and I could feel it in the floors, in the draught beneath the doors, in the particular way the taproom smelled of cold stone and old ale at this hour. I wiped down the bar with a rag, checked the hinge on the cellar hatch that had been sticking since September, and made a note to ask Jem about it. Then I crossed the note out. Jem would see it himself. He always did.

I was in the yard when I found him already at work. He had the bay gelding's bridle across his knee and was working a stiff buckle with his one hand and the blunt end of his stump, his fingers moving with the practised economy of a man who has long since stopped thinking about what he cannot do. He did not look up.

"Hinge on the cellar hatch," I said, and then remembered I had crossed the note out.

He nodded once. He had already seen it.

I went back inside. Nan came through the kitchen door three minutes later, her cap pins not yet set, her apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar and began laying out the glasses for the morning trade. We did not speak. We had done this six hundred times.

The London coach was late. This was not unusual. What was unusual was that it arrived carrying only two passengers instead of the expected four, and the first of these was a woman with a split trunk and a grievance she intended to share with every person between here and York.

"The hinge gave way at Hatfield," she announced, as though Hatfield were my personal responsibility. "I have been holding the lid shut with my hand for seven miles."

"Jem," I said, without raising my voice. He appeared at the stable-yard door. "The lady's trunk wants a new hinge. See what we've got."

He looked at the trunk. He looked at the hinge. He took the trunk away without a word, and the woman stared after him with her mouth half open, her grievance stolen from her before she'd had the full enjoyment of it.

The coachman climbed down stiffly and found me at the yard gate.

"The road above Hatfield is washed out past the second milestone," he said. "Axle-deep in places. If it rains again tonight you'll want to warn the northbound."

"I'll tell the next driver. Have you eaten?"

"Haven't time. We're behind as it is." He pulled his collar up. "There's talk of the turnpike trust patching it, but talk is all it's been since August."

"Since July," I said. "Go on, then. Nan will have something you can eat on the box if you stop arguing about it."

He stopped arguing about it. I sent Nan out with bread and cold beef wrapped in cloth, and the coach was away again within twenty minutes. The second passenger had gone upstairs without requiring anything from me, which was the best kind of passenger there was.

The yard cleared. Jem took the woman's trunk around the back. Nan returned to the bar and resumed wiping it down, one eye on the door to the dining room. I saw her glance and said nothing. She had worked here long enough to know which arrivals warranted watching and which did not, and her judgment was better than mine on the days I was too tired to judge.

Mr Poole, the chandler, arrived at half past ten with his cart and his quarterly smile, which was the smile of a man about to name a price he knew I would not like.

"Miss Dale." He set the crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table. "Twelve dozen, as agreed. At ninepence the dozen."

"The price last quarter was sevenpence."

"Tallow's dear this autumn. I'm sure you've heard."

"I've heard you tell me so. I've also heard what you charge the Bull at Baldock, which is sevenpence ha'penny, and what you charge the Cock at Hatfield, which is eightpence, and neither of them shifts your volume the way I do. Twelve dozen a quarter, Mr Poole. Every quarter. Rain or shine."

He rubbed the back of his neck. "Eight and a half."

"Seven and a half."

"You'll ruin me, Miss Dale."

"I'll do nothing of the sort. Your tallow comes from Jessop's in Welwyn at fourpence a pound and it has done for three years. Seven and a half leaves you a margin any honest man would be comfortable explaining to his wife."

He looked at the candles. He looked at me. He knew, as I knew, that the next chandler was in Stevenage, four miles further, and that his cart was already here, and that loading the crate back onto it would cost him more in time and pride than the penny per dozen he was surrendering.

"Eightpence," he said. "And I'll not go lower."

"Eightpence. And you'll hold that price through March."

He left. The candles stayed.

I permitted myself, once the door had closed, a small and private satisfaction. It was the only wage I collected for that particular kind of work, and it would have to do.

The thought arrived as it always did, between one task and the next, when my hands were busy and my mind was not. The numbers in the ledger, the chandler's price, the rent in six weeks. All of it mine to manage, mine to fight for, mine to lose the moment I signed a marriage register. I had refused two proposals for exactly this reason. The inn was not sentiment. It was livelihood, and the law would hand it to any husband as neatly as Mr Poole handed me a receipt.

I picked up the crate and carried it to the storeroom.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked. Young. Thin. The bones of his face too close to the surface. His coat had been army-issue once and was now merely threadbare, and his boots were coming apart where the sole met the upper, the stitching gone, the leather separating at the welt like a mouth trying to speak. He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand raised and then lowered it, and then raised it again.

He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to knock.

I opened the door before he could decide otherwise.

He startled. His eyes were brown, and very young, and they went immediately to a point just past my shoulder, which was where men looked when they were ashamed of what they were about to ask.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

"Not today, ma'am. Not since yesterday, properly."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there."

He sat. I put bread in front of him, and broth from the stockpot that had been on since morning. He picked up the spoon and held it for a moment before he began to eat, and his grip was careful, deliberate, as though he were remembering how the thing worked. He ate steadily, without rushing. He was hungry enough to bolt it, and he did not bolt it, and I respected him for that.

When the bowl was empty he set the spoon down and looked at me directly for the first time.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North. My mother's people are near Biggleswade." He paused. "I think they're still there."

I gave him another piece of bread for the road. He took it, and he thanked me again, and he went.

I stood in the kitchen doorway and watched him cross the yard. His boots left uneven prints in the frost. He was perhaps nineteen. He had held the spoon the way my mother held her rosary in her final year, as though the object were not quite familiar, as though the hand had been trained to grip something else entirely.

I closed the door and went back to the stockpot.

The afternoon brought the usual small crises. Two men in the taproom disagreed about the outcome of a horse race at Newmarket, and the disagreement had reached the stage where chairs were being pushed back and voices were climbing toward the register where reason departs and fists arrive.

I stepped between them. "Gentlemen. Not in my house."

They sat down. Nan, behind the bar, set down the glass she had picked up by the neck, and resumed drying another.

The rest of the afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at a coaching inn between rushes. No coaches. No drama. Rooms to be turned, the kitchen to be prepared for the evening meal, accounts to be reviewed, a delivery of firewood to be stacked. Nan at the bar. Jem in the yard. The work. I have found that the hours between two and five are where an inn either holds or fails, because those are the hours when nothing demands your attention and everything requires it. A taproom left unswept at two o'clock is a taproom with sticky floors at six. A fire left unbanked at three is a cold dining room at seven. I swept. I banked. I checked the rooms on the second floor and found a cracked windowpane in number eight that I had not noticed before, and I stood looking at it for longer than it warranted, because the crack ran from corner to corner in a clean diagonal, and it was the kind of problem that could be solved with putty and glass, and I was so tired of problems that could only be solved with money.

Evening came. The last guests were settled by nine. The last fire checked by half past. I tallied the bar, closed down the kitchen, and began the circuit I had made every night for two years, and every night before that alongside my mother.

Taproom fire: down to coals. Good. Cellar hatch: locked, the sticking hinge still sticking. Front door: bolted. Stable yard: quiet, a lantern burning low by the far stall, Jem's work. I stood in the yard and let the cold air settle against my face. The second floor, across the way: a lit shape at Bridget's window, her silhouette behind the glass, watching or not watching, as she did every night. I did not look long.

My hands, when I turned them over in the lantern light, were red from the cold water and the scrubbing and the ordinary friction of a day spent holding things together. There was a small cut on my right index finger where the edge of Mr Poole's delivery note had caught the skin. I had not felt it at the time. I felt it now.

I locked the last door and set the bolt. The inn settled around me in the particular silence of a building that has been used hard and is resting, the timbers ticking as they cooled, the fire muttering to itself behind the grate. I stood alone in the kitchen with my back against the door and let myself know it.

Alone. There it was, the word I kept in the cupboard with the best china, brought out only when no one could see me hold it.

Then I saw the letter.

It was on the kitchen table where Nan must have left it, half tucked beneath the ledger. The handwriting on the direction was precise and angular, a clerk's hand, and the seal was Stainforth's: dark red wax, pressed with the signet he used for all his correspondence, a wheat sheaf above his initials. I knew the seal the way I knew the sound of the cellar hatch, or the particular groan of the third stair. It was a feature of my landscape. It had never before made my stomach drop.

I picked it up. I turned it over. The paper was heavy, good quality, the kind a man used when he wanted the weight of the letter to do some of the work before the words began.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, which was one of very few things we had in common. He wrote that he had received an offer from a Mr Eustace Greeley of London for the purchase of the freehold on the White Hound and its adjoining properties. He wrote that Mr Greeley intended to redevelop the site as a modern posting-house and hotel. He wrote that the terms of my mother's lease permitted its termination at the landlord's discretion upon twelve months' notice, and that he was prepared to discuss the matter with me at my earliest convenience, which he defined as the fourteenth of November. Seventeen days from now.

He closed with a sentence that sat on the page like a stone on a grave: I trust you will agree that the interests of the parish are best served by an establishment under more conventional management.

I set the letter down. I pressed both hands flat against the table, on either side of it, and I stood there until my breath came back.

Seventeen days. A meeting. A buyer already found. And the word conventional deployed like a cannon across a field, aimed at the single fact Stainforth had never forgiven: that the person holding his lease was a woman who had not had the decency to fail.

The kitchen was empty. The fire was low. The inn was locked, and mine, and under threat in a way that the ledger's six weeks and three days had not yet named, because the ledger was arithmetic, and this was a man with a buyer and a date and the law on his side.

I looked at the letter once more. I read the sentence about conventional management again, slowly, the way one reads a line that has been written to wound and has succeeded.

Then I folded the letter, slid it beneath the ledger, and said aloud to the empty room:

"Mr Stainforth, you are going to need a better word than conventional."
