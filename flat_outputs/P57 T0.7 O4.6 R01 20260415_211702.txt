The ink had bled through to the second page of the ledger, which meant I'd pressed too hard on the nib again, which meant the entry for tallow -- four shillings, nine -- was now a ghost of itself on the column below, smudging into the figure for oats. I blotted it with my sleeve and did the sum again in my head. Six weeks. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the numbers between here and there were not so much tight as airless.

I closed the ledger.

The kitchen was cold in the way that October kitchens are cold before anyone has earned the right to complain about it -- the fire built but not yet giving back, the flagstones pulling warmth out through the soles of my boots. I'd laid the fire myself twenty minutes ago. I always did. There was a particular satisfaction in it, the first task of the day being the one that made every other task possible, and I was not above admitting I liked the silence of the inn at this hour. No coaches, no guests, no requests. Just the sound of the grate ticking as the wood caught, and the small, specific pleasure of a woman who has not yet been asked for anything.

I wanted one morning -- just one -- when the numbers came out differently. When I could open that ledger and find a margin wide enough to breathe in. I did not get that morning. I got this one instead, and this one had work in it, so I pushed back from the table and went to do it.

The taproom fire was next. I raked the ash, set the kindling, struck the flint. The windows along the front were rimed at their edges, a thin crust of frost that would melt by nine and leave the glass streaked. I'd have Nan wipe them down. The bar stock I checked by touch as much as sight -- the ale cask nearest the tap was a third full, the gin wanted replacing, the port was fine. I ran my hand along the row of bottles on the shelf behind the bar and counted without counting, the way you count stairs in a house you've lived in all your life.

Last night's accounts were still on the bar where I'd left them. I tallied the final column standing up, one hand flat on the wood. Correct. It was always correct. I did not make errors with money, because errors with money were the kind that ate you from the edges inward, and I had seen what that looked like.

I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists. The cold had got into my hands already.

Out the back, the stable yard was sharp with frost and quiet in a way that meant Jem had been there before me. The tack on the near wall had been straightened -- bridles rehung, a stirrup leather that had been twisting for two days now set right. He was at the far end, running a brush down the flank of the bay coaching horse, his one hand working in long steady strokes while the pinned sleeve stayed still at his side. He did not look up. He did not need to. We had done this enough mornings that looking up would have been a comment, and neither of us was in the business of commenting on the obvious.

I checked the water trough -- full, not yet iced over -- and went back inside.

Nan came through the kitchen door four minutes later, pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar and took up her cloth without a word. I heard her start on the glasses from the back room, the small bright sound of her hands working while the inn woke up around us.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual. The London coach was late the way rain was wet -- it was a feature, not an event. But it was also running short: two passengers instead of four, and the first of them was already unhappy before her feet touched the yard. She had a trunk with a split hinge, and she had opinions about the split hinge, and she had further opinions about the road, the weather, the state of English coaching generally, and the particular inadequacy of the boy who had loaded her trunk at the Bull and Mouth.

I directed Jem to the trunk with a look. He appeared at the woman's elbow as though he'd been standing there all along, took the handle in his hand, and bore it inside without acknowledging the hinge or the opinions. The woman stopped mid-sentence, slightly wrongfooted by the efficiency, and I took the opening.

"Your room is the second on the left at the top of the stairs. Breakfast is laid in the dining room. The hinge will be seen to before you leave."

She went. They almost always went, when you gave them a clear direction and no room to argue in.

The coachman was still in the yard, stamping his boots and looking aggrieved. He was a regular on this route -- Hobson, thick-necked, reliable, perpetually cold.

"The road north of Hatfield's gone to pieces," he said. "Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel in. Someone ought to tell the turnpike trust."

"Someone has. Twice. They've promised gravel before the frost sets."

"Promised gravel." He said it the way a man says promised gravel when he has been driving coaches for twenty years and knows what promises are worth.

"I'll write again," I said. "Was there anything else?"

He looked at me, then at the yard, then shook his head. "No, Miss Dale. That'll do."

He went. The yard cleared. Jem took the second passenger's bag without being asked. Nan had been wiping down the bar with one eye on the door through all of it. I noticed. Said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his new price, which was -- as I had expected, as I had in fact predicted to the penny -- fourpence higher per dozen than last month.

"Cost of tallow's gone up," he said. He said this every time. He said it the way Hobson said promised gravel -- with the weary conviction of a man who has settled on his excuse and means to ride it until it drops.

"The cost of tallow has gone up a halfpenny the pound since March," I said. "Your price has gone up twopence the dozen, which is rather more than a halfpenny's worth of tallow per candle, Mr. Fenton, unless your candles have got heavier and I haven't noticed."

He shifted his weight. "There's the carriage. The roads--"

"You deliver to the Red Lion and the George on the same route. I know because I've seen your cart. If the roads are costing you more, they're costing you more for all three, and I doubt Mrs. Haddon at the George is paying what you're asking me."

A pause. He had a broad, windburned face and small eyes that did their calculating openly, which I appreciated. A man whose arithmetic you could watch was a man you could work with.

"I could take my candles elsewhere, Miss Dale."

"You could. And I could buy from Jessop in Welwyn, who would be glad of the custom and who would bring his cart this far for a standing monthly order. But I would rather not, and I suspect you would rather not, so shall we agree on last month's price and save us both the trouble of pretending?"

He looked at me. I looked at him. The candles sat between us on the kitchen table in their paper wrapping, smelling of tallow and commerce.

"Last month's price," he said.

"Thank you, Mr. Fenton."

He took his money and left. The candles stayed.

I permitted myself, in the silence after the door closed, one small breath of satisfaction. Not triumph -- I could not afford triumph at fourpence a dozen -- but the private, clean pleasure of having done a thing well and knowing it.

I carried the candles to the storeroom and came back to the kitchen, and somewhere between the storeroom and the kettle I let the thought arrive that always arrived after a negotiation like that one. The rent, the suppliers, the ledger, the license -- all of it mine to manage only as long as I remained unmarried. One husband and the law would hand every farthing to him, along with the right to run my inn into the ground if he chose. I had refused two proposals already for exactly this reason. I did not regret either refusal. I regretted that the refusing was necessary.

The thought lasted as long as it took the kettle to heat. Then I set it aside and went back to work.

I saw him through the window before he knocked.

He'd come round the back, as the sign asked. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way that is not natural thinness but the kind that has been carved by weeks of too little food. His coat had been decent once. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the sole of the left one separating from the upper so that I could see a stripe of grey stocking underneath.

He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand half-raised and did not knock. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at a door when he is not sure what will happen when it opens -- when the last several doors have not opened kindly -- and he is calculating whether the risk of knocking is worth the chance of being turned away again.

I opened it before he could decide.

He startled. Recovered. Straightened his shoulders in a way that had been trained into him somewhere that was not a kitchen yard in Hertfordshire.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He met my eyes. He had the decency not to lie. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there."

He sat. I put bread in front of him, and broth from the stockpot that had been going since dawn. He picked up the spoon and ate with the careful, steady discipline of someone who has learned that eating too fast after hunger makes you sick. He did not speak while he ate. I did not watch him while he ate. I went about the kitchen and let him have the dignity of a meal taken without an audience.

When he'd finished he stood, and the spoon was placed exactly parallel to the bowl's edge, and his hands went to his sides.

"Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, as if he owed me something more specific for the broth: "My mother's in Nottingham."

"The coach road will take you to Baldock. After that, ask for the Stamford road."

He nodded. He left.

I stood at the kitchen door and watched him cross the yard, and the boots -- the broken boots, the sole flapping with each step -- stayed with me after he'd gone through the gate. He was someone's son walking north in boots that would not last the week. I felt the specific, useless ache of knowing this and being able to do nothing further about it.

I went back to the stockpot.

The afternoon was ordinary. Two men in the taproom got loud over something I never identified -- a bet, a slight, the particular grievance of men who have been sitting too long with too little purpose. I heard the pitch change from the kitchen and was through the door before Nan had set down her cloth.

"Gentlemen," I said. "The yard is available if you need air. The taproom is not available for this."

They looked at me. They looked at each other. They sat down. Nan, behind the bar, did not need to move.

The hours between the afternoon coach and the evening stretched out in the way they always did -- not empty, never empty, but quieter. Nan polished glasses. Jem was in the yard. I reset the two upstairs rooms that had been vacated, checked the kitchen stores against tomorrow's needs, and wrote the order for the butcher on the back of an old invoice. The work was not interesting. It was not meant to be interesting. It was meant to be done, and I did it, and the inn kept running because I did it, and that was sufficient.

By evening the last guests were settled, the last fire checked, the taproom closed. I moved through the locking-up the way I always did -- taproom fire down to coals, cellar hatch bolted, the door to the yard barred from inside. I checked the stable yard through the back window: quiet, dark, Jem's lantern already out. On the second floor, a faint glow behind Bridget's window -- the shape of her, sitting, watching the yard below as she did every night, as she had done for more years than I'd been alive. I did not look long.

My hands, when I held them under the kitchen lamp, were red from the day's cold water and rough at the knuckles. There was a small cut on my index finger -- the edge of Fenton's delivery note, I realized, the paper sharp enough to open skin without my noticing. I had not felt it happen. I'd been too busy winning the argument.

I set the bar on the front door. I turned the key. The inn was locked, and mine, and silent, and I was alone in it.

I let myself know that for exactly as long as it took to cross the kitchen. Then I saw the letter.

It was on the table near the door -- Nan must have taken it from the afternoon post and left it for me. The seal was dark red, pressed deep, and I knew the hand on the direction before I turned it over. Stainforth. Mr. Phineas Stainforth, whose family owned the land beneath my feet and the walls around me and the roof above my head, and who had written to me with a courtesy that meant nothing three times in the last year and never once with good news.

I broke the seal. I unfolded the paper.

His hand was neat, his sentences measured. He wrote that he hoped I was well. He wrote that he had received an offer from a Mr. Collis of London for the purchase of the White Hound's lease and the adjoining property, and that Mr. Collis intended to renovate the premises into a posting house of a more modern character. He wrote that he thought it only fair to advise me of this development and to offer me the opportunity to discuss the matter in person before any decision was reached. He wrote that he would expect my reply within a fortnight, and that if no reply was received, he would proceed on the assumption that I had no objection.

No objection.

I set the letter on the table. The kitchen was empty. The fire was down to its last heat. The inn I had been given by my mother, who had been given it by hers, was being sold out from under me by a man who had put the word fair in a sentence that contained no fairness at all, and he had given me two weeks to agree to my own dispossession.

I said it aloud, to the empty room, to the walls that were mine and were not mine and had never, under the law, been mine at all:

"A fortnight. Then we'll see what Mr. Collis makes of me."
