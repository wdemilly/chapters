The ink had dried crooked on the last column because I'd been adding figures in the dark again, and the sum hadn't improved for the looking.

I closed the ledger. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the numbers between here and there were not numbers that inspired confidence. They were, in fact, numbers that made a woman want to pour herself a measure of gin at half five in the morning and stare into the middle distance. I did not pour the gin. The gin was stock, and stock was money, and money was the thing I did not have enough of, which was the entire reason I'd been sitting in a cold kitchen squinting at figures by the stub of last night's candle.

The fire needed building up. I knelt at the grate and raked the ashes back, set the kindling, struck the flint twice before it caught. The kitchen hearth was old and deep and drew well when the wind came from the west, which it did this morning -- I could feel the pull of it before the flame took hold. The wood was dry enough. Small mercies.

I stood and brushed my knees and went to the taproom. That fire was dead. I built it from nothing, which took longer, and while I waited for the kindling to catch I checked the bar: bottles counted, the ale cask marked at last night's level, the till locked. I ran my thumb along the edge of the counter where the varnish had started to lift. It needed sanding. It had needed sanding for a month. I added it to the list in my head, which was not a short list, and moved on.

The windows were rimed at their edges with a thin crust of frost that caught the first grey light. October had no business being this cold. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and tucked my hands under my arms for a moment, standing in the middle of the taproom like a woman with nowhere to be, which was so far from the truth it was almost funny.

I wanted -- briefly, stupidly, in the part of my mind I did not generally consult -- to still be in bed. Not for the warmth, though the warmth would have been welcome. For the five minutes of not yet. The five minutes before the day began its relentless taking of things from me: time, energy, candles, patience, the last of the good flour. I wanted five minutes in which nothing was required.

I did not take them. I went to check the cellar hatch, and then the storeroom, and then the yard.

Jem was already in the stable, which I'd expected. He had a bridle over his arm -- the good one, the one with the stitching that needed watching -- and he was working the leather with his one hand and the crook of his stump with the focused attention of a man who has solved a thousand such problems and resents none of them. He did not look up when I opened the back door. He didn't need to. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

I went back inside. Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar and took her place behind it and began wiping down the counter with the rag she kept folded on the shelf beneath. No greeting. No need for one. The inn was waking up in the way it always woke up -- by the hands of the people who kept it breathing.

The London coach was late, which was not unusual, and when it finally clattered into the yard it was running short: two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them looked as though the road had personally offended them.

The first was a stout woman in a brown travelling cloak whose trunk had a split hinge and who wished everyone within earshot to understand that this was not her fault. I directed Jem to the trunk with a look. He materialized at the coach door, took the offending article under his arm as though it weighed nothing, and bore it inside before the woman had finished her third sentence about the blackguard who had loaded it in London.

"The hinge will hold if you don't force it," I told her. "There's a room at the top of the stairs on the left. Breakfast is laid in the dining room. The eggs are fresh."

She blinked at me. Whatever she'd been preparing to say next -- and she had clearly been preparing something extensive -- it died on her lips. She went upstairs.

The coachman climbed down stiffly and found me in the yard. He was a big, red-faced man I'd seen a dozen times before, name of Hatchett, and he had the particular expression of someone who has been composing a grievance for the last nine miles.

"The road above Hatfield is a disgrace, Miss Dale. Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel in. I've reported it twice and nothing's done."

"Report it to the turnpike trust again. In writing this time -- they lose the verbal ones."

"I haven't got time to write letters. I've got a route to finish."

"Then I'll write it for you. Give me the details at breakfast and I'll send it today. But I need you back on the road by half nine, Mr Hatchett, or you'll miss the Stevenage connection and that becomes my problem."

He grunted, which was as close to agreement as Hatchett ever came, and went inside.

The yard cleared. Jem took the horses. The second passenger -- a thin, silent man who had not complained about anything, which made him practically a saint by coaching-inn standards -- disappeared into the dining room. Nan was behind the bar with one eye on the door through the whole of it, the rag still moving in slow circles on the counter. I noticed. Said nothing.

The chandler arrived at eleven with his cart and his monthly delivery and his particular way of standing in the kitchen doorway as though he were doing me a tremendous favour by showing up at all.

His name was Garvey. He was forty-odd, with thinning hair and thick fingers and an instinct for leverage that would have served him well in Parliament. He set the crate of tallow candles on the table and named his price.

"Tenpence ha'penny the dozen," he said. "Cost of tallow's gone up again."

"It hasn't. I buy from the same suppliers you do, Mr Garvey. The price at Smithfield dropped a farthing last month."

He shifted his weight. "There's transport to consider."

"Your transport costs haven't changed since April. You're charging the Bull at Hatfield ninepence, and they order half what I do."

A pause. He picked at the edge of the crate. "I could take them elsewhere."

"You could. And you'd lose the largest regular order between here and Baldock, and you'd spend a fortnight finding someone to replace it. Ninepence ha'penny, Mr Garvey. Same as last month."

He looked at me. I looked at him. The kitchen was very quiet.

"Ninepence ha'penny," he said.

"Thank you. Leave them on the table."

He left. The candles stayed.

I allowed myself, in the silence after the door closed, one small breath of satisfaction. Not triumph -- triumph was for people who could afford to alienate their suppliers. But I knew his margins down to the farthing, and he knew I knew, and that was enough. I was good at this. It was not nothing, being good at this.

The thought came as it always did, somewhere in the ordinary stretch between one task and the next: the ledger, the rent, the chandler's price, all of it stacked against the same immovable fact. If I married, the lease would pass to my husband. The income, the accounts, the right to negotiate my own candle prices -- all of it, gone into some man's name the moment the register was signed. I had done the calculation before. I had refused two proposals because of it. The arithmetic had not changed.

I wiped down the table where Garvey's crate had left a ring of grease, and went to see about the afternoon bread.

I saw him through the window before he knocked.

He'd come around the back, as the sign asked. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way that means weeks, not days. His coat had been decent once and was not decent now, and his boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather pulling away from the sole on the left foot so that I could see a stripe of dirty stocking underneath.

He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand half-raised and did not knock. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at something he is not sure he is allowed to want. Measuring whether the knock would be welcome. Whether he would be told to move on.

I opened the door before he could decide.

He startled. Up close he was even thinner -- cheekbones sharp under skin that had gone sallow, hair cropped close in the military fashion. His eyes were brown and very tired.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He hesitated. Not from pride, or not only from pride -- from the honest uncertainty of a man trying to remember.

"Not recent," he said.

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He sat. I cut bread -- the end of the morning loaf, which was going stale anyway -- and ladled broth from the pot that had been on the fire since dawn. I set both in front of him. He looked at the food for a moment with an expression I had seen before and still could not bear, and then he ate.

He ate carefully. Not wolfing it, the way some did, but steadily, the spoon held in a grip that suggested his hands were not entirely steady and he did not want me to see. He finished the broth. He ate every crumb of the bread. He set the spoon down and placed his hands flat on the table.

"Thank you, miss."

"Where are you headed?"

He named a direction -- north, toward the midland counties. He did not name a destination. I did not ask for one.

"There's a carrier's cart leaves from the crossroads at two," I said. "He goes as far as Biggleswade. He won't charge a soldier."

He nodded. He stood. He thanked me again, and he went.

I stood at the kitchen door after he'd gone and watched him cross the yard -- the broken boots, the careful walk of a man conserving what little he had left. He was somebody's son. That was the thought I could not stop, the one that came every time: he was somebody's son, and he was twenty years old and walking north with no destination and holes in his boots, and I had given him bread and broth and directions to a carrier's cart, and it was not enough, and there was nothing else I could do.

I went back inside and washed his bowl.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say, in work. The coach had gone. The guests were settled or departed. Nan wiped the bar and restocked the glasses. Jem was in the yard. I did accounts, reset the upstairs rooms, started the evening soup, and mended a torn curtain in the dining room with a needle and thread that I kept in my apron pocket because there was always something that needed mending.

In the taproom, two men who had been sitting too long over too little ale began a dispute about something I could not hear and did not need to. The pitch of it changed -- that particular shift from grumbling to sharp that means the next sound will be a chair pushed back.

I crossed the room.

"Gentlemen. You'll settle it outside or you'll settle it nowhere, but you will not settle it in my taproom."

They settled it nowhere. Nan watched from behind the bar and did not need to move.

Evening came down early, the way it does in late October -- not a sunset so much as a slow withdrawal of light, the sky going from grey to iron to black while you're still wiping tables. I locked the kitchen door. I checked the cellar hatch. I banked the taproom fire and counted the till and locked it. I walked the second floor: all quiet, the corridor dark, the smell of tallow and old wood and the particular staleness of a building that has held too many people and is now holding none.

In the stable yard, everything was still. Jem would have settled the horses. I could hear nothing from the stable -- no restless stamping, no complaint. He kept them well.

I looked up at the second floor of the inn's west wing, where the private rooms were. There was a lit shape at Bridget's window -- the faint amber glow of a candle behind glass, and the outline of her, sitting, watching the yard as she always watched the yard. I did not look long.

I bolted the front door. I set the bar across it. I checked the back door, which I had already checked, and checked it again, because the day was done and the checking was what I did instead of stopping.

My hands, when I finally held them still under the kitchen lamp, were red from the cold water and the cleaning, the knuckles rough, a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Garvey's delivery note had caught the skin. I hadn't felt it at the time. I felt it now -- a thin bright sting, the body's way of telling you it has been paying attention even when you haven't.

The inn was quiet. The particular quiet of a place that has been used hard and is resting now, not peaceful so much as spent. I stood in the kitchen with the lamp burning and the fire down to its last coals and I was, for the length of one breath, aware that I was alone. Not busy, not managing, not adding figures or mending curtains or feeding soldiers or negotiating the price of candles. Just alone, in a kitchen, at the end of a day, with no one to hear me if I spoke.

The letter was on the table.

It had not been there this morning -- someone had brought it during the day, or it had come with the coach and Nan had set it here, and I had not seen it until now. The seal was dark red, pressed deep into the wax. The handwriting on the direction was precise and slanting and immediately recognizable.

Stainforth.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The paper was heavy, cream-coloured, the kind a man buys when he wants you to understand that he can afford good paper. I broke the seal.

The letter was short. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy came from a different source -- not necessity but the confidence of a man who expects to be obeyed without elaboration.

He had a buyer for the property. A nephew -- I knew the nephew, a soft-faced young man with plans to turn the White Hound into something more suited to the modern traveller, by which he meant something that did not have a woman's name on the lease. Stainforth was offering me a meeting to discuss the terms of surrender. He called it a discussion of the lease's future, but the meaning was plain enough. He had set a date: the fourteenth of November. Three weeks. If I did not attend, he would proceed without me, as was -- he noted, with the courtesy of a man sliding a knife between your ribs -- his legal right as freeholder.

I set the letter down on the table. The cut on my finger was still stinging. The fire was almost out. Somewhere above me, Bridget's candle would still be burning in the window, and somewhere to the north a young man with broken boots was walking toward nothing, and the inn was quiet and mine and three weeks from being taken from me.

"The fourteenth, then, Mr Stainforth," I said to the empty kitchen. "I'll be there. And I'll be bringing the accounts."
