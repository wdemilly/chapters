The ink had bled through to the second page of the ledger, which meant I'd pressed too hard on the nib again, which meant the entry for Tuesday's coal delivery now lived on both sides of the paper like a bruise that wouldn't stay where you put it. I blotted it, shut the book, and sat for a moment with my hands flat on the kitchen table.

Six weeks. That was what the numbers said when I stripped away the small optimisms I'd been permitting myself since September. Six weeks of careful spending, full coaches, no surprises, and I could meet the quarter's rent with a little left over for the coal man. Six weeks of anything less, and I'd be writing a letter I did not want to write to a man I did not want to ask.

I pushed back from the table. The kitchen was cold enough that my breath showed, just barely, in the grey light from the window. The fire I'd laid half an hour ago had caught but not yet thrown real heat -- it was still working through the kindling, popping and ticking in that uncertain way fires have before they commit to the business of warming a room. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to check the flue.

The inn at this hour was mine in a way it never was once the coaches came. No voices, no boots on the stairs, no one wanting anything. Just the building settling around me -- timber and plaster and cold stone floors that I'd walked since I was old enough to carry a tray. I moved through the taproom and knelt at the second hearth. The ash from last night's fire was still warm underneath. I raked it, laid the new fire, struck the flint. Moved behind the bar and counted bottles by touch in the dimness: gin, gin, brandy, the good whisky that I kept on the higher shelf because men who had to reach for it sometimes thought twice about a second glass.

The accounts from the previous evening were still on the bar in Nan's handwriting, which was better than mine and considerably more legible. Three shillings eightpence from the taproom, another two and six from the dining room. The Hendersons in room four had paid through Thursday. I noted the figures in my head and moved on.

The cellar hatch was bolted. The stable yard, when I cracked the back door and looked out, was quiet -- Jem's lantern already lit in the far stall, a yellow smear in the dark. He would have been up before me. He always was, though neither of us had ever acknowledged the competition, if that's what it was.

I shut the door against the cold and went back to the kitchen. The fire had committed itself. I hung the stockpot and began the morning's bread.

---

Jem was at the tack rail when I crossed the yard an hour later, working saddle soap into a bridle with his one hand and the stump of his left arm braced against the post for leverage. He didn't look up. He didn't need to. We had done this particular morning a thousand times -- I came out, he was already working, I noted whatever needed noting, and we each carried on. This morning the yard was swept, the water trough filled, and the mounting block had been nudged back into position after last night's mail coach had knocked it askew. I noted all of it, said nothing, and went back inside.

Nan came through the kitchen door six minutes later, pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar and began wiping it down with the cloth she kept folded on the shelf beneath the taps. No greeting. No need for one.

---

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual -- the London coach was late with the regularity of a church bell that had been hung crooked -- but it was later than its usual lateness, which put the whole morning behind by the amount of time I could not afford to lose. When it finally clattered into the yard, it carried two passengers instead of four and a coachman whose expression suggested the road had personally offended him.

The first passenger was a stout woman in a brown travelling cloak who descended the step, surveyed the yard, and said, "My trunk has a broken hinge and I hold this establishment partly responsible."

I had never seen this woman before in my life. The hinge had nothing to do with me or my establishment. I said, "Jem will see to the trunk. If you'll follow me, I'll show you to the dining room -- there's tea and bread, and the fire's been lit this past hour."

She opened her mouth to continue her argument, found no purchase for it, and followed me inside.

The second passenger went upstairs without comment. Jem appeared in the yard -- not summoned, simply present, the way he always was when something heavy needed moving -- and took charge of the wounded trunk.

The coachman caught my arm as I came back out. "A word, miss."

"You've got two minutes. I've bread proving."

"The road north of Hatfield's gone to pieces. There's a stretch past the Bell where the surface has washed out entirely -- three days of rain and no one's laid a stone. I lost twenty minutes going round it."

"Take the Essendon lane," I said. "It adds a mile but the surface holds. Tell Burrows at the Bell I said the parish surveyor's been written to. He hasn't, but Burrows doesn't know that, and it'll keep him from charging you for the detour."

The coachman looked at me for a moment, then touched his hat and climbed back up to his seat. The coach pulled out. The yard settled. Through the window, I could see Nan behind the bar, cloth in hand, watching the door with the particular quality of attention she gave to everything -- not anxious, just ready. I caught her eye. She looked away first, which was how I knew she'd been watching me and not the door at all.

---

Mr. Fenton the chandler arrived at half eleven with his cart and his prices and his smile that did not reach anywhere near his eyes.

"Miss Dale." He set the crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table as though he were presenting me with a gift. "Fourteen dozen, as agreed. At the new price -- sixpence ha'penny the dozen."

"The agreed price," I said, "was fivepence."

"Tallow's gone up. You'll have heard."

"Tallow's gone up a farthing the pound since Lady Day. Your price has gone up three ha'pence the dozen in seven months. That's not tallow, Mr. Fenton. That's ambition."

He shifted his weight. "I've costs, Miss Dale. Same as anyone."

"You have. And I know what they are, because I know what Jessop in Welwyn pays you for tallow by the hundredweight, and I know what you charge the Red Lion, and I know the Red Lion gets a better rate than I do because Mr. Hapgood drinks with you on Thursdays." I folded my arms. "Fivepence ha'penny. That's a farthing more than I was paying in the spring, which is generous of me, and it's a farthing more than you'd get if I bought direct from Jessop, which I will do if we have this conversation again in January."

Mr. Fenton looked at his candles. He looked at me. He looked at the door, as if calculating the inconvenience of carrying fourteen dozen tallow candles back to his cart.

"Fivepence ha'penny," he said.

"Thank you. Nan will let you out."

He went. The candles stayed. I permitted myself, in the silence after the door closed, one small breath of satisfaction -- the kind you take when a hinge holds that you weren't entirely sure would hold. Then I unpacked the crate and put the candles away.

The numbers were still the numbers. The farthing I'd saved would not keep the roof on, would not fill the rooms the coaches weren't filling, would not change the figure in the ledger. But it was mine, that farthing. I had won it with what I knew, and no one could take the knowing from me.

Which was, of course, the whole of it. What I knew, what I'd built, the way I could walk into that taproom and manage a negotiation that a man twice my age would fumble -- none of it would survive a wedding. The lease was in my name because my mother had died unmarried and her mother before her had outlived her husband by twenty years. If I married, the inn belonged to my husband. The ledger, the stockpot, the candles, the farthing -- his. I'd refused two proposals already for precisely this reason, and I had not found the refusals difficult. What I found difficult was never being asked whether I minded.

I wiped down the table and went to check the stockpot.

---

I saw him through the window before he knocked.

Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way of men who have been thin for a long time, not the leanness of youth but the drawn look of someone whose body has been eating itself for fuel. His coat had been decent once. His boots were coming apart where the sole met the upper, the stitching gone, the leather splitting along the welt. He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand raised and then lowered it. Raised it again. He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to try.

I opened the door before he could knock a third time.

He startled. Recovered. Took his cap off, which told me something -- he'd been taught manners, or the army had taught them to him, which came to the same thing.

"Have you eaten today?" I said.

A pause. Honesty won out over pride, as it generally does when the pride has been starved long enough. "Not since yesterday, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He sat. I cut bread, ladled broth from the stockpot, set both in front of him. He ate the way hungry men eat -- steadily, without looking up, one hand curled around the bowl as if someone might take it. I did not watch him. I went back to the stockpot and stirred it, which did not need doing, because a woman standing over a hungry man while he eats is a species of cruelty even when she means it as kindness.

When the bowl was empty, he set down the spoon and sat with his hands in his lap.

"Where are you headed?" I asked.

"North, ma'am. There's work in the mills, I'm told."

I did not ask which regiment. I did not ask about the war. He stood, put his cap back on, and thanked me with a formality that belonged to a different life than the one he was living.

"The road north of Hatfield is washed out," I said. "Take the Essendon lane."

He nodded and went.

I stood at the kitchen door and watched him cross the yard -- the careful walk of a man conserving what he had left, the boots that would not last another week. He was someone's son. That thought arrived without my permission, and I let it stay for the space of a breath before I shut the door and went back to the stockpot.

---

The afternoon passed the way afternoons pass in a coaching inn between rushes -- slowly, and with just enough work to keep you from sitting down. I reset the rooms on the second floor, checked the linen cupboard, tallied the bar accounts against Nan's figures, and carried two buckets of water from the well because the pipe from the yard pump had frozen overnight and Jem had not yet had time to thaw it.

At half three, two men in the taproom fell to arguing about a bet one of them claimed the other had welched on. The argument had reached the stage where chairs get pushed back and voices carry through walls. I crossed the taproom floor and stood between them.

"Settle it outside or settle it quietly. Those are your choices, and you have until I finish this sentence to pick one."

They picked quietly. Nan, behind the bar, did not look up from the glass she was polishing, but the corner of her mouth moved, just slightly, in a way that might have been approval.

---

Evening came on early, the way it does in late October -- not a dramatic darkening but a slow withdrawal of light, as if the day were losing its nerve. I lit the taproom candles. Served the dining room. Settled the Hendersons' bill through Friday, because Mrs. Henderson had decided to extend their stay, which was four more nights of income I had not expected and received with the silent gratitude of a woman who keeps her relief inside the ledger where it belongs.

By nine, the last guests were upstairs or gone. Nan had tallied the bar and left. Jem's lantern was dark in the stable yard, which meant he'd finished and gone to his room above the tack room. The inn was quiet.

I made my circuit. Taproom fire: down to coals, the grate secure. Cellar hatch: locked. Front door: bolted. Stable yard: still, the horses settled, Jem's work evident in the swept cobbles and the neatly hung harness. I looked up at the second floor. A light behind Bridget's window -- her shape there, watching the yard, as she always was at this hour. I did not linger.

Back in the kitchen, I banked the fire and wiped down the table. My hands, when I looked at them, were reddened from the cold water and the work, the knuckles rough, a small cut on my right index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught the skin. I hadn't noticed it at the time. I noticed it now, in the way you notice things about your own body only when you stop long enough to look.

The inn was locked. The day was done. And I was alone in the kitchen, which was where I always was at this hour, and which was usually enough, and which tonight -- for no reason I could name and no reason I cared to examine -- was not.

I turned to go upstairs and saw the letter.

It was on the kitchen dresser, half-hidden beneath a stack of clean cloths -- Nan must have put it there during the afternoon, when the post came and I was upstairs with the linen. A heavy envelope, cream-coloured, the handwriting precise and slanted. I knew the hand before I turned it over. I turned it over anyway.

Stainforth's seal. The dark red wax, the impression of the family crest he used on all his correspondence, as if a letter about a coaching-inn lease required the same gravity as a parliamentary dispatch.

I picked it up. I held it. I turned it over once more, as if the back might say something different than the front.

Then I broke the seal and read.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer for the purchase of the freehold of the property known as the White Hound, together with its stabling, outbuildings, and appurtenant land. The offer is a generous one and I am disposed to accept it. As the current leaseholder, you are entitled to be heard before any transfer is agreed. I therefore propose a meeting at my offices on the fourteenth of November, at which time you may present any representations you wish to make. I must be candid: the terms of your lease, while valid, are not such as would prevent a sale, and the prospective purchaser intends to assume management of the property upon completion. I trust you will see the wisdom of an orderly transition. Yours, etc., P. Stainforth.*

I read it again. The fourteenth of November. Three weeks. He was giving me three weeks to present representations I wish to make, as though the sum of my life and my mother's life and my grandmother's life before her could be reduced to representations, delivered on schedule, in his offices, at his convenience.

The prospective purchaser intends to assume management of the property. Which meant the prospective purchaser intended to assume my property. My kitchen, my taproom, my rooms, my stables, my road, my work. Everything I had held together with two hands and no help and the sheer stupid endurance of women who do not have the option of giving up.

I set the letter down on the table. I smoothed it flat with my palm, pressing the creases out as if neatness would change what it said.

Then I said, to the empty kitchen, to the banked fire and the scrubbed table and the candles I had bargained for that morning: "Right, Mr. Stainforth. Let's see your buyer meet the November coach schedule with the Hatfield road washed out and the coal man three days late."

I folded the letter, put it in my apron pocket, and went upstairs to plan.
