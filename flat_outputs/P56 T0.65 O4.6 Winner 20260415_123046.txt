The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the smear before it could ruin the facing page.

Six weeks. That was what the numbers said when I tallied them honestly, without the small hopeful rounding I sometimes allowed myself at the end of a long month. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between what the White Hound earned and what it owed was the width of my thumbnail. I closed the ledger and set my palm flat on its cover, as though I could hold the figures still by force.

The kitchen was cold. Not the killing cold of deep winter -- October had not yet earned that -- but the particular chill of a room whose fire has been dead for six hours and whose stones have surrendered every trace of yesterday's warmth. I could see my breath if I looked for it, so I did not look. I knelt at the hearth instead and built the fire the way my mother had taught me: kindling first, then the smaller logs crosswise, then the draught opened just enough to pull but not enough to waste. The flames caught. I held my hands near them for ten seconds -- a luxury, and I counted it as one -- then stood and pulled my sleeves down over my wrists.

The taproom fire next. I carried the coal scuttle through and knelt again on the flagstones, which were colder than the kitchen's because the taproom faced north and the windows were rimed at their edges with a thin, almost decorative frost. The fire took longer here. I adjusted the grate, checked the flue, and left it to build while I went behind the bar.

Stock: three casks of ale, one nearly spent. The gin was adequate. The brandy was not -- I had been stretching it with judicious pours for a week, and the level had reached the point where judicious became noticeable. I made a note in my head, which was where I kept the notes that mattered, and moved to the accounts from last night. Two shillings, eightpence over what I had expected, thanks to a party of four who had ordered supper late and drunk more than they ate. I entered the figure in the day-book and closed it.

Everything accounted for. Nothing wasted. This was the way I kept the inn, and the way the inn kept me, and most mornings the exchange felt fair.

I checked the dining room -- chairs pushed in, table wiped, the window latch that liked to work itself loose in the night secured again. The hinge on the cellar hatch wanted oiling. I noted it. The boot-scraper by the front door had shifted on its mount; I kicked it straight with my heel as I passed. These were small things. They were also the things that, left unattended, became large things, and large things cost money I did not have.

Through the kitchen window I could see Jem in the stable yard, already at work. He was straightening tack on the wall hooks, moving with the practised economy of a man who has learned to do everything with one hand and the stump of an arm and does not care to be admired for it. He did not look up. He did not need to. The morning required the work, and Jem did what the morning required.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet fully set in her reddish hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar without a word and took her place behind it, running one hand along the wood to check that last night's wiping had been thorough. It had. She knew it had. She checked anyway, because that was what we did.

We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

The London coach was late, which surprised no one, and short, which surprised me. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both in foul temper. The first was a stout woman in a brown travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the air of someone preparing to hold the entire county responsible for her discomfort. Her trunk had a split hinge, and she wished everyone within earshot to understand that this was not her fault, that the coachman had handled it abominably, and that she expected the matter to be rectified before she took another step.

I directed Jem, who had appeared in the yard without being summoned, to take the trunk to the back and see what could be done with the hinge. I turned to the woman and told her that her room was ready, that hot water would be sent up within the quarter hour, and that the trunk would follow as soon as it could be managed without losing its remaining contents across the yard. She opened her mouth, found nothing in my tone that invited argument, and closed it again. She went upstairs.

The coachman was a solid, weathered man named Briggs whom I had known for three years. He climbed down stiffly and jerked his chin toward the north.

"Road's breaking up past Hatfield. Whole stretch between the two elms and the Welham turning. You'll want to warn your evening passengers."

"I'll tell them. How bad?"

"Bad enough I lost twenty minutes picking a line through it. Another hard frost and it'll take a wheel off."

"I'll send word to the turnpike trust, for what good it will do." I handed him a mug of ale that Nan had drawn without being asked. "You've four minutes, Mr Briggs. The horses are being changed now."

He drank, nodded, and did not waste his four minutes on further conversation. I liked Briggs. He understood that time was not his to spend at my door.

The coach went. The passengers settled upstairs or drifted to the dining room. Jem took the split trunk around to the workshop. The yard cleared, and the particular hush that follows a coach's departure settled over the inn -- the sound of a held breath being released. Nan was wiping down the bar with one eye on the door, as she always did. I noticed. I said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past ten, as he did on the first Tuesday of every month, with his cart of tallow candles and his air of a man performing a generous favour. His name was Jessup, and he had a red face, a careful smile, and a gift for adjusting his prices upward in increments just small enough that a less attentive buyer might not notice.

I noticed.

"Morning, Miss Dale." He set the crate on the kitchen table with a thump that was meant to convey the weight and therefore the value of its contents. "Three dozen, as agreed. I've had to put the price up a farthing per dozen, I'm afraid. Tallow's dear this month."

"Tallow's been the same price at Smithfield for six weeks, Mr Jessup. I check."

His smile thinned slightly. "There's the transport to consider."

"Your transport costs haven't changed since March. You're still using the same cart and the same horse, unless you've bought a new one and neglected to tell me."

"Miss Dale --"

"I'll pay what I paid last month. Eightpence the dozen."

"I can't do eightpence. Not with my costs."

"Your costs, Mr Jessup, are fivepence the dozen, and I know what you charge the Woolpack and the Red Lion, and it is not a farthing more than what I am offering you now. If you'd prefer to load those candles back on your cart and find another customer on this road who buys three dozen a month without fail and pays on the day, you are welcome to try."

A silence. Jessup looked at the crate. He looked at me. He looked at the crate again.

"Eightpence," he said.

"Eightpence. Thank you, Mr Jessup. Nan will see you out."

He left. The candles stayed.

I permitted myself one moment -- standing at the kitchen table with my hand on the crate, the door still settling in its frame -- to feel the small, private satisfaction of a thing done well. It would not make the ledger's numbers kind. But it would keep them from growing worse this month, and some days that was the victory available.

The thought followed, as it always did when I had been counting pennies and fighting for margins: that everything I had just done -- the negotiation, the accounts, the decisions about stock and supply and what the inn could bear -- all of it existed only because I held the lease in my own name. If I married, the law would hand it to my husband as surely as Jessup had handed me the crate. The inn, the income, the right to tell a chandler what I would and would not pay -- gone, transferred, absorbed into a man's authority whether he deserved it or not. I had refused two proposals for exactly this reason, and I did not regret either refusal. I regretted only that the calculation was necessary.

I wiped my hands on my apron and went to check the kitchen stores.

I saw him through the window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the signs asked, and he was standing outside the kitchen door with the particular stillness of a man deciding whether he was welcome enough to announce himself. Young -- not much past twenty, if that. Thin in the way of someone who has been thin for long enough that his clothes have given up trying to fit. His coat was army issue, or had been before the elbows wore through. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather cracked and separating from the sole on the left foot.

He looked at the door. He looked at his own hands. He raised one to knock, then lowered it, then raised it again.

I opened the door before he could decide a third time.

He startled. Up close he was younger than I had thought -- fair-haired, hollow-cheeked, with the weathered complexion of someone who has spent months outdoors and the wary eyes of someone who has learned not to expect much from a closed door.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

He met my gaze. I watched him consider lying, and then decide against it. "Not since yesterday morning, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the work table.

He understood. He sat without argument, setting his hands on his knees as though reporting for inspection. I cut bread, ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since dawn, and set both in front of him. He looked at the food for a moment before he picked up the spoon.

He ate carefully. Not slowly -- he was too hungry for that -- but with a deliberateness that suggested he knew this might be the last meal offered for some time and he intended to be conscious of every mouthful.

When he had finished, he set the spoon down and said, "Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, after a pause, as though the word had been insufficient: "Leeds. I've a sister there."

I nodded. He stood, and I saw him register the distance between the bench and the door -- measuring it, the way men who have been turned out of places measure the distance to the exit. He thanked me again. He went.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and looked at the bench where he had sat. The bowl was empty. The bread was gone. He had held the spoon the way a child holds a pencil -- too tightly, with all his fingers wrapped around it, as though someone might take it from him. He was somebody's son. He was somebody's brother, walking to Leeds in boots that would not last the week.

I picked up the bowl and washed it.

The afternoon brought the usual small emergencies of an inn between coaches. Two men in the taproom -- commercial travellers who had been sitting too long over too little ale -- began to disagree about something I could not hear and did not care to identify. The disagreement was conducted in rising voices and the particular leaning-forward posture that precedes a shove.

I crossed the room and set my hand flat on their table. "Gentlemen. You will settle this outside or you will settle it nowhere, but you will not settle it in my taproom."

They settled it nowhere. The louder of the two muttered something into his glass. The other leaned back in his chair. Nan, behind the bar, had not moved. She had not needed to.

The hours between the morning coach and the evening trade were the hours that kept the inn alive, though no guest ever saw them. I tallied the accounts for the week. I reset the rooms that had been vacated -- stripped the beds, aired the linens, checked the washstands for cracks in the basins. I went through the kitchen stores and made a list for market day. Nan polished glasses. Jem reshod a horse in the yard; I could hear the ring of his hammer through the open window. The ordinary work. The work that did not stop.

The last guest was settled by nine. The last fire checked -- taproom coals banked, the grate swept, the fender set. The bar tallied: Nan had left the figures on the slate in her clear, careful hand, and they were correct, as they always were. The kitchen closed down -- pots scoured, surfaces wiped, the morning's bread dough set to rise under a cloth near the banked hearth.

I moved through the inn's closing routine alone. I had done it hundreds of times, and the rhythm of it was so deeply set in my body that I could have managed it blind: taproom, dining room, front door bolted, cellar hatch locked, the loose window latch in the dining room checked once more. Upstairs, the corridor quiet, every door closed. Back down. Through the kitchen to the yard door.

The stable yard was still. Jem would have settled the horses; I could hear nothing from the stalls but the occasional shift of weight and the soft sound of an animal breathing. The air was sharp and smelled of frost and hay and the last trace of woodsmoke from the kitchen chimney.

I looked up. The second floor: a lit shape behind Bridget's window, the outline of her watching. I did not look long.

My hands, when I turned them over in the lamplight by the kitchen door, were reddened from cold water and the day's work. There was a small cut on my index finger -- the edge of Jessup's delivery note, I thought, though I could not remember the moment it happened. I had not felt it at the time. I felt it now.

I bolted the yard door. I set the bar across it. The inn was locked, and I was alone inside it, and for one breath I let myself know that this was true -- that the silence on the other side of every closed door was not peace but absence, and that I had chosen it, and that the choosing did not always feel like freedom.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

I had not seen it arrive. It must have come with the afternoon post, left by Nan on the table where I would find it during the final circuit. I recognised the handwriting on the direction before I turned it over -- a precise, slanting hand that gave nothing away -- and when I did turn it, the seal confirmed what I already knew. Stainforth.

I picked it up. I held it. I turned it over once, though there was nothing on the reverse I had not already seen. The paper was heavy, the seal cleanly pressed. A man who took care with his correspondence because he took care with everything, and because care, applied with enough patience, was indistinguishable from pressure.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, which was one of several things I would have respected about him if he had not been trying to take my inn.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable interest regarding the White Hound property and its associated lease. My nephew, Mr Edward Stainforth, proposes to take the lease at the next renewal and to undertake such improvements as will better suit the establishment to the needs of the modern traveller. I would be glad to discuss the terms of a transition at your earliest convenience. I suggest we meet before the fifteenth of November, as I intend to give my answer to the interested party by that date. I remain confident that you will see the reasonableness of this arrangement. Yours &c., P. Stainforth.*

I set the letter down on the table. The fifteenth of November. Three weeks. He was not asking whether I wished to give up the lease. He was informing me that he had found someone to give it to, and offering me the courtesy of being told before it happened.

The kitchen was quiet. The fire ticked in the grate. The bread dough rose under its cloth, patient and indifferent.

I looked at the letter once more -- at his confident hand, his *reasonableness*, his nephew's name set down as though the matter were already settled -- and I said, to the empty room, to no one, to the man who thought three weeks was enough to make me disappear:

"You'll need a better offer than your nephew, Mr Stainforth."
