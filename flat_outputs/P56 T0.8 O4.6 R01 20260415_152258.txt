The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumbnail against the smudge as though I could push the number back into sense. Six weeks. I had six weeks before the quarter's rent came due, and the ledger did not care that I had been staring at it since before dawn.

I closed the book and set my hand flat on its cover, feeling the cracked leather under my palm. The kitchen was cold. The fire I had built twenty minutes ago was still finding its courage, sending up thin threads of smoke that could not yet be called heat. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to the hearth to give the coals a reason to try harder.

What I wanted -- and I did not allow myself this thought often, so I held it briefly, the way one holds a coal before dropping it -- was one morning where the numbers were not the first thing. One morning where I came down the stairs and the ledger was someone else's problem and I could simply stand in this kitchen and drink my tea and think about nothing at all. But wanting was an indulgence, and indulgences did not pay quarterly rent, and so I took my candle through to the taproom.

The taproom fire needed building from scratch. The grate had gone to ash overnight, and the draught from the chimney was pulling cold air down instead of drawing smoke up, which meant the flue damper had slipped again. I reached up inside the stonework, found the iron plate, and knocked it back into position with the heel of my hand. Soot rained down onto my forearm. I wiped it on my apron -- the apron was clean, or had been -- and laid the fire properly: paper, kindling, two splits of oak set at an angle so the air could move between them.

While the taproom fire caught, I checked the bar stock. Three casks of ale, one newly tapped, two sealed. The gin was low. The brandy was adequate, which meant I would not need to argue with Mr Hewson at the spirit merchant's until next week, and I was grateful for the reprieve, because Mr Hewson had recently discovered the word "surcharge" and had been applying it with the enthusiasm of a child who has learned to whistle.

The accounts from last night were still on the shelf behind the bar where I had left them. I tallied them standing, running the figures in my head. Fourteen shillings, eightpence. Not terrible. Not enough. I set the paper down and moved to the windows.

The glass was rimed at the edges, October making its presence felt. Through the frost I could see the yard, the stable block, and the grey nothing of a morning that had not yet decided whether to commit to daylight. The Great North Road ran past the front of the inn like a river that never stopped, carrying its traffic north and south regardless of whether I was ready to receive it.

I was always ready. That was the point of me.

By the time I had checked the cellar hatch, confirmed the dining room was laid for breakfast, and returned to the kitchen to set the porridge on, the fire had finally committed itself. The kitchen was almost warm. I hung the kettle and waited for it to sing.

Through the window over the yard, I could see Jem. He was in the stable, visible through the open half-door, straightening tack on its hooks. He had not been asked. He never needed to be asked. His one hand moved with the efficiency of most men's two, settling bridles and breast-collars into their proper places with a brisk, unhesitating rhythm. He did not look toward the kitchen. I did not call out to him. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins in her hair not yet properly set, apron already tied. She took a piece of bread from the crock without asking, ate it in four bites standing at the counter, and went through to the bar. I heard her cloth on the wood -- long, steady strokes -- and then the particular sound of Nan settling into her place, which was no sound at all, only the sense that the bar was now occupied and the inn could open.

The London coach was late, which surprised no one. It was late three mornings out of five, and on the fourth it was early enough to catch me with my hair still pinned from sleep, which I considered a personal affront. This morning it arrived at half past nine, twenty minutes behind its time, and it was running short -- two passengers instead of the expected four.

The first passenger descended without incident. The second had a trunk with a split hinge, and she had a great deal to say about it.

"The clasp gave way at Barnet," she announced, as though Barnet were a personal enemy. "I shall require someone to attend to it immediately."

I directed Jem, who had materialised at the coach door without being summoned, to take the trunk through to the boot room. "We'll see to the hinge," I told the woman. "Your room is the second on the left at the top of the stairs, and breakfast is laid in the dining room."

She opened her mouth, possibly to tell me more about Barnet. I was already turning to the coachman.

He had climbed down from the box and was stamping his boots against the cold, his breath clouding in the morning air. He was a regular -- Tom Jessup, who drove the northern route twice weekly and considered himself an authority on road conditions from here to York.

"The road above Hatfield's washed out at the dip," he said, pulling off his gloves. "Ruts deep enough to break an axle. Someone's put brush in, but it wants gravel."

"It wanted gravel in August," I said. "I've written to the turnpike trust twice."

"Write again. I near lost a wheel."

"I'll write again. But you'll want to swing wide at the dip until they mend it. Keep to the left -- the verge is firmer on that side."

He nodded, satisfied or at least finished, and went in to his breakfast. The yard cleared. Jem took the trunk. The coachman's horses were led to water. I stood in the yard for a moment, feeling the cold air against my neck where my hair had come loose, and then I went inside, because there was always something else.

Nan had been wiping down the bar with one eye on the door through all of it. I noted this. Said nothing.

Mr Fenton, the chandler, arrived at eleven with his cart and his monthly delivery and his particular way of placing his hands on his belt as though the tallow candles he sold were made of gold and he was doing me a tremendous personal favour by parting with them.

"Tuppence ha'penny the dozen," he said, setting the first box on the counter.

I looked at the box. I looked at him.

"It was tuppence last month, Mr Fenton. And a penny three-farthings in April."

"Tallow's gone up." He said this with the regretful air of a man who had no hand in the matter, as though the price of tallow were determined by weather or divine will and not by Mr Fenton's own supplier in Hitchin, who I happened to know charged him fourpence the pound.

"Tallow has gone up a farthing the pound since March," I said. "Which does not account for a ha'penny increase per dozen candles, unless your chandlery has developed some very unusual expenses."

He shifted his weight. "There's the cartage."

"Your cartage has not changed. You drive the same route you have driven for three years, Mr Fenton, and you deliver to every inn between Hatfield and Baldock on the same day, which means I am not paying for a special journey. I am paying for candles."

A pause. He looked at the box. He looked at the door, calculating, I knew, whether he could afford to take his candles elsewhere and whether elsewhere would pay his inflated price. The answer to both was no, and we both knew it, because I made it my business to know what every establishment on this stretch of road paid for tallow, and Mr Fenton's other customers were even less inclined to charity than I was.

"Tuppence farthing," he said.

"Tuppence."

"Miss Dale--"

"Tuppence, Mr Fenton. The same as last month. And if tallow truly does go up, you may bring me your supplier's invoice and we will revisit the matter like reasonable people."

He held my gaze for a moment, then set the second box beside the first. "Tuppence," he said, and took his coin, and left.

I stood behind the counter and allowed myself one small, private breath of satisfaction. Not triumph -- triumph would have required a worthier opponent. But the candles were on the shelf at the right price, and the door was closed, and I had done what I do, which is to hold the line.

The thought came, as it sometimes did between tasks, uninvited and unwelcome. The ledger, the rent, the chandler's price, the quarterly accounts -- all of it held together by the single fact that the lease was in my name and no one else's. If I married, that would end. The lease, the income, the negotiation I had just won -- all of it would pass to a husband as surely as water runs downhill. Two men had asked. I had refused them both, and this was why, and I did not dwell on it further, because dwelling changed nothing and there were rooms to turn over before the evening.

The afternoon was quiet in the way of coaching inns between rushes -- not empty, but held in suspension, waiting for the next wave of arrivals. Nan polished glasses. Jem was somewhere in the stable yard. I worked through the linen inventory, reset the two upstairs rooms that had been vacated, and started the stock for the evening's soup. The ordinary grind. The work that kept the place standing.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the signs asked. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way of men who have been thin for a long time, not the leanness of youth but the whittled look of someone whose body has been consuming itself for fuel. His coat was too large for him, as though it had been made for the man he was before whatever had happened to him. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather separating from the sole on the left foot so that I could see a flash of stocking when he shifted his weight.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. He was looking at the door, then at the yard, then at the door again, performing some private calculation about whether he was welcome enough to announce himself. He was not sure.

I opened the door before he could decide.

He startled, then recovered. He had a young face, weather-roughened, with the particular blankness behind the eyes that I had learned to recognise over the past year -- not vacancy, exactly, but a kind of deliberate distance, as though the person behind the face had stepped back from the window.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

He considered this with a seriousness that told me the honest answer. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He understood. He sat. He did not ask for more than what was offered, and he did not pretend he did not need it. I gave him bread, half a bowl of the broth I had made that morning, and a heel of cheese that was past its best for guests but perfectly sound for a hungry man. He ate with the careful, steady pace of someone who has learned that eating too fast on an empty stomach is a mistake you only make once. He held the spoon the way Jem held tools -- precisely, with attention, as though the instrument mattered.

"Thank you, ma'am," he said when he had finished. He stood and looked at me directly for the first time. "I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, after a pause, as though the word required clarification: "Home. If it's still there."

He went. I closed the door and stood in the kitchen with the empty bowl in my hands. His boots. The left one, with the sole gaping open. He was walking north in boots that would not survive another week of road. He was somebody's son, and his boots were falling apart, and I had given him soup.

I washed the bowl and put it back on the shelf and went to see about the evening.

The trouble in the taproom was nothing -- two men who had been sitting too long over too little ale, disagreeing about something to do with a horse sale in Welwyn. Their voices had risen to the pitch where other guests were beginning to notice.

"Gentlemen," I said from the doorway. "If you wish to continue your discussion, the yard is at your disposal. If you wish to remain in my taproom, you will lower your voices."

They lowered their voices. Nan, behind the bar, did not look up from the glass she was polishing, but the corner of her mouth moved a fraction of an inch.

The afternoon gave way to evening. The second coach came and went. Supper was served, eaten, cleared. The taproom filled and emptied in its usual rhythm. I moved through it all with the efficiency of long practice -- this is what I am for, this is what I do -- and by nine o'clock the last guests had settled and the inn was winding down toward quiet.

I made the final circuit alone. Taproom fire: down to coals, the grate safe. Cellar hatch: locked, the bolt seated properly. Dining room: cleared, the table wiped, the chairs pushed in. Bar: tallied, the cloth folded, Nan's glasses gleaming in a row. I checked the front door, the side door, the latch on the boot-room window that had a habit of working loose.

Outside, the stable yard was quiet. Jem would have settled the horses. I could see the stable block, dark and solid against the sky, and above it, on the inn's second floor, a lit shape at Bridget's window. The faint glow of a candle, the suggestion of a figure. I did not look long. She was there. She was watching. She was always watching.

Back in the kitchen, I checked the range and banked the fire for the night. My hands, when I looked at them, were reddened from cold water and the day's work. There was a small cut on my index finger -- the edge of the chandler's delivery note, which I had not noticed at the time and which was now making itself known with a thin, precise sting. I had fought a man over tuppence and taken a wound from his invoice. There was a kind of justice in that.

I dried my hands on the cloth and stood in the kitchen, and for a moment -- just a moment -- I let myself feel the quiet. The inn was locked. The fires were down. The day was done. And I was alone, as I was every night at this hour, standing in a kitchen that smelled of bread and woodsmoke, with no one to hear if I said anything and nothing to say if there were.

The letter was on the shelf by the door, where the afternoon post was always left. I had seen it when I came in from the yard but had not touched it. I touched it now.

Stainforth's seal. His handwriting on the direction, neat and slanted, addressing me as Miss D. Dale -- never my full name, as though I did not merit the ink.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Mr Stainforth wrote the way he spoke -- measured, certain, and arranged so that every sentence arrived at the same conclusion. He had received an offer for the White Hound's lease from a gentleman of means -- his nephew, though he did not name the relation, as though I would not know -- who wished to acquire the property and undertake substantial improvements to the premises. Mr Stainforth believed this to be in the best interest of the parish and the travelling public. He was prepared to offer me fair terms for the surrender of the lease. He proposed a meeting at his offices on the fourth of November to discuss the particulars. If I did not attend, he would take it as my consent to proceed without consultation.

The fourth of November. Eleven days.

I set the letter on the kitchen table. I looked at it lying there on the scrubbed wood, Stainforth's neat handwriting catching the last light of the banked fire.

Fair terms. Consent to proceed. As though the lease were a hat I had borrowed and he was politely asking me to return it.

I said it aloud, to the empty kitchen, to no one: "You'll have your meeting, Mr Stainforth. And you had better bring more than your nephew."
