The ink had bled through to the second page of the ledger, which meant I'd pressed too hard on the nib again, which meant the numbers were worse than I'd hoped.

Six weeks. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the column of figures I'd been staring at since before dawn did not rearrange themselves into a more agreeable sum no matter how long I let the candle gutter over them. I closed the ledger and set my palm flat on the cover as though I could press the trouble back inside.

The kitchen was cold. Not the kind of cold that announces itself -- the kind that has been sitting in the walls all night, patient and familiar, waiting for you to notice. The fire I'd built twenty minutes ago was only now beginning to argue with it. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists, tucked the ledger onto its shelf behind the flour bin, and got on with the morning.

There is a particular kind of want that lives in the body rather than the mind -- not a wish, not a daydream, but a low ache that settles behind the breastbone when the house is dark and still and you are the only thing moving in it. I wanted -- well. What I wanted had no place in the accounts. I wanted one morning where the numbers came out right without my having to shave a halfpenny off every line to make them balance. I wanted to sit at this table with a cup of tea that I had not made myself and feel, just for a quarter of an hour, that someone else was minding the gates.

I did not sit. I checked the kitchen fire, then crossed through to the taproom and knelt at the second hearth. The ash was cold and pale. I raked it out, laid the kindling, struck the flint, blew until the flame caught. Behind the bar I counted bottles by touch in the half-dark -- gin, brandy, the last of the decent claret. Fewer than yesterday. I noted it. Then I wiped down the bar with a rag that needed replacing, tallied the previous night's take against the chits Nan had left in the tin, and found the discrepancy I'd expected: fourpence short, which meant Mr. Howell had talked his way into a second measure again and Nan had let him.

The windows were rimed at their edges. Beyond the glass the yard was grey, the cobbles slicked with overnight damp. Nothing moved out there yet except the stable cat, who had opinions about the cold and was expressing them from the mounting block.

I adjusted the fire screen, straightened two chairs, and set the dining room for breakfast with the efficiency of a woman who has done this several thousand times and no longer requires light to find the salt cellar.

Jem was in the stable yard when I opened the back door to check the weather. He had the big bay's bridle across his knee and was working at the cheekpiece with his one hand and the blunt hook he'd rigged to the stump of the other. He did not look up. He did not need to. The yard was swept, the water trough was full, and the hay net outside the first stall had been rehung at the height the coach horses preferred. I noted all of this in the time it took to draw a breath, and I went back inside without a word.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins half-set in her hair, apron already knotted. She took her place behind the bar as though she had materialised there. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times. The inn opened the way a clock strikes -- not because anyone announced it, but because every part moved when it was supposed to.

The London coach was late, which was not unusual. What was unusual was the shortness of it: two passengers instead of the expected four, and both cross enough to strip paint. The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who had a trunk with a split hinge and wanted the world to know about it. I sent Jem to deal with the trunk before she could get properly started, and the sight of him lifting it one-handed onto the barrow silenced her more effectively than anything I could have said.

The second passenger went upstairs without comment. I did not learn his name. I did not need to.

The coachman found me in the yard. He was called Burrell, and I had known him four years. He had the particular stiffness of a man who has been on the box since Islington and wants to complain but knows he has only two minutes in which to do it.

"North road's gone to ruin above Hatfield," he said. "Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel. Someone ought to speak to the turnpike trust."

"Someone has. Twice. They've promised gravel before the frost."

He snorted. "They promised gravel last year."

"Then I suggest you keep to the left side past the Wrestlers. It's firmer ground." I stepped back from the horses. "Anything else?"

He shook his head, climbed back up, and was gone. The yard settled into the particular quiet that follows a coach's departure -- the dust still turning in the air, the smell of horse and road lingering, and then nothing but the sparrows in the eaves.

Nan had been wiping the same stretch of bar through all of this, one eye on the door. I noticed. I said nothing.

The chandler came at half past ten, which was his custom, carrying the month's order of tallow candles in a crate on his shoulder and the expression of a man who believed he was about to get away with something. His name was Ferris. He was not unpleasant. He was merely predictable.

"Morning, Miss Dale. Three dozen, as agreed. I'll need one and four the dozen this month."

I set down the cloth I'd been using on the dining room table and looked at him. "You charged one and two last month."

"Tallow's gone up."

"Tallow's gone up a farthing the pound since March. You've raised your price twopence the dozen in seven months. That's a margin of sixpence you didn't have in April, Mr. Ferris, and I doubt Mrs. Partridge at the King's Arms is paying it."

He shifted the crate on his shoulder. "Mrs. Partridge orders twice what you do."

"Mrs. Partridge waters her gin and hasn't scrubbed her kitchen floor since the peace. I'll give you one and three, and I'll take four dozen instead of three, and you'll have the money today rather than on account."

A pause. The crate shifted again. I could see him calculating -- the walk back to his cart with thirty-six unsold candles against the coins I was offering him now.

"One and three," he said.

"Set them by the cellar hatch."

He set them by the cellar hatch. He left. The candles stayed.

I allowed myself one moment -- standing alone in the kitchen with the door still open and the October air coming through it -- to feel the small, precise satisfaction of having paid less than he wanted and more than he deserved. It was not triumph. It was arithmetic. But it was mine.

The thought snagged, as it sometimes did, on the word. Mine. The lease, the accounts, the negotiations, the early mornings and the last-locked doors -- all of it was mine only so long as I remained as I was. Unmarried. Unattached. Under the law, a husband would own every farthing of it the moment the register was signed, and no amount of tallow arithmetic would change that. I had refused two proposals for this reason, and I had stopped entertaining the possibility of a third. It was not bitterness. It was the same calculation I had just performed with Ferris, only the stakes were the whole of my life instead of twopence a dozen.

I closed the door and went to check the cellar stores.

He came around the back, as the sign asked. I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked -- young, thin, standing on the step with the careful stillness of someone deciding whether he was welcome enough to try the door. His coat was too large for him and had been mended at the shoulder with thread that did not match. His boots were coming apart at the welt. He could not have been older than twenty.

I opened the door before he knocked.

He startled, then steadied. His eyes went to my face, then to my apron, then back. Not threatening. Measuring.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

A beat. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He sat. I cut bread, ladled broth from the pot I kept going through the day, and set both in front of him without ceremony. He ate the way hungry people eat -- not fast, but thoroughly, as though each mouthful required his full attention. He held the spoon carefully, the way you hold something that might be taken away.

When he finished, he stood and touched his forehead. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm obliged."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, as though that were insufficient: "There's family in Nottingham. Or there was."

I nodded. He went out the way he'd come, around the side of the building and onto the road, and I watched through the window until he was past the gate.

His boots had been army issue. I had seen enough of them to know. The leather was cracked straight through the sole, and he had stuffed paper into the gap to keep out the wet. He was somebody's son, walking north on paper soles, and the broth I had given him would last him until evening if he was careful and not at all if he wasn't.

I washed the bowl. I dried it. I put it back on the shelf with the others.

In the taproom, two men who had been sitting too long over too little ale had found something to disagree about. I heard the pitch of it change -- the shift from grumbling to the particular sharp note that precedes broken furniture -- and crossed from the kitchen in four strides.

"Gentlemen. You'll settle it outside or you'll settle your accounts and go. Choose now."

They chose to go quiet. Nan, behind the bar, had not moved. She did not need to.

The afternoon passed the way afternoons pass at a coaching inn between rushes -- in small tasks that accumulate into hours without marking themselves. I reset the rooms the morning passengers had used. I reconciled the kitchen stores against tomorrow's needs. I wrote out the week's order for the brewer and left it on the bar for the drayman. Nan polished glasses. Jem reshod the grey cob in the yard, the ring of the hammer carrying through the open window with the steadiness of a clock.

No incidents. No drama. Only the work, and the ordinary weight of it.

Evening came on early, the way it does in late October -- not falling so much as thickening, the grey light turning to dusk while your hands are still busy. I served the remaining guests their supper, cleared the plates, washed up, and began the closing circuit.

Taproom fire raked down to coals and the screen set in front. Bar tallied, the tin locked, the key on its hook. Cellar hatch bolted. Dining room chairs pushed in, the cloth folded for tomorrow. Kitchen banked and the chimney damper half-closed. I moved through it all with the ease of long practice, the inn settling around me the way a house does when it has been used hard and is finally permitted to rest.

My hands at the end of the day were what they always were -- reddened from cold water and the scrubbing brush, the knuckles rough, a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Ferris's delivery note had caught me when I wasn't paying attention. I noticed it the way you notice a bruise you don't remember getting. It stung when I flexed my hand. I flexed it again anyway, because the front door still needed locking.

I crossed the yard on the last circuit. The stable was quiet -- Jem would have settled the horses and gone to his room above the tack store. The cobbles were dark with damp. Above me, on the second floor, there was a lit shape at Bridget's window: the small, upright silhouette I had seen a thousand times, watching the yard the way she always watched it, missing nothing. I did not look long.

I bolted the front door. Set the bar. Checked the latch on the yard gate. Came back through the kitchen and stood for a moment with my hand on the back of the chair where I'd sat that morning with the ledger.

The inn was quiet. I was alone in it, the way I was alone in it every night, and most nights I did not let myself notice. Tonight I noticed.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

It had not been there at supper. Nan must have taken the afternoon post and left it for me, and I had walked past it twice without seeing it. The seal was dark red, pressed deep into the paper. I knew the hand on the direction before I read the name -- the cramped, precise letters, the particular way he formed his capital D.

Stainforth.

I picked it up. Turned it over once. Broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth's letters were always brief. He did not waste words any more than he wasted advantage.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable interest regarding the property on which the White Hound presently stands. The gentleman in question proposes a use for the premises that I believe would better serve the needs of the parish and the traffic of the road. I would not, of course, act without first extending to you the courtesy of a meeting. I shall expect you at Stainforth House on the fifth of November, at which time we may discuss the terms under which your present lease might be concluded. I trust you will appreciate that this matter cannot be indefinitely deferred.*

I read it twice. I set it down on the table, smoothed the fold with my thumb, and looked at it lying there on the scrubbed wood next to the salt cellar and the stub of the morning's candle.

He had a buyer. He had a date. He expected me to come to his house and discuss the terms under which I would agree to lose everything my grandmother had built.

I stood in the empty kitchen with the fire burned low and the draught coming under the door, and I said, to no one, to the room, to the letter on the table:

"Then I suppose Mr. Stainforth had better be told what my terms are."
