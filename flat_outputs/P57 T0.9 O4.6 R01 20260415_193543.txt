The ink had bled through to the second column again, which meant the nib wanted replacing, which meant fourpence she'd rather spend on salt.

I blotted the ledger and did the sum a third time. The answer had not improved. Six weeks and two days until the quarter's rent came due, and between now and then the inn would need to eat its way through more flour, more tallow, more coal than the coaching trade was currently supplying in ready coin. The numbers were not impossible. They were merely unforgiving, in the way of numbers that have been unforgiving for a long time and see no reason to relent.

I closed the ledger and set my palm flat on its cover, as though the pressure of my hand might keep the figures from worsening while my back was turned.

The kitchen was cold. Not the killing cold of deep winter -- we were not there yet -- but the particular October chill that crept along the flagstones before dawn and settled into every joint and hinge of a building that had been standing since the second Charles sat the throne. The fire I'd laid twenty minutes ago was catching but not yet giving, and the windows wore a fine rime at their edges that would not burn off until midmorning. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to check the flue.

What I wanted -- what I wanted and did not often permit myself to name -- was one morning, just one, when I might come downstairs and find the fire already lit, the water already drawn, and someone else's hand on the ledger. Not because I couldn't manage. I could always manage. But there is a kind of tiredness that lodges behind the breastbone, and it has nothing to do with sleep.

I shook the grate, watched the coals brighten, and got on with it.

The taproom fire next. I raked the ash, laid the kindling, struck the flint twice before it caught. The bar stock I checked by touch as much as sight in the dim room -- the level in each cask, the state of the taps, the small matter of a sticky handle on the second pull that Jem had promised to look at and hadn't. Last night's accounts were already tallied; I'd done them before bed because I could not sleep and the numbers, at least, would sit still and be counted. The dining room wanted airing. I cracked the window an inch and felt the cold press in like a creditor.

Back through the passage. The cellar hatch: locked. The storeroom: flour low, sugar adequate, the last cheese beginning to turn at one corner. I cut the bad edge off with a knife and wrapped the rest in cloth. Nothing wasted. Nothing ever wasted, because waste was a luxury, and the White Hound did not trade in luxuries.

Jem was in the stable yard when I looked out the back, already at work on a bridle that had frayed at the cheekpiece. He did not look up. His one hand moved with the practised certainty of a man who has long since stopped thinking about what is missing and concerns himself only with what remains. The horses were quiet. The yard was swept. He had been here since before I came down, which was always, and which I never mentioned, because mentioning it would imply that I had noticed, and noticing would imply that I was grateful, and gratitude between us had been settled so long ago that raising it now would have been an insult to us both.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar and took her position without a word. Her hands went to the cloth and she began wiping down the counter in the long, efficient strokes of someone who has wiped this counter six thousand times and will wipe it six thousand more. We had done this before. Many times. The doing was the conversation.

---

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual -- the London coach had been late more often than not since September, when the rains turned the north road above Hatfield into something between a river and a punishment from God -- but today it arrived nearly forty minutes behind and carrying only two passengers instead of the expected four. The first was a clergyman who descended with the weary grace of a man who has been praying for deliverance and received a coaching inn instead. The second was a woman of perhaps fifty, travelling with a trunk whose hinge had split clean through, and she wished everyone present to know that this was somebody's fault.

"The roads," she announced to the yard at large, "are a disgrace."

I directed Jem to the trunk with a look. He was already moving.

"Ma'am, if you'll step inside, Nan will see you settled. There's tea, and the dining room fire is laid."

She looked at me as though I had offered her a live eel. "I require someone to attend to my trunk immediately. The hinge is destroyed. My things will be ruined."

"Jem will see to the hinge. Nan will see to you. Both will happen faster if you're inside."

Something in the way I said it -- perhaps the flatness, perhaps the fact that I had already turned to the coachman -- surprised her into cooperation. She went. The clergyman had already found his own way in, God apparently providing for those who do not stand in stable yards demanding miracles.

The coachman climbed down stiffly. He was a man I knew by sight -- Hargraves, who ran the Tuesday and Friday route. His face had the particular redness of someone who has been arguing with weather for six hours and lost.

"Miss Dale. The road north of Hatfield is near impassable past the Lemsford turning. There's a sinkhole wide enough to lose a wheel."

"Is it marked?"

"I put a branch across it. Won't last the day."

"I'll send word to the turnpike trust." Which would accomplish nothing, but it would be sent, and the sending would be noted, and when the trust failed to act, the noting would be useful. "Was it the sinkhole that cost you the other two passengers, or did they simply not book?"

"Didn't book. Trade's thin this week." He rubbed his jaw. "Thin last week too."

"It'll pick up before the quarter." I did not know if this was true. I said it because the coachman needed to continue his route and could not do so while contemplating the decline of the coaching trade. "There's ale inside if you've ten minutes."

"Five," he said, and went.

I stood in the yard after he'd gone and let the quiet settle. Two passengers. The inn took a fixed fee per coach, plus whatever the passengers spent on food and drink, plus stabling. Two passengers instead of four meant less at the bar, less in the dining room, less against the column in the ledger that was already too thin. I did not dwell on it. I noted it, the way I noted the rime on the windows and the fraying bridle and the state of the cheese, and I folded it into the running calculation that occupied a permanent and unfurnished room in my mind.

Jem took the trunk inside. The yard cleared. Through the window I could see Nan behind the bar, cloth in hand, one eye on the door to the dining room. She had been watching the whole of it. She said nothing. Neither did I.

---

Mr. Campion, the chandler, arrived at half past eleven with his cart and his quarterly invoice and his particular way of smiling that meant the price had gone up again.

"Miss Dale. A fine morning."

"It is October, Mr. Campion. The morning is adequate. What are you charging me?"

He set the crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table with the care of a man presenting jewels. "Tenpence ha'penny the dozen. Tallow's dear this season, as I'm sure you're aware."

"Tallow is sixpence a pound at Hitchin market. It was sixpence a pound last month, and the month before that, and the month before that. Your costs have not risen. Your prices have."

"There's the cartage to consider--"

"Your cartage has not increased either, unless your horse has taken to eating gold."

He shifted his weight. He was not a bad man, Mr. Campion. He was a man with a margin, and he had calculated -- reasonably, from his position -- that a woman running an inn alone would absorb a ha'penny rise rather than risk losing her supplier. He had been calculating this for seven months. He had been wrong for seven months, but the beauty of Mr. Campion's position was that he could afford to keep trying.

"Ninepence the dozen," I said. "Same as the Woolpack, same as the Red Lion, same as every other house on this stretch of road. I know because I asked."

A pause. He looked at the crate. He looked at me. He looked at the door, as though measuring the distance he would have to carry the candles back to his cart.

"Ninepence ha'penny," he said.

"Ninepence. And I'll take an extra two dozen at the same rate, paid on delivery next month."

He sucked his teeth. He thought about it. He extended his hand.

"Ninepence," he said.

I shook his hand, and he unloaded the candles, and he left.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and allowed myself three seconds of quiet satisfaction. It was not a fortune. It was a ha'penny per dozen on eight dozen candles a month, which came to fourpence saved, which was precisely the cost of a new nib for the pen that had bled through the ledger this morning. The world was full of small economies that connected to each other like the workings of a clock, and I knew every gear.

The knowing was what I had. The ledger, the margins, the negotiation -- these were mine because I had learned them, and no one could unlace that knowledge from my keeping. If I married, the inn itself would pass to my husband under the law as it stood. The lease, the income, the right to stand in this kitchen and argue with chandlers -- all of it, transferred like goods on a bill of sale. I had done the calculation twice, years ago, when two separate men had proposed and I had refused them both. The calculation had not changed. The law had not changed. I had not changed, except to become more certain.

I wiped the table where the crate had been and went to see about dinner.

---

He came around the back, as the sign asked.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked -- a young man, thin, standing just outside the door with his weight on one foot and his head slightly turned, as though listening for permission that had not yet been given. His coat was too large for him. Army issue, I thought, from the cut of it, though the colour had gone to a brownish nothing that might once have been red. His boots were the worst of it. The soles had separated from the uppers at the welt, and he'd wrapped them with what looked like strips of sacking to keep them together. He could not have been more than twenty.

He stood there for a long moment, assessing whether he was welcome enough to knock. He was not sure.

I opened the door before he had to decide.

He startled -- just barely, a flinch in the shoulders that he covered by straightening. "Ma'am. I'm sorry to trouble you."

"Have you eaten?"

He looked at me directly then. His eyes were older than the rest of him. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom."

He nodded. He understood. He stepped inside and sat where I pointed -- the bench by the hearth, away from the window -- and he held his hands in his lap while I ladled broth from the pot and cut bread from the loaf and set both before him with a spoon. He picked up the spoon carefully. He ate without rushing, which told me he had been hungry long enough to know that eating too fast would bring it back up.

I did not watch him. I went about the kitchen work -- the potatoes wanted peeling for the evening, and the joint wanted turning -- and I let him eat in the privacy that a man who has been reduced to asking for food at back doors deserves.

When the bowl was empty, he set the spoon across it and stood.

"Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, after a pause: "Nottingham. Thereabouts."

He did not elaborate. I did not ask. He touched his forehead -- not a salute, exactly, but the memory of one -- and went out the way he'd come.

I closed the door. I stood with my hand on the latch and looked at the empty bowl on the bench. The spoon laid across it just so, as though someone had taught him that much at least -- that you leave things neat when you have been a guest, even when the guest was uninvited and the kindness was bread and broth in a kitchen that smelled of tallow and onions.

He was somebody's son. He was twenty years old, and his boots were held together with sacking, and he had held the spoon the way a child holds a thing that has been given to him and might be taken back.

I washed the bowl and put it away.

---

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say, in work. The coach had gone. The passengers had settled or departed. Two men in the taproom disagreed about something to do with a horse sale, and their voices began to climb the walls in the way of men who have been sitting too long and have found a grievance to make the sitting feel purposeful.

I crossed to their table. "Gentlemen. You'll settle this outside or you'll settle it elsewhere, but you will not settle it in my taproom."

They settled it by going quiet, which was the usual outcome, and which Nan observed from behind the bar with the expression of a woman who has seen this particular play performed many times and no longer requires the programme.

The rest was the rest. Accounts. Rooms turned over. The kitchen set right for the evening meal. Nan at the bar. Jem outside. The ordinary grind of a day that asks nothing of you except everything, and gives nothing back except the assurance that tomorrow will ask the same.

---

Evening came down early, as it does in late October, and the light went out of the yard by half past five. I made the final rounds alone.

Taproom fire banked to coals. The cellar hatch locked -- I checked the bolt twice, a habit my mother had drilled into me at an age when I did not understand what lived in cellars that wanted locking out, and which I had never broken. The bar tallied. The kitchen closed down: pots scoured, surfaces wiped, the morning's bread dough set to prove under a cloth by the banked fire.

I moved through the inn the way water moves through a channel it has cut for itself -- not thinking about direction, only flowing along the path that use has worn smooth. The dining room. The passage. The front door, bolted. The stairs creaked on the third and seventh step; I knew their voices as well as I knew my own.

The second floor was quiet. At the far end of the corridor, a line of light showed beneath Bridget's door. I paused at the landing window and looked down into the yard. A lit shape at her window above -- I could see it reflected in the glass, the outline of her sitting in her chair, watching the yard as she always watched it, recording whatever she recorded in the long private accounting of a woman who has outlived nearly everyone she knew.

I did not look long.

My hands at the end of the day were what they always were: reddened from cold water and lye soap, the knuckles rough, a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Mr. Campion's invoice had caught the skin when I unfolded it. I had not noticed at the time. I noticed now, in the way that the body saves its complaints for the moment when you are finally still enough to hear them.

I set the last bolt. I stood in the passage with my hand on the iron and let myself know what I already knew, which was that the inn was locked and quiet and mine, and that I was alone in it. Not unhappily. Not happily either. Alone in the way of a woman who has chosen this and would choose it again and still, on certain nights, when the bolt slides home and the silence is very thorough, feels the shape of the choice like a room she has furnished with everything except the one thing she cannot bring herself to name.

I went back down to the kitchen.

---

The letter was on the table.

It had not been there an hour ago. Nan must have taken it from the afternoon post and left it for me, knowing I would come back through the kitchen last, as I always did. The seal was red, pressed deep, and even before I turned it over I knew the hand. Stainforth's clerk wrote the direction -- I recognised the cramped, forward-slanting script -- but the seal was Stainforth's own, which meant the letter was not routine business. Routine business came from the clerk alone. When Stainforth set his seal to a letter, he wanted you to know that what was inside had his personal weight behind it.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The paper was good -- heavier than anything I used, cream-coloured, the kind that costs more than it needs to because the cost is part of the message. My name on the front: Miss D. Dale, The White Hound, Great North Road.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy had a different quality -- the brevity of a man who believes his time is worth more than yours and prices his sentences accordingly.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer for the freehold of the property known as the White Hound, Great North Road, from a buyer whose terms are favourable and whose intentions for the property are, I believe, to the advantage of the parish. As your lease is subject to renewal at Lady Day next, I think it proper to discuss the implications for your tenancy before any agreement is concluded. I propose a meeting at my offices on the 4th of November. I trust this is convenient. Yr obedient servant, P. Stainforth.*

Lady Day was the twenty-fifth of March. Five months. He was not threatening to break the lease -- the law would not allow it before the term expired. He was doing something more careful than that. He was telling me that the lease would not be renewed. That a buyer was waiting. That the inn I had run for two years, that my mother had run for fourteen, that my grandmother had run for twenty before that, would pass to someone else's hands on a date that was already fixed, and that the meeting he proposed was not a negotiation but a courtesy -- the kind of courtesy a man extends when he has already decided and wishes to appear generous in the deciding.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed my fingertips against the wood and felt the grain of it, the scars and rings left by a hundred years of pots and knives and the elbows of people who had sat here and eaten and gone.

The kitchen was silent. The fire ticked softly as the coals settled.

I looked at the letter once more, at the neat clerk's hand and the heavy seal and the single most important line: *a buyer whose terms are favourable.*

"Five months," I said aloud, to the empty room, to the inn, to no one. "Then we'd best make them unfavourable."
