The ink had frozen in the ledger's gutter overnight, and I had to breathe on the nib twice before the numbers would come.

Six weeks. That was what the column told me when I tallied it honestly, which I always did, because the numbers did not care whether I found them agreeable. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between what the White Hound earned and what it owed had been thinning since midsummer like broth stretched one bowl too many. I closed the ledger and set my palm flat on its cover, as though I could press the figures into better behaviour.

The kitchen was cold. Not the vicious cold of deep winter -- that would come soon enough -- but the mean, creeping chill of late October, the kind that got into your knuckles and stayed. The fire I'd built up twenty minutes ago was only now beginning to throw heat past the hearth, and the windows were rimed at their edges with frost so fine it looked like needlework. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists, tucked the ledger onto its shelf behind the flour bin, and got on with it.

There is a particular order to opening an inn, and I had learned it the way I had learned most things worth knowing -- by watching my mother do it a thousand times and then doing it myself a thousand more. Kitchen fire first, because everything begins with the kitchen. Then the taproom fire, which was slower to draw and needed coaxing with a twist of paper and a prayer. Bar stock checked: the cask of ordinary ale was a third full, which would see us through two days if the coaches ran light, one day if they didn't. Last night's accounts tallied against the cash box. I counted the coins twice, not because I distrusted Nan but because I distrusted the universe, which had a habit of removing shillings when one wasn't looking.

The taproom smelled of last night's tallow and the ghost of spilled porter. I opened the shutter on the east window to let in what grey light the morning had to offer, and the draught that came with it made me wish I hadn't. I checked the hinge on the settle nearest the fire -- it had been wobbling for a week -- and added it to the list I kept in my head of things that needed mending and the shorter list of things I could afford to mend. I wiped down the bar with a rag, set the chairs straight, and noted that someone had left a heel of bread on the windowsill. A mouse had been at it. I threw it into the yard for the birds and latched the window properly.

I wanted, very briefly and with an intensity that surprised me, to sit down. Not to rest -- I was not tired yet, or rather I was always tired and had stopped distinguishing it from any other condition -- but simply to be still in my own place for one uninterrupted moment before the day took it from me. I did not sit down. There was no time, and wanting things you could not have before seven in the morning was a poor way to begin.

Out the back, the stable yard was sharp with frost. Jem was already there. He had the big bay's bridle over his arm -- his one arm, the right, the left sleeve pinned neatly at the elbow as always -- and he was straightening the tack on its hooks with the unhurried precision of a man who considered disorder a personal affront. He did not look up when I appeared in the doorway. He did not need to. We had done this so many mornings that my presence was no more remarkable to him than the cold, and about as welcome. I checked the water trough -- not frozen, not yet -- noted that the gate latch wanted oiling, and went back inside.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word, and the White Hound had its crew. Such as we were.

The London coach was late, which was not unusual, and running short -- two passengers instead of the expected four, which was becoming usual in a way I did not like to think about. The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the air of someone preparing to lodge a formal complaint with God. The second was a thin man who said nothing and went directly upstairs. The woman had a trunk with a split hinge, and she had opinions.

"That road past Hatfield is a disgrace," she announced to the yard at large. "A positive disgrace. I have been rattled half to death."

"Jem," I said, and he was already there, taking the trunk with his one hand braced beneath it and the stump steadying the side, smooth as anything. The woman stared. I stepped between her and the sight of it, not for Jem's sake -- he couldn't have cared less -- but because I did not have time for her to find her manners.

"Your room is the second on the left at the top of the stairs, ma'am. Breakfast is laid in the dining room until nine. Nan will show you up."

She went. They nearly always went when I used that voice. I did not know precisely what the voice was -- my mother had possessed it too -- but it seemed to communicate that further discussion was not among the morning's offerings.

The coachman wanted a word. He was a barrel of a man named Hatch whom I'd known for three years, and he stood in the yard with his hands shoved into his coat and his nose the colour of beetroot.

"North road's gone to pieces above Hatfield, Miss Dale. Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel in. Someone ought to speak to the turnpike trust."

"Someone has. Twice. They've promised gravel before the first frost."

"Frost is here."

"Then I expect the gravel will follow shortly, or I shall write a third time and make myself very tedious." I handed him the cup of ale Nan had drawn without being asked. "You've twenty minutes if you want breakfast."

He took the ale and the twenty minutes, and I turned back to the inn. The yard cleared. Jem took the trunk upstairs. Nan was behind the bar, wiping it down with one eye on the door, the way she always did when there were strangers in the house. I saw her watching. I said nothing. There was nothing to say that the watching didn't already cover.

The chandler arrived at half past ten with his cart and his quarterly smile, which was the smile of a man who believed he was about to get the better of a woman. His name was Fenton, and he had been raising his price on tallow candles for seven months running, a shilling here and tuppence there, in the apparent hope that I would not notice or would not mind.

I noticed. I minded.

"Fourteen shillings the gross," he said, setting the crate on the kitchen table with a thump that suggested the matter was settled.

"It was twelve and six in April."

"Tallow's dear this autumn, Miss Dale. The renderers have put their prices up. I'm passing the cost along, same as everyone."

"The renderers raised their price by a halfpenny a pound in August. I know because I asked them. That accounts for perhaps a shilling on the gross, Mr Fenton, and you have raised yours by one and six. Which means sixpence of your increase is yours and not the renderers' at all."

He opened his mouth. He closed it. He had the look of a card player who has just discovered his opponent can count.

"Thirteen shillings," I said. "Which is sixpence above your April price and more than covers the tallow increase. You may take it or leave the candles, but I should tell you that Mrs Haddon at the Red Lion pays twelve and nine, and if you walk out of here with that crate, I shall walk into her kitchen tomorrow and ask how she managed it."

A silence. Fenton rubbed his jaw.

"Thirteen and three," he said.

"Thirteen. And I'll take an extra dozen tapers for the difference, if you have them."

He had them. He left the candles, took his thirteen shillings, and went out through the yard without his quarterly smile. The door closed behind him, and I permitted myself one moment -- just one -- of the private, sharp-edged satisfaction that came from knowing exactly what a thing was worth and refusing to pay a farthing more. My mother had taught me that. Among other things.

I carried the candles to the storeroom and stood for a moment in the narrow dark, counting what we had. The ledger's arithmetic followed me everywhere. Every candle, every pint of ale, every scuttle of coal fed into the same unforgiving column, and the column always ended at the same place: the rent. The lease was mine -- had been my mother's, and her mother's before that -- for as long as I held it in my own name. If I married, the law was blunt about what happened next. The lease, the income, the right to stand in my own kitchen and tell a chandler his price was too high -- all of it would pass to a husband, because that was what the law called reasonable and what I called the reason I had refused two proposals and stopped entertaining the possibility of a third.

I did not dwell on it. I had dwelt on it enough. I set the candles on their shelf and went back to work.

He came around the back, as the sign asked. I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked -- young, thin, standing outside the door with the particular stillness of a man who was deciding whether he was welcome enough to try. His coat was too large for him and had been someone else's before it was his. His boots were coming apart where the sole met the upper, the welt splitting in a way that meant they had been wet too many times and dried too few. He could not have been older than twenty. He stood there for perhaps ten seconds, looking at the door, and then he raised his hand and knocked once, softly, as though he did not wish to impose on the wood.

I opened the door.

He took his cap off. His hair was brown and needed cutting, and there was a hollow look about his cheeks that I had seen before, on other men, in other months. "Begging your pardon, ma'am."

"Have you eaten?"

He hesitated, which was answer enough, but he gave me the honest version anyway. "Not since yesterday morning, ma'am."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He sat. I cut bread and ladled broth from the pot that had been on since dawn -- it was only stock and root vegetables, nothing that would be missed from the evening's supper -- and set it in front of him. He looked at it for a moment as though he had forgotten what to do, and then he ate, steadily and without speaking, holding the spoon the way a man holds something he is not certain will be taken from him.

I busied myself at the kitchen table and did not watch him eat, because being watched while hungry is its own small cruelty. When the bowl was empty, he set the spoon down and looked up.

"Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. "Yorkshire, if I can get there."

I nodded. He stood, put his cap back on, and went out through the yard without asking for anything more. The door closed behind him, and I stood at the table with my hands in the flour I'd been measuring and thought about his boots. The way the stitching had given out along the welt, so that you could see the pale edge of his stocking through the gap. He was somebody's son, walking north in boots that would not last the week.

I wiped my hands on my apron and went back to the flour.

The afternoon brought the usual small fractures. Two men in the taproom -- commercial travellers, both of them, who had been sitting too long over too little ale and had found something to disagree about. The pitch of their voices told me what was coming before the words did.

I walked to their table. "Gentlemen. You'll settle this outside or you'll settle your accounts and leave. I don't mind which."

They settled it by going quiet, which was the third option and the one I preferred. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and looked away. She had not needed to move. That was the point.

The hours between the afternoon coach and the evening meal were the inn's held breath. Nan polished glasses. Jem was in the yard, doing whatever Jem did when the horses were settled and the tack was clean -- I suspected he simply stood and looked at things, which was his version of contentment. I reset the rooms that needed resetting, checked the kitchen stores against tomorrow's needs, and tallied the day's accounts in my head because the ledger was in the kitchen and I was upstairs and I was not going to make the trip twice.

The work filled the hours the way it always did, and I was grateful for it, and I did not ask myself whether gratitude and satisfaction were the same thing, because I already knew the answer.

Evening came down early, as it does in late October, and the inn drew into itself. The last guests were settled by eight. The last fire was checked by nine. I moved through the closing routine the way I always did -- alone, because the end of the day belonged to me and I preferred it that way, or had at least convinced myself that I did.

Taproom fire: down to coals, the grate swept, the fender set. Cellar hatch: locked, the key on its hook. Bar: tallied, the cash box locked, Nan's cloth folded neatly on the counter. She had already gone up. Kitchen: the range banked, the morning's bread dough set to prove under a cloth, the pots scoured and hung. Stable yard: quiet. Jem would have settled the horses an hour ago. I could see the dark bulk of the stable from the back door, and above it, on the second floor of the inn, a lit shape at Bridget's window. She was there, sitting in her chair, watching the yard as she did every evening. I did not look long. I had seen it too many times for it to require anything from me.

I bolted the back door and stood in the kitchen. My hands were red from the cold water and the cleaning, and there was a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught the skin. I hadn't felt it at the time. I felt it now. The kitchen was quiet in the way a room is quiet when it has been used hard all day and has finally been allowed to stop, and I was alone in it, and for one unguarded moment I let myself know that I was alone in it, the way I was alone in it every night, and that the silence was not peace but only the absence of anyone to break it.

Then I saw the letter.

It was on the table, half-hidden beneath the cloth I'd thrown over the bread dough. Nan must have put it there when the afternoon post came, and I had missed it in the rush of the evening meal. I picked it up. The seal was dark red, pressed deep into the wax with a signet I knew. The handwriting on the direction was small, precise, and unhurried.

Stainforth.

I turned the letter over once. I broke the seal.

The paper was good -- heavier than anything I would buy for myself -- and the hand was the same measured script I had seen on the lease renewal three years ago. I read it standing up, by the light of the single candle I had not yet snuffed.

He was writing to inform me, with the greatest respect for my late mother's memory and the service my family had rendered to the parish, that he had received an offer from a gentleman of means who wished to acquire the White Hound's lease for the purpose of improving and modernising the establishment. Mr Stainforth considered it his duty to present this offer to me in person, and proposed a meeting at his offices on the fourth of November. He wished me to understand that the current terms of the lease, which had been extended as a courtesy following my mother's death, were not indefinite, and that he would require my answer regarding the transfer before the quarter day in December.

I set the letter down on the table. The candle guttered once and steadied.

He wanted the lease. He had a buyer. And he had given me six weeks -- the same six weeks the ledger had already counted down for me, as though the numbers and the man had been conspiring together all autumn.

I looked at the letter on the table, at Stainforth's careful, unhurried hand, at the neat way he had phrased a threat as a courtesy and a deadline as a duty.

"The fourth of November," I said to the empty kitchen. "I'll be there at nine, Mr Stainforth, and I will bring my own terms."
