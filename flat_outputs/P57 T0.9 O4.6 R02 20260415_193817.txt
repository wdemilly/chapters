The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the smudge before it could ruin the page beneath.

Ledger work in October meant cold fingers and a pen that would not cooperate, and this morning both conspired against me with particular enthusiasm. I flexed my hand, breathed on it, and returned to the figure I had been circling for a quarter of an hour. Six weeks. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the numbers in front of me were not going to rearrange themselves into comfort no matter how long I stared at them. I closed the book.

The kitchen fire had caught properly at last. I'd built it up from the banked coals before the ledger, and now the room was approaching something warmer than miserable, though not yet warm. The windows were rimed at their edges with the kind of frost that wouldn't burn off until midmorning, if it burned off at all. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists, tucked the ledger into its shelf beside the flour bin, and began.

Taproom fire next. I knelt at the grate, raked the ash, and laid the kindling in the pattern my mother had taught me -- three sticks angled, two across, tinder beneath -- and struck the flint twice before it took. The taproom smelled of last night's ale and this morning's damp, which was to say it smelled like every morning of my life. I checked the bar: three casks needed turning, one was nearly empty, and someone -- Nan, probably -- had left a rag over the tap of the porter, which was the sort of small mercy that kept a morning from becoming a disaster.

I tallied the previous night's accounts standing up, because if I sat down I would lose ten minutes to the chair and the fire and the dangerous thought that I might like a cup of tea before the coach arrived. Tea was for after the coach. Everything was for after the coach. The accounts were short by one shilling fourpence, which I marked in the margin and would settle with Nan later -- an honest error, almost certainly, the kind that happens when a man orders a fourth pint and you are already pulling the fifth for his companion. I noted the candle stubs in the taproom sconces, two of which had guttered down to nothing and would need replacing. The chandler was due today. I would deal with him when he came.

The back door opened and did not close. This meant Jem, who left doors open behind him because closing a door with one arm while carrying a bridle required a contortion he refused to perform before seven o'clock. I heard his boots cross the yard, heard the stable door scrape -- the lower hinge still wanted fixing -- and then the low, steady sound of a man talking to horses as though horses were the only sensible company at this hour. He was right. They were.

Nan appeared through the kitchen some minutes later, pins half-set, apron already tied, and took her place behind the bar without a word. She ran her hand along the counter, found the rag she'd left, folded it once, and set it beneath the tap. Then she looked at me, looked at the empty sconces, and reached for the candle box. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

The London coach was late. This was not unusual -- the London coach was late in the way that sunrise was easterly, a fact of natural philosophy one simply accounted for -- but it was also running short. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and one of them descended with a trunk whose hinge had split clean through, spilling the edge of a chemise into the mud of the yard like a flag of surrender.

"This," the woman said, gripping the trunk's handle with both hands as though it had personally betrayed her, "was not broken when I boarded."

I directed Jem with a look. He appeared from the stable door, assessed the trunk, the woman, and the dangling chemise in a single glance, and took the whole affair under his one good arm without comment. The woman watched him go with the startled expression of a person whose grievance has been resolved before she has finished airing it.

The second passenger was already inside, settled at the dining-room table and making it clear through posture alone that he expected eggs. Nan would manage him.

The coachman found me in the yard. He was a broad, red-faced man named Jessup, and he had been driving this route since before my mother died.

"North road's gone to pieces above Hatfield," he said, pulling off his gloves. "Ruts a foot deep past the turnpike. Broke a spoke last Tuesday."

"Was it repaired?"

"Aye, but it shouldn't have broken. The surveyor's not touched that stretch since July." He squinted at me as though I might personally compel the turnpike trust to do its work. "If it freezes hard this week, the whole surface'll go."

"I'll write to the trust again. They've had two letters from me already and I expect this one will be just as effective." I handed him the ale Nan had drawn without being asked. "You've ten minutes if you want breakfast. Less if you want to argue about roads."

He took the ale. He did not argue about roads.

The yard cleared. The coachman went. The passengers settled into the rhythms of their own small urgencies -- the woman with the mended trunk had gone upstairs, the man with the eggs was applying himself to them with silent concentration. Jem had returned to the stable. Nan stood behind the bar, wiping down the counter with one eye on the door through all of it. I noticed. I said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past ten with his cart and his cheerful expression and his adjusted price.

"Tuppence ha'penny the dozen," he said, setting the first crate on the kitchen table as though he were presenting me with a gift.

I did not touch the crate. "It was tuppence last month. And tuppence the month before, which was itself a halfpenny above your price in March."

"Tallow's gone up, Miss Dale. I don't set the market."

"No, but you do buy from Haskell in Welwyn, who charges you a penny farthing the pound, which makes your cost per dozen just under a penny even with the wicking. Tuppence ha'penny is not tallow going up. It's your margin going up."

He had the grace to look uncomfortable, though not enough grace to concede. "I've other customers on this road, Miss Dale. Every one of them pays what I ask."

"Every one of them has fewer rooms and burns fewer candles. You sell me nine dozen a month. That is a hundred and eight candles, Mr Fenton. Name me another single customer between here and Baldock who takes that quantity." I waited. The kitchen was very quiet. "Tuppence," I said. "Same as last month. And I'll take ten dozen instead of nine, because the evenings are drawing in and I'd rather buy now than send Jem to Welwyn in November."

He looked at the crate. He looked at me. He picked up his hat, which he had set on the table beside the candles as though the negotiation might take long enough to require comfort.

"Tuppence," he said. "Ten dozen."

"Thank you, Mr Fenton. Nan will see you out."

He went. The candles stayed.

I allowed myself one breath of satisfaction -- the kind that had no outward expression and no audience, which was exactly how I preferred my satisfactions. Then I moved the crate to the storeroom and counted the candles to make sure there were a hundred and twenty.

There were. Mr Fenton was many things, but he was not a cheat.

The midday lull settled over the inn like a held breath. I stood at the kitchen table, the ledger open again, and ran the numbers with the chandler's price factored in. Better. Not good, but better. The six weeks looked less like a cliff edge and more like a steep hill, which was the kind of distinction that mattered only to someone standing at the bottom of it.

It was always like this -- the calculation, the weighing, the quiet arithmetic of how long I could keep the walls standing and the fires lit and the casks full without anyone's help. If I married, the arithmetic would belong to someone else. The ledger, the lease, the negotiation I had just won -- all of it transferred to a husband the moment I spoke the words. I had refused two proposals for precisely this reason, and I had not regretted either refusal, and I did not intend to start.

I closed the ledger and went to check the dining room.

The afternoon was ordinary in the way that running an inn required ordinary to be: relentless, unglamorous, and composed of thirty small tasks that no one would notice unless they were left undone. I reset the upstairs rooms. I checked the kitchen stores and planned the evening meal around what we had, which was mutton enough for a stew and potatoes enough to stretch it. Nan polished glasses. Jem mucked stalls. The inn breathed.

I was at the kitchen window when I saw the soldier.

He came around the back, as the signs asked. Young -- not much past twenty, though the war had a way of making that difficult to judge. Thin. His coat had been military once but was no longer anything in particular. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the left sole gapping open at the toe like a mouth.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. I watched him through the glass. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at a thing he wants but is not sure he is permitted to want -- assessing not whether he was hungry, which was obvious, but whether he was welcome enough to ask.

I opened the door before he knocked.

He straightened. "Ma'am. I don't mean to trouble you."

"Have you eaten today?"

A pause. The honesty of it cost him something. "Not recently, no."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the fire. He understood. He sat.

I gave him bread and broth -- the end of the morning's pot, still hot, with enough substance in it to do a hungry man some genuine good. He ate carefully, not wolfishly, which told me he had been hungry long enough to have learned that eating too fast would make him sick. He held the spoon the way a man holds a tool he is grateful for. When he finished, he set the bowl down and looked at me directly for the first time.

"Thank you, ma'am. That was -- thank you."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, as though this were insufficiently specific for the woman who had fed him: "My sister's in Huntingdon. If she's still there."

He went. I closed the door behind him and stood in the kitchen with my hand on the latch. His boots. The left one, the sole gapping like that, and the weather not yet truly cold. He was somebody's brother, walking north on feet that were half-bare to the road, and he had thanked me for broth as though I had offered him the world.

I washed his bowl and put it away.

The taproom trouble, when it came, was the ordinary kind -- two men who had been sitting too long with too little to occupy them, their voices climbing the register from disagreement to declaration. I crossed the room before it reached performance.

"Gentlemen. If you'd like to continue this conversation, you are welcome to do so in the yard. If you'd prefer to remain in the warm, you will do so quietly."

They remained in the warm. They did so quietly. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and returned to her glass without expression.

The evening came on early, as October evenings do -- not a dramatic darkening but a slow withdrawal of light, the kind you don't notice until you reach for a candle and realize you needed one twenty minutes ago. The last guests settled. The dining room emptied. The kitchen fire burned down to its working coals, and I began the closing.

Taproom fire: raked, damped, the guard set. Bar: tallied, the numbers matching tonight -- Nan had been careful. Cellar hatch: locked, the bolt firm. I moved through the inn the way I moved through it every night, checking each room, each lock, each window latch, in the order my mother had taught me and her mother had taught her. The building settled around me as I went, its timbers creaking in the cold, the particular quiet of a place that has been used hard and is now resting.

The stable yard was dark and still. Jem would have settled the horses. I could see, on the second floor, a lit shape at Bridget's window -- her candle, her outline behind the glass, watching. I did not look long.

I came back to the kitchen. My hands were reddened from the cold water and the wiping down, and there was a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught me. I hadn't noticed it at the time. Bled a bit now, though -- a thin line across the pad of the finger, the kind of cut that stings out of all proportion to its size, as though the day had wanted to leave one last mark.

I dried my hands on the cloth. I bolted the kitchen door. The inn was locked, the work was done, and I was alone in the kitchen with the fire and the silence, and for one breath I let myself feel it -- the aloneness. Not as philosophy or principle or the price of anything. Just the fact of it. The empty chair across the table. The quiet that nobody else would break.

Then I saw the letter.

It was on the kitchen dresser, half-tucked beneath a stack of clean linen where Nan must have put it when the afternoon post came and I was upstairs. I had not noticed it. I noticed it now.

The seal was Stainforth's. His handwriting on the direction, neat and measured, the penmanship of a man who did not waste ink on flourishes. I picked it up. I turned it over once. I broke the seal.

*Miss Dale,* it began. *I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable interest regarding the property and lease of the White Hound Inn. My nephew, Mr Harold Stainforth, proposes to assume the lease at the next quarter and to undertake improvements to the premises that will, I am confident, benefit the parish considerably. I am prepared to discuss terms with you at your earliest convenience, though I must advise you that the matter will require resolution before the quarter day of 25 December. I trust you will see the reasonableness of this arrangement.*

I set the letter on the table. I read it again. The words did not change. *Resolution before the quarter day.* Six weeks. The same six weeks I had been counting in the ledger this morning, only now they were not mine to manage -- they were his to impose.

He wanted the lease. He had a buyer. He was giving me until Christmas to agree or be turned out, dressed up in the language of reasonableness as though reasonableness were anything other than a man with power explaining to a woman without it why she ought to be grateful for the terms of her own dispossession.

I stood in the kitchen with Stainforth's letter in my hand and the fire at my back and the whole of the White Hound locked and dark and silent around me.

"You'll have to do better than your nephew, Mr Stainforth."
