The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the smudge before it could ruin the facing page.

Six weeks. That was what the numbers gave me when I added them honestly, without the small hopeful lies I sometimes allowed on Sundays. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between what I had and what I owed was the width of a candle flame -- present if you watched it, gone if you blinked.

I closed the ledger and set my palm flat on its cover, as though I could hold the figures in place by force of will. The kitchen was cold. The fire I'd built twenty minutes earlier was still finding its courage, more smoke than heat, and my breath made a faint ghost in the air between me and the lamp. I wanted -- just once -- to come down in the morning and find the fire already lit, the kettle already singing, the day already started by someone else's hands.

I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to work.

The taproom fire first. I riddled the grate, set the kindling, struck the flint. The wood was dry -- Jem saw to that -- and the flame took on the second attempt. I checked the bar stock by touch and by habit: the casks in order, the gin at the level I'd marked last night, the small beer untouched. Two pennies sat on the counter where someone had left them, either as payment or as a test of my honesty. I pocketed them. Both.

Last night's accounts I'd tallied before bed, but I ran through them once more standing at the bar, the figures moving through my head like beads on a wire. Fourteen shillings, eightpence. Not enough. Never quite enough, and never so little that I could justify despair. The White Hound existed in the narrow country between solvency and ruin, and I navigated it the way the coaches navigated the north road -- by knowing where every rut was, and never looking too far ahead.

The windows were rimed at their edges, October asserting itself against the glass. I rubbed a circle clear with my elbow and looked out at the yard. The sky was the colour of old pewter, and the cobbles were dark with last night's rain.

Jem was already in the stable yard.

I could see him through the kitchen window when I came back to check the fire -- not because I'd asked him to be there, but because the morning required it and Jem understood mornings the way he understood horses: as things that would go badly without intervention. He was straightening tack on the rail with his one hand and the practised brace of his stump, his canvas coat buttoned to the throat, his breath clouding in the cold air. He did not look toward the house. He did not need to.

Nan arrived a few minutes later through the kitchen door, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word, ran her cloth along the counter, and began setting out the morning's glasses. We had done this before. Hundreds of times. The inn woke the same way every day, and there was a comfort in that sameness I would not have traded for any luxury I could name.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual -- the London coach was late with the regularity of a church bell that had never been properly hung -- but it was also running short. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both emerged from the coach in states of grievance so advanced that I suspected they had been nursing them since Islington.

The first was a woman of perhaps fifty, travelling with a trunk whose hinge had split so dramatically that the lid gaped open like a mouth mid-protest. She had opinions about this. She shared them with me, with the coachman, with the yard at large, and with Jem, who appeared at her elbow without being summoned, lifted the trunk as though it weighed nothing, and carried it inside before she could finish her sentence. She stared after him, startled into silence, and I seized the opportunity.

"Nan will show you to your room. Breakfast in twenty minutes."

She went. The second passenger, a thin clergyman with a cold, followed without comment, which was the most useful thing a man of the cloth had ever done for me.

The coachman wanted a word. He was a big, red-faced man named Harker whom I'd known for three years, and he stood in the yard with his arms folded and his hat pushed back, looking as though the north road had personally offended him.

"There's a stretch above Hatfield that'll take an axle before the month's out," he said. "Ruts deep enough to bury a dog in. Someone ought to speak to the turnpike trust."

"Someone has. Twice. They've promised gravel before November."

"They promised gravel in August."

"They did. And if you'd like to take it up with Mr Fenton at the trust, I'll give you his direction and wish you the very best of luck." I handed him the ale I'd poured without being asked. "You've ten minutes before you need to be back on the box."

He drank, nodded, and said no more about it. Harker was a practical man. He knew the difference between a complaint and a solution, and he knew I could offer him the ale but not the gravel. He finished, set the glass on the rail, touched his hat, and climbed back up.

The coach pulled out. The yard cleared. Through the window I could see Nan wiping down the bar with one eye on the door, as she always did -- watching for whatever the day intended to deliver next. I noticed. I said nothing.

Mr Purvis, the chandler, arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his newest price, which he presented to me in the kitchen with the careful expression of a man who hopes you won't do the arithmetic.

"Tenpence ha'penny the dozen," he said, setting the first box of tallow candles on the table.

I looked at the candles. I looked at Mr Purvis. I did the arithmetic.

"That's a penny up from last month, Mr Purvis, which was a ha'penny up from the month before. You're paying fourpence a pound for tallow from Jessop's in Hatfield -- the same as you paid in March. Your costs haven't risen. Your price has."

He shifted his weight. "There's the cartage to consider --"

"Your cartage hasn't changed either. You pass this door on your way to the Woolpack and the Bell, both of whom pay you ninepence, which I know because Mrs Hadley at the Bell told me so last Thursday over a glass of sherry she did not pay for."

A silence.

"I'll pay ninepence," I said. "Same as the Bell, same as the Woolpack. And I'll take an extra two dozen this month, which saves you a delivery in November."

Mr Purvis opened his mouth, reconsidered, and closed it. He was not a fool. He knew that walking away from the White Hound meant losing the largest single order on his route, and he knew that I knew it, and he knew that I would let him walk if he pushed me again.

"Ninepence," he said.

"Ninepence. I'll have Jem bring the rest in."

He unloaded the candles and left without further negotiation. The door closed behind him, and I stood alone in the kitchen with three dozen tallow candles and the small, private satisfaction of a woman who has just won a fight so quietly that her opponent may not have noticed he lost.

I carried the candles to the storeroom and came back to the kitchen to start the midday bread. My hands worked the dough without instruction -- they had done this so many times that my mind was free to wander, which was not always a kindness.

The ledger. The rent. The chandler's price, and the coal merchant's price, and the brewer's price, all of them pressing upward against the same immovable fact: the inn was mine only so long as I remained unmarried. The lease was in my name. If I took a husband, it would pass to his. The law did not care that my grandmother had built this place, or that my mother had held it through a war, or that I had kept it running since the night she died. The law cared that I was a woman, and it had arranged things accordingly. I had refused two proposals for exactly this reason, and I would refuse twenty more if it came to it. The thought passed through me like weather -- familiar, unwelcome, already moving on -- and I punched the dough and shaped it for the oven.

He came around the back, as the signs asked.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked -- young, thin, standing outside the door with the particular stillness of a man who is calculating whether he is welcome enough to try. His coat was too large for him, as though he had borrowed it from someone broader, and his boots were coming apart at the welt. I could see the stitching giving way from six feet off. He was perhaps twenty, perhaps younger. He looked at the door. He looked at his hands. He did not knock.

I opened the door.

He startled, then steadied. Up close he was even thinner than I'd thought, and his eyes had the flat, careful blankness of someone who has learned not to expect much.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

He considered this as though it were a question with several possible answers, most of them dangerous. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not the taproom. Sit there."

He sat. I set bread and broth in front of him -- what the kitchen had, nothing special, nothing that would be missed from the evening's accounts. He ate with the controlled steadiness of a man who has been hungry long enough to know that eating too fast would make him sick. He held the spoon carefully, as though it were borrowed and he intended to return it in good condition.

When the bowl was empty he looked up. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said, and offered nothing more. Neither of us said anything about the war.

He thanked me again at the door, settled his too-large coat around his shoulders, and went. I watched him cross the yard and turn onto the road, his broken boots raising small puffs of dust that the October wind carried away. He was somebody's son. He held the spoon the way a boy holds a spoon when he has been taught to hold it properly, and whoever taught him was somewhere right now, not knowing whether he had eaten today.

I washed the bowl and put it back on the shelf.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say, in work. Nan at the bar. Jem outside. I reset the upstairs rooms, checked the kitchen stores against the evening's requirements, tallied what the morning had cost against what the evening might bring. The coach had gone. The guests were settled or departed. The inn breathed in the gap between rushes, and I moved through it the way I always did, filling the quiet with tasks because tasks were the one thing I never ran short of.

In the taproom, two men who had been sitting too long over too little ale found something to disagree about. I heard the voices rise from the kitchen -- not yet shouting, but heading there with purpose.

I walked in.

"Gentlemen. You'll settle this outside or you'll settle your accounts and leave. I don't mind which."

They settled it by going quiet, which was the third option and the one I preferred. Nan watched from behind the bar, cloth in hand, and did not need to move.

Evening came the way it always did -- not all at once, but in stages, like a door closing by degrees. The last guests went upstairs or out into the dark. The last fire burned down to coals. Nan tallied the bar and said good night, and I heard her boots on the stairs and then the creak of her door and then nothing.

I made my circuit alone. Taproom fire: coals banked, screen set. Cellar hatch: locked. Kitchen: closed down, the range cooling, the surfaces wiped clean. I checked the stable yard through the back door -- quiet, the horses settled, Jem's lamp already out in the room above the stalls.

My hands, when I looked at them in the lamplight, were reddened from cold water and rough work, and there was a small cut on my right index finger where the edge of Mr Purvis's delivery note had caught me. I hadn't felt it at the time. I felt it now, a thin sting that seemed disproportionate to its cause, as though my hands had waited until the day was over to register their complaints.

I crossed the yard toward the front of the inn. The second floor: a faint lit shape at Bridget's window, the familiar silhouette of her watching. I did not look long.

I bolted the front door. I set the bar. I stood in the passage with my hand still on the wood and let myself know, for the space of one breath, that the inn was quiet and I was the only person awake in it, and that this was how every day ended -- with me, and the dark, and the bolt driven home.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

It had not been there an hour ago. Someone had pushed it under the front door while I was doing the rounds, or it had been left with Nan and she had set it down and forgotten to mention it, or -- it did not matter how it had arrived. It was here. I knew the handwriting on the direction. I knew the seal.

Stainforth.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The wax was a deep, deliberate red, stamped with the Stainforth crest -- a device I had seen on enough documents to recognise without effort. The paper was heavy and good. A man like Stainforth did not waste good paper on casual correspondence.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth wrote the way he spoke -- measured, precise, and arranged so that every courtesy landed like a weight.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable interest for the property on which the White Hound presently stands, inclusive of the coaching yard and stabling. The prospective purchaser intends a material improvement of the premises. As your current lease provides for a review of terms at the lessor's discretion upon any transfer of interest, I think it proper to offer you the opportunity to discuss the matter before arrangements are concluded. I shall expect you at Stainforth House on Thursday next, the thirty-first of October, at eleven o'clock. I trust this is convenient.*

I read it twice. The second time was worse, because the second time I understood what he was not saying. A transfer of interest meant the lease could be rewritten. A review of terms meant the rent could be raised beyond anything I could pay. *I trust this is convenient* meant he did not care whether it was convenient or not.

He had a buyer. He had a plan. And he was giving me six days to come to his house and be told, with impeccable courtesy, that the White Hound was no longer mine.

I set the letter down on the kitchen table, on top of the closed ledger, where the numbers waited with their six weeks of borrowed time.

"Thursday, then, Mr Stainforth." I said it aloud, to the empty room, to the banked fire, to no one. "I'll be there. And I'll bring my own terms."
