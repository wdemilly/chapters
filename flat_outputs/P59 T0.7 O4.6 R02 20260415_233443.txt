The ink had frozen in the ledger's well overnight, and I had to hold the bottle between my palms for a full minute before it would give me anything useful.

Six weeks. That was what the numbers said when I finally got them down, the nib scratching across paper I could not afford to waste. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the column of figures that should have met it fell short by four pounds, eleven shillings. I closed the ledger and pressed my fingertips against the cover as though I might improve the sum by force of will.

I could not. I had tried.

The kitchen was cold. Not the killing cold of deep winter, which was still weeks away, but the particular grudging chill of late October, when the walls held no warmth from the day before and the fire had to earn every degree. I knelt at the hearth and built it up from the banked coals, laying kindling in the pattern my mother had taught me: smallest sticks first, then the split oak, then the single precious log that would hold until the first delivery. The flames caught. I stayed crouching a moment longer than necessary, my hands extended toward the heat, and allowed myself the briefest inventory of what I wanted.

I wanted the four pounds, eleven shillings.

I wanted one morning, just one, when I came downstairs and found the ledger already balanced and the fire already lit and someone else's hands doing the work.

I wanted something I could not name and therefore refused to examine further.

I stood, wiped my palms on my apron, and went to open the taproom.

The windows were rimed at their edges, a thin crust of ice that caught the grey morning light and held it without warmth. I checked the bar stock: two casks of ale in good order, the third nearly spent and wanting replacement by Thursday. Last night's accounts were tallied and pinned to the board behind the bar. Three shillings, eightpence. A slow night. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and moved through the room, straightening chairs, checking the grate, noting the hinge on the window shutter that had worked loose again. It would hold another week if no one forced it. After that it would need a screw I did not have.

Everything accounted for. Nothing wasted. This was how my mother had run the White Hound, and her mother before her, and I ran it the same way because it was the only way that worked: attend to what is in front of you, and do not look up until it is done.

I crossed the yard to check the back. Jem was already in the stable, his one hand working a bridle onto its hook with the practised economy of a man who had long since stopped thinking about what he was missing. He did not look up. He did not need to. The tack was straight, the stalls mucked, the water troughs filled. I turned back toward the kitchen without a word.

Nan came through the kitchen door as I reached it, pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar with the settled air of someone resuming a post she had never truly left. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual. The roads north of Hatfield had been rutted since the September rains, and every coachman on the Great North Road had an opinion about it. What was unusual was the coach arriving with only two passengers instead of four, both of them cross, one of them in possession of a trunk whose hinge had split open and spilled a quantity of undergarments onto the yard.

I dealt with the trunk first. Jem appeared at my shoulder before I called for him, which was his way. He took the trunk to the stable where he could brace it and work the hinge back into shape. The woman who owned it stood in the yard with her arms folded and her mouth set in a line that suggested she held me personally responsible for the state of English luggage.

"Your room is the second on the left at the top of the stairs," I said. "Nan will bring hot water. Your trunk will follow within the quarter hour."

She opened her mouth. I was already turning away.

The coachman caught me at the mounting block. He was a big man named Howell with a red face and hands like shovels, and he had been driving this route longer than I had been alive.

"North road's gone to pieces above the toll," he said. "Rut near Woolmer Green damn near took my axle."

"I'll send word to the turnpike trust again. They had the surveyor out last month and did nothing."

"Surveyor couldn't find his own arse with a map and a lantern."

"I'll note your assessment." I stepped back from the block. "Safe journey, Mr Howell."

He grunted, gathered his reins, and drove on.

The yard cleared. Jem took the trunk upstairs. The woman with the opinions went to the dining room. The second passenger, a silent man in a brown coat, went to his room without requiring anything at all, which made him my favourite guest of the morning. Nan had been wiping down the bar through all of this with one eye on the door. I noted it. Said nothing.

Mr Fenton, the chandler, arrived at half past eleven with his cart and his monthly delivery and his new price.

"Tenpence the dozen," he said, setting the first box of tallow candles on the kitchen table with the careful placement of a man presenting a gift.

"The price was eightpence in April."

"Tallow's dear this year. The renderers have put their rates up."

"The renderers have put their rates up by a farthing a pound since midsummer. I buy from Jessop in Welwyn when your prices don't suit, and Jessop buys from the same renderer you do, so I know what he pays."

Mr Fenton's expression did not change, but his hand stayed on the box.

"I also know," I continued, "that the Woolpack and the George both cancelled half their standing orders last month because the coaches are running lighter. You have candles to sell, Mr Fenton, and fewer people buying."

"Ninepence ha'penny."

"Eightpence ha'penny, which is what I paid in July and a fair price for us both."

A pause. He removed his hand from the box.

"You'll take the full three dozen?"

"I will."

He unloaded the rest without further discussion. I counted them, signed his delivery note, and watched him go. When the door closed behind him, I allowed myself a moment of private satisfaction. It was a small victory. It was also the only kind available to me, and I had learned to value them.

I stood at the kitchen table and looked at the candles, neatly stacked, and thought about the ledger in the next room. The eightpence ha'penny mattered. Every eightpence ha'penny mattered, because the four pounds, eleven shillings would not conjure itself from air, and the quarter's rent would come due whether I was ready or not. And beneath the rent, beneath the candle price and the slow nights and the rutted roads, lay the fact I carried always: if I married, none of this would be mine to fight for. The lease, the income, the inn my grandmother had built from nothing would pass to a husband the moment I spoke the words.

I had refused two proposals already for this reason. I did not regret either refusal.

I picked up the candles and carried them to the storeroom.

He came around the back, as the signs asked.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked. Young. Twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. His coat had been a soldier's coat once, though the regiment's facings had been unpicked and the wool was felted with rain and wear. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the sole of the left one separating from the upper in a way that meant he had been walking a long time on wet roads. He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand raised and did not knock immediately. He was looking at the door, then at the yard, then at the door again, and I understood that he was calculating whether he was welcome enough to ask.

I opened the door.

He startled. Recovered. Stood straighter, which cost him something.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He met my eyes. His were pale and steady and older than his face. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not the taproom. Sit there."

He understood. He sat at the bench by the hearth without argument, his ruined boots placed carefully together as though they might yet be made presentable. I ladled broth from the stockpot into a bowl, cut bread from the morning's loaf, and set both in front of him. He ate the way starving people eat when they are trying not to show it: measured spoonfuls, the bread torn small, every crumb attended to.

I went back to the stockpot and stirred it so he could eat without being watched.

When he had finished, he stood and brought the bowl to the basin, which I had not asked him to do.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. "There's work in the mills, I'm told."

I said nothing to that, because there was nothing honest to say. He touched his forehead and went out through the kitchen door, and I listened to his boots on the cobbles until the sound was gone.

I looked at the bowl he had carried to the basin. He had wiped it nearly clean with the last of the bread. I thought of his hands around the spoon, the knuckles chapped raw, the nails bitten to nothing. He was someone's son. The thought arrived and I let it stay for a moment, sharp and specific, and then I went back to the stockpot.

The afternoon brought a dispute in the taproom. Two men, both guests, both restless from sitting too long over ale, their voices climbing toward the register that preceded broken furniture.

I crossed the room. "Gentlemen. The next round is on the house if you can drink it quietly. Otherwise I'll ask you to settle your accounts and find another establishment."

They drank it quietly.

Nan watched from behind the bar and did not need to intervene.

The hours between the morning coach and the evening trade were the hours that kept the inn alive, though no guest ever saw them. Rooms turned, linens checked, the kitchen set for supper, accounts reviewed, firewood counted, the cellar inspected for damp. I moved through each task with the efficiency of long practice, Nan beside me or ahead of me, Jem visible through the window whenever I passed the yard side of the house. The ordinary work. The work that held everything together and that no one would notice unless it stopped.

Evening came. The last guests settled. The last fire checked and banked. The bar tallied, the kitchen scrubbed down, the cellar hatch locked. I moved through the inn's closing routine alone, room by room, the way I had done every night for two years and the way my mother had done for fourteen years before that. The building was quiet in the particular way of a space that has held noise all day and is resting from the effort.

I checked the taproom grate. Down to coals. The cellar hatch: bolted. The stable yard: quiet, Jem's lantern already dark, the horses settled. I looked up at the second floor of the inn. A candle burned behind Bridget's window, and I could see her shape there, still and watchful. I did not look long.

My hands, when I finally stopped to notice them, were reddened from cold water and the day's work. There was a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught me. I had not felt it at the time. I felt it now.

I bolted the front door. I set the bar. I stood in the passage and let myself know, just for a moment, that the inn was quiet and I was alone in it.

Then I went back to the kitchen.

The letter was on the table where Nan must have left it with the afternoon's post. I had not seen it earlier, or I had seen it and set it aside, which amounted to the same evasion. Stainforth's seal, pressed deep into dark wax. His handwriting on the direction, small and exact.

I picked it up. Turned it over once.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth wrote the way he spoke: measured, courteous, and leaving no room for misunderstanding.

He had received an offer for the White Hound's lease from a Mr Haddon of London, who wished to acquire the property and convert it to a posting house under new management. Stainforth considered the offer a sound one and advantageous to the parish. He was prepared to discuss terms with me, as a courtesy and in respect of my family's long association with the property. He proposed a meeting at his offices on the fourteenth of November. If I did not attend, he would proceed on the assumption that I had no objection to the transfer.

The fourteenth of November. Three weeks.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed my palms flat against the wood on either side of it and looked at the words until they stopped swimming.

Three weeks to answer for sixty years of my family's work. A courtesy, he called it. A meeting, as though we would sit across a desk and discuss the weather and he would not already have decided.

I straightened. I picked up the letter and folded it once, precisely, and put it in my apron pocket.

"Mr Haddon of London," I said to the empty kitchen, "is going to need a better inn."
