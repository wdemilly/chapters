Chapter One

The numbers had not changed overnight, which was a pity, because I had rather hoped they might.

Six weeks until the next quarter was due on the lease. I closed the ledger, set the pencil along its spine, and pressed my palms flat on the cover as if I might keep the figure inside by sheer weight of hand. The kitchen was cold. The windows were rimed white at the corners where the glass met the lead, and the fire I had built up a quarter-hour ago was still more smoke than heat. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and considered, not for the first time, that a woman who wanted to eat had no business being a sentimentalist about autumn.

I lit the taproom fire next. I checked the bar. Two casks of the Baldock ale, one of the cheaper stuff, a half-dozen bottles of claret that no one this side of Hatfield was going to pay for, and a jug of small beer for the servants and for anyone who looked hungrier than they looked thirsty. I tallied the night's takings against the book and found them short by threepence, which was Bill Harrow cheating himself again; he could not count, and he always cheated in my favour, which meant I owed him threepence and a stern word about arithmetic. I wrote it on the slate.

The kitchen girl was not yet down. Nan was not yet down. Jem would already be in the yard, because Jem was always already in the yard, and the horses liked him better than they liked anyone, which was a sound recommendation in a man.

I went to the back door and looked through the little square of glass. There he was, straightening a bridle one-handed on the peg by the stall. He had taught the stump to hold the strap while the whole hand buckled. He did not look up. He never looked up until the work told him to. I watched him for a moment because he was steady and the morning was not, and a woman is allowed her small comforts before the first coach.

Nan came in through the kitchen door with pins in her teeth and her apron already tied, which was a trick I had never mastered. She went past me to the bar without a word. I heard the cloth start its circuit of the counter. Seven years she had been doing that, and the counter still shone for her and sulked for me.

I thought, as I had been thinking on and off for eighteen months, about the letter from Mr Haberdine, the solicitor in Stevenage, which explained in three careful paragraphs what marriage would do to the lease. A husband would have it. A husband would have the licence, the takings, the very cups. I had been offered a husband twice. I had declined a husband twice. There had been nothing wrong with either gentleman and a great deal wrong with the law, and a woman who could do her own sums did not need a man to ruin them for her.

I put the thought away in the place I kept it, between the stock cupboard and the back stairs, and I went to meet the coach.

The London mail was late, which was not unusual, and short, which was. Two passengers instead of four. The coachman, Peter Deeks, came down from the box with the careful stiffness of a man who has been cold for six hours, and he did not bother with a greeting.

"Miss Dale," he said. "Road above Hatfield's broke through. Near Welwyn there's a ruck in it would swallow a wheel whole. You'll want to tell the next post."

"I'll tell them. How long to Baldock?"

"Two hours if I'm lucky. I'm not lucky."

"Tea in the taproom or hot water in the yard?"

"Water."

"Jem," I called, and he was already coming. "Mr Deeks wants hot water and a look at the near-side trace. Split hinge on a trunk, too, if you'll see to it."

Jem nodded once and took the coachman off by the elbow as if he were a piece of luggage himself. That left me the passengers.

The woman with the split trunk had a face like a ship's figurehead and the voice to go with it. She was standing over the broken thing in the yard saying "Abominable" to no one in particular.

"Ma'am," I said. "The hinge is a half-hour's work and my man will do it without charge. If you'll come into the dining room, there's a fire and coffee, and we'll have your trunk sound before the coach goes on."

She opened her mouth to be abominable at me and I smiled, which I have been told by reliable witnesses is disconcerting because I do not do it often. She shut her mouth. She came into the dining room. She drank her coffee. This is the whole art of innkeeping: not letting people finish the sentence they have started.

The second passenger was a thin clergyman who wanted only to be left alone, so I left him alone, which is a service people are not charged enough for.

By ten the yard was clear and the coach was gone. Nan wiped the bar a second time because she had been watching the door through the first wipe. I noted it. I said nothing. Some mornings the inn runs on what we do not say to one another.

Mr Oakes the chandler came at eleven with his cart and a face composed for bad news.

"Miss Dale."

"Mr Oakes."

"I'm sorry to say the tallow's gone up again."

"Is it indeed."

"Fourpence the dozen."

I considered him over the kitchen table. Mr Oakes was a long, thin man with a long, thin nose and the doleful patience of a fellow who has spent his life selling things that burn down. His coat was good. His boots were not new. I knew what he paid for tallow because his brother-in-law did the Baldock trade and his brother-in-law's wife was Nan's second cousin, and nothing happens in Hertfordshire that does not, within a fortnight, reach the counter of the White Hound.

"Fourpence," I said. "That's the fourth rise since Easter."

"The market, Miss Dale."

"The market's done nothing since August. What's done something is that you've lost the Black Bull account and you're making it up on me."

He blinked. It was a small blink, but a blink.

"Miss Dale."

"Mr Oakes. You've been my mother's chandler and mine for twenty years, and I should like you to be so for twenty more. I'll pay threepence the dozen, which is a halfpenny over what you're charging the George at Stevenage, and you'll thank me for it in January when you've run short and I've not gone to Mr Culley in Hitchin to see what he'll do."

He stood very still, considering Mr Culley in Hitchin.

"Threepence ha'penny," he said.

"Threepence."

"Threepence," he agreed.

"Bring them round."

He brought them round. He counted them out onto the kitchen table and he touched his hat at the door and I nodded him away, and when the yard door had shut I allowed myself to lean against the dresser for the length of one breath. I had done that rather well. My mother would have done it in fewer words, but my mother had thirty years in hand; I had only the seven since she let me keep the books.

I thought then, because I could not help it, of the coverture problem in its plain clothes. Everything I had just done, Mr Oakes would not have done with a husband of mine. He would have gone round the husband. He would have raised the price by fivepence and smiled at the man while he did it, and the man would have paid, because a man who has not bought tallow does not know what tallow ought to cost. I had refused Mr Hollis the farmer in 1813 and Mr Gent the corn-factor in 1815, and they had been decent enough men, and I had liked one of them a little, which was the part that was unpleasant to remember. The lease was the lease. It was mine as long as I was Miss Dale. I was not going to be sorry for it.

I went to the scullery and began on the pots.

I was at the scullery window when I saw the soldier.

He had come round the back, as the board by the front door asked vagrants to do. He was young. His coat had been blue once. His boots were coming apart at the welt, both of them, and he had bound one with a length of cord that had worked itself loose and was trailing at his heel. He stood four paces from the kitchen door and did not knock. He was looking at the step as if he were trying to decide whether he had the right to put a foot on it.

I dried my hands. I opened the door.

"Have you eaten?"

He lifted his head. He had pale eyes and the hollowed look of a boy who had been walking for a long time on not much.

"Not today, miss."

"Yesterday?"

"Bit of bread."

"Kitchen, not taproom. You sit where I tell you and you go when I say. Is that understood?"

"Yes, miss."

I sat him at the end of the table in the corner by the range, where he would be out of the girl's way when she came in with the coals. I cut him bread. I ladled broth from the pot that had been on since breakfast, thick with barley and the end of yesterday's mutton. I put a cup of small beer at his hand. He looked at it as if he had forgotten what cups were for, and then he picked up the spoon.

He held it with both hands. My father had held a cup that way the winter he was ill, the last winter, when his hands would not close properly around a single handle. I had not thought of that in a long time.

"Where are you bound?" I said.

"North, miss. My sister's in Grantham. I think."

"You think."

"It's been a while since I heard."

"Eat slow. You'll be sick if you don't."

He ate slow. He finished the bread. He finished the broth. He drank half the beer and pushed the rest back across the table like a man who had been taught not to take more than his share.

"Thank you, miss."

"There's a piece of cheese on the sill by the door. Take it for the road."

"Miss, I can't--"

"Take it."

He took it. He stood. He was not much older than Nan, I saw now that he was on his feet. He touched where his hat should have been, found no hat, and made the gesture anyway, which was somehow worse than if he had had one.

When he had gone I stood at the door a moment with my hand on the jamb. He had been someone's boy. Someone had sent him out of a door once with his collar straight. I went back to the stockpot and stirred it, because the stockpot required stirring, and because the alternative was standing at the door like a woman who had nothing else to do.

The afternoon went as afternoons did. A dispute broke out in the taproom between a grazier and a draper's traveller over the respective merits of Leicestershire sheep and Norfolk ones, which neither of them had any authority to discuss, and I went in and said, "Gentlemen, you will keep your voices inside your collars or you will drink elsewhere," and they kept their voices inside their collars. Nan did not have to come out from behind the bar. She gave me a look as I passed that I pretended not to see.

Between the coach and the evening meal the inn was quiet. It was always quiet between coaches. I did the rooms with Nan, turning the mattresses in three and twelve, putting fresh water in the jugs, and I sat for a quarter-hour at the kitchen table with the accounts for the chandler and the butcher and the man in Hitchin who supplied the coffee, and I found fourpence I had not accounted for, which was a small victory and a small vexation both.

By eight the dining room was down to two commercial gentlemen arguing over a newspaper. By ten they had gone to their rooms. By eleven the taproom had emptied itself and Bill Harrow had taken himself home to his wife. I did the last circuit alone.

Taproom fire down to coals, banked. Cellar hatch bolted. Kitchen door locked, back door locked, front door locked, key on the ring at my waist. Stable yard: quiet, the lamp out, the stalls settled. Jem had been through. The horses breathed in the dark like sleepers. In the window above the scullery a candle burned behind a green curtain, and the shape in the chair was Bridget, watching the yard as she watched the yard.

My hands at the end of the day were not hands I would have shown a guest. They were red at the knuckles from the scullery water and chapped across the backs, and there was a thin clean cut on my right forefinger where I had caught it on the edge of the chandler's delivery note. I had not noticed it when I caught it. I noticed it now. Three pence a dozen and a cut finger: I had, it seemed, been paid in full.

I bolted the front door and slid the top bolt home, and in the quiet between the bolt and the next thing, I let myself notice that the inn was large and I was the only person awake in it. I noticed it, and I set it aside, because noticing is not the same as indulging.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

I had seen it at six and left it. The seal was Mr Stainforth's, pressed deep and clean, and the direction was in his own hand, which was narrow and slightly backward-slanting, as though the letters were leaving the page reluctantly. He did not write personally when a clerk would do. He had written personally.

I sat down. I turned the letter over once. I broke the seal.

*Miss Dale,* it began, *I trust this finds you in health.* He trusted nothing of the kind; he was merely the sort of man who began a letter as a lawyer ends one. *It has come to my notice that the term of the present lease upon the White Hound, being in the fifteenth year of a twenty-one year grant, admits of reconsideration at the landlord's discretion in the event that the property is not being maintained in keeping with the interests of the parish.*

I read on. The interests of the parish. The frequency of vagrant traffic. The decline of the establishment's reputation among travellers of the better sort. A nephew, a Mr Edward Stainforth, lately of London, possessed of capital and ambition and several ideas for the improvement of the premises. An offer to purchase the remainder of the lease from me at a valuation to be agreed. A meeting proposed for the afternoon of the fourteenth of November, at Mr Stainforth's house in Knebworth, to discuss the terms, which he was pleased to describe as generous given the circumstances.

*Should no accommodation be reached by that date,* the last paragraph read, *I shall be obliged to lay the question of the lease's continuance before the quarter sessions at their winter sitting, where I have no doubt the matter will be settled according to the general sense of what is due to the district.*

I read it twice. I read the last paragraph three times. Then I folded the letter along its own creases and laid it beside the ledger, which did not seem surprised to see it.

A fortnight. He had given me a fortnight, on a road he thought I could not travel, to a valuation he meant to set himself, with a nephew standing by to take what my grandmother had built with her own two hands and her husband's death and twenty years of not going to bed before midnight.

I looked at my cut finger. I looked at the letter. I looked at the kitchen that my mother had died in, between the range and the dresser, ladle in her hand.

"Mr Stainforth," I said to the empty room, "you have picked the wrong fortnight, and you have picked the wrong woman, and by the fourteenth of November you are going to know both."

I put the letter in the drawer where I kept the rent receipts, and I turned the key.
