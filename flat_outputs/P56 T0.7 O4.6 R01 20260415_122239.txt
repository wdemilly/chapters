The ink had bled through to the second column again.

I pressed my thumb against the ledger page where the figures for October's tallow had smudged into November's coal, and I thought, not for the first time, that if I could afford better paper I would not need to spend so much on candles to read the worse paper by. Six weeks. That was what the numbers said when I added them honestly, which was the only way I knew how to add them. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between what the White Hound earned and what it owed was the width of my thumbnail and shrinking.

I closed the ledger before the figures could rearrange themselves into something more hopeful. They never did.

The kitchen was cold. The fire had been banked overnight and I'd built it up again twenty minutes ago, but the warmth hadn't reached the corners yet, and the flagstones sent their chill straight through the soles of my boots. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to check the bread. Two loaves from yesterday, still sound. The crock of dripping was low. I made a note of it in my head, where the real accounts lived -- the ones that mattered, the ones no smudged ink could soften.

I wanted -- and this was a foolish thought for five o'clock in the morning, the kind of thought I permitted myself only when no one was awake to see it in my face -- I wanted one day when the numbers were not the first thing and the last thing. One day when I might come down to this kitchen and think about something other than what was owed and what was owing. I did not know what I would think about instead. That was part of the problem.

The taproom fire needed building. I carried the ash bucket through and knelt on the cold hearth, raking out last night's remains and laying the new fire with the efficiency of a woman who has lit approximately four thousand taproom fires and does not intend to waste a lucifer on the four thousand and first if the kindling is properly set. It caught on the first match. I watched it take for ten seconds -- the only idle ten seconds I would have before noon -- and then I was up again, checking the bar stock. Three casks of ale, one nearly spent. The gin wanted measuring. Two bottles of claret remaining from a case that had lasted longer than it should have, which told me the autumn custom was thinner than last year's.

I wiped down the bar with a rag that needed replacing, tallied last night's accounts against the coin in the box, and found them correct to the ha'penny. They always were. My mother had taught me to count before she taught me to read, and she had done both before I was five, and the lesson underneath both was the same: if you do not know exactly what you have, you cannot know what you can afford to lose.

I could not afford to lose anything.

The yard was grey with early light when I stepped out the back door. Jem was already in the stable, moving between the stalls with the particular economy of a man who has learned to do everything with one hand and the stump of an arm and will not thank you for remarking on it. He was straightening a harness on its peg, his back to me, and he did not look up. He had heard the kitchen door. He knew it was me. There was nothing else to say about it.

I checked the water trough -- full -- and the feed store -- adequate -- and the stable yard gate -- latched -- and went back inside.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later. Her pins were half-set, her cap already on, her apron tied before she'd finished crossing the room. She took her place behind the bar without a word, and I heard her pull the rag from where I'd left it and start again on the section I'd already wiped, because Nan's standards for a clean bar were fractionally higher than mine and she would not say so but she would fix it every single morning. We had done this before. Many, many times. The inn breathed in around us like a body waking, and I let it.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual. The London coach was late in the way that rain was wet -- a fact so persistent it had ceased to be remarkable and had become instead the texture of the day itself. But it was also short: two passengers instead of four, both women, and the first had a trunk with a split hinge and a great deal to say about it.

"The clasp gave way at Barnet," she announced, as though Barnet were a personal failing of mine. "The whole thing nearly came open on the roof. My good linen is inside."

"Jem," I said, without raising my voice, because Jem was already crossing the yard. "The trunk. See to the hinge."

He took it from the coachman's hands with his one good grip and carried it toward the stable, where he kept tools for exactly this sort of thing, and the woman watched her trunk depart with the startled expression of someone whose complaint has been answered before she finished making it. I directed her to the dining room, settled the second passenger -- quieter, tireder, wanting only tea -- and turned to the coachman, who was stamping his boots on the cobbles and looking aggrieved.

"The road above Hatfield," he said. "It's gone to pieces past the Wrestler's. Ruts deep enough to break an axle."

"It was the same last week. The parish surveyor knows."

"The parish surveyor can go to the devil. I near lost a wheel."

"Take the lower fork past the mill," I said. "It adds ten minutes and it's sound. I'll have a word sent to the turnpike trust, but I cannot mend the road for you this morning, Mr. Hale, and you've got Baldock to make by two."

He knew I was right. He also knew I'd given him the only useful information he was going to get between here and his next stop, and that standing in my yard complaining about it would not improve the surface of the Great North Road. He touched his hat, climbed back up, and was gone.

The yard cleared. The passengers went upstairs and to the dining room respectively. Jem took the trunk. Nan was behind the bar with one eye on the door, the way she always was when coaches came and went, watching for trouble with the calm attention of a woman who had learned to spot it early. I noted it. I said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past ten.

Mr. Fossett was a lean man with a careful face and a habit of naming his prices as though they grieved him personally. He set the crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table and told me what he wanted for them, and the number was higher than last month's, which had been higher than the month before that, which had been higher than the month before that.

"Fourteen pence the dozen," he said, with the expression of a man attending a funeral.

"It was twelve in April."

"Tallow's dear, Miss Dale. The renderers have put their price up."

"The renderers are charging you a farthing more per pound than they charged you in the spring. I buy from the same market, Mr. Fossett. I know what tallow costs."

He shifted his weight. "There's transport to consider."

"Your cart makes this road every fortnight regardless. You deliver to the Wrestler's and the George before you reach me, and you'll deliver to them after. I am not adding to your transport. I am on your route."

"Thirteen, then."

"Twelve. The same as April. If you'd like to take your candles to someone who doesn't know your margins, you are welcome to try, but the Wrestler's wife does her own dipping and Mrs. Cartwright at the George will tell you the same thing I'm telling you, because she and I had this conversation last Tuesday."

A silence. Mr. Fossett looked at his candles. He looked at me. He picked up the crate, set it down again in exactly the same spot, and said, "Twelve."

"Thank you, Mr. Fossett. I'll take two dozen."

He counted them out, took his money, and left without the pleasantries that might have softened the transaction into something resembling warmth. I did not require warmth from Mr. Fossett. I required candles at a price I could meet, and I had them.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and permitted myself one moment -- just the one -- of private, sharp-edged satisfaction. I was good at this. I was good at knowing what things cost and what people would accept and where the line sat between firmness and foolishness. My mother had been good at it before me, and her mother before her, and the fact that this particular skill would keep the White Hound lit for another month was not nothing. It was not everything, either. But it was mine.

The thought came the way it always did -- not as philosophy, but as arithmetic. The ledger, the rent, the chandler's price, the coal, the ale, the quarterly sum I owed to a landlord who would never owe me anything in return: all of it balanced on the fact that the lease was in my name and would remain in my name only so long as I remained unmarried. If I took a husband, the law was blunt. What was mine became his. The inn, the income, the right to stand in my own kitchen and argue a chandler down to twelve pence -- all of it would pass to a man by virtue of a ceremony, regardless of whether that man knew the price of tallow or the name of a single supplier on the Great North Road.

I had refused two proposals for exactly this reason, and I did not regret either refusal. I regretted only that the question kept needing to be answered.

I wiped down the table where the candle crate had stood and went to see about the midday soup.

He came around the back, the way the signs asked.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked -- young, thin, standing on the step with the particular stillness of a man who is deciding whether he is welcome enough to ask. His coat was too large for him. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather separating from the sole in a way that meant he had been walking a long time and would not be walking much further in those boots. He was perhaps twenty, perhaps younger. It was difficult to tell. The war had a way of adding years to faces that had not earned them.

He had not knocked yet. He was looking at the door the way a dog looks at a hand that might feed it or might strike it, and he was not sure which.

I opened the door.

He straightened. "Ma'am. I'm sorry to trouble you. I saw the sign."

"Have you eaten?"

A pause. The honesty of it cost him something. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth. He came in and sat where I told him, and I set out what the kitchen had: bread, a bowl of yesterday's broth heated through, a cup of small beer. He ate the way hungry men eat, which is to say quickly and without looking up, as though the food might be taken back if he acknowledged it too openly.

When he had finished, he stood. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

He named a direction -- north, toward the midlands -- in the vague way of a man who does not have a destination so much as a bearing. Neither of us said anything about the war. There was nothing to say about it that the state of his boots did not already say.

He left by the back door, and I cleared the bowl and the cup and wiped the bench where he had sat. His spoon had been held very carefully, I noticed. The way a person holds a thing when he has not had many things to hold lately and is conscious of the borrowing.

He was somebody's son. That was all. I did not need to make it more than that.

I went back to the soup.

The afternoon brought the usual small frictions. Two men in the taproom -- commercial travellers, by their bags -- found something to disagree about. I did not discover what it was, because I did not let it get that far.

"Gentlemen," I said from the doorway. "The next round is on the house if the volume comes down, and the door is behind you if it doesn't."

The volume came down. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and did not need to say anything.

The hours between the afternoon coach and the evening meal were the inn's held breath. Coach gone, guests settled or departed. Nan polishing glasses. Jem outside, doing whatever Jem did in the long afternoons -- mending tack, I suspected, or sitting with the horses in the companionable silence he preferred to any human conversation. I worked through the accounts for the week, reset the two rooms that had been vacated, checked the kitchen stores against what I would need for supper, and calculated, as I always calculated, whether the day had cost more than it earned.

It had not. By a narrow margin, it had not. This was as close to triumph as most days came.

Evening settled over the inn the way it always did -- not all at once, but in stages, like a door closing slowly. The last guests went up. The last fire was checked, the coals raked and the guard set. The bar was tallied, the kitchen closed down, the back door bolted. I moved through the White Hound's closing routine with the ease of long repetition, touching each lock and latch the way a woman touches the beads of a rosary -- not thinking about the gesture, but trusting it.

The taproom fire was down to coals, breathing its low orange heat into the empty room. Cellar hatch locked. Stable yard quiet -- Jem would have settled the horses and taken himself to his quarters above the tack room, where he slept with the door open regardless of the weather because closed doors, I suspected, reminded him of things he did not wish to be reminded of. I did not ask. He did not tell.

I crossed the yard on my last circuit. The second floor: a lit shape behind Bridget's window, the outline of her in her chair, watching. I did not look long. She was there. She was always there.

My hands, when I stopped to notice them, were reddened from the cold water and the cleaning, the knuckles rough, a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Mr. Fossett's delivery note had caught the skin. I had not felt it at the time. I felt it now, a thin sting that was the day's only injury, and I thought, with the kind of dry clarity that tiredness brings: that is what a chandler's invoice costs. Blood and all.

I set the last bolt. The inn was locked. The inn was mine, for now, for tonight, and I was alone in it in the way I was alone in it every night -- not unhappily, not with any great sorrow, but with a quietness that sometimes, if I stood still long enough, had a weight to it I could not quite set down.

I went back to the kitchen to put out the last candle, and the letter was on the table.

It had not been there an hour ago. Nan must have brought it in from the afternoon post and left it where I would find it, and I had been too occupied with the closing to notice. The seal was dark red, pressed deep into the wax with the particular firmness of a man who wished his correspondence to look official. The handwriting on the direction was precise, slanted, and familiar.

Stainforth.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The wax was cool and hard under my thumb.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Mr. Stainforth wrote the way he spoke -- measured, civil, and entirely without doubt that the world would arrange itself according to his preferences. He had received an offer of purchase for the White Hound property from a gentleman of means -- his nephew, though the letter did not say so -- who intended to develop the premises into an establishment better suited to the improving traffic of the North Road. Mr. Stainforth wished to advise me, as a courtesy and in recognition of my family's long association with the property, that he intended to review the terms of the lease at the next quarter, with a view to its non-renewal. He proposed a meeting at his earliest convenience, which, the letter made clear, was also my earliest convenience whether I liked it or not. I had until the end of December to present any case I wished to make for the lease's continuation.

Six weeks. The same six weeks the ledger had given me this morning, only now they meant something different. Not just the margin between solvency and debt, but the margin between having a home and having nothing.

I set the letter down on the table. The candle guttered in the draught from the chimney, throwing the shadow of my hand across Stainforth's careful script.

I said it aloud, to the empty kitchen, to no one, because there was no one to hear it and because saying it aloud made it an act and not merely a feeling:

"You'll have your meeting, Mr. Stainforth. And you had better bring more than a nephew."
