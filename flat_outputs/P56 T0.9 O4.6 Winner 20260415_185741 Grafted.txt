The ink had dried on the last column before I noticed my fingers were numb.

I flexed them over the ledger--eight pounds, fourteen shillings, and a handful of coppers that would not add up to anything resembling comfort no matter how many times I tallied them. The ledger did not balance, and I had known it would not balance before I opened it. Six weeks to quarter day. Six weeks for the roof to hold, the beer to pour, the coaches to keep stopping, and for none of the dozen small disasters that could swallow a margin like ours to bother showing up. I closed the book and pressed my palms flat against the kitchen table, feeling the grain of it, the familiar grooves where my mother's knife had scored the wood ten thousand times.

The kitchen fire was still finding its legs. I'd built it up twenty minutes ago, but the October cold had got into the stones overnight, and the chimney drew reluctantly, as though the whole building needed convincing. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to check the taproom.

The taproom hearth was dead ash. I knelt, cleared it, laid the fire fresh--kindling first, then the split logs Jem had stacked by the door yesterday. The match caught on the second strike. I watched the flame lick upward until I was satisfied it would hold, then stood and wiped my hands on my apron.

Bar stock next. I counted bottles by touch in the half-dark: the gin was low, the brandy adequate, the ale casks in the cellar sound enough when I'd checked them last night. I pulled the cloth off the bar top and ran my hand along the surface. Sticky near the far end, where someone had set down a glass without a word to Nan. I fetched the rag and the vinegar and cleaned it myself, because it was five o'clock in the morning and there was no one else to do it, and because a sticky bar at opening was the kind of thing my mother would have risen from the dead to correct.

Last night's accounts were still on the shelf behind the bar. I pulled them down and checked Nan's figures against the till. She'd been off by tuppence--in our favour, which meant she'd shortchanged someone or miscounted, and either way I'd need to mention it. Later. When the day had enough hours in it for small corrections.

I wanted--just for a moment, standing behind the bar in the grey almost-light with the fire beginning to take--I wanted someone to walk in and tell me the roof would last the winter. Not fix it. Not pay for it. Just tell me, with the kind of certainty I could not manufacture for myself, that the next six weeks would not undo everything.

Nobody walked in. I set the accounts back on the shelf and went to check the yard.

Jem was already there.

He had his back to me, working a length of harness through his one hand and the hook he'd rigged to the stable post, checking the stitching by feel. The leather moved through his grip with a rhythm that said he'd been at it awhile. He didn't turn. I didn't call out. The morning required it, and he was doing it, and there was nothing more to say about that.

I went back inside. Nan arrived a few minutes later through the kitchen door, pins half-set, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word and began laying out the glasses for the morning. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual--the road north of Hatfield had been worsening since August, and every coachman who stopped at the Hound had an opinion about whose fault it was--but late coaches meant cold passengers, and cold passengers meant complaints, and complaints meant a taproom full of people who wanted everything at once and would judge the whole establishment by the temperature of the first cup of tea.

When the coach finally clattered into the yard, it was running short: two passengers instead of four, and both of them in the kind of temper that suggested the missing two had been the agreeable ones. The first was a stout woman in a brown pelisse who descended the step with her mouth already open. The second was a thin man in a greatcoat who said nothing and went straight for the dining room.

The woman had a trunk. The trunk had a split hinge.

"This has been banging about since Barnet," she announced to the yard at large, "and not a soul has troubled to secure it, and if my things are damaged I shall want to know who is responsible."

I directed Jem with a look. He was already moving.

"The hinge will be mended within the hour, ma'am," I said. "Your room is the second on the left at the top of the stairs. Nan will bring tea directly. If anything in the trunk has suffered, you may tell me when you've had a chance to look, and we will settle it."

The woman blinked. She had been prepared for an argument, and I had not given her one, and this left her with nothing to do but go upstairs. She went.

The coachman was waiting by the horses, stamping his feet against the cold. He was a regular--Dawson, who ran the Tuesday and Friday route--and he had the tight jaw of a man who'd been holding his patience for thirty miles.

"The stretch above Hatfield," he said, not bothering with a greeting. "It's gone to mud. Axle-deep in places. If it freezes before they mend it, you'll lose the Friday coach altogether--no driver with any sense will risk it."

"They won't mend it before spring. You know that. Then you'll want to take the Welwyn fork until it's mended. It adds twenty minutes and saves you the axle."

He opened his mouth, reconsidered, and closed it. "Twenty minutes."

"Twenty minutes. And someone ought to be writing to the turnpike trust about the Hatfield stretch."

"Someone has. Twice." I handed him the cup of ale Nan had brought to the door. "Take it up with the trustees if you've got an hour to spare. I haven't."

He drank, handed back the cup, and climbed up to the box. The coach pulled out. The yard cleared. Jem took the split-hinged trunk into the stable to work on it. Nan was behind the bar again, wiping down the counter with one eye on the door through all of it. I noticed. Said nothing.

The chandler came at half past ten, as he always did on the last Tuesday of the month. Mr. Pollard was a thin man with an expression of habitual injury, as though the world had been specifically designed to undervalue tallow. He set the crate of candles on the kitchen table and named his price.

"One and four the dozen," he said.

"It was one and two last month."

"Tallow's gone up."

"Tallow has not gone up. Tallow is exactly where it was in September, because I asked Mr. Harker at the chandlery in Welwyn what he was paying, and he told me, and he had no reason to lie about it since I'm not his customer."

Pollard's face did something complicated. His left hand found the edge of the crate and held it, as if the candles might make a break for the door. "There's the cost of carriage."

"Your carriage cost has not changed since April. You are on the same route you have always been on, Mr. Pollard, with the same horse and the same cart, and unless the horse has developed expensive tastes, I cannot see where the increase arises."

A silence. He looked at the crate. He looked at me.

"One and threepence," he said.

"One and two. As it was. As it will remain until the price of tallow actually changes, at which point you are welcome to show me the invoice and we will discuss it like reasonable people."

He took the one and two. He left the candles. He went.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and allowed myself one small, private moment of satisfaction. Not because I enjoyed the negotiation--I did not, particularly--but because I had won it, and winning it meant the candles cost what they should cost, and that was one less thing eating at the margin between now and quarter day. I was good at this. It was worth remembering, on mornings when the ledger made my stomach clench, that I was good at this.

The thought arrived the way it always did--not as philosophy, but as arithmetic. The chandler's price, the rent, the six weeks in the ledger: every number sat on the same foundation, which was that the lease was in my name and the decisions were mine to make. If I married, the arithmetic would not change, but the name on it would. The lease, the income, the negotiations--all of it would pass to a husband as surely as water runs downhill, and whether he was kind or competent or neither would be entirely beside the point, because the law did not ask. I had refused two proposals for this reason, and I did not regret the refusals. I regretted that they had been necessary.

I wiped down the table where Pollard's crate had left a ring of dust and went to check the dining room.

The afternoon was half gone when I saw him through the kitchen window.

He'd come around the back, as the signs asked. Young--younger than Jem, younger than me. Thin in the way that said weeks, not days. His coat had been a soldier's coat once; the dye had faded to a colour that was not quite brown and not quite anything else. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather cracked and separating from the sole on the left foot. He had stuffed paper into the gap to keep out the wet. He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at a thing he is not certain he is allowed to want.

I opened it before he knocked.

He startled. Steadied himself. He was perhaps twenty, with a face that had not yet decided what shape it would settle into.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

A pause. He was calculating whether honesty would help or hurt. "Not since yesterday, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He came in and sat where I told him. I cut bread, ladled broth from the pot that had been on since morning, set both in front of him. He ate carefully, not fast--the way a man eats when he is hungry enough to bolt it and disciplined enough not to. He held the spoon the way Jem held tools: precisely, as though it mattered.

When he'd finished, he stood. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm obliged."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. And then, with the faintest suggestion that this was not a plan so much as a direction: "There's family. In Lincolnshire."

I nodded. He left by the same door he'd come in. I watched him cross the yard and pass through the gate without looking back, and I stood there a moment longer than I needed to, because he was somebody's son and his boots would not last to Lincolnshire, and there was nothing I could do about either of those things. I cleared his bowl and washed it and put it back on the shelf.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way--rooms to check, the kitchen to manage for the evening meal, a candle to replace in the upstairs corridor where the draught always killed them. Two men in the taproom got loud over something trivial, the way men do when they've been sitting too long and the ale is working faster than their sense.

I stepped into the doorway. "Gentlemen. You'll settle it quietly or you'll settle it outside, and outside is cold."

They settled it quietly. Nan watched from behind the bar, cloth in hand, and did not need to move.

The rest of the afternoon was work. Not the kind that makes a story--the kind that makes an inn. Nan at the bar. Jem in the yard. Me between the kitchen and the accounts and the dozen tasks that filled the space between one coach and the next. No incidents. No dramas. Only the ordinary grinding machinery of a day that earned just enough to justify the next one.

Evening came on slowly, the way it does in late October--not a darkness so much as a thickening, the light draining out of the yard by degrees until the windows gave back reflections instead of views. The last guests were settled. The last fire checked. I tallied the bar, closed down the kitchen, and began the circuit I had made every night for two years, and my mother had made every night for fourteen years before that.

Taproom fire down to coals. Good. Cellar hatch locked. The stable yard was quiet--Jem would have settled the horses. I crossed the yard in the dark, feeling the cold air against the places where my sleeves had ridden up. My hands were raw, reddened from cold water and the vinegar I'd used on the bar at dawn. There was a small cut on my index finger, right along the pad, where the edge of Pollard's delivery invoice had caught me when I'd folded it. I had not noticed it at the time. I noticed it now, in the particular way you notice small injuries only when the day has stopped requiring you to ignore them.

The second floor. A lit shape at Bridget's window--the glow of a candle and the outline of her sitting where she always sat, watching the yard. I did not look long.

I bolted the front door. I set the bar. I checked the kitchen door and turned the key. The inn was closed, and I was alone in it, and for one breath I let myself know it--the specific quality of an empty kitchen at the end of a day that had asked everything and offered nothing in return except the right to do it again tomorrow.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

It had not been there at supper. Someone--Nan, probably, collecting the last of the post from the box by the front door--had placed it squarely in the centre of the table where I would find it. The seal was Stainforth's: a neat impression in dark red wax, the same device I had seen on every piece of correspondence from his office since my mother's death. His handwriting on the direction, precise and unhurried.

I picked it up. Turned it over once. Broke the seal.

The letter was short. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy had a different quality--the compression of a man who expects to be obeyed and sees no reason to be persuasive about it.

He had a buyer for the property. A gentleman of means--his nephew, though he did not say so here--who wished to acquire the freehold and the coaching licence and develop the premises into a more suitable establishment. The current lease would be reviewed at quarter day. He invited me to meet with him and his solicitor at his offices in Stevenage on the fourteenth of November to discuss terms for the surrender of the lease. He expressed his confidence that I would see the wisdom of a timely resolution. He remained my obedient servant.

I set the letter down on the table. The cut on my finger was stinging where I'd gripped the paper.

Six weeks. He'd given me exactly the time I already knew I had, which meant he knew the numbers as well as I did, which meant this had been coming longer than the letter suggested. The chandler's rising prices. The pressure on the suppliers. The quiet, patient work of a man who held the structural advantage and had only ever needed to wait.

I looked at the letter on the table. I looked at the kitchen--my kitchen, my mother's kitchen, my grandmother's kitchen--and I said, to no one, to the empty room, to the walls that had heard three generations of women refuse to be moved:

"You'll need a better offer than that, Mr. Stainforth."
