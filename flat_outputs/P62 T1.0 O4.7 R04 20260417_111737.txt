Chapter One

The ledger gave me six weeks before the rent quarter became something other than a figure on a page. I closed the book with my thumb marking the column, then opened it again, because the numbers had not reformed themselves while my back was turned. Six weeks. I would have liked, on this particular morning, to find a different answer at the bottom of the page. I would have liked a great many things. I would have liked my mother back, for a start, and after that a pot of real tea instead of the second steeping I had poured myself, and after that the luxury of sitting down for ten minutes together. The ledger offered none of these. It offered six weeks and a column of small, tidy sums, and I closed it the second time for good.

The kitchen was cold in the close, mean way of a room whose fire has only just been coaxed awake. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and crossed to the hearth to feed it properly. The coals had a sulky look. I was sympathetic; I had a sulky look myself most mornings before the first coach. I set the kettle back on the hob. I checked the larder, the bread crock, the jar where we kept the good sugar and the jar where we kept the sugar that was tolerable for the taproom. I counted candles. I counted loaves. I counted, out of habit, the eggs in the bowl on the sill, although I had counted them last night before I went up to bed.

Eighteen eggs. The cold had not eaten any of them. Small mercies.

I went through the taproom and set the fire there. The rugs smelled of last night's tobacco and spilled ale and the particular boot leather of men who have walked a long way on a damp road. I liked the smell. It was the smell of the inn being used. Unused rooms have a thin, accusing smell, and thin, accusing rooms do not pay rent.

The windows at the front were rimed at the edges. I rubbed a patch clear with my sleeve and looked out at the yard. The cobbles were dark with dew and the light was the cold, washed kind that means the sun is present in theory only. Someone had swept the yard already. That would be Jem, who swept the yard at the hour he swept it regardless of whether anyone asked him to, because the yard wanted sweeping and he was the one awake to do it.

I checked the bar stock. I tallied last night's accounts against the coin in the drawer, which balanced, because Nan had been on the bar, and Nan never miscounted anything in her life, including the number of times a gentleman in a bottle-green coat had looked down the front of her apron between ten and midnight. I had a word with her about the bottle-green coat later. For the moment I wrote the figures into the daybook and set the drawer back.

Outside, a horse stamped in the stable yard and Jem said something short and firm to it in the tone one uses on a creature who knows better. I heard the horse settle. Jem had taught the stump of his left arm to do the work of the hand he had not had since Salamanca, and he used it now with the bracing trick he had worked out for a stubborn bridle. I did not watch him at it. He preferred not to be watched.

Nan came through the kitchen door with her apron already tied and three pins in her hair not yet set, which she was setting as she walked. She did not greet me. I did not greet her. She took down the taproom keys from the hook, turned them in her hand the once, and went through to the bar. We had done this every morning for seven years, and on the occasions when one of us had tried a good morning the other had nearly dropped something from the strangeness of it.

I heard the coach before I saw it. Late and light, by the sound, which meant trouble of one kind or another. I went out into the yard as the wheels came through the gate.

Two passengers. A trunk with a split hinge, balanced on the roof in a way that had clearly kept the coachman anxious since Hatfield. The coachman himself looking as if he had spent the last six miles composing a speech about the state of the north road. The first passenger down was a woman of forty in a bonnet she had paid too much for, and she had opinions about her trunk, which she began delivering to the coachman, to the air, and eventually to me.

"Jem," I said, and Jem appeared at my elbow, because Jem appeared at one's elbow when one said his name and not a moment later. "The hinge, please. Gently."

"Gently," said Jem, in the tone that meant he had already intended gently and did not need to be told.

I turned to the woman. "Madam. We'll have your trunk inside within five minutes and the hinge seen to before you leave. If you'll step through to the dining room, there's a fire and hot coffee. Nan will see to you."

She drew breath to tell me a great many things. I had already turned to the coachman, and she, finding her audience gone, followed her trunk indoors, which was what I had wanted.

"Miss Dale," said the coachman. He had the broad, raw face of a man who lived in weather. "The road above Hatfield. I've said before."

"I know you have."

"It's worse. There's a gully opened between the second and third milestone that'll have a wheel off by Christmas."

"Have you written to the surveyor?"

"Three times."

"Write a fourth," I said. "I'll add my name to it and so will the Green Dragon, I should think. Bring it when you come down tomorrow."

He nodded once. "Two minutes more of your time, Miss Dale."

"I haven't got two minutes. Write it plain. I'll sign it."

He nodded again, because he was a practical man, and practical men know when they have been given what they came for. He climbed back up to his bench. The coach rolled on.

Jem had the split trunk on his shoulder by then and was carrying it in through the side door. Nan had already taken the lady's hat and got her seated by the fire with something hot in her hand. The yard was clear. The whole thing had taken perhaps six minutes.

I allowed myself, standing a moment in the cold, the small, private satisfaction of a job done the way it ought to be done. Then I went inside, because Hollis the chandler was due at ten, and Hollis the chandler was the other piece of bad news I had been saving for my morning.

He came on the hour, as he always did, with his cart and his ledger and a face arranged to suggest he had not slept for worry on my behalf.

"Miss Dale."

"Mr Hollis."

He produced his invoice. I read the figure on it. I read it again, because the first reading had been so striking I thought perhaps I had imagined a digit.

"Four and twopence the dozen," I said.

"Tallow's up."

"Tallow isn't up fourpence the dozen since August."

"Tallow's up," said Hollis.

"Tallow is up a halfpenny the stone at Smithfield, Mr Hollis. I know because I asked on Tuesday. Your price to the George at Stevenage is three and tenpence the dozen. I know that because the George's cook drinks in my taproom and she's a woman who cannot keep a secret to save her soul."

Hollis adjusted his hat. He had the look of a man whose ground had moved half an inch.

"Miss Dale, I've my own costs."

"I've mine. Three and ninepence the dozen, Mr Hollis. I'll take the two hundred today and the same again at month's end."

"Three and elevenpence."

"Three and ninepence. You and I both know you'll not shift these candles faster anywhere on this road, and I'll not stand in my yard arguing the price of tallow with a man who's known me since I was twelve."

He looked at me. I looked at him. We had done this before. He knew I knew his margins. He knew he would carry the tallow back in the cart if I made him, and he knew that carrying tallow back in a cart was no man's idea of a profitable morning.

"Three and tenpence," he said, "and I'll throw in the wicks."

"Three and ninepence and keep your wicks. I've wicks."

"Miss Dale, you're a hard woman."

"Mr Hollis, I'm a solvent one. Three and ninepence."

He wrote the figure into his book with the air of a man signing articles of surrender. The candles came down off the cart. I signed for them. He tipped his hat and climbed back up and rattled out of the yard, and I stood a moment on the step with the chit in my hand, and I allowed myself one dry, interior grin, because I had done that rather neatly, and no one but the cat had seen.

The cat had seen. The cat was not impressed. The cat had been more impressed when I had opened a pilchard in March.

I went back into the kitchen and set the chit on the spike with the others, and as I did it I let myself think, for the space of perhaps four heartbeats, about the other thing the chandler's price meant. It meant that I had shaved fivepence the dozen off an account I could not afford to lose. It meant that if I had a husband, that fivepence would have been his fivepence to concede or keep, and the conversation in the yard would not have been mine to win. Coverture was not a word I used much out loud. It was a fact I ran my accounts against the way one runs a finger along a wall to feel where the damp is. Two men had asked me, since my mother died, to let them take the fivepence on my behalf, and I had thanked them both and shown them out, and I had reasons. Those were the reasons. I put the reasons back where I kept them and got on with the morning.

There was bread to put in the oven and a mutton to bone and a complaint from room four about a window that would not shut, which turned out to be a window that would shut perfectly if one had the knack and was willing to put a shoulder to it, which I did and was.

At a little past noon I was wiping the kitchen table down when I saw him through the window. The back door, as the sign at the side of the inn instructed. Young. Thin. His coat had been a good one two winters ago. His boots were going at the welt on the right foot. He stood on the step without knocking, and I understood, watching him, that he was not debating whether to knock. He was debating whether he was the sort of man a kitchen door would open to.

I went and opened it.

"Come in," I said. "Sit."

He came in. He sat at the end of the scrubbed table, carefully, as if the chair might object.

"Have you eaten?"

"Not today, miss."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Those are the terms."

"Yes, miss."

I cut bread. I ladled broth from the pot at the back of the range. I put both in front of him with a spoon and a cup of small beer, and I sat down across from him for the minute it took him to begin, because sitting down meant he was not being watched eat, which is a different thing from being fed.

He ate slowly at first, then less slowly. He held the spoon with both hands, as my father had the winter he was ill. I looked at the table. I did not look at the table for long.

"Where are you headed?"

"North, miss. Place called Pickering. My mother's people."

"That's a good walk."

"I've done some of it."

I nodded. "Finish that. There's a bit of cheese in the crock there. Take what you want of it when you go."

"Thank you, miss."

"Don't thank me. Eat."

He finished the bread and the broth and a second cup of the small beer, and he took the cheese when I pushed it across, and he stood up and put his cap back on and said thank you again, and I told him again not to, and he went out through the yard toward the road. I watched him as far as the gate. His boots were not going to hold to Pickering. His boots were not going to hold to Baldock.

I stood a moment at the door after I closed it. He was somebody's. That was all I let myself think, in as many words. He was somebody's, and the boots were going at the welt, and there was nothing in the world I could do about it past a bowl of broth and a piece of cheese. I went back to the stockpot.

The taproom nearly boiled over in the middle of the afternoon. Two carters who had been sitting too long over their ale began a dispute about a harness strap, and the dispute looked to be moving from harness to politics, which is the direction these things go. I came out of the kitchen and stood at the end of their table.

"Gentlemen. You'll lower your voices or you'll take it into the yard. Choose."

One of them opened his mouth. He looked at my face. He closed his mouth.

"Lower voices, miss," he said.

"Thank you."

I went back to the kitchen. Nan did not look up from the bar. She had not needed to move.

The inn was quiet after that. It was always quiet between coaches. I did the accounts for the morning, set the dining room for supper, stripped the sheets from number twelve because the gentleman in number twelve was paying for clean sheets and was going to get them, and spoke to the cook about mutton. The afternoon got on with itself.

Evening came early in late October, and with it the north coach, and with the north coach two commercial gentlemen who knew the house, and a clergyman who did not, and a young couple travelling with a maid who looked at Nan as if Nan might be encouraged to do the maid's work for her. Nan disabused her of this notion in three sentences, all of them civil. Supper was served. Supper was cleared. The taproom filled, and drained, and filled again. By ten the house was settled.

By eleven it was my own.

I went round with the lantern. Taproom fire down to coals, the guard across. Cellar hatch bolted. Dining-room shutters. Kitchen range banked. The bar tallied and the drawer in the safe behind the brick in the scullery wall that my grandmother had put there in 1774 and nobody had ever mentioned aloud. The back door, the side door, the front. The yard, empty. Jem had been through. I could tell because the pail by the pump had been turned upside down on the post to drain, and Jem turned pails. Above me, on the second floor, a single square of light: Aunt Bridget's window. She would be at it now, as she was most nights, in her chair. She did not wave and I did not wave. We had passed the age of waving a great while ago.

My hands in the lantern light looked like somebody else's. Red across the knuckles, a small cut on the pad of my right forefinger where I had run it down the edge of Hollis's invoice without thinking. I had not noticed when I did it. I noticed now. It was the stupid small hurt of a working day, and I thought, dryly, that a woman who could take fivepence off a chandler ought to be able to hold a piece of paper without bleeding on it.

I bolted the front door. I set the bar.

The house went very quiet around me, the quiet of a place that has been used hard and is resting. I stood in the passage with the lantern for a moment and let myself notice, just the once, that there was no one to say goodnight to. Then I picked up the lantern and went through to the kitchen, because the last thing on a day was always the kitchen.

The letter was on the table.

It had come, I supposed, with the evening post, and Nan had put it there on her way up to bed and not thought to call me down, because Nan did not open my correspondence and did not fuss. I saw the seal before I saw the direction. A dark wax, pressed with the Stainforth crest, and the direction in the careful, upright hand of the man himself. Not his clerk's. His.

I set the lantern on the table and sat down. I turned the letter over in my hands. I turned it back. The seal cracked cleanly under my thumb and the paper opened like something that had been waiting.

I read it once fast and once slowly.

He regretted, Mr Stainforth wrote, the necessity of troubling me on a matter of business at this hour of the year. He had been approached, he wrote, by a gentleman of his acquaintance, a relation, indeed, who had formed the intention of acquiring a coaching house on the Great North Road with a view to improvement. The lease of the White Hound fell due for renewal at Lady Day next. He had, after careful consideration, determined that the lease should pass, on that date, to his nephew, Mr Rowland Stainforth, on terms already agreed between them. He was certain, he wrote, that I would understand the reasoning. He trusted that I had, through the prudence for which I was known, laid by sufficient means to establish myself in some other situation suited to my abilities. He had instructed his steward to call upon me on the fourteenth of November to discuss the particulars of vacating. He remained my obedient servant, et cetera, et cetera.

Lady Day. The twenty-fifth of March. Five months.

The fourteenth of November was a fortnight from Thursday.

I read the letter a third time to be sure I had not softened any of it in my head. I had not. He had written it as plain as a man can write. He wanted the inn. He had a buyer. The buyer was his nephew. He had set the clock.

I folded the letter along its creases and laid it flat on the table with my palm on top of it, because my hand wanted something to hold and the letter was the thing nearest to hand. The cut on my forefinger stung against the paper. I noticed that too.

The house was quiet around me. The kitchen fire had banked down to a low red under the ash. Somewhere out in the yard a horse shifted and settled. Above me, I knew without looking, Bridget would have put out her candle by now.

I said it aloud, because it was the sort of thing that wanted saying aloud once in a room where no one would hear it and offer an opinion.

"Then you've picked the wrong woman, Mr Stainforth, and I'll see you on the fourteenth."

I picked the letter up and carried it with me to the stairs.
