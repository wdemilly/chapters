The ink had bled through the third column again, which meant the nib wanted replacing and the ledger would need a fresh page before quarter day, and I could not afford to waste either.

I set down the pen and flexed my fingers over the open book. The numbers were not disastrous. They were not comfortable, either. Six weeks stood between me and the next rent payment, and the margin between meeting it and not meeting it was so narrow I could have ruled a line with it. Forty-two days. I had counted them twice this morning already, as though the second count might conjure a different sum, and I was not so far gone as to attempt a third.

What I wanted -- what I wanted so badly that it sat behind my ribs like a stone I could not cough up -- was three months of good custom without a single thing breaking, souring, or catching fire. I closed the ledger and pressed my palm flat on its cover, as if I could keep the numbers still by force.

The kitchen was cold. Not the damp, penetrating cold of midwinter, which would come soon enough, but the thin sharp cold of late October, the kind that crept under doors and settled in the hearthstones overnight. The fire I had built twenty minutes ago was still finding its temper, the logs hissing with yesterday's rain. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and got on with it.

The taproom fire was laid and lit before the kitchen one had properly caught, which was a matter of priority rather than comfort. Guests would forgive a slow breakfast. They would not forgive a cold room. I checked the bar stock -- the small beer was low, the gin nearly full, the brandy at a level that suggested someone had been helping themselves or I had miscounted last night, and I did not miscount. I made a note. I tallied the previous evening's accounts against the coin in the cashbox, and the figures matched to the ha'penny, which was the only kind of satisfaction available at half past five in the morning.

The windows along the front passage were rimed at their edges, a delicate frosting that caught the first grey light and made the glass look expensive. It was not expensive. Two of the panes had cracks I had been nursing through since August, and the putty around the lower sashes had gone chalky and soft. I added it to the list in my head -- the list that grew longer every week and never grew shorter, because an inn is a living creature that eats money and excretes problems, and the only thing standing between it and ruin is the person who gets up first.

I was always the person who got up first.

Out the back, the yard was still and grey. Jem was already there -- I could see him through the kitchen window, moving between the stalls with his particular economy of motion, settling a headcollar with his one hand while the stump of the other braced the post. He had not been asked. He never was. The morning required certain things, and Jem did them, and we had long since stopped performing the courtesy of instructions.

Nan arrived through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word and began wiping down the counter. I heard the cloth moving in slow, purposeful strokes as I passed through the passage. We had done this before. Hundreds of times. The rhythm of it was so familiar it had become invisible, which is the highest compliment an operation can pay itself.

The London coach was late, which was not unusual, and running short -- two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them unhappy about it. The first was a stout woman in a travelling pelisse who descended from the coach with the expression of someone who has been personally wronged by the entire county of Hertfordshire. The second was a thin, silent man who took himself directly to the dining room without speaking to anyone, which I respected.

The woman had a trunk with a split hinge, and she had opinions.

"It was perfectly sound when I left Islington," she informed me, as though Islington were a moral condition and not a place. "The roads have done this. The roads are a disgrace."

I directed Jem, who had appeared at my elbow without being summoned, to take the trunk to number six and prop the hinge with a wedge until it could be mended. The woman watched this operation with deep suspicion but could find no grounds for objection, which seemed to disappoint her. I settled her toward the dining room with a directness that surprised her into cooperation -- the trick being not to offer choices but to state what would happen next, in a tone that presumed agreement.

The coachman wanted a word. He stood by the horses, leather gloves tucked under one arm, and waited until the passengers were out of earshot.

"North road's breaking up above Hatfield," he said. "Ruts a foot deep past the Wrestler's turnpike. I near lost a wheel."

"I heard the same from the Baldock coach on Tuesday. Did you come through the lower road or the ridge?"

"Ridge. The lower's worse -- standing water across the full width."

"Take the ridge on your return and tell Gregson at the Red Lion to do the same. I'll send word to the turnpike trust, for what good it will do." I had already written to them twice this autumn. The roads would not be mended until someone important enough broke an axle on them, and that was a calculation I could not control. "You'll want to change the offside leader. He's favouring his near fore."

The coachman looked at me, looked at the horse, and looked at me again. He did not argue. He had been driving this route long enough to know that I noticed these things, and that arguing with me about horses was not a productive use of his seven minutes.

He went. The passengers went upstairs or to the dining room. Jem took the trunk. The yard cleared with the speed of a stage after the curtain falls, all the urgency collapsing into silence. Through the passage I could see Nan behind the bar, cloth still in hand, her eyes on the front door. She had been watching through all of it. I noted this. Said nothing.

Mr Fenwick, the chandler, arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his new price.

"Tenpence the dozen," he said, setting down the first crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table as though he were presenting a gift.

"They were ninepence last month, Mr Fenwick. And eightpence the month before that."

"Tallow's gone up. Rendering costs. The war, you know."

"The war ended a year and a half ago."

"Effects linger, Miss Dale." He had the grace to look almost sorry about it. Almost.

I folded my arms. "You pay fourpence ha'penny a dozen for tallow from Jessop's yard, which I know because Jessop told me so last Thursday when he was three pints into his evening. Your rendering is done by your eldest boy, whom you do not pay. Your actual cost is fivepence, and you have been charging the Crown at Hatfield ninepence since June, which means your margin on this stretch of road is already more generous than you deserve."

A silence. Mr Fenwick had the look of a man recalculating.

"Ninepence," I said. "Same as the Crown. Or you can load them back on your cart and explain to Mrs Fenwick why you lost the White Hound's custom over a penny."

"Eight dozen at ninepence," he said, after a pause that was three seconds longer than it needed to be.

"Eight dozen at ninepence. And the next time you raise the price, Mr Fenwick, I would appreciate being told the reason rather than being told a story."

He left. The candles stayed. I stood alone in the kitchen and allowed myself one slow breath -- the particular breath of a woman who has just held her ground and knows exactly what it cost, and exactly what it was worth. Not triumph. Satisfaction. The quiet kind, which is the only kind that keeps.

The thought came, as it always did, unbidden and practical: every shilling I held down in that negotiation was a shilling I could only hold because the ledger was in my name. The lease, the accounts, the right to stand in my own kitchen and tell a chandler his business -- all of it rested on the fact that I had not married. Two men had asked. I had refused both, and I had refused them for this -- for the candles and the cashbox and the cold satisfaction of knowing that what I held, I held alone. If I married, the law would hand it all to a husband, and the next time Mr Fenwick raised his price, it would not be me he negotiated with. That was a calculation I had made before, and the answer had not changed. I picked up the crate and carried it to the storeroom.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the signs asked. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way of men who have been thin for a long time, not the leanness of youth but the drawn quality of want. His coat was army issue, much mended. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the sole of the left one separating from the upper so that the pale flash of a stocking showed through with every step. He stood outside the kitchen door without raising his hand, and I watched him assess the yard, the door, the window where I stood. He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to knock. He was not sure.

I opened the door.

He straightened. He did not salute, but something in the set of his shoulders wanted to.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

"Not since yesterday, ma'am. Morning."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there."

He understood. He came in and sat where I pointed, at the far end of the table, and kept his hands on his knees until I put the bowl in front of him. Broth from the stockpot, bread from the morning's baking, a heel of cheese that was harder than I would have served a guest but softer than anything he had likely seen in a week. He ate steadily, without haste, holding the spoon the way a man holds a tool he has not used in some time -- carefully, with attention.

"Where are you headed?" I asked, when the bowl was nearly empty.

"North, ma'am. Leeds, if I can get there. My sister's there."

I nodded. Leeds was a long walk from Hertfordshire in boots that were not going to survive another fifty miles. He knew it. I knew it. Neither of us said so.

He stood, thanked me, and went. I watched him cross the yard and pass through the gate, the split boot catching on the cobbles as he turned onto the road. He was somebody's son. He was somebody's brother, walking to Leeds in October with the sole coming off his boot, and there was nothing else I could do for him, and the nothing sat in my chest like a swallowed coin.

I washed the bowl and put it back on the shelf.

In the taproom, a disagreement had been building between two guests who had been sitting too long over their ale -- something about a horse trade, or a wager, or one of the dozen trivial things that men will sharpen into grievances when they are bored and warm and slightly drunk.

"Gentlemen." I did not raise my voice. I did not need to. "If you wish to continue this discussion, you may do so outside. If you wish to remain inside, you will do so quietly."

They looked at me. They looked at each other. The louder of the two opened his mouth, reconsidered, and closed it. They drank in silence. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and returned to her work.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at an inn between coaches -- the particular quiet that falls when the morning's urgency has spent itself and the evening's has not yet arrived. Nan polished glasses. Jem was outside, doing whatever Jem did in the long hours between arrivals, which was everything that needed doing and several things that did not, because Jem could not be idle any more than a clock could stop ticking. I reset two rooms, checked the kitchen stores against the evening's requirements, and brought the accounts up to the hour. The ordinary grind. The work that is never finished and never dramatic and keeps the entire enterprise from sliding into disorder.

By evening the last guests were settled or gone. I moved through the closing routine with the efficiency of long practice -- taproom fire raked down to coals, the guard set before it. Cellar hatch locked and the key returned to its hook. Bar tallied, the figures entered in the ledger in my increasingly unreliable ink. Kitchen closed down: pots scoured, surfaces wiped, the stockpot moved to the back of the range to keep warm through the night. I checked the front door, the side door, the passage windows. The bolt on the cellar. The latch on the storeroom.

The stable yard was quiet. Jem would have settled the horses -- I could hear nothing from the stalls, which meant they were fed and watered and content, because a discontented horse is never silent about it. Above the yard, on the second floor, there was a lit shape at Bridget's window. I did not look long. She was there. She was watching. She was always watching.

My hands, when I looked at them under the kitchen lamp, were reddened from cold water and cleaning. There was a small cut on my index finger, a thin line just below the nail where the edge of Mr Fenwick's delivery note had caught me as I'd filed it. I had not noticed it at the time. It stung now, faintly, in the way of small wounds that wait until you are still before announcing themselves.

The last door was locked. The bolt was set. The inn was closed, and it was mine, and I was alone in it. I let myself notice this -- the silence, the particular quality of a space that holds no other breathing -- and then I turned to the table to put out the lamp.

The letter was where Nan must have left it, propped against the salt cellar where I would see it. I had not seen it. I had been too occupied with the closing to look, and Nan had said nothing, which meant she had read the handwriting on the direction and known I would not want to be told.

Stainforth's seal. Stainforth's hand on the direction: Miss D. Dale, The White Hound, Great North Road, Hertfordshire.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The wax was dark red, pressed with his signet -- the rampant something-or-other that I had never troubled to identify. The paper was heavy and cream-coloured, the paper of a man who did not buy his stationery in bulk.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, which was the only thing we had in common.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer for the freehold of the property known as the White Hound, including all buildings, yard, and stabling, from a party whose terms I am inclined to accept. As a courtesy to your family's long association with the property, I am prepared to meet with you before any agreement is concluded, should you wish to discuss the terms of your continued tenancy or the orderly conclusion of your lease. I would propose the 7th of December for such a meeting. I trust this gives you sufficient time to arrange your affairs.*

*Your servant, P. Stainforth*

I read it twice. The seventh of December. Six weeks. The same six weeks I had counted in the ledger this morning, the same narrow margin, only now it was not a margin at all -- it was a deadline.

He had a buyer. He was offering me a meeting the way a man offers a condemned prisoner a last meal -- not because it changed the outcome, but because it allowed him to believe he was being civilised about it.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed my fingers flat against the wood, the cut on my index finger stinging where it met the grain.

"Well, Mr Stainforth," I said to the empty kitchen. "You'll have your meeting. And I'll have my terms."
