The ink had bled through again.

I pressed my thumb against the ledger page where the nib had caught, as though pressure alone might keep the number from worsening. Six weeks and three days until the quarter rent came due, and the column would not balance no matter how many times I ran it. Forty-one pounds, eight shillings in hand. Fifty-two pounds owed. The difference sat in my chest like a stone I had swallowed and could not cough up.

I closed the ledger and set the pen in its tray. The kitchen was cold enough that my breath showed when I sighed, which was reason enough not to sigh, so I didn't. I pushed my sleeves down over my wrists and went to build up the fire.

The White Hound woke in a particular order, and I was always the first part of it. Kitchen fire, then the range set to rights, then through to the taproom where last night's coals had gone to ash. I knelt on the stone floor and raked the grate, laid the kindling, struck the flint. The tinder caught on the second try, which was better than yesterday. Small mercies. I fed the flame until it held, then stood and checked the bar stock by touch in the half-dark: the ale cask nearly empty, the gin at a quarter, the port untouched because no one who stopped at the White Hound in late October was drinking port. I made a note in my head. I always made notes in my head. Paper cost money.

The windows were rimed at their edges, a thin crust of frost that caught the first grey light and made the glass look sugared. I wiped the nearest pane with my sleeve and looked out at the yard. Empty. The mounting block, the water trough with its skin of ice, the stable doors still shut. The Great North Road beyond the gate was a dark ribbon going nowhere in both directions.

I wanted, very briefly and with an intensity that embarrassed me, to go back to bed.

Instead I tallied last night's accounts from the notes I'd left on the bar. Three dinners, two overnight rooms, a private parlour let for an hour to a solicitor who had wanted somewhere quiet to read his papers and had left without tipping. The numbers were not terrible. They were not enough.

I heard Jem before I saw him. The particular scrape of the stable yard gate that meant he was already up and had not bothered to oil the hinge, because Jem considered oiling hinges a concession to comfort that a man with one arm should not be expected to make. When I checked out the back, he was in the yard, straightening tack on the rail with his one good hand and the practised brace of his stump against the post. He did not look up. He did not need to. The morning required what it required, and Jem had never once needed me to tell him what that was.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word, ran her cloth along its surface once, and began setting out the glasses. We had done this before. Hundreds of times. The efficiency was its own language, and none of us needed to translate it.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual -- the London coach had been late more often than not since the road above Hatfield had begun to deteriorate -- but I stood in the yard with my arms folded and my irritation sharpening with each passing minute, because the coach being late meant the passengers would be in a temper, and passengers in a temper meant the morning would cost me an extra half-hour of management I could not spare.

When it finally came, it was running short. Two passengers instead of the expected four. The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the compressed fury of someone who had been personally wronged by every mile of road between here and London. She had a trunk, and the trunk had a split hinge, and she had opinions about this.

I directed Jem with a glance. He appeared at the coach step without being called, took the trunk's weight on his shoulder, and carried it inside before the woman could finish her first sentence of complaint. She blinked, recalibrated, and allowed herself to be shown to the dining room, where Nan was already setting out bread and butter.

The coachman wanted a word.

"That stretch above Hatfield's gone to ruin, Miss Dale." He was a broad man named Farrow who had been driving this route since before my mother died. He stood by the horses with his hat pushed back, and he was not happy. "Ruts deep enough to break an axle. I've reported it twice."

"To the turnpike trust?"

"Aye, and had nothing back but a letter saying they're aware of the condition."

"Then report it a third time, Mr Farrow, and tell them the White Hound will be writing as well. I'll have the letter ready for your return journey."

He looked at me for a moment, then nodded. "I'll hold you to that."

"You won't need to."

He touched his hat and went to see about the horses. The second passenger -- a thin, silent man who had descended from the coach without complaint and gone directly upstairs -- required nothing from me at all, which was the best kind of passenger.

The yard cleared. Jem took the broken trunk to the back to see what could be done about the hinge. Nan was behind the bar with her cloth, one eye on the dining room door, and I noted the steadiness of her attention without comment. She had been watching the arrivals with the same quiet vigilance she always did, cataloguing faces and luggage and the particular quality of each traveller's shoes. Nan missed very little. I said nothing about it, because saying something would have implied she needed instruction, and she did not.

The chandler arrived at half past ten.

Mr Poole was a neat, careful man with a neat, careful moustache and the habit of adjusting his cuffs before naming a price, as though the state of his linen somehow justified the number. He set the crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table and adjusted his cuffs.

"Eightpence the dozen, Miss Dale."

I looked at the candles. I looked at Mr Poole. "They were sevenpence last month."

"Tallow's gone up."

"Tallow has not gone up. Tallow went up in August, and you adjusted for it then. What's gone up is your ambition."

His moustache twitched. "I have costs, Miss Dale."

"You do. And I know what they are, Mr Poole, because I know what you pay Jessop for your tallow and what you charge the Red Lion and the George and every other house between here and Baldock. Sevenpence was fair. Eightpence is you seeing whether I'll notice."

"Seven and a half," he said, after a pause that was slightly too long.

"Seven. As it was. And I'll take an extra two dozen at that price, which saves you a second delivery."

He looked at the candles. He looked at his cuffs. He looked at me. "Seven," he said. "And the extra two dozen."

"Thank you, Mr Poole. Nan will settle the account."

He gathered his dignity and left. The candles stayed.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and permitted myself one small, private breath of satisfaction. It was not a victory. It was a Tuesday. But I had held the line, and the line was all I had, and I was very good at holding it.

The thought arrived as it always did, somewhere between one task and the next, settling into the gap like draught through a window frame. The ledger, the rent, the chandler's penny -- all of it weighed against the same immovable fact. If I married, none of this would be mine to fight for. The lease, the accounts, the right to stand in my own kitchen and tell Mr Poole his price was robbery -- all of it would pass to a husband the moment I said the words. I had refused two proposals already for precisely this reason, and I would refuse twenty more if it came to it. The thought completed itself, and I set it aside, and I went to check the cellar stores.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the signs asked. Young -- not much past twenty, if that. Thin in the way that meant weeks, not days. His coat was too large for him, as though he had been a bigger man when he put it on, and his boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather separating from the sole in a way that could not be repaired, only replaced. He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand half-raised, not quite knocking. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at something he is not sure he is permitted to want.

I opened it before he could decide.

He startled. Steadied himself. His eyes were pale and very careful.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

"Not recently, ma'am." He said it honestly, without performance, and I was grateful for that.

"Kitchen, not taproom. There's bread and broth. Sit where I show you."

He nodded once and came in. I sat him at the end of the kitchen table, away from the door to the taproom, and set a bowl of broth and half a loaf in front of him. He ate carefully, not wolfishly -- pacing himself, which told me he had been hungry long enough to know that eating too fast would make him ill. He held the spoon with a deliberateness that suggested his hands were not entirely steady and he did not wish me to see it.

When he had finished, he stood. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. And then, as though the word were not quite enough: "Home. If it's still there."

He left through the kitchen door and crossed the yard without looking back. I watched him go. His boots made no sound on the cobbles, which meant the soles were worn through entirely, and I thought -- just for a moment, before I stopped myself -- that he was someone's son, and that someone did not know where he was, and that the boots were the worst part of it, because boots could be mended and no one had mended them.

I cleared the bowl. I wiped the table. I went back to work.

The trouble in the taproom was nothing -- two men who had been sitting too long over too little ale, their voices rising over some question of a coach fare that had nothing to do with either of them and everything to do with boredom and the particular friction of a wet afternoon.

"Gentlemen," I said, from the doorway. "The next round is on your own coin or not at all, and I'll have quiet either way."

They went quiet. Nan, behind the bar, did not look up from the glass she was polishing, which was her way of telling me she had been ready to intervene and had decided not to.

The afternoon settled into its ordinary shape. Coach gone, guests upstairs or departed, the inn in the gap between rushes that was not rest but a different kind of work. I reset the rooms on the second floor, checked the kitchen stores against tomorrow's needs, and sat for twenty minutes with the accounts while Nan minded the bar and Jem did whatever Jem did in the stable yard during the hours when no horses required his attention. I suspected he talked to them anyway. I had never asked.

Evening came the way it always did -- not all at once but in stages, the light pulling back from the yard, the fires needing attention, the last tasks of the day arriving in their familiar order. I checked the taproom fire, banked it down to coals that would hold until morning. I locked the cellar hatch. I wiped the bar, because Nan had gone upstairs and I preferred to do it myself one final time, running my hand along the wood to feel for the sticky patches that meant something had been spilled and not properly cleaned. There were none. Nan was thorough.

The stable yard was quiet. Jem would have settled the horses and gone to his room above the tack store. I could see the faint line of light under his door. I stood in the yard and listened to the particular silence of an inn that had been used hard all day and was now resting -- not empty, but held, like a breath between sentences.

I looked up at the second floor. There was a lit shape at Bridget's window -- the familiar silhouette of her in her chair, watching the yard as she always did, seeing everything and commenting on none of it until she was ready. I did not look long.

My hands, when I held them up in the last light from the kitchen doorway, were reddened from the cold water and the cleaning. There was a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Mr Poole's delivery note had caught the skin -- a paper cut, the most trivial injury in the world, and I had not noticed it until now. I pressed my thumb against it and felt the sting. A whole day's work, and this was what my body had chosen to remember.

I bolted the kitchen door. I set the bar across it. I stood in the kitchen alone, and for one breath -- just one -- I let myself notice that I was alone, that the inn was quiet, that there was no voice in any room expecting mine to answer it.

The letter was on the table where the afternoon post had left it.

I had seen it earlier and set it aside, because there had been broth to make and candles to argue over and a hungry boy to feed, and because I had recognised the seal and had not wanted to open it while there was still daylight and work to be done. Stainforth's seal -- the pressed wax with its fussy little crest that he used on every piece of correspondence as though he were writing to Parliament rather than to a woman who ran a coaching inn.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. I broke the seal.

The handwriting was not Stainforth's own -- he had a clerk for that -- but the signature at the bottom was his, and the substance was perfectly clear. He had received an offer for the property. His nephew, Mr Edward Stainforth, wished to acquire the lease of the White Hound with a view to its improvement and modernisation. He was prepared to offer me a sum -- he named it, and it was insultingly small -- in recognition of my family's long tenure. I was invited to a meeting at his offices in Stevenage on the fourteenth of November to discuss the terms of transfer. If I did not attend, he would take it as acceptance of the terms as stated.

The fourteenth of November. Three weeks from today.

I set the letter down on the kitchen table. I pressed both hands flat against the wood, feeling the grain of it under my sore fingers, the familiar surface I had scrubbed ten thousand times. The fire was down to its last coals. The inn was silent.

"Three weeks," I said aloud, to the empty room and the dying fire and the letter with its fussy little seal. "Then I had better be ready for you, Mr Stainforth."
