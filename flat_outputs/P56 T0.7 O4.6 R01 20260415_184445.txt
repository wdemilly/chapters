The ledger did not balance, and I had known it would not balance before I opened it.

I sat at the kitchen table with the book flat under both hands, the column of figures marching down the right side of the page in my own careful ink. Forty-seven days until the next quarter's rent came due. Forty-seven days and roughly six pounds short, assuming no disaster between now and then, which was assuming a great deal. I closed the ledger and set my palm flat on its cover, as though the numbers might crawl out and cause further trouble if left unsupervised.

The kitchen fire needed building up. I'd banked it the night before and it had gone sulky, as kitchen fires will when they sense you need them. I knelt on the stone floor and worked the bellows until the coals caught, then fed in kindling and two split logs from the basket Jem had filled yesterday. The wood was not as dry as I'd have liked. Nothing was as dry as I'd have liked. October had been wet and mean, and the damp had got into everything -- the wood, the walls, the hinges on the cellar hatch, my knees when I knelt too long.

The inn was quiet in the way it only ever was at this hour, before the day arrived to fill it. I could hear the building itself: the tick of cooling plaster, the faint complaint of the sign bracket when the wind caught it, the particular creak of the second stair that I had been meaning to have seen to since July. I knew every sound this place made. I knew which door stuck in wet weather and which window let in a draught no matter how firmly you latched it. I knew the precise rattle of a full coach pulling into the yard versus a half-empty one, and I could tell by the sound of boots on the taproom floor whether a man intended to pay his bill or argue about it.

What I wanted -- just once, just for one morning -- was to open that ledger and find a number I had not expected. A surplus. A margin wide enough that I might stop counting days.

But wanting was a poor use of a Tuesday, so I got up off my knees and went to work.

The taproom fire next. I swept the grate, laid the fire, lit it. The room smelled of last night's ale and tallow smoke, and the windows were rimed with cold at their edges, a thin lace of frost that would melt by midmorning and leave the glass streaked. I wiped down the bar, checked the casks -- the brown ale was running low, the small beer adequate, the cider untouched because nobody wanted cider in late October -- and tallied the previous night's take against what Nan had left in the cashbox. Nan's figures were exact. They were always exact. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists against the chill and moved on.

Bar stock. Candle supply. The hinge on the dining room door that had begun to grind. I noted each item in the running list I kept in my head, the one that never closed, the one that woke me sometimes at three in the morning with an addition I had not asked for.

Through the back window I could see Jem in the stable yard, a dark shape moving between the stalls. He was straightening tack on the hooks outside the nearest box -- bridles hung evenly, reins looped and secured, everything in its place. He had not been asked. The morning required it, and Jem did what the morning required, and that was the whole of our understanding.

I was back in the kitchen when Nan came through the door. Pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied, eyes not fully open but her hands already reaching for the bar cloth she kept on the hook by the passage. She crossed through the kitchen without a word and took her place behind the bar. I heard the cloth hit the wood, the small efficient sounds of a woman settling into the shape of her day.

We had done this before. Hundreds of times. The machinery of the morning did not require conversation, only presence, and they were present, and so was I, and the White Hound stirred itself awake around us like a creature remembering what its limbs were for.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual -- the London coach was late more often than it was punctual, owing to the road, the weather, the horses, and whatever other excuse the driver had assembled during the final miles. What was unusual was that when it finally clattered into the yard, steam rising from the team and the coachman's face set in a particular expression I recognized as a man composing a grievance, it carried only two passengers instead of the expected four.

Two passengers and one trunk with a split hinge, which the owner -- a woman of perhaps fifty in a travelling cloak that had seen considerably better decades -- was already loudly lamenting before the coach had fully stopped.

I directed Jem to the trunk with a look. He appeared at the coach door without being called, took the damaged luggage under his one good arm, and carried it inside as though split hinges were a personal affront he intended to remedy. The woman watched him go with the startled silence of a person whose complaint has been handled before she finished making it.

The second passenger went to the dining room without fuss. I turned to the coachman, who had climbed down and was standing in the yard with his arms folded and his hat pushed back, which meant he wanted a word and intended to have one whether I had time for it or not.

"Road's gone to pieces north of Hatfield," he said. "Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel in. Someone's got to speak to the turnpike trust."

"Someone has. Twice. They've promised gravel before the frost sets."

"Gravel won't answer. It wants proper drainage. I near lost a horse in that stretch last Thursday."

"Then you'll want to take the Welwyn fork until it's mended. It adds twenty minutes and saves you the axle."

He opened his mouth, reconsidered, and closed it. "Twenty minutes."

"At most. And you'll still make Baldock by four if you don't dawdle here."

He gave me the look men give when they have been managed by a woman and are deciding whether to mind. He decided not to. He touched his hat and went to see about the horses, and I went back inside, because the morning was not going to run itself and I had already given him his two minutes.

The yard cleared. The passengers settled -- the woman with the trunk had been installed in room six, where Jem had already done something competent to the hinge, and the quiet gentleman was eating bread and cheese in the dining room as though bread and cheese were all he had ever wanted from the world. Nan stood behind the bar with the cloth in her hand and one eye on the door to the passage, watching the rhythm of the morning resume. I saw her watching. I said nothing. There was nothing to say. We both knew what we were looking at: a coach that should have been full arriving half-empty, and what that meant for the week's take.

Mr. Fenton, the chandler, arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his particular way of smiling that meant he was about to make my morning worse.

"Miss Dale." He set the crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table with the careful placement of a man presenting a gift. "Fourteen dozen, as agreed."

"At what price?"

"Tuppence ha'penny the dozen."

I looked at the candles. I looked at Mr. Fenton. He had a broad, well-fed face and the permanently hopeful expression of a man who believed that if he said a number with enough confidence, it would become true.

"They were tuppence the dozen in March, Mr. Fenton."

"Tallow's gone up."

"Tallow's gone up a farthing the pound since March. That does not account for a ha'penny on the dozen."

"There's cartage to consider."

"Your cartage has not changed. You pass this door on your way to the Bell at Welwyn every month regardless. I am not additional cartage. I am a stop you would make anyway."

The smile held, but it thinned at the edges. "Miss Dale, I have other customers who'd be glad of the supply."

"You do. And they pay tuppence farthing, which is what Mrs. Pratt at the Bell told me last week when I asked, because I make it my business to ask." I folded my arms. "Tuppence farthing, Mr. Fenton. The same as last month and the month before. Or you may load them back on your cart and explain to your other customers why you've arrived late and short."

A silence. Mr. Fenton looked at his candles. He looked at me. He performed a small, private calculation behind his eyes that I could follow as clearly as if he'd written it on the wall.

"Tuppence farthing," he said.

"Thank you. Nan will see you out."

He took his coin and left. The candles stayed. I stood in the kitchen with the crate on the table and allowed myself one moment -- just one -- of the quiet, sharp satisfaction that comes from knowing exactly what a thing costs and refusing to pay more. It was a small victory. Most of mine were. But the inn ran on small victories, and I had learned to collect them the way other women collected compliments.

The thought arrived without invitation, as it always did, somewhere between the chandler's departure and the next task. The ledger, the rent, the candle price -- all of it balanced against the single fact that governed every calculation I made: if I married, none of this would be mine to fight for. The lease, the accounts, the right to tell Mr. Fenton what his candles were worth -- all of it would pass to a husband the moment I spoke my vows. I had refused two proposals already, for exactly this reason. The refusals had not been difficult. The proposals had not tempted me. What was difficult was the thing I did not let myself examine too closely afterward -- the brief, unwelcome awareness that I had chosen the inn over the possibility of being chosen, and that the inn would never, in return, choose me back.

I wiped down the table where the crate had been and went to see about the midday bread.

I noticed him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the signs asked. Young -- twenty at most, possibly less. Thin in the way that means weeks, not constitution. His coat had been military once, though whatever regiment it belonged to had been worn past recognition. His boots were the worst of it: the leather split along the welt of the left one, the sole of the right separating at the toe. He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand half-raised and did not knock. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at something he is not certain he is permitted to want.

I opened it before he had decided.

He startled. Up close he was even younger than I'd thought -- fair-haired, hollow-cheeked, with a stillness about the eyes that I had seen before in men who had come back from the war and not entirely arrived.

"Have you eaten today?"

He considered lying. I watched him consider it. "Not since yesterday, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there."

He sat. I put bread in front of him, and broth from the pot that had been on since morning, and he ate with the steady, methodical attention of someone who has learned not to eat too fast after going without. He held the spoon carefully, almost formally, as though someone had once taught him table manners and the lesson had survived everything else.

When he had finished, he stood and thanked me with a brevity that was not rudeness but economy -- he did not have words to spare any more than he had food.

"Where are you headed?"

"North, ma'am. There's work in the mills, I'm told."

I did not say what I thought about the mills, or the likelihood of work, or the roads between here and there in boots that would not last another week. I said, "The Great North Road forks past Baldock. Take the left at the post -- it's shorter and the turnpike's free for foot traffic."

He nodded once, put on his cap, and went.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and looked at the bowl he had left on the table, wiped nearly clean. He had held the spoon the way a child holds a pencil when first learning to write -- with the whole of his concentration, as though the act itself were something to be got right. He was somebody's son. Somewhere, a woman was waiting for him, or had stopped waiting, and either way the bread and broth I'd given him would not be enough, and I knew it, and I gave it anyway, and that was all I could do.

I washed the bowl and put it back on the shelf.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say, in work. The coach had gone, the passengers with it or settled into their rooms, and the inn entered the long, quiet stretch between the morning rush and the evening trade. Nan polished glasses behind the bar. Jem was in the yard, doing something to a harness that involved a great deal of leather and very little conversation. I did accounts at the kitchen table, reset the rooms that needed resetting, checked the stores for the evening meal, and mended a tear in the curtain of room four that had been bothering me for three days.

Two men in the taproom got loud over something -- I heard the pitch of it change from argument to performance, the particular note that means an audience is being played to rather than a point being made. I went in.

"Gentlemen. You'll settle this outside or you'll settle it quietly, but you will not settle it in my taproom."

They settled it quietly. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and did not need to say anything. I went back to the kitchen.

The light was going by five. The last of the day came in low through the west-facing windows and turned the taproom amber for twenty minutes before it faded. I lit the candles -- Mr. Fenton's candles, at tuppence farthing the dozen -- and served the evening meal, and poured the ale, and collected the money, and wiped the tables, and checked the fires, and did it all again, and again, until the last guest went up and the last glass was washed and the inn began its slow exhalation into silence.

I made the final circuit alone. I always made it alone.

Taproom fire: down to coals, safe behind the grate. I closed the draught. The cellar hatch: locked, the bolt thrown and checked with my hand because I did not trust my eyes at this hour. The stable yard: quiet, the horses settled, Jem's lantern already dark in the room above the tack store. The second floor: I looked up from the yard and saw the lit shape at Bridget's window, the familiar silhouette behind the glass. She was watching. She was always watching. I did not look long.

Back inside, I locked the front door and set the bolt. Then the kitchen door. Then the passage to the yard. My hands at the end of the day were what they always were -- reddened from cold water and work, the knuckles rough, a small cut on my index finger where the edge of the chandler's delivery note had caught me when I'd unfolded it. I had not noticed it at the time. I noticed it now, in the way you notice things about yourself only when you have stopped moving long enough to look.

The inn was locked. The inn was mine. The inn was quiet, and I was alone in it, and for one breath I let myself feel the weight of that -- the specific gravity of a woman standing in her own kitchen at the end of a sixteen-hour day with no one to speak to and nothing left to do and nowhere else she would rather be and no one, not one soul in the world, who would notice if she did not open the door again tomorrow.

I noticed. That would have to be enough.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

It had not been there an hour ago. Nan must have brought it in with the last of the evening's post and left it where I would find it, which was like Nan -- efficient, unobtrusive, and possessed of an instinct for knowing which things should be handed over in person and which should be left to find their own moment.

I knew the seal before I picked it up. Stainforth. The dark wax pressed with his family crest -- a stag's head, pretentious for a man whose grandfather had been a wool merchant -- and beneath it, his handwriting on the direction. Neat, slanted, assured. The handwriting of a man who had never been refused anything he considered reasonable.

I turned the letter over once. I could leave it until morning. I could leave it until morning and sleep without knowing, and the not-knowing would be a kind of mercy, because whatever was inside would still be inside at dawn and I would be better rested to face it.

I broke the seal.

The letter was short. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy had a different quality -- mine came from having too much to do, his from the conviction that the recipient should be grateful for every line.

*Miss Dale,* it began. *I write to inform you that I have received an offer for the property and lease of the White Hound from a party whose means and intentions are, in my judgment, favourable to the long-term interests of the parish. I would be pleased to discuss the terms of a transition at your earliest convenience. I propose a meeting at my offices on the 14th of November. You will understand that the current arrangement, while it has served adequately, cannot be presumed to continue indefinitely.*

The fourteenth of November. Three weeks. He was giving me three weeks to come to his office and be told, in his careful, reasonable voice, that the inn my grandmother had built was being sold out from under me to a man whose means and intentions Stainforth found favourable, which meant a man, full stop, because that was the only qualification Stainforth had ever required.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed my fingertips against the wood, feeling the grain of it, the familiar scratches and stains, the small burn mark near the edge where my mother had once set down a hot pan in a hurry. My kitchen. My table. My inn.

I looked at the letter once more. Then I looked at the empty room -- the banked fire, the clean surfaces, the closed ledger, the quiet -- and I said, to no one and to Phineas Stainforth and to the stag's head on his ridiculous seal:

"You'll need a better offer than that."
