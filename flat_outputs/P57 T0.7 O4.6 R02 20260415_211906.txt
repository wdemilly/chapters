The ink had bled through to the second page of the ledger, which meant I'd pressed too hard on the nib again, which meant the cold had got into my fingers before I'd noticed.

I noticed now. The kitchen fire was still climbing from the coals I'd fed it twenty minutes ago, and the warmth hadn't reached the table yet. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists, tucked my hands under my arms for a ten-count, then went back to the column of figures that had been telling me the same thing for a week.

Six weeks. That was what the numbers said, no matter how I arranged them. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between what the inn would earn and what it owed was the width of a hair. A fair-weather hair, at that -- dependent on full coaches, no breakages, and suppliers who didn't decide November was the month to renegotiate their prices.

I closed the ledger. The leather cover was soft with years of handling, the corners rounded by my mother's thumbs and mine. I set it on the shelf above the range where it lived, spine out, between the salt crock and the tin of beeswax.

The kitchen wanted sweeping, but the kitchen always wanted sweeping. I left it and went through to the taproom.

Cold in here too, and dark. The shutters were still latched. I opened them one by one, letting in the thin grey light of an October morning that had no particular ambitions. The taproom smelled of last night's ale and the ghost of the fire I'd banked at midnight -- woodsmoke and tallow and the faintly sour undertone of a room where men had been sitting too long. I raked out the grate, laid a new fire, and had it drawing before the draught from the chimney could argue with me.

Bar stock next. I counted bottles, checked the ale casks -- the second one was running low and would need replacing by Thursday -- tallied last night's take against what Nan had chalked on the slate behind the bar. The numbers matched. They always matched. Nan did not make errors with money, a quality I valued above almost any other in a human being.

The windows were rimed at their edges, a lace of frost that would melt by midmorning and leave the glass streaked. I'd clean them later. Or Nan would. Or neither of us would, because there were forty things more urgent than window glass, and this was how days at the White Hound worked: you did what mattered first and hoped the rest would forgive you.

I wanted -- just once -- to open the ledger and find a number that meant breathing room. A month where the margin was wide enough that I could mend the dining room ceiling without calculating whether the plaster would outlast my ability to pay for it.

That was the wanting I allowed myself. Modest, practical, aimed at lime plaster rather than the moon. Anything larger than that, I had learned to keep shut in a drawer.

Outside, the yard was quiet. I could see Jem's shape moving in the stable block -- not hurrying, because Jem did not hurry, but working with the steady economy of a man who had been handling horses since before dawn and would continue handling them until the light failed. He had a bridle over his good arm and was adjusting the cheekpiece with his teeth and his one hand, a procedure that looked impossible and was, for Jem, entirely routine. He did not look up. He did not need to. The morning had its order, and we both knew our places in it.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar, ran her hand along the wood to check that it was dry, and began setting out the morning's glasses. No greeting. None needed. We had done this so many times that the silence between us was its own kind of conversation -- comfortable, efficient, and free of anything that required an answer.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual. The London coach was late with the regularity of a church bell that had been hung crooked, and I had stopped expecting it on time somewhere around my twenty-fourth birthday. What was unusual was that when it finally clattered into the yard -- horses blowing, wheels throwing mud -- it carried two passengers instead of four, and both of them looked as though the road had personally offended them.

The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the air of someone dismounting a torture device. The second was a thin man who said nothing and went directly upstairs, which was the best thing anyone had done all morning.

The woman had a trunk. The trunk had a split hinge. She had opinions about this.

"It was perfectly sound when I left Islington," she said, as though Islington were a place where trunks enjoyed constitutional protections.

"Jem," I said. He was already crossing the yard. I didn't need to say more than his name -- he saw the hinge, assessed it, and had the trunk off the ground and moving toward the inn before the woman could produce her next sentence. She watched him go with the startled expression of a person who has prepared a speech and lost her audience.

"Your room is the second on the left at the top of the stairs," I told her. "Breakfast is laid in the dining room until nine."

She went. The coachman did not.

He was a large, wind-chapped man named Burrell whom I'd known for three years. He drove the Tuesday and Friday runs and had a professional grievance about every road in Hertfordshire, which he distributed among the innkeepers on his route like a man paying calls.

"North road's gone to pieces above Hatfield," he said, climbing down from the box. "Ruts deep enough to bury a dog. I lost twenty minutes."

"The parish surveyor was out last week. They've promised gravel before the frost sets."

"They promised gravel in August."

"They did. And I wrote to the turnpike trust about it, and I'll write again. In the meantime, you might try the lower road past the mill -- it adds ten minutes but it won't break an axle."

He grunted. This was Burrell's version of agreement. He took the mug of ale I'd had Nan pour and drank it standing in the yard, and then he was back on the box and gone, because the road would not wait and neither would the passengers he was collecting in Baldock.

The yard cleared. Jem took the trunk inside. The thin man had not reappeared. Nan was wiping down the bar with one eye on the door to the dining room, and I caught the glance -- brief, measuring -- before she turned back to her work. She was watching to see if the stout woman would be trouble. I noted it and said nothing. Nan's instincts about guests were better than mine, and I had learned to let them operate without interference.

The chandler arrived at half past ten, which was early for him and therefore suspicious.

His name was Fenton. He was a neat, dry man with a neat, dry manner and a gift for presenting a price increase as though it were a favour he was doing you. He set his crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table and named his figure.

"Eightpence ha'penny the dozen," he said. "Up from eight."

I picked up one of the candles and turned it in my fingers. Good enough tallow -- not the best, not the worst. The wick was cotton, evenly set. I put it back.

"You were at sevenpence in April," I said.

"Tallow's dear this autumn. The renderers --"

"The renderers are charging you what they charged you in July. I buy from the same market, Mr Fenton. I know what tallow costs."

He adjusted his cuffs. "There are other considerations. Transport. Labour."

"Your cart and your son. Neither of which has changed since April."

A pause. Fenton was not a fool, and he knew I was not a fool, and the negotiation lived in the narrow space between these two facts.

"I could take them to the Bull in Baldock," he said mildly. "They've not complained about the price."

"The Bull buys forty dozen a quarter. I buy sixty. You'd make up the ha'penny and lose the volume."

He looked at me. I looked at him. The kitchen fire cracked, and somewhere in the stable yard a horse stamped.

"Eight," he said.

"Sevenpence three-farthings. Same as last month. And I'll take an extra dozen now against December, paid on delivery."

He considered this. The extra dozen was the concession -- it cost me nothing today and guaranteed him a sale he'd otherwise have to chase. He knew it. I knew he knew it.

"Done," he said, and began unloading.

After he left, I stood in the kitchen with the candles stacked on the shelf and allowed myself one moment of satisfaction -- small, private, the kind that doesn't survive being spoken aloud. I was good at this. The margins were thin and the stakes were real and I was good at this, and some days that was the only solid thing I had to stand on.

The thought came as it always did, uninvited and practical: the lease, the rent, the chandler's price -- all of it mine to manage only so long as I remained unmarried. A wife's property belonged to her husband. The law was not ambiguous on this point, and I had refused two proposals rather than test it. I did not dwell on the refusals. They were made, and they were right, and the inn was still mine.

I went back to work.

He came around the back, as the sign on the gate asked. I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked -- young, thin, standing in the yard with the particular stillness of a man who was deciding whether he'd be welcome. His coat was too large for him, as though he'd lost weight inside it. His boots were coming apart where the sole met the upper, the stitching gone white with strain.

He hadn't knocked yet. He was looking at the kitchen door the way you look at something you want but have been refused before.

I opened it.

He straightened. He was younger than I'd first thought -- twenty, perhaps, or not much more. His face was clean but drawn, the skin tight over the bones in a way that had nothing to do with his age.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He paused, as though the question required consideration. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the range.

He came in and sat where I'd told him. I cut bread -- the end of yesterday's loaf, still good enough -- and ladled broth from the stockpot into a bowl. I set both in front of him with a spoon and went back to the range, because standing over a hungry man while he eats is a cruelty no one thinks to name.

He ate carefully. Not slowly -- he was too hungry for slow -- but with a deliberateness that suggested he'd learned what happened when you ate too fast on an empty stomach.

When the bowl was finished, he set the spoon down and looked at me directly for the first time.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, as though this were insufficient: "My mother's people are in Leicestershire."

I nodded. He stood, and I could see him gathering himself -- the effort of standing up straight when your boots are failing and the road ahead is long and there is no certainty at the end of it.

"The sole on your left boot," I said. "There's a cobbler on the high street. He'll stitch it for sixpence if you tell him Dinah Dale sent you."

He looked at me, and something moved behind his eyes -- not gratitude, exactly, but the thing that comes before gratitude, which is surprise that anyone bothered.

"Thank you, ma'am," he said again, and went.

I closed the door. The bowl was still on the bench, the spoon laid neatly beside it. He'd wiped the bread around the inside to get the last of the broth, and the ceramic was nearly clean.

He was somebody's son, walking north in boots that wouldn't last the week. I knew that, and I felt it -- a sharp, specific ache below my ribs that I did not have time for and could not entirely suppress. I picked up the bowl and put it in the basin to wash, and I went back to the stockpot, and the ache stayed where it was.

The afternoon brought a dispute in the taproom -- two men who'd been sitting over their ale since noon and had found something to disagree about. I heard the voices rising from the kitchen and was through the door before the first chair scraped.

"Gentlemen," I said. "You'll settle it outside or you'll settle your bill. Those are your choices, and I'd like your answer before I finish this sentence."

They settled it by going quiet, which was a third option I was willing to accept. Nan, behind the bar, had not moved -- her cloth was still in her hand, her expression unchanged. She hadn't needed to intervene, and she knew it, and the knowledge sat between us like a shared joke neither of us would bother to tell.

The rest of the afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say it was filled with work that was neither dramatic nor optional. I reset two rooms, checked the kitchen stores against tomorrow's needs, mended a tear in a tablecloth that had been mended twice before and was running out of fabric to give. Nan polished glasses and restocked the bar. Jem was in the yard. The inn breathed in its between-coaches stillness, and I moved through it the way I had moved through it every day for two years, and before that with my mother for twenty-five years, and the rhythm of it was so deep in me that I sometimes forgot it was work at all.

Evening came on early, the way it does in late October -- not a dramatic darkening but a slow withdrawal of light, as though the day were losing interest in itself. I lit the taproom candles -- Mr Fenton's candles, at sevenpence three-farthings the dozen -- and served the evening meals, and tallied the bar with Nan, and banked the dining room fire, and sent Nan home with a nod that meant the same thing it meant every night: well done, and I'll see you tomorrow.

Then I was alone with the closing.

Taproom fire down to coals. I spread the embers and set the screen. Cellar hatch -- locked, the bolt stiff with the cold, requiring both hands and a sharp upward push. The kitchen: range damped, surfaces wiped, the stockpot moved to the cool end of the hob for morning. The back door: bolted. I checked the latch twice, because I always checked the latch twice, because my mother had checked it twice and her mother before her, and some habits are inherited along with the furniture.

I crossed the yard for the last circuit. The stable block was dark and quiet -- Jem would have settled the horses an hour ago. The air smelled of frost and hay and the faint iron tang of the water pump. Above me, on the second floor, a light burned behind Bridget's window. I could see the shape of her -- small, upright, watching the yard from her chair as she did every evening, her white hair catching the candlelight. I did not look long. She was there. She was always there.

The front door. I drew the bolt home, turned the key, and stood for a moment in the passage with my hand still on the iron. My hands were red from the cold water and the day's work, and there was a thin cut on my index finger -- the edge of Fenton's delivery note, sliced across the pad when I'd unfolded it. I hadn't felt it at the time. I felt it now, a small bright sting that had been waiting all day for me to be still enough to notice.

The inn was quiet. The particular quiet of a place that has held noise and people and need for sixteen hours and has finally been allowed to rest. I was alone in it, as I was alone in it every night, and most nights I did not let myself notice this, because noticing served no practical purpose and I had built my life on practical purposes.

Tonight I noticed.

I went back to the kitchen. The letter was on the table.

It had not been there this morning -- or if it had, I'd missed it under the deliveries and the post bag, which was possible but unlike me. The handwriting on the direction was not familiar, but the seal was. I knew that seal. Every leaseholder on this stretch of road knew that seal.

Stainforth.

I picked it up. Turned it over once. The paper was heavy, cream-coloured, the kind that costs more per sheet than Fenton's candles cost per dozen. The seal was dark red, pressed deep and clean.

I broke it.

The letter was brief. Stainforth's hand was small and precise, each word set down with the confidence of a man who did not expect to be questioned.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer of purchase for the freehold of the property known as the White Hound, Great North Road, presently held by you on lease. The prospective buyer intends to undertake a substantial renovation of the premises. I am disposed to accept this offer. As a courtesy, I invite you to meet with me at your earliest convenience, and no later than the 30th of November, to discuss the terms under which the current lease may be concluded. I trust you will appreciate that this matter requires prompt attention.*

I set the letter down on the table. The cut on my finger was bleeding again -- a single bright drop that fell onto the edge of the page and sat there, round and dark against the cream paper.

The thirtieth of November. Five weeks. He was giving me five weeks to lose everything my grandmother had built.

I looked at the letter. I looked at the kitchen -- my kitchen, my mother's kitchen, the room where I had learned to walk and count and pour ale and judge a man's character in thirty seconds and bury my grief under the next task because the next task would not wait.

"Mr Stainforth," I said to the empty room, "you are going to need a better offer."
