The ink had frozen in the ledger's gutter overnight, a thin black skin I had to crack with the edge of my thumbnail before the nib would take.

Six weeks. That was what the numbers gave me when I worked them through--six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between what the White Hound earned and what it owed was the width of a knife blade. I knew this already. I had known it yesterday and the day before. But I opened the ledger each morning the way a surgeon checks a wound: not because you expect improvement, but because you must know exactly how bad it is before you can decide what to do about it.

I closed the book and set it on the shelf above the flour bin, spine out, where I could reach it without thinking. The kitchen was cold. Not the sharp, bracing cold of a clear morning but the damp, sullen kind that crept through the stone flags and settled in your knuckles. The fire I'd built twenty minutes ago was only now beginning to draw properly, the coals clicking and shifting as the draught found its way. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to fill the kettle.

There is a particular pleasure in being the first person awake in a house you know down to its bones. I had walked these rooms since I could carry a tray, and I knew every creak in the staircase, every draught under every door, every tap and rattle the building made when the wind came from the north. The taproom fire I laid and lit without a candle. The bar stock I checked by touch--three casks of ale, one nearly spent; the gin bottle two-thirds full; the brandy low enough to worry about. Last night's accounts I'd tallied before bed, but I ran through them again in my head as I wiped down the counter, because a number you check twice is a number you can trust, and a number you trust only once will betray you by Thursday.

The windows were rimed at their edges, a fine crust of ice that caught the first grey light coming in from the yard. I scraped a clear patch with my fingernail and looked out. The stable yard was still dark, but I could see the shape of Jem moving near the tack room, a lantern set on the mounting block beside him. He was straightening harness on the wall pegs, working with the unhurried precision of a man who had been doing this since before I asked him to and would continue doing it whether I watched or not. His pinned left sleeve swung slightly as he reached. I did not call out. There was nothing to say that the work was not already saying.

I turned back to the kitchen and set the bread on the table for slicing. The loaf was yesterday's, going stiff at the crust. I cut it anyway. Waste was a luxury I could not afford, and stale bread toasted well enough if you had butter, which I did, barely.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins half-set in her reddish hair, apron already tied. She took one look at the kettle, judged it not yet boiling, and went straight through to the taproom without a word. By the time I carried the tea things out, she was behind the bar with a cloth in her hand, wiping down the wood as though she and it had a private understanding. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times. The doing was the conversation.

I heard the coach before I saw it--the particular grinding rhythm of iron-rimmed wheels on a frozen road, slower than it should have been. The London coach was late, which was not unusual. When it finally swung into the yard, steaming and mud-spattered, it was also running short: two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them were already unhappy about something before their boots touched the ground.

The first was a stout woman in a brown travelling cloak who descended with the careful dignity of someone convinced the world owed her a softer landing. She had a trunk, and the trunk had a split hinge, and she had opinions about this that she began sharing with the yard at large before Jem had even reached her side. I intercepted her at the door.

"The hinge can be mended within the hour, ma'am. My man will see to it. If you'll step through to the dining room, there's tea and bread, and I'll have your trunk brought up when it's sound."

She opened her mouth, reconsidered, and closed it. Something in my tone, perhaps, or simply the fact that I had answered the question before she'd finished asking it. She went.

The second passenger followed without complaint--a thin clergyman who wanted only directions to the privy and received them.

The coachman was still on the box, working his fingers inside his gloves. I knew him by sight: Harding, who drove the Tuesday and Friday run. He climbed down stiffly and stood with his hands on his hips, surveying the yard as though it had personally offended him.

"The road above Hatfield's gone to pieces," he said. "Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel in. Took me an extra forty minutes."

"The parish won't mend it until the frost breaks. You'll want to hold to the left verge past the Hatfield turn--the drainage is better on that side and the surface holds."

He squinted at me. "You're certain?"

"I had a carter through here Saturday who'd come down from Welwyn. He said the same stretch, and he said the left side was passable. The right is where the water pools."

Harding grunted, which was his version of thanks, and went to see about his horses. I did not have time to give him more than two minutes, and he did not expect more. That was the economy of the coaching road: information exchanged quickly, between people who could not afford to be wrong.

The yard cleared. The passengers went upstairs or to the dining room. Jem took the trunk. Nan was behind the bar with one eye on the door through all of it, her cloth moving in slow circles over wood that was already clean. I noted it. Said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past ten, as he did on the last Tuesday of every month, with his handcart of tallow candles and his cheerful, lying face.

"Morning, Miss Dale. Lovely morning."

"Mr. Fenton." I did not comment on the morning. It was not lovely. It was October in Hertfordshire, and the sky looked like the underside of a horse blanket. "What's the price this month?"

"Ah." He set the crate on the kitchen table with the care of a man presenting a gift. "Well, now. Tallow's gone up again, I'm afraid. Eightpence the dozen."

"It was sevenpence last month. And sixpence-halfpenny in April."

"The cost of rendering, Miss Dale. The cost of rendering. I don't set the price of fat."

"No, but you do set your margin, Mr. Fenton, and I know what you charge the King's Arms in Hatfield, because Mrs. Brookes and I had a very interesting conversation last week. You're charging her sevenpence. You've been charging her sevenpence since June."

A small silence. Mr. Fenton's cheerfulness did not waver, exactly, but it thinned.

"The King's Arms takes a larger order--"

"The King's Arms takes eight dozen a month. I take six. The difference does not account for a penny a dozen, and we both know it."

"Miss Dale--"

"Sevenpence, Mr. Fenton. Or you may take your candles to the next inn on the road, which is the Red Lion at Baldock, and I wish you joy of Mr. Harker, who will haggle you down to sixpence and make you wait three months for payment. I pay on delivery. I always have."

He looked at me. I looked at him. The kitchen fire popped.

"Sevenpence," he said.

"Thank you. I'll take the full six dozen."

He unloaded the crate without further conversation, counted out his money, touched his hat, and left. The door closed behind him, and I stood for a moment with my hand on the stack of candles, their waxy smell sharp in the cold kitchen. I allowed myself one breath of something that was not quite satisfaction--closer to the grim pleasure of a woman who has just held a line she could not afford to lose.

The thought came as it always did, uninvited, while I was putting the candles away. The ledger, the rent, the chandler's penny--all of it weighed against the same immovable fact. If I married, the inn would pass to my husband under law. The lease, the accounts, the right to stand in my own kitchen and argue a chandler down from eightpence--none of it would be mine to defend. I had refused two proposals already for precisely this reason, and I would refuse twenty more if it came to it. The calculation was not new. I did not dwell on it. I put the candles on the shelf and went to check the dining room.

The afternoon was ordinary in the way that keeps an inn alive: no crisis, no drama, only the steady accumulation of small tasks that, taken together, constitute a day's work. I reset the two upstairs rooms the morning passengers had used, stripped the linens, aired the mattresses as well as the cold would allow. Nan managed the bar through the midday lull, serving bread and cheese to a local farmer who came in every Tuesday and drank one ale with the solemn concentration of a man performing a sacrament. Jem was in the yard, mending a stall latch that had been threatening to give way since last week. I heard the tap of his hammer through the kitchen window--steady, unhurried, certain.

I saw the soldier through the window before he knocked.

He came around the back, as the sign on the gate asked. Young--twenty at most, though the war aged men so that you could never be sure. Thin in the way that meant weeks, not days, of not enough food. His coat was too large for him, as though he had either borrowed it or shrunk inside it. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather separating from the sole on the left foot so that I could see a stripe of dirty stocking beneath.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. He was looking at the door, then at the yard, then at the door again, and I understood what he was doing. He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to ask.

I opened the door before he could decide he wasn't.

He startled, then steadied himself. Up close, his face was wind-chapped and very young. "Beg pardon, ma'am. I saw the sign."

"Have you eaten today?"

A pause. He could have lied. He didn't. "Not since yesterday, ma'am. A bit of bread."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the fire.

He sat. I set bread in front of him, and a bowl of the broth I'd had on the back of the range since morning--nothing fine, just bones and onion and what was left of Sunday's carrots. He picked up the spoon and ate with the careful, measured pace of a man who had learned that eating too fast on an empty stomach would bring it all back up again. He did not speak while he ate, and I did not watch him, because hunger is not a spectacle. I wiped down the table at the far end of the kitchen and let him be.

When the bowl was empty, he set the spoon down and looked at me. "Thank you, ma'am. That was--thank you."

"Where are you headed?"

"North, ma'am. I've people near Biggleswade. I think." The last two words were quieter than the rest.

I nodded. Biggleswade was two days' walk in good weather, longer in this cold, and longer still in boots that were barely holding together. I said nothing about any of that. I wrapped the heel of the bread loaf in a cloth and held it out to him.

He took it. He thanked me again. He went.

I stood at the kitchen door and watched him cross the yard--the careful way he set his feet, favouring the boot that was splitting. He was somebody's son. He was perhaps somebody's brother. He held the bread against his chest with both hands, the way you hold something you are not sure you deserve.

I closed the door and went back to the broth pot, and for a moment my hands were still on the rim of it, and I could not have said what I was feeling except that it sat behind my breastbone like a stone, and then I picked up the ladle and stirred, and the feeling went where I put it, which was away.

The afternoon tipped into evening. Two men in the taproom got loud over something--a disputed card debt, or perhaps just the restlessness of men who had been sitting too long with too little to do. I heard Nan's cloth stop moving behind the bar, which was how I knew it was about to become a problem.

I stepped through the doorway. "Gentlemen. If you'd like to continue your discussion, the yard is available. If you'd like to continue drinking, you'll do it quietly."

They looked at me. They looked at each other. They drank quietly.

The rest of the evening passed as evenings do--the last coach passengers fed and settled, the kitchen put to rights, the fires checked and banked. Nan finished at the bar and hung her apron on the hook by the cellar door. Jem's lantern moved across the stable yard one final time, checking the horses, and then it went out. The inn contracted around me the way it always did at the end of the day, settling into its own particular quiet--not silence, exactly, but the deep, ticking stillness of a building that has been used hard and is resting now.

I made my last circuit alone. Taproom fire down to coals, the grate swept. Cellar hatch locked, the bolt stiff with cold--I had to use both hands. Stable yard quiet through the window; Jem would have settled everything that needed settling. I climbed the stairs to check the second floor. A lit shape at Bridget's window, the faint glow of her candle behind the glass and the outline of her sitting where she always sat, watching the yard. I did not look long. She was there. She was watching. She had been there every night for as long as I could remember, and her constancy was not a thing that required acknowledgment. It simply was.

Back in the kitchen, I bolted the back door. My hands at the end of the day were what they always were: reddened from cold water and the scrubbing of pots, the knuckles rough, a small cut on my right index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught the skin when I unfolded it. I noticed the cut without interest. It would heal, or it would not, and either way there would be another one tomorrow.

I dried my hands on the cloth by the basin and stood for a moment in the kitchen with the fire low and the house locked and no sound anywhere except the building itself, breathing around me. I was alone. I noticed it the way you notice a draught--not with surprise, but with the plain awareness that it was there, that it was cold, and that there was no one to close the gap.

The letter was on the mantelpiece where Nan must have put it when the afternoon post came. I had not seen it until now. The seal was dark red, pressed deep into the wax, and I knew the handwriting on the direction before I turned it over. Stainforth.

I picked it up. I held it. I turned it over once, looking at the seal, the careful copperplate of my name. Then I broke the wax and unfolded the paper.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy had a different quality--the terseness of a man who considers his time more valuable than yours. He had received an offer for the White Hound property from a Mr. Josiah Campion of London, who wished to acquire the lease and premises for the purpose of conversion into a modern hotel suited to the coaching trade. Stainforth considered the offer a strong one. He was prepared to discuss terms with me at my earliest convenience--by which he meant his convenience, which was the fourteenth of November. If I did not present myself at his offices by that date with a compelling reason to maintain the current arrangement, he would proceed with the transfer.

The fourteenth of November. Three weeks.

I read it again. The words did not change. I set the letter down on the kitchen table, pressing it flat with both hands, and looked at it there on the scrubbed wood--this single sheet of paper that proposed to take from me the only thing I had ever had.

Three weeks. A buyer already found. A meeting already set. And Stainforth's particular genius: the letter framed not as a threat but as a courtesy, as though he were doing me the kindness of letting me know that my life was about to be disassembled.

I picked up the letter and folded it once, precisely, and put it in my apron pocket.

"Mr. Campion of London," I said to the empty kitchen, "is going to need a better inn."
