The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumbnail against the smudge as though I could push the number back into legibility. Three pounds, fourteen shillings, and ninepence. That was what stood between the White Hound and the quarter's rent, and the figure had not improved since I had last checked it at midnight, nor at five this morning when I dragged the ledger onto the kitchen table and opened it by the light of a tallow stub.

Six weeks. I had six weeks to close the gap, and the gap did not care that I had been closing gaps like it since I was twenty-five years old.

I shut the ledger and set both palms flat on the table, feeling the grain of the oak against skin that was already rough from the morning's first task. The kitchen fire was built up but not yet generous. I'd used the smaller logs--the ones that caught fast and burned short--because the coal would not last the week if I fed it to every hearth at dawn. The cold sat in the room like a paying guest who refused to leave.

I stood, tucked the ledger onto the shelf behind the flour bin where it lived between consultations, and went to work.

The taproom fire first. I raked the ash from last night, laid the kindling in the pattern my mother had taught me--crosshatch, never parallel, because parallel smothers--and struck the flint until the tinder caught. The room smelled of old ale and cold stone. I opened the shutters on the front windows and the October light came in thin and grey, barely enough to see by, but enough to show me what needed seeing. The rims of the windowpanes were furred with frost. A glass on the second table had not been cleared--Nan must have missed it in the rush last night--and I carried it to the bar and wiped the ring it had left with my sleeve.

Bar stock next. I counted bottles by touch as much as sight: the brandy was down to three fingers in the last bottle, the gin adequate, the ale casks in the cellar still had weight when I'd checked them yesterday. I made a note in my head--brandy before Friday--and moved on to the accounts from last night, which I had left in a neat stack beside the till. Fourteen shillings and twopence. A decent Tuesday. Not enough.

The kitchen again. I checked the bread stores, the butter crock, the state of the ham. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists because the cold had found the gap between cuff and skin, and I stood for a moment in the centre of my own kitchen, in the silence before anyone else was awake, and I wanted--

I wanted one morning when the numbers came out right without my having to make them.

That was all. A small want, and a foolish one, and I set it aside the way I set aside the ledger: on a high shelf, behind something practical.

Out the back door, the stable yard was already occupied. Jem had the big bay's bridle across his knee and was working a stiff buckle with his one hand and the edge of his stump, his canvas coat dark with damp. He did not look up. The tack was straight on its pegs, the water trough filled, the yard swept in the particular pattern that meant Jem had been out here since before I'd lit the kitchen fire. I noted it. Said nothing. Went back inside.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins half-set in her reddish hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word, checked the glasses, adjusted a bottle that was a quarter-inch out of line with the others. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times. The inn woke up around us like a body drawing its first breath, and by the time the light had strengthened enough to read by, the White Hound was ready for whoever the road chose to send.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual--the London coach had been late more often than not since the north road above Hatfield had gone to pieces in the autumn rains--but the delay compressed the morning's schedule in ways that would ripple through the rest of the day. When it finally clattered into the yard, it was carrying two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them were displeased about everything the journey had offered them so far.

The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the air of someone who had been personally wronged by the laws of physics. Her trunk, which Jem was already lifting down, had a split hinge, and she had opinions about this.

"The road did it," she announced to no one in particular. "The road and the driver and the shocking state of this entire county."

"Jem will see to the hinge," I said. "If you'll step inside, there's tea and a fire in the dining room."

She looked at me as though she had not expected to be answered by someone competent, and the surprise reorganised her face into something almost agreeable. She went inside.

Jem appeared at my elbow, took the trunk without comment, and carried it toward the back where he kept his tools. The second passenger had already vanished into the dining room. The coachman climbed down from the box and stood in the yard with his hat pushed back, looking at me with the particular expression of a man who had been driving since before dawn and had not yet been offered anything to drink.

"The road's gone above Hatfield," he said. "Properly gone. There's a rut past the Lemsford turning deep enough to break an axle."

"I know. I sent word to the turnpike trust last week."

"And?"

"And they have received my letter, I am sure, with the same enthusiasm they receive all letters from people who are not their shareholders. I'll write again. In the meantime, if you take the left fork past the toll, the ground is firmer."

He considered this. "Left fork adds ten minutes."

"Left fork keeps your axle. Your choice, Mr Haddon."

He grunted, which was as close to agreement as Mr Haddon ever came, and went inside for his tea. I gave him two minutes because two minutes was what I had, and then the yard was mine again.

The passengers went upstairs. Jem took the trunk. The coach stood empty and steaming in the thin October light, and the yard cleared as though it had never been full. Through the window I could see Nan wiping down the bar with one eye on the door. I noted it. Said nothing. There was dinner to plan and a chandler to manage, and the morning was already half spent.

Mr Fenton arrived at eleven with his cart and his candles and his quarterly adjustment to the price, which he presented as though it were an act of God rather than an act of Mr Fenton.

"Tuppence more the dozen," he said, setting the crate on the kitchen table with the careful placement of a man who wanted me to see how heavy it was. "Tallow's gone up. Nothing I can do about it."

"Tallow has gone up a farthing the pound since July," I said. "I know because I asked Mr Hargreaves at the chandlery in Welwyn what he pays for it."

Mr Fenton's face did something complicated. "Hargreaves doesn't have my costs."

"Hargreaves has your costs exactly, because you and he buy from the same renderer in Hatfield, and he was good enough to tell me so when I asked."

A pause. Mr Fenton was not a bad man. He was a man who had been raising his price by small increments for seven months because he believed I would not notice, or would not challenge him, or would eventually find it easier to pay than to argue. He had been wrong on all three counts, and he knew it, but knowing it and admitting it were different things.

"A penny more the dozen," I said. "Not tuppence. And I'll take four dozen now instead of three, which saves you a delivery next month."

He looked at the crate. He looked at me. He did the arithmetic, which I had already done for him, and arrived at the conclusion I had arranged for him to reach.

"A penny, then," he said.

"A penny. And I'll settle on delivery, as always."

He took his money and his cart and went. The candles stayed. I stood in the kitchen with four dozen tallow candles and the particular satisfaction of a woman who has just paid a fair price for them and not a farthing more. It was a small victory. Most of mine were.

Between the chandler's departure and the next task that required my hands, the thought arrived as it always did--not invited, not welcome, but punctual as a coach on a good road. The ledger, the rent, the chandler's increeping prices: all of it pressed against the same immovable fact. If I married, the lease would pass to my husband. The inn, the income, the forty years of work my grandmother and my mother had built into these walls--all of it would belong to a man the moment I stood before a clergyman and said the words.

I had refused two proposals already for precisely this reason. The thought did not linger. It never did. I wiped the table where the chandler's crate had stood and went to check the dining room.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the signs asked. Young--younger than Jem, younger than me. Thin in the way of men who have been thin for long enough that the body has stopped complaining about it and settled into the shape of its own deprivation. His coat had been a soldier's coat once. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather cracked and separating from the sole in a way that meant he had been walking in them long past the point where walking should have stopped.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at something he is not sure he is allowed to want. He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to ask.

I opened the door before he could decide against it.

He straightened. Up close he was even younger than I'd thought--twenty, perhaps, or a hard nineteen. His eyes were careful.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He considered lying. I watched him consider it, and I watched him decide against it, and the decision cost him something. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth. "I'll bring you something."

He sat. I cut bread, ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since morning, and set it in front of him without ceremony. He ate the way hungry men eat when they are trying not to show it--steadily, carefully, with a control that was more painful to watch than desperation would have been.

When the bowl was empty he set down the spoon and looked at me directly for the first time.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, as though the word had been too large and needed trimming: "Nottingham way. There's family."

I nodded. He stood, and I saw him hesitate again--the same hesitation as before, the calculation of what was owed and what could be offered and whether the distance between the two was survivable. He settled it by touching the brim of his cap, and then he went.

I closed the door. The kitchen was quiet. The bowl sat on the table with the spoon laid neatly beside it, and I looked at it for a moment longer than the task required, because he had held the spoon the way my mother held a pen--carefully, with the grip of someone who had been taught that borrowed things must be returned in the condition they were found.

He was somebody's son, walking north in boots that would not last the week.

I washed the bowl and put it away.

The afternoon brought the usual small fires. Two guests in the taproom had found something to disagree about--the quality of the ale, or the weather, or the essential nature of the universe; it hardly mattered--and the disagreement had reached the pitch where voices carry and chairs begin to shift.

"Gentlemen," I said from the doorway. "The next round is on the house if the volume comes down, and on your own heads if it doesn't."

The volume came down. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and returned to her glasses without comment.

The rest of the afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at a coaching inn between rushes: no coaches, no crises, only the steady accumulation of tasks that kept the place standing. I tallied the midday accounts. I reset the rooms that needed resetting. I checked the kitchen stores against tomorrow's needs and made a list that I would attend to in the morning. Nan polished the bar. Jem was somewhere in the stable yard, doing whatever Jem did in the hours when no horses required his attention, which I suspected was very little different from what he did when they did.

The light failed early, as it had been failing early for weeks now, and by five o'clock the candles were lit and the evening felt like night.

I locked up alone.

The last guests were settled--the stout woman with the split trunk had retired early, and the other had gone out and not returned, which was his business and not mine. The taproom fire was down to coals. I checked it, set the screen, and moved to the bar. Tallied the till. Locked the cellar hatch. Wiped the counter one last time, though Nan had already done it, because the doing was the point, not the need.

The kitchen I closed down in order: banked the fire, covered the bread, checked the locks on the back door. The stable yard was quiet through the window--Jem would have settled the horses and gone to his room above the tack store. I crossed the yard on my final circuit, the cold sharp enough now to make my breath visible, and I looked up at the second floor.

A lit shape at Bridget's window. The outline of her, watching. I did not look long.

Back inside, I stood in the kitchen and let the silence settle. My hands at the end of the day were what they always were: reddened from cold water, roughened from work, and marked--I noticed it now--with a small cut on my index finger where the edge of Mr Fenton's delivery note had caught the skin. I had not felt it happen. I felt it now, a thin sting that was too minor to dress and too present to ignore.

I was alone. I let myself notice it--the specific quality of an inn at rest, the way the silence was not empty but held the shape of everyone who had passed through it during the day, and the way none of them were here now, and the way that fact sat in my chest like a stone I had swallowed so many times I knew its exact weight.

The letter was on the table.

It had not been there this morning. Someone--Nan, most likely--had placed it beside the flour bin where the ledger lived, and I had not seen it until now because I had not been looking for it, because I was not expecting it, because no one wrote to me.

I picked it up. The seal was dark red, pressed deep into the wax with the particular force of a man who wanted his correspondence to arrive looking official. The handwriting on the direction was small, precise, and familiar. Mr Phineas Stainforth. I turned the envelope over once. The paper was heavy, cream-coloured, the kind that cost more per sheet than I spent on candles in a week.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth wrote the way he spoke--measured, civil, and arranged so that the blade was halfway in before you felt the edge.

*Miss Dale, I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable interest regarding the property and lease of the White Hound inn. The prospective party wishes to take possession at the earliest opportunity. I am prepared to discuss terms with you at your convenience, though I must be frank in stating that the matter requires resolution before the quarter. I trust you will see the wisdom of meeting promptly so that we may reach an arrangement satisfactory to all parties.*

Before the quarter. Six weeks. The same six weeks I had been counting in the ledger this morning, only now they were not merely the distance between me and the rent. They were the distance between me and the loss of everything my grandmother had built, my mother had held, and I had kept standing with my own two hands.

I set the letter down on the table. I looked at it. I looked at the kitchen--my kitchen, my mother's kitchen, my grandmother's kitchen--and I felt the cut on my finger sting, and I felt the cold in the room, and I felt the precise weight of that stone in my chest shift from something I carried to something I could throw.

"You'll have your meeting, Mr Stainforth," I said to the empty room. "And I'll have my terms."
