The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the ledger page as though I could push the numbers back into order by force of will.

Six weeks. That was what the figures gave me when I tallied them honestly, which I always did, because the numbers did not care whether I found them agreeable. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between what the inn held in ready money and what it owed was the width of a knife blade. I closed the ledger and set my hands flat on the kitchen table, feeling the grain of the oak against my palms.

The kitchen was cold. The fire I'd built twenty minutes ago had not yet taken proper hold, and the windows were rimed at their edges with the lace-fine frost that October brings to Hertfordshire when it means business. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and stood.

There is a particular kind of want that comes to you at five o'clock on a dark morning when no one else is awake and the day has not yet made its demands. It is not dramatic. It does not announce itself with trumpets. It is only the quiet thought -- so brief you could miss it between one breath and the next -- that it would be good, just once, to walk into a warm room instead of being the one who makes it warm.

I did not dwell on it. I never did. There was the taproom fire to lay, and the bar stock to check against last night's tally, and a patch of damp on the ceiling above the third window that I had been watching for a fortnight and that would need a man on a ladder before it became a man on a ladder and a plasterer and a bill I could not pay. I moved through the rooms in the order my mother had taught me, and her mother before her: kitchen, taproom, dining room, cellar hatch, front door. Everything accounted for. Nothing wasted.

The taproom smelled of last night's fire and spilled ale. I knelt at the grate and laid the kindling in the pattern that catches fastest -- a trick my mother learned from a coachman in 1794 and never forgot. The floorboards wanted sweeping. The settle nearest the window had a loose leg I'd been meaning to have seen to. I noted these things the way I noted everything: precisely, without sentiment, and with the understanding that noting them and fixing them were separated by the distance between what I had and what they cost.

By the time I'd finished the circuit and returned to the kitchen, the fire there had found its courage. I held my hands over it for a moment longer than was strictly efficient, and then I set the kettle on.

---

Jem was in the stable yard when I checked out the back, which was no surprise. He was straightening tack on the hooks outside the first stall, his one hand moving with the practiced economy of a man who had long since stopped thinking about what he'd lost and started thinking about what he could do with what remained. He did not look up. He did not need to. We had done this before, he and I -- hundreds of mornings, the same unspoken agreement that the work was the greeting and the greeting was the work.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word, ran her cloth along the counter once to test for the sticky residue of last night's porter, and began setting out the glasses. She moved fast, as she always did, as though the hours were a finite resource she intended to spend wisely.

I did not remark on their punctuality. I did not need to. The efficiency was the thing itself.

---

The London coach was late, which surprised no one, and short, which surprised me. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them unhappy about it in the way that only people who have spent six hours on a bad road in a cold coach can be unhappy -- thoroughly, vocally, and with a conviction that someone ought to be held responsible.

The first was a woman of perhaps fifty with a trunk whose hinge had split clean through, spilling the corner of a petticoat onto the yard like a white flag of surrender. She had opinions about this. She had opinions about the road, the weather, the coach springs, and the general decline of English transport since the death of the old king. I directed her to the dining room with the kind of brisk warmth that does not invite further conversation, and I sent Jem -- who had appeared at my elbow without being called, as was his habit when luggage was involved -- to deal with the trunk.

The coachman was a different matter. He was a big man named Horrocks whom I'd known for three years, and he wanted a word about the state of the north road above Hatfield.

"Ruts past the Lemsford turning are two feet deep in places," he said, climbing down from the box with the stiffness of a man who has been holding reins since before dawn. "Axle nearly went at the bridge."

"The parish was meant to have it gravelled last month."

"Parish hasn't done a damned thing. Begging your pardon."

"I'll write to the surveyor again. In the meantime, take the lower road past the mill -- it adds ten minutes but it won't cost you an axle."

He considered this, then nodded. "Aye. That'll do."

"There's coffee in the kitchen if you've time."

He did not have time. He never did. He touched his hat and climbed back up, and the coach pulled out of the yard with the particular groan of wood and iron that meant it would need new springs before Christmas. I watched it go and thought, briefly, about what new springs cost, and then I stopped thinking about it because it was not my coach and I had enough things that were mine to worry about.

The yard cleared. The woman with the ruined trunk had been settled in the dining room by Nan, who had emerged from behind the bar with one eye on the door, as she always did when voices carried. I noted this. Said nothing.

---

Mr Fenton, the chandler, arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his particular way of smiling that meant he was about to try something.

"Morning, Miss Dale." He set the first crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table with a thump that suggested they weighed more than they did. "Fourteen dozen, as agreed. Though I'm afraid the price has gone up a halfpenny per dozen since last month."

I did not look up from the crate. I was counting. "That's the fourth increase in seven months, Mr Fenton."

"Tallow's dear this autumn. The renderers in Barnet have raised their --"

"The renderers in Barnet are charging you tuppence ha'penny the pound, same as they charged you in March. I know because Mrs Alcott at the Red Lion told me what she pays you, and Mrs Alcott has no reason to lie to me about candles."

A pause. Mr Fenton's smile did not waver, but it thinned.

"I've costs of my own, Miss Dale. The cart alone --"

"Your cart makes this road every month regardless, because you supply four establishments between Hatfield and Baldock, and I am the second stop, not a detour. The halfpenny is not your costs, Mr Fenton. It is your hope that I am too busy to check."

He looked at me. I looked at him. The candles sat between us on the table, smelling of rendered fat and negotiation.

"The old price," I said. "And I'll take sixteen dozen instead of fourteen, which gives you a larger order to show for the trip."

He worked the arithmetic behind his eyes. I could see the moment he arrived at the answer, because his smile changed from the one that meant he was trying something to the one that meant he'd been caught at it and had decided to be gracious about the loss.

"Sixteen dozen at the old price," he said. "You'll be the death of me, Miss Dale."

"I sincerely doubt it. You may leave them by the cellar door."

He left. The candles stayed.

I permitted myself one moment -- standing alone in the kitchen with the smell of tallow and the sound of his cart wheels on the yard stones -- to feel the small, private satisfaction of a thing done well. It was not triumph. Triumph was for people who could afford to lose. It was only the knowledge that I knew my margins, and I knew his, and today that had been enough.

---

The thought came, as it always did, somewhere in the ordinary work of midday -- between the chandler's departure and the next task, between one set of numbers and the next. The ledger, the rent, the candle price, the damp patch on the ceiling: all of it added against the same immovable fact. If I married, none of it would be mine to fight for. The lease, the income, the business my grandmother had built with her own hands -- all of it would pass to a husband the moment I spoke the words.

I had refused two proposals on the strength of that calculation. I did not regret either refusal. I did not think about the men who had made them, because they were not the point. The point was the inn, and the inn was mine, and I intended to keep it.

I picked up the cloth and wiped down the bar where Nan had missed a spot, and I moved on.

---

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He was young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way that means weeks, not days, without enough food. His coat had been a soldier's coat once, though the regiment's marks had been taken off or worn away. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the left sole separating from the upper so that I could see a stripe of grey stocking beneath.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at a thing he wants but is not certain he is permitted to want. He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to ask.

I opened the door before he had to.

He startled, then steadied himself. Up close he was even thinner than he'd looked through the glass, and his eyes had the flat, careful blankness of a young man who has learned not to expect much.

"Have you eaten today?" I asked.

He met my eyes. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He understood. He sat without argument, setting his pack on the floor beside him with the care of a man whose possessions are few enough to be precious. I cut bread, ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since morning, and set both in front of him. He looked at the food for a moment before he picked up the spoon, and in that moment I saw something I recognised -- not gratitude, which is what people expect to see, but the brief, private struggle of a person deciding whether it is safe to want the thing being offered.

He ate. Not fast, not slow. Steadily, the way a man eats who knows that steadiness is what keeps you from being sick when your stomach has been empty too long.

When the bowl was finished he set the spoon down and looked at me directly. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm obliged."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, after a pause, as though the word needed clarifying: "Home. If it's still there."

He picked up his pack and went. I did not offer him money, because money would have changed the nature of the thing, and I did not offer him work, because I had none to give. I stood in the kitchen doorway and watched him cross the yard and turn onto the road, and I thought about his boots -- the left one, gaping at the welt -- and the way he had held the spoon, lightly, as though even a spoon could be taken away if you gripped it too hard.

He was somebody's son. That was all. I went back inside and cleared the bowl.

---

The trouble in the taproom was nothing -- two men who'd been sitting too long over too little ale, disagreeing about the outcome of a horse race neither of them had seen.

"Gentlemen," I said from the doorway. "You'll settle your accounts or you'll settle your voices. I don't mind which, but you'll do one of them now."

They did both. Nan watched from behind the bar and did not need to move.

---

The afternoon passed in the way that afternoons at a coaching inn pass when the coach has gone and the next one is not expected until morning: slowly, usefully, without event. I reset the rooms that needed resetting. I checked the kitchen stores against the evening's requirements. I tallied the accounts a second time, hoping the numbers would be different, and they were not, because numbers have no interest in hope. Nan wiped the bar. Jem was outside. The inn breathed around us in its midafternoon quiet, and we worked inside it the way you work inside any living thing -- attending to what it needs, not what you wish it needed.

---

Evening came down early, the way it does in late October, and I moved through the closing routine alone.

The last guests were settled. The taproom fire had burned down to coals that ticked and shifted in the grate. I checked the cellar hatch -- locked. The stable yard was quiet; Jem would have settled the horses and gone to his room above the tack store. I bolted the front door and stood for a moment in the darkened taproom, listening to the particular silence of a building that has been used hard all day and is now, finally, resting.

Upstairs, on my way back through, I passed the second-floor landing and glanced out the window that overlooked the yard. A lit shape behind Bridget's window -- the faint glow of a candle and the outline of a figure sitting upright in a chair, watching the yard below. I did not look long. I had seen it too many times for it to require acknowledgment. She was there. She was watching. That was all.

My hands, when I looked at them in the kitchen lamplight, were reddened from cold water and the day's work. There was a small cut on my index finger -- the edge of the chandler's delivery note, which had been sharp enough to draw blood without my noticing until now. I pressed my thumb against it. It stung, which seemed about right. A whole day's labour, and the only wound was a paper cut from a bill I'd already won.

I set the bolt on the kitchen door. I banked the fire. I stood in the kitchen that had been my mother's kitchen and her mother's before that, and I let myself notice -- just for a moment, the way you let yourself notice a draught you cannot afford to fix -- that I was alone.

Then I saw the letter.

It was on the table beside the ledger, where Nan must have placed it during the afternoon when I was upstairs. I had walked past it twice without seeing it, which told me something about the state of my attention and nothing good. The seal was dark red, pressed deep into the wax with a signet I knew. The handwriting on the direction was precise, slanting, and familiar.

Mr Stainforth.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth's letters were always brief; he was not a man who wasted words when the words he had were sufficient to do damage.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable interest for the property and grounds currently held under lease by yourself, including the premises known as the White Hound. The prospective purchaser wishes to take possession at the earliest opportunity. I am prepared to discuss terms for the surrender of your lease at a meeting to be arranged at your convenience, provided such convenience falls within twenty-one days of the date of this letter. I trust you will see the wisdom of an orderly resolution. Yours, &c., P. Stainforth.*

I read it twice. The second time was not because I had failed to understand it the first time. The second time was because I wanted to be certain -- perfectly, coldly certain -- of what he was asking me to do.

He was asking me to give up the inn. Not in those words, because Stainforth never used the words that meant what he meant. *Surrender of your lease.* *Orderly resolution.* As though I were a problem to be tidied away, a loose thread to be snipped, an irregularity in his accounts that could be corrected with a meeting and a signature and twenty-one days' notice.

Twenty-one days.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed both hands flat against the oak, the way I had done that morning with the ledger, and I felt the grain of the wood against my palms -- solid, familiar, mine.

I looked at the empty kitchen. The banked fire. The clean hearth. The row of copper pots my grandmother had bought in 1775, still hanging in the order she had chosen for them.

"Twenty-one days," I said aloud, to no one, to the room, to the inn itself. "Then you had better come and take it from me, Mr Stainforth, because I am not walking out that door."
