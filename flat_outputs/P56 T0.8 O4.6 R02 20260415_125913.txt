The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the smudge before it could ruin the whole page.

Six weeks. That was what the numbers said when I added them honestly, which was the only way I knew how to add them, much as I sometimes wished otherwise. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the ledger would not be flattered into telling me anything different. I closed the book and set my palm flat on its cover, as though I might keep the figures from escaping into the cold kitchen and making themselves at home there.

The fire wanted building up. I had laid it an hour ago, but the October damp had crept in overnight and the first flames had done little more than take the edge off. I knelt on the stone floor and fed the coals until the heat began to push back against my hands. Then I stood, wiped my palms on my apron, and went to do the rest.

The taproom fire next. The grate had been swept last night but the ash pan needed emptying, and I carried it out through the back passage and tipped it into the bin by the stable wall. The air outside was sharp enough to make my eyes water. Frost had stitched itself along the edges of the kitchen windows when I came back through, and I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists before I started on the bar stock.

Ale: three casks remaining, one nearly kicked. Brandy: sufficient for the week if nobody ordered doubles, which somebody always did. Candles in the taproom sconces: four had burned to nothing and would need replacing. I noted this without writing it down. The numbers lived in my head as readily as they lived in the ledger, and they were rather better behaved there.

The last of the night's accounts I tallied standing up, the coins sorted into their columns on the bar. Tuppence short. I counted again. Still tuppence short. I would find it later, or I would not, and either way the morning would not wait for me to crawl under tables looking for pennies.

I wanted -- and this was a foolish thing to want at half past five on a Tuesday morning, with no one awake to hear it -- I wanted someone to tell me the numbers would come right. Not to fix them. Just to say it, once, so that I might set the worry down for the time it took to drink a cup of tea. But there was no one to say it, and the tea would not make itself, and so I put the kettle on.

---

Jem was in the stable yard when I checked out the back, which was where Jem always was at this hour, which was why I checked. He had a bridle over the stall rail and was working at the buckle with his one hand and the practiced use of the hook he'd rigged to the post for exactly this purpose. He did not look up. I did not expect him to. We had done this before. Several hundred times, at rough estimate, and the efficiency of the arrangement was that neither of us needed to say good morning for it to be one.

Nan arrived through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar and began laying out the tankards without a word, checking the arrangement against some private standard that I had never needed to articulate because she already knew it. She had known it since she was fourteen, when I had put a cloth in her hand and told her where the glasses went, and she had not once put them anywhere else.

---

The London coach was late by twenty minutes, which I noted the way one notes that the sun has risen in the east -- accurately, and without surprise. When it did arrive, the sound of it preceded the sight: iron-rimmed wheels on the gravel, the driver's shout, the particular groan of a coach that has been running hard since Barnet.

Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them unhappy about it. The first was a woman of perhaps fifty with a trunk that had split at the hinge and a conviction that this was somehow the fault of every person within speaking distance. The second was a curate who said nothing and looked as though he regretted having a body that required transport.

I directed Jem to the trunk before the woman could finish describing its injuries. He appeared at the side of the coach without being called -- he had a gift for this, for materializing at the precise moment a situation required a man who would not speak and could lift things -- and bore the damaged luggage toward the inn with the split hinge clamped under his arm as neatly as if he'd been born with one hand and designed trunks to match.

The woman watched him go and found herself with no one left to shout at but me. She opened her mouth. I said, "Room six, first floor, second on the left. Breakfast is laid in the dining room until nine. The trunk will be there before you are." She closed her mouth. She went.

The coachman wanted a word, and I gave him two minutes because that was what I had.

"The road north of Hatfield is breaking up past the toll," he said, climbing down stiffly. "Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel in. Somebody ought to tell the turnpike trust."

"I told them in August. They sent a man who looked at it and went away again."

"Well, he didn't fix it."

"No," I said. "He did not. I'll write again. In the meantime, keep to the left past the second milestone -- the camber's better on that side."

He gave me the look men give when a woman tells them something useful about a road, which is to say he looked as though he were deciding whether to be grateful or offended. He settled on a nod, took his ale, and went.

The yard cleared. Jem took the horses. The curate drifted toward the dining room like a man who had given up on earthly comforts but would not say no to toast. Nan stood behind the bar with a cloth in her hand and one eye on the door, watching the last of the morning's disruption settle into order. I saw her watching. I said nothing. There was nothing to say.

---

Mr. Hadwell, the chandler, arrived at half past ten with his cart and his prices and the particular expression of a man who believes he is about to get the better of a woman.

He was wrong about this, but I let him begin.

"Tallow's gone up again, Miss Dale." He set the crate of candles on the kitchen table with a thump that was meant to convey the gravity of the situation. "Threepence ha'penny the dozen, I'm afraid. Can't be helped."

"It was tuppence ha'penny in March," I said.

"Aye, and tallow was cheaper in March."

"Tallow is fourpence a pound at Hitchin market. It was fourpence a pound in March as well. I know because I asked."

A small silence. Mr. Hadwell adjusted his hat.

"There's the cartage to consider," he tried.

"Your cart makes this road every month regardless. You deliver to the Woolpack and the Red Lion on the same route. The cartage is the same whether you charge me tuppence ha'penny or threepence ha'penny. The only difference is the ha'penny in your pocket."

"Now, Miss Dale--"

"Threepence the dozen," I said. "Which is still more than you're charging the Woolpack, and we both know it, but I won't begrudge you the difference if you'll stop raising the price every time you think I'm not paying attention."

He looked at me. I looked at him. The candles sat between us on the table, smelling of mutton fat and commerce.

"Threepence," he said.

"Thank you, Mr. Hadwell. Nan will see you out."

He went. The candles stayed. And I allowed myself, in the quiet after the door closed, one moment of private satisfaction -- not smugness, nothing so grand, but the small, dry pleasure of having done a thing well and known that I was doing it well while I did it. It was the closest I came to vanity most days, and I did not begrudge myself the indulgence.

---

I was stacking the candles in the store cupboard when the thought arrived, as it always did, without invitation and without apology. The ledger. The rent. The chandler's threepence instead of tuppence ha'penny, and the six weeks until the quarter came due, and all of it -- every shilling I scraped and saved and fought for -- balanced against the single fact that none of it would be mine if I married. The lease was in my name. A husband's name would replace it, and the law would call this natural, and the inn my grandmother had built would belong to a man who had done nothing to earn it but stand in a church and say two words. I had refused two proposals on this arithmetic alone, and I did not regret either refusal, and I did not think about them for longer than it took to shelve the last candle and close the cupboard door.

---

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the sign on the gate asked, and he stood outside the door with his arms at his sides, not quite ready to raise his hand. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way of men who have been thin for long enough that their clothes have learned a new shape around them. His coat had been military once. His boots were coming apart where the sole met the upper, the stitching gone at the welt, and I could see the pale line of his stocking through the gap.

He was not threatening. He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to knock.

I opened the door before he could finish deciding.

He looked up. His eyes were pale and surprised, as though he had not expected the door to open from the other side.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

He considered lying. I could see him consider it -- the small flicker of pride that crosses a man's face when he is about to say he is fine. Then he said, "Not since yesterday morning, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He understood. He sat. I cut bread and ladled broth from the pot that had been on since dawn, and I set it in front of him without ceremony, because ceremony was not what a hungry man needed. He needed bread and broth, and I had both, and the calculation was that simple.

He ate carefully, not fast. He held the spoon the way my mother used to say you could tell a man's history -- close to the bowl, with his thumb along the handle, as though someone had once taught him table manners and he had not entirely forgotten them.

When he finished, he set the spoon down and said, "Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. "There's a cousin in Nottingham. Or there was."

He did not say anything about the war, and I did not ask. He stood, and I saw the way he put his weight on the table edge to push himself up, as though his legs were not entirely reliable, and then he straightened and touched his forehead and went out through the kitchen door into the yard.

I stood at the window and watched him go. He walked with the particular care of a man who knows his boots will not survive another week. He was somebody's son. He was perhaps somebody's brother. He had held the spoon the way a boy holds a spoon when his mother is watching, and his mother was not watching now, and I did not know if she was alive to watch, and the not knowing sat in my chest like a stone for a moment before I turned back to the kitchen and put his bowl in the basin to wash.

---

The trouble in the taproom was nothing -- two men who had been sitting too long over too little ale, disagreeing about the mail schedule with a vehemence that suggested the mail schedule was not the real subject.

"Gentlemen," I said from the doorway. "The next coach is at four. If you'd prefer to catch it, I suggest you settle your bill now."

They settled. Nan watched from behind the bar with her cloth in her hand and the expression of a woman who had been prepared to intervene and was mildly disappointed that she did not need to.

---

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say, it passed in work. The coach came and went. Guests settled or departed. I reset the rooms that needed resetting, checked the kitchen stores against the evening's requirements, and tallied the afternoon's receipts while Nan wiped down the bar for the third time and Jem moved between the stable yard and the tack room with the unhurried regularity of a clock.

There was no incident, no crisis, no drama. There was only the work, and the work was enough to fill the hours without leaving room for anything else, which was, if I am honest, partly the purpose.

---

Evening came the way it always did -- not all at once, but in stages, each one requiring something of me. The last guests settled. The last fire checked, the coals raked and the screen set. The bar tallied, the coins counted and locked in the box. The kitchen closed down: pots scoured, surfaces wiped, the morning's bread dough set to prove under a cloth by the hearth.

I moved through the inn's closing routine alone. I had done this hundreds of times -- enough that my feet knew the path without consulting my mind, enough that my hands reached for bolts and latches in the dark with the certainty of long practice. The taproom fire was down to coals, giving off the low orange light that made the room look almost handsome. Cellar hatch locked. The stable yard quiet through the back window -- Jem would have settled the horses and gone to his room above the tack store. The second floor: a lit shape behind Bridget's window, the familiar silhouette of her sitting in her chair. I did not look long.

My hands at the end of the day were what they always were: reddened from cold water, roughened from work, and tonight marked with a small cut on the index finger where the edge of Hadwell's delivery note had caught me when I wasn't paying attention. I noticed this without interest. It would heal. Everything healed, or it didn't, and either way the morning would come.

I set the last bolt on the front door. The inn was quiet in the way of a place that has been used hard and is now resting -- not peaceful, exactly, but emptied, the noise of the day still faintly present in the walls like heat in a brick after the fire goes out.

I was alone. I noticed this the way I noticed it every night -- briefly, clearly, the way you notice a draught from a window that will not quite close. Then I went back to the kitchen.

---

The letter was on the table where Nan must have left it with the afternoon's post, half-hidden under the evening's accounts. I had not seen it earlier, or I had seen it and set it aside, which amounted to the same thing.

I saw it now. The seal was Stainforth's -- his family crest pressed into dark red wax, the kind of seal a man uses when he wants you to know exactly who is writing before you open the envelope. The handwriting on the direction was his clerk's, precise and impersonal. Miss D. Dale, The White Hound, Great North Road, Hertfordshire.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The wax was thick and neatly stamped. I broke it.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though he wasted them on different things.

He had received an offer from a Mr. Eustace Gull -- his nephew's man of business, though the letter did not say so, because Stainforth preferred to present his nephew's ambitions as though they had nothing to do with him -- for the purchase of the remaining term on the White Hound's lease. The sum named was not insulting. It was worse than insulting: it was precise, and it was low enough to tell me exactly how much Stainforth thought the inn was worth in a woman's hands. He proposed a meeting at his house on the fourteenth of November to discuss terms. He expressed his hope that I would see the wisdom of the arrangement. He closed with his regards to my late mother's memory, which was the kind of thing a man writes when he wants you to know he considers you already finished.

I set the letter down on the kitchen table, in the circle of light from the single candle I had not yet snuffed.

The fourteenth of November. Three weeks. He had given me three weeks to agree to lose everything.

I looked at the letter on the table, at Stainforth's clerk's tidy hand and Stainforth's heavy seal, and I said aloud to the empty kitchen:

"You will need a better offer than that, Mr. Stainforth, because I am not selling you my inn."
