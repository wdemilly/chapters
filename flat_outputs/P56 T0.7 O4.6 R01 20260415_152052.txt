The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the page to stop it spreading before I remembered that blotting paper existed for precisely this purpose and my thumb did not.

Half past five by the kitchen clock, and the numbers in the ledger were the same ones I had tallied at midnight. Forty-three pounds, seven shillings, and ninepence stood between me and the quarter's rent. Six weeks to find the difference. I could do it if the coaches ran full and the weather held and nothing broke that cost more than a shilling to mend -- three conditions that had never, in the recorded history of the White Hound, aligned at the same time.

I closed the ledger and set my hands flat on the table. The kitchen was cold. The fire I'd built twenty minutes ago was still finding its courage, the coals ticking and shifting beneath the first true flames. Outside, the yard was dark and perfectly still, and the windows had that fine white rime at their edges that meant the night had been harder than I'd expected. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and went to work.

The kitchen fire first, fed and coaxed until it would hold. Then the taproom, where last night's ashes needed clearing before the grate could be relit. I knelt on the hearthstones and raked them out, the ash fine enough to ghost up into my hair and settle on my lips. The taproom smelled of old ale and cold stone and the particular staleness that accumulated overnight when the windows stayed shut. I opened one, just a crack, and the October air bit through.

Bar stock next. I counted bottles by touch in the dim light -- gin, brandy, the rough cider that the coach drivers preferred. Three bottles of claret left from a case that should have lasted the month. Someone had been generous with the measures, and I had a reasonable suspicion who. I would speak to Nan.

I checked the cellar hatch, tested the lock, and climbed back up to tally the previous night's accounts against the cashbox. The figures matched. They always matched, because I was the one who kept them, and I did not make errors with money. With other things, perhaps. With money, never.

By the time I'd finished, the kitchen fire had steadied into something useful, and the first grey light was pressing at the windows. I set the kettle on the range and stood with my back to the warmth, letting it soak through the wool of my dress. There was a moment -- just one, small and foolish -- when I let myself want something other than the numbers to come right. Something I could not have named if pressed, only that the kitchen felt very large for one person, and the morning very quiet, and I was twenty-seven years old and tired in a way that sleep did not touch.

Then the kettle hissed, and I had work to do.

Jem was already in the stable yard when I looked out the back door. He had the big bay's harness across the mounting rail and was working at a stiff buckle with his one hand and the hook he'd rigged to his belt, his breath clouding in the cold air. He did not look up. He did not need to. He knew I checked, and I knew he'd be there, and neither of us required the other to say so.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes after, pins half-set in her reddish hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word and began wiping down the counter with the cloth she kept folded in her apron pocket. I heard the soft, rhythmic sound of it from the kitchen -- back and forth, back and forth -- and felt the inn settle into its working shape around me, the way a body settles when it draws its first proper breath of the day.

The London coach was late. This was not unusual -- the London coach was late with the reliable frequency of a church bell rung by a drunk sexton -- but it was also running short. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them had opinions.

The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who had a trunk with a split hinge and a conviction that this was somehow my fault. I directed her to the dining room, directed Jem to the trunk, and directed my attention to the coachman, who had climbed down from the box with the particular heaviness of a man who has been arguing with the road for thirty miles.

"North road's gone to pieces above Hatfield," he said, stamping mud from his boots on my clean step. "Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel. Someone needs to speak to the turnpike trust."

"Someone has. Twice this month."

"And?"

"And the trust has a meeting in November, at which point the road will be frozen and the question academic. You'll want to take the lower fork past the mill if it's rained. The ruts are shallower."

He considered this. "The lower fork adds twenty minutes."

"The lower fork adds twenty minutes. A broken axle adds two days. I can offer you coffee before you go, but I cannot offer you a better answer than that."

He took the coffee.

The yard cleared. The stout woman was settled in the dining room with toast and the particular silence of a person who has run out of complaints and is recalibrating. Jem had the trunk and was carrying it upstairs with an ease that made the split hinge look like a personal failing on the trunk's part rather than a structural one. Nan stood behind the bar with her cloth folded over one arm, watching the door the way she always watched it -- not anxious, not expectant, just ready. I noted it. Said nothing.

Mr Fenton, the chandler, arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his newest price.

"Tuppence ha'penny the dozen," he said, setting the first crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table as though he were presenting me with a gift.

I did not pick up a candle. I did not need to. I had been buying Mr Fenton's candles for three years, and they had not improved.

"They were tuppence last month, Mr Fenton."

"Tallow's gone up. The renderers in Barnet have raised their price, and I'm obliged to pass the cost along."

"The renderers in Barnet raised their price in June. You passed the cost along in July. And again in September. That is a great deal of passing for a single increase."

He shifted his weight. "There are other considerations. Transport. Labour."

"Your transport is the same cart and the same horse you have driven for six years, and your labour is yourself. Unless you have recently hired an assistant, in which case I congratulate you, but I do not intend to pay his wages."

Mr Fenton's mouth opened, then closed. He was not a dishonest man. He was an optimistic one, which in a chandler amounted to much the same thing.

"Tuppence farthing," he said.

"Tuppence. Which is what I paid last month and what every inn between here and Baldock pays, as you know perfectly well, because Mrs Hewitt at the George told me her price when I saw her at the Stevenage market."

A silence. Mr Fenton looked at his candles. His candles did not look back.

"Tuppence," he said.

"Thank you, Mr Fenton. I'll take four dozen."

He unloaded the candles, took his money, and left without the pleasantries that sometimes padded the end of our transactions. I did not mind. The door closed behind him, and I allowed myself one small, private moment of satisfaction -- the kind that comes from knowing exactly what a thing costs and refusing to pay a farthing more. It was not a grand victory. But the inn was built on small ones, and I had learned to take them where I found them.

I carried the candles to the storeroom and came back to the kitchen, where the ledger sat on the table like a conscience. Forty-three pounds. Six weeks. Every tuppence mattered, and every tuppence I saved on candles was a tuppence I did not have to find elsewhere -- and all of it added against the fact that sat beneath every calculation I made, quiet and immovable as bedrock. If I married, the lease would pass to my husband. The inn, the income, the business my grandmother had built and my mother had held -- all of it would belong to a man the moment I spoke two words in a church.

I had refused two proposals on the strength of that arithmetic, and I did not regret either one.

I wiped the table, hung the cloth, and went to check on the dining room.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or younger, with the thin, unfinished look of a boy who had stopped growing when the food ran out. His coat was too large for him, army-issue by the cut, and his boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather separating from the sole in a way that meant he had been walking a long time on wet roads. He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand raised but not yet touching the wood, and I recognised the pause. He was not deciding whether to knock. He was deciding whether he was welcome enough to try.

I opened the door before he could finish the calculation.

He looked at me with the startled expression of a man caught between hope and the expectation of refusal. Up close, he was thinner than I'd thought. His cheekbones stood out sharp beneath skin that had gone sallow, and his eyes had that flat, careful quality I had learned to recognise in the men who came through -- not empty, exactly, but guarded against something I could not see.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He hesitated. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the range. "I'll bring you something."

He sat. He did not argue or thank me or attempt to explain himself, which told me he had done this before and knew the terms. I cut bread from the loaf, ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since morning, and set both in front of him. He ate the way hungry men eat -- quickly, with his head down, one arm curled around the bowl as though someone might take it from him.

I busied myself at the range and did not watch him. When the bowl was empty, he set down the spoon and sat very still.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

He named a direction -- north, toward the midlands -- in a voice that suggested the destination mattered less than the motion.

I nodded. He stood, touched his forehead, and went out through the kitchen door into the yard. I watched him cross to the road and turn north, his broken boots dark against the pale mud, and I stood there longer than I needed to. He was somebody's son. He held the spoon the way a child holds it, with his whole fist, as though he had forgotten there was any other way.

I turned back to the kitchen and wiped the place where he had sat.

The afternoon brought the usual small frictions. Two men in the taproom had been sitting too long over their ale and had found something to disagree about -- the price of barley, or the state of the turnpike, or whatever thin excuse men used when what they really wanted was to raise their voices at someone.

"Gentlemen." I did not raise mine. "If you wish to continue this conversation, you may do so in the yard. If you wish to continue drinking, you will do so quietly."

They continued drinking. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and went back to her work.

The hours between the afternoon coach and the evening trade were the inn's dead time -- the lull when the building exhaled and the work became invisible. Nan polished glasses. Jem was outside, doing whatever Jem did when the yard was empty, which generally involved improving something no one had asked him to improve. I reset the two upstairs rooms that had been used, checked the linen, reviewed the kitchen stores for the evening meal, and wrote out the next day's orders in the small, tight hand my mother had taught me, because paper cost money and margins were not for wasting.

The work was ordinary. It was also everything. It was the thing that kept the doors open and the fires lit and the coaches stopping, and if it was not enough -- if it was never quite enough -- it was mine, and I did it well, and on most days that was sufficient.

Evening came down early, the way it did in late October, the light draining from the sky as though someone had pulled a stopper. The last guests were settled by eight. I tallied the bar with Nan, checked the kitchen stores against tomorrow's needs, and sent her home. The dining room was cleared, the fires checked, the cellar hatch locked.

I made my final circuit alone. Taproom fire down to coals, the grate ticking as the metal cooled. Front door bolted. The stable yard was quiet, and through the dark I could see the faint shape of the stable block where Jem would have settled the horses for the night. I looked up. On the second floor, a lit shape at Bridget's window -- her silhouette behind the glass, watching the yard as she always watched it, steady and unsleeping. I did not look long.

Back in the kitchen, I banked the range and washed my hands in the basin by the door. The cold water stung, and I looked down at them -- reddened across the knuckles, rough at the fingertips, and on my right index finger a thin cut from the edge of Mr Fenton's delivery note, which I had not noticed until now. A small injury. The kind that accumulated without announcement and healed without attention and marked the difference between a woman who worked and a woman who did not.

I dried my hands on the cloth and stood in the kitchen doorway. The inn was quiet in the way of a place that has been used hard and is now resting -- not peaceful, exactly, but spent. The bolt was set. The doors were locked. And I was alone, which was not the same thing as lonely except on the nights when it was, and tonight it was, and I let myself know it for the space of a single breath before I turned back to the table.

The letter was where Nan had left the afternoon post, half-hidden beneath a bill from the farrier. I had seen it earlier and set it aside, because I knew the handwriting on the direction and I knew the seal, and there are some things you do not open until the day's work is done and your hands are steady.

Stainforth's seal. Stainforth's hand. I picked the letter up and turned it over once. The paper was heavy, cream-coloured, the kind that announced its cost before you read a word. I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Mr Stainforth wrote the way he spoke -- measured, courteous, and perfectly clear.

He had received an offer for the White Hound property from a Mr Josiah Campion of London, who wished to acquire the lease and convert the premises into a posting house of a more modern character. Mr Stainforth considered the offer a sound one. He was prepared to discuss terms with me, as the current leaseholder, as a matter of courtesy, but he wished me to understand that the decision was ultimately his to make. He proposed a meeting at his house on the fourteenth of November. If I did not attend, he would take my absence as consent to proceed.

Six weeks. The same six weeks I had counted in the ledger this morning, the same margin I had been calculating and defending and scraping together tuppence by tuppence -- and none of it mattered, because Stainforth was not raising the rent. He was taking the ground out from under it.

I set the letter down on the kitchen table. I looked at it for a long moment, at the careful handwriting and the courteous phrasing and the word *consent*, which was doing a great deal of work in that final sentence and doing none of it honestly.

"The fourteenth, then," I said to the empty room. "I'll be there early."
