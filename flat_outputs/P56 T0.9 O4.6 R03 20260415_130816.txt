The ink had bled through to the next page again, which meant the nib wanted replacing, which meant another tuppence I could not spare. I pressed the blotter to the ledger and stared at the figures until they stopped swimming. Forty-three days to quarter-rent. The number had been fifty-one when I last allowed myself to count, and the intervening week had not produced a miracle. I closed the book.

The kitchen was cold enough that my breath showed when I turned from the table, and the fire I'd laid twenty minutes ago had not yet decided whether it meant to catch or merely smoulder. I knelt and blew into the grate until the kindling took, then fed it two careful splits of ash--not three, because the woodpile was another number I preferred not to examine too closely. The flames climbed. I held my hands to them longer than I should have, because no one was awake to see me do it, and because there is a particular kind of want that only visits before dawn: not for anything so grand as rescue, but for a morning that starts warm.

I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and got on with it.

The taproom fire came next. I raked out last night's ash, rebuilt the grate, and set the draught so it would draw slowly and be ready when the first coach passengers wanted something hot. The bar stock was as I'd left it--two barrels of ale, one of small beer, the gin bottle three fingers lower than it ought to have been, which meant one of last night's guests had helped himself after I'd gone up, or Nan had poured a measure she hadn't recorded. I made a note. The accounts from the previous evening were on the shelf behind the bar where I always left them. I checked the total against what was in the cashbox. It matched to the ha'penny. It always matched. My mother had taught me to count before she taught me to read, and I had never seen the purpose of doing either imprecisely.

The windows along the front were rimed with frost at their edges, and through the wavery glass the yard lay empty and grey. October had turned bitter early this year. I'd heard a drover say last week that there'd been snow in the north already, which was wrong for the season, and that the harvest had been thin from Lancashire to Kent. I did not need a drover to tell me what flour cost. I knew what flour cost the way I knew my own name--by handling it daily and watching it change.

I wiped down the bar, set the chairs straight, and checked the hinge on the dining-room door that had been sticking since Tuesday. Still sticking. I added it to the list I kept in my head, which was longer than the one I kept on paper, because paper cost money and my memory was free.

Out the back, the stable yard was already occupied. Jem had the big bay's harness across the half-door and was working saddle soap into the leather with his one hand, steady as a clockmaker. He did not look up. He would have been out here since before I came down--he always was--and he would not consider this remarkable. The yard was swept. The water trough was full. The muck heap had been forked to the far wall. None of this had been asked for. It was simply what the morning required, and Jem was the sort of man who saw what was required and did it, and if you thanked him he looked at you as though you'd said something faintly ridiculous.

I went back inside. Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word and began checking the glasses. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times. The rhythm of it was so familiar it had ceased to be a routine and become something closer to a language--one in which neither of us needed to speak.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual. What was unusual was that when it finally clattered into the yard--forty minutes past its time, the horses blowing hard and the coachman's face the colour of old brick--it carried only two passengers instead of the expected four. The first was a clergyman who descended with the careful dignity of a man pretending he had not been rattled half to death. The second was a woman of perhaps fifty, travelling alone, who stood in the yard and stared at her trunk with the expression of someone composing a letter of complaint in her head.

The trunk's hinge was split clean through. The lid gaped like a broken jaw.

"That," the woman said to no one in particular, "was not like that when I left Islington."

I was already crossing the yard. "Jem," I said, and he appeared at my shoulder without my needing to say what for. He took the trunk's weight from the bottom and carried it toward the door as though it were a hatbox. The woman watched his single arm manage the operation and opened her mouth, then closed it, then followed him inside.

The coachman caught me before I could go after them. He was a big man named Harker whom I'd known for three years and liked well enough when he was not in a temper.

"Road's gone above Hatfield," he said, climbing down. "Ruts deep enough to lose a wheel. Someone's got to tell the turnpike trust before they kill a horse."

"I'll write to the surveyor this afternoon. You'll want the water trough--Jem's filled it."

"It's not a matter for writing, Miss Dale. It wants filling and grading before the frost sets in proper."

"Then I'll write today and they'll ignore it next week, and we'll both have done what we can. Go and water your horses, Mr Harker."

He went, grumbling, because there was nothing else to do and he knew it as well as I did. I watched him lead the team across to the trough, then turned and went inside to settle the woman with the broken trunk, which I did by the simple method of showing her to a room, sending Nan up with tea, and promising that Jem would see to the hinge before she left. She began to say something about the coaching company's liability. I told her the dining room served breakfast until half past nine and closed the door.

The yard cleared. The coach stood empty. Jem was already at the trunk with a length of wire and a look of professional concentration. Nan, back behind the bar, wiped down the counter with one eye on the front door, the way she always did when the morning rush had been thinner than expected. I noticed it. I said nothing. There was nothing to say. Thin coaches meant thin custom, and we both knew the arithmetic.

The chandler arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his fourth price increase in seven months.

"Tallow's gone up," he said, setting the crate of candles on the kitchen table as though this settled the matter. "Fifteen pence the dozen."

"It was twelve in April, Mr Haddon."

"And tallow was cheaper in April."

"Tallow is ninepence a stone at Hitchin market. I was there last month." I had not been there last month. Nan had been there last month and reported the figure, but Mr Haddon did not need to know the source. "You're rendering at home, which saves you the chandler's yard fee, and you sell to every inn and public house between Hatfield and Baldock, which gives you volume enough to keep your margin at threepence a dozen even at the old price."

He stared at me. He had small, sharp eyes and the permanently aggrieved expression of a man who believed the world owed him an extra penny on every transaction.

"Thirteen," he said.

"Twelve and a half. Same as last month. And I'll take an extra two dozen at that price, which saves you a stop at the Red Lion, where Mrs Fenton will argue with you for an hour and buy six."

A pause. He did the arithmetic behind those sharp little eyes. I could see the moment it resolved.

"Twelve and a half," he said. "But I'll want paying today, not end of month."

"You'll have it before you leave the yard."

He left. The candles stayed. I counted them--three dozen, uniform and straight, smelling of rendered fat and economy--and stacked them in the store cupboard where they belonged. When the door had closed behind him and his cart had rattled out of the yard, I permitted myself one small, private breath of satisfaction. Not triumph. Satisfaction. I was good at this. It was the thing I was best at, and there were days when being good at it was the only pleasure available to me, and I would not apologise for taking it.

I stood in the kitchen with my hand still on the cupboard latch and let the thought arrive, because it always arrived at moments like this, when I had just proved I could manage and the proof reminded me of what management cost. The ledger, the rent, the chandler's price--all of it held together by the fact that the lease was in my name and no one else's, and every decision I made about this place was mine to make. If I married, the lease would pass to my husband. The candles, the accounts, the taproom, the stabling--all of it would belong to a man by operation of law, regardless of whether he knew a tallow candle from a church taper. Two men had asked me, and I had refused them both, and this was why. I did not dwell on it. I had done the calculation before and the answer had not changed. I moved on.

The afternoon brought the usual work and none of the usual custom. I reset two rooms, checked the cellar stores, and spent twenty minutes on the kitchen accounts while Nan managed the bar and Jem reshod a horse that had thrown its near fore. The coaching traffic had been thin all week. I did not allow myself to count the days to quarter-rent again, because counting it twice in one day was the kind of indulgence that led to panic, and panic was a luxury I could afford even less than candles.

I was scrubbing the kitchen table when I saw him through the window.

He came around the back, as the sign on the gate asked. Young--twenty at the outside, though his face had the hollowed look that made age difficult. Thin. His coat hung on him as though it had been made for someone else, or for a version of himself that had eaten regularly. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather cracked and separating from the sole in a way that told me he had walked a considerable distance in them and would not walk much further.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. I watched him through the glass. He was looking at the door, then at the yard, then at the door again, with the particular stillness of a man who was not sure whether he was welcome enough to knock. He was not threatening. He was deciding.

I opened the door before he could finish deciding.

He straightened. Up close he was even thinner than he'd looked through the window, and his eyes had a guarded quality I had seen before--not hostility, but the careful blankness of someone who has learned that hope is expensive.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He considered this. "Not recently, ma'am."

I stepped back from the door. "Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there."

He came in and sat where I pointed--the bench by the hearth--without argument or the performance of gratitude that some men offered in place of honesty. I cut bread, ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since morning, and set both in front of him. He ate steadily, not fast, with the deliberate concentration of someone who knew that bolting food on an empty stomach would bring it back up again. He held the spoon the way a man holds a tool he hasn't used in some time--carefully, as though relearning the weight of it.

I wiped down the far end of the table and let him eat.

When the bread was gone and the bowl nearly empty, he looked up. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm obliged."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, after a pause that held more in it than the word: "Home, I think."

He left through the back as he'd come. I stood at the door and watched him cross the yard and go out through the gate, his broken boots careful on the cobbles. He was somebody's son. I knew that without sentiment--it was simply a fact, as plain as the bread I'd given him and the broth that had cost me nothing because it was made from yesterday's bones. He was somebody's son, and he was walking north in boots that would not last the week, and I could not mend that. I could feed him once and send him on. I turned back to the kitchen and picked up the bowl and the spoon and washed them.

The taproom altercation, when it came, was so minor it barely deserved the name. Two men who'd been sitting over their ale since midday, arguing about a coach fare or a card debt or something equally worth fighting over, had reached the stage where their voices carried to the kitchen.

I walked in. "Gentlemen. Not in my house."

They looked at me, looked at each other, and subsided. Nan, behind the bar, had not moved. She had not needed to.

The afternoon stretched and emptied. The coach had gone, the guests had settled or departed, and the inn entered that long, quiet interval between the midday rush and the evening trade. Nan polished glasses. Jem was somewhere in the yard. I worked through the evening's kitchen preparation--pies to assemble, the joint to season, bread to check--and then returned to the accounts, because the accounts were always there and they did not improve for being ignored.

Evening came on slowly, the way it does in late October--not a dramatic darkening but a gradual withdrawal of light, as though the day were simply losing interest. I served the evening meal, settled two new guests who arrived by private carriage, poured ale until the taproom thinned, and tallied the bar with Nan before she went up. Jem had banked the stable-yard fire and gone to his room above the tack store. The inn was mine.

I made my last circuit alone. Taproom fire down to coals--safe. Cellar hatch locked. Dining-room door latched, the hinge still sticking. I added it to tomorrow's list. The stable yard was quiet; Jem's lamp was out. Above the yard, on the second floor, a faint light showed behind Bridget's window--the shape of her there, watching, as she watched every night. I did not look long. I had seen it too many times for it to need acknowledgment. She was there. She was watching.

I came back inside and locked the kitchen door and shot the bolt. My hands, when I looked at them in the lamplight, were red from cold water and rough with the day's work, and there was a thin cut on my index finger--the edge of the chandler's delivery note, I supposed, though I could not remember the moment it had happened. It stung faintly. I flexed the finger and thought, with the dry inward honesty that only comes at the end of a sixteen-hour day, that I had done well and that the house was sound and that I was alone in it.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

It had not been there at supper--or if it had, I had not seen it, which amounted to the same failure of attention. Someone had left it, or it had come with the afternoon post and Nan had set it down and forgotten to mention it, or I had walked past it three times without registering what it was. None of these explanations mattered. What mattered was the seal.

I knew the seal. Heavy, dark red, pressed with the Stainforth crest--a stag's head above a bar sinister--that I had seen on lease documents and legal correspondence for as long as I had handled the inn's papers. I picked the letter up. It was heavier than a single sheet. I turned it over once.

I broke the seal.

The hand was not Stainforth's own--it was too neat, too measured; a clerk's hand, or a solicitor's--but the signature at the bottom was his, and the substance was clear enough that I did not need to read it twice, though I read it twice regardless, because the first time through, my heartbeat was louder than the words.

He had a buyer for the property. A Mr Gregson, of London, who wished to acquire the freehold and the coaching licence together. Stainforth was prepared to offer me first refusal on new terms--a meeting at his house, a week from Thursday, to discuss the continuation of the lease at a revised and significantly increased rent, or, failing agreement, the termination of the existing arrangement at the next quarter day. Forty-three days. He had put it very politely. He had even expressed his respect for my mother's memory in the second paragraph, which was the kind of courtesy that functions as a blade.

I set the letter down on the table. I looked at it there, beside the lamp, beside the ledger, beside the remains of the day's work. The kitchen was silent. The fire ticked in the grate. The bolt I had just set on the door gleamed dully in the light.

I said, to the empty room, to no one, to the quiet and the dark and the forty-three days:

"Mr Stainforth, you are going to need a better offer than that."
