The ink had bled through to the second page of the ledger again, and I pressed my thumb against the stain as though I might push the number back to where it belonged. Forty-three pounds, seven shillings, and ninepence. That was what stood between the White Hound and the next quarter's rent, due the sixth of December. Six weeks. The money would come or it would not, and no amount of staring at the figures would change them, but I stared anyway, because the kitchen was cold and the fire was still finding its courage and there was no one awake to see me worry.

I closed the ledger.

The kitchen hearth took another few minutes of coaxing. The wood was damp from the store and the draught wanted adjusting, and I knelt on the flagstones and blew until the kindling caught properly and my knees ached from the cold. Then the taproom fire, which I'd banked the night before and which had survived well enough. I raked the coals forward, added two splits of ash, and left the door of the stove cracked to draw.

Bar stock next. I counted bottles by touch in the half-dark: gin, brandy, the last of the October ale. Three casks broached, one nearly spent. I noted it in my head the way my mother had taught me, running the inventory like a prayer, each item in its place. The rum was low. The rum was always low. I would speak to Hapwell on Friday if he had any at a price I could stomach, and if he did not, we would do without, and the coaches heading north would simply have to drink ale like honest men.

The windows were rimed at the edges, the glass sweating where the warmth of the taproom met the cold outside. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and tucked the cuffs into my palms. October had teeth this year. The almanac predicted hard frost before November, and the almanac was, for once, not lying.

I wanted a cup of tea. I wanted ten minutes sitting down with it, and no one needing anything, and the particular silence of a house that has not yet remembered it has work to do.

I put the kettle on instead.

By the time I'd tallied last night's accounts against the cash drawer and found the expected discrepancy of tuppence, which lived somewhere between Nan's generous pouring hand and my own failure to notice it, the sky through the kitchen window had gone from black to the colour of old pewter. I wiped my hands on my apron. The tuppence could wait. Everything else could not.

I checked the back door on my way through. The bolt was stiff with cold and I shouldered it loose, stepping out into the yard to look at the morning.

Jem was already in the stable yard.

He had his back to me, working over the tack rail with his one hand and the practised pressure of the stump beneath his pinned sleeve, straightening a bridle that had been hung crooked. He did not look up. He would not have expected me to greet him, any more than I would have expected him to explain what he was doing. The horses were his. The yard was his. We had established this two years ago without discussion, and the arrangement held because neither of us was fool enough to talk about it.

I went back inside.

Nan arrived through the kitchen door eight minutes later, pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word, running her cloth along the wood in the long practised stroke that meant she was awake but did not wish to prove it through conversation. I heard her yawn once, softly, behind her hand. Then the cloth resumed its circuit.

We had done this before. Many times.

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual. The London coach was late more often than it was on time, owing to the state of the road above Hatfield, which had been promised repair by the turnpike trust since August and received nothing but more rain. But the coach was also short. Two passengers instead of the expected four. Both in foul temper. The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who had a trunk with a split hinge and a voice that carried across the yard like a church bell.

I directed Jem to the trunk with a look. He was already moving.

The woman turned her displeasure on me. I settled her with a room number, a promise of hot water within the quarter hour, and a tone that did not invite further discussion. She went. The second passenger, a thin man with a cough, wanted only breakfast and directions to Baldock. Nan had him seated before he could finish asking.

The coachman found me in the yard. He was a big man named Prescott whom I had known for years, and he had the particular expression of a man who has been arguing with a road and lost.

"The stretch past Hatfield is a disgrace, Miss Dale. Sunk a wheel to the nave this morning."

"I'll write to the trust again."

"You've written three times."

"Then they shall have a fourth letter, and it shall be very firm indeed. Was there damage?"

He shook his head. "Lost an hour digging free, is all. But I'll not run this route past November if it's not mended. The mail won't stand for it either."

"I'll write today," I said, and meant it, though I knew perfectly well what the trust would do with my letter, which was precisely what they had done with the other three. Prescott knew it too. He touched his hat and climbed back up. The coach pulled away, and the yard was quiet again.

Nan had been wiping down the bar with one eye on the door through all of this. I noted it. Said nothing.

The chandler came at half past ten.

Mr Fenton was a lean, careful man who delivered tallow candles on the last Tuesday of each month and who had, over the course of seven months, adjusted his price upward four times. He set the crate on the kitchen table with the reverence of a man presenting crown jewels and named his figure.

"Seven and six the dozen," he said.

"It was seven shillings last month, Mr Fenton."

"Tallow's gone up."

"Tallow has gone up a halfpenny the pound since March. You are charging me a full sixpence more per dozen than you charge the Red Lion, and a shilling more than the Woolpack, and I know this because Mrs Carswell at the Woolpack has no gift for discretion."

He blinked.

"Seven shillings," I said. "As last month. And I'll take four dozen rather than three, which saves you a second delivery and the cost of your horse's feed for the trip."

"Seven and threepence."

"Seven. And I pay on the day, Mr Fenton, which is more than the Red Lion can say. You told me yourself their account is sixty days in arrears."

A silence. He looked at the crate. He looked at me. He knew his margins. I knew his margins. The difference was that I did not pretend otherwise.

"Seven shillings," he said.

"Thank you. Nan will show you out."

The candles stayed. The chandler went. I allowed myself, in the quiet after the door closed, one breath of satisfaction. It was a small victory and would not survive the next quarter's rent, but I had won it cleanly and I knew what it was worth.

The ledger was still on the table. The figures had not improved since dawn, and they would not improve by staring, but they pressed at me all the same. Forty-three pounds. Six weeks. Every shilling I saved on candles and coal and bread was a shilling between the White Hound and the possibility that I might lose it.

If I married, of course, the arithmetic would change. A husband's income, a husband's name on the lease, a husband's right to every decision I had made alone for two years. I had refused two proposals already, both from decent men who would have kept me comfortable and taken the inn out of my hands without understanding what they'd done. I did not regret refusing. I regretted that refusing was necessary.

The thought lasted four sentences in my head, as it always did, and then I stood up and went to check the stockpot.

I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked.

Young. Thin. A coat that had once been army-issue and was now merely inadequate. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather cracked and separating from the sole on the left foot. He stood outside the kitchen door with his hand raised and did not immediately bring it down against the wood. He was looking at the door the way a man looks at a thing he is not certain he is permitted to touch.

I opened it before he could decide.

He was perhaps twenty, though hunger aged him. His eyes were large and wary. He held his cap in both hands.

"Have you eaten today?" I said.

"Not as such, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there."

He sat. I cut bread, ladled broth from the pot, and set them in front of him without ceremony. He ate the way men eat who are not sure when they will eat again: steadily, without looking up, one hand curved around the bowl as though someone might take it. I did not watch him. I went back to the stockpot and skimmed it and added an onion that wanted using, and I did not watch him, and I thought about his boots.

When the bowl was empty he looked up.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North. My sister's in Nottingham."

I nodded. He stood, replaced his cap, and went out the way he'd come, through the kitchen door and around the back of the yard, where the sign directed vagrants. The door closed behind him. I stood at the stove with the ladle in my hand and let myself feel it for a moment: the particular ache of feeding a boy who was someone's son, someone's brother, and who had been given a war and then given nothing else. I set the ladle down. The stockpot needed salt.

The afternoon brought the small, predictable friction of men with too much time and too little purpose. Two guests in the taproom, commercial travellers both, had found something to dispute. I could hear the pitch rising from the kitchen.

I walked in. They looked up.

"Gentlemen. If you wish to continue this discussion, you may do so in the yard, where I charge nothing for the privilege. In my taproom, I charge for ale and I expect quiet."

They went quiet. Nan, behind the bar, had not moved.

The rest of the afternoon was the ordinary grind. Coach gone, guests settled. Nan restocked the bar. Jem reshod a horse that had thrown a shoe on the Hatfield road. I turned over the two upstairs rooms, beat the dust from the curtains, checked the mattresses for damp, replaced a candlestick that had lost its base. Downstairs, I reviewed the kitchen stores for the evening meal and found we were shorter on flour than I'd thought. I made a note. I made many notes. The notes were the architecture of the days, and I laid them down one after another like bricks, and the wall held.

Evening came the way it always came: slowly, then all at once, the light dropping out of the sky as though someone had pulled a stopper. The last guests were settled by eight. The last fire was checked by nine. Nan finished her tally and left it on the bar for me to review in the morning, and I heard her footsteps on the stairs, and then the particular silence of a house that has been used hard and is resting.

I made my circuit.

Taproom fire down to coals. Good. Cellar hatch locked, the padlock cold under my fingers. Stable yard quiet through the window, Jem's lantern out. He would have settled the horses an hour ago. The back door bolted, the front door bolted, the kitchen banked and swept. I climbed the stairs to check the second floor. A lit shape at Bridget's window, the faint amber glow behind the glass. She was sitting up. She was always sitting up. I did not look long.

Back in the kitchen, I washed my hands in the basin and felt the sting of cold water on skin that had been scrubbed and wetted and dried too many times in one day. My fingers were red. There was a thin cut on my right index finger from the edge of Fenton's delivery note, a paper cut so small I had not felt it happen and only noticed now because the water found it. I dried my hands on the cloth and stood in the kitchen, which was warm and dark and mine, and I was alone in it.

I was alone in it. I noticed this the way I noticed it every night, briefly, like a draught from under a door, and then I turned to put the cloth away.

The letter was on the mantelpiece.

I had not seen it arrive. Nan must have taken it from the afternoon post and set it there, knowing I would find it on my last pass through the kitchen, as I always did. The seal was dark red, pressed with an impression I recognized. Stainforth. The handwriting on the direction was precise, slanted, unhurried. Miss D. Dale, The White Hound, Great North Road, Hertfordshire.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth wrote the way he spoke: measured, deliberate, every sentence shaped to sound like reason.

He had received an offer of purchase for the White Hound's lease from a Mr Edward Stainforth, his nephew, who intended to redevelop the property as a posting house serving a better class of clientele. He wished to advise me, as a courtesy and in recognition of my family's long tenancy, that he would be reviewing the terms of the lease at the next quarter day, the sixth of December. He invited me to call upon him at his convenience, but no later than the fourteenth of November, to discuss the future of the arrangement. He expressed his confidence that I would see the wisdom of a negotiated transition and remained, et cetera, my obedient servant.

Six weeks. The same six weeks I had counted in the ledger this morning, only now the number meant something different. It was not a question of whether I could pay the rent. It was a question of whether paying it would matter.

I set the letter on the table. I smoothed it flat with both hands, the paper cut stinging under the pressure.

"The fourteenth of November, Mr Stainforth," I said to the empty kitchen. "I'll be there at nine o'clock, and I'll bring the ledger."
