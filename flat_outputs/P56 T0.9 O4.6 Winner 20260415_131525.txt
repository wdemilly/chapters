The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the page to stop it spreading before the figure underneath became unreadable.

Six weeks. That was what the ledger gave me, if I was careful. Six weeks before the quarter's rent came due and the numbers stopped being a problem I could manage and became one I could not. I closed the book and set my palm flat on the cover, as though the gesture might hold the accounts in place by force of will.

The kitchen was cold. Not the killing cold of deep winter, but the raw, damp chill of late October that crept in under the door and settled in the flagstones and stayed. I had built the fire up twenty minutes ago, and the room was only now beginning to lose its edge. The windows were rimed at their corners with the thin, feathery frost that meant the temperature had dropped overnight. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and tucked the cuffs into my palms.

There was a time -- a brief, stupid time, in the weeks after my mother died -- when I had thought about what it might be like to simply not open the ledger one morning. To leave the numbers unread, the accounts untallied, and walk out the front door into a life that did not require me to know, down to the last farthing, exactly how close to ruin I stood. I had entertained the fantasy for perhaps nine seconds before I put the kettle on and got back to work.

That was two years ago. I had not entertained it since.

I crossed to the taproom and knelt at the hearth. The ashes from last night were still warm enough to take the kindling without coaxing, and I had the fire drawing well before the cold in the stone floor worked through my skirts to my knees. I checked the bar stock by touch and by count: three casks of ale, one nearly spent; the gin bottle down by a third, which meant someone had been generous with their measure last night, and I would have a word with Nan about it. The night's accounts were where I had left them on the bar top, weighted down with a pewter tankard. I ran through the figures standing up, one hand braced against the wood. They tallied. They always tallied. I did not make errors with money, because I could not afford to.

The taproom smelled of last night's smoke and old ale and the beeswax I had rubbed into the bar top a week ago, already wearing thin under the elbows of men who leaned too hard. The table nearest the door had a loose joint that would need attention before it gave way under someone's dinner. The window on the north wall had a draught I could feel from four feet away, which meant the putty had cracked again. I noted both. Added them to the list I kept in my head, the one that never grew shorter no matter how many items I struck from it.

I wanted -- just once -- to walk through a room in this inn and find nothing that needed mending.

I went back through the kitchen and out the rear door into the stable yard. The air hit my face like a slap, and I tucked my chin into my collar and crossed the yard quickly.

Jem was already there. He had the tack down from its hooks and laid across the partition rail, and he was working saddle soap into a bridle with his one hand, his stump braced against the leather to hold it steady. He did not look up. He did not need to. He knew my step as well as I knew the sound of the inn settling at night, and neither of us required a greeting to confirm what was already understood.

I checked the stable: horses watered, stalls mucked, the bay in the third box favouring her off fore again. I would need to have the farrier out. Another line in the ledger, another figure to find.

When I came back through the kitchen door, Nan was there. Pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied, her hands busy with the kettle before I could tell her it wanted filling. She took her place behind the bar without a word, and I heard her cloth moving across the wood in the steady rhythm that meant she was already thinking about the day's work and not about whatever had kept her up reading by candlelight past midnight. I had seen the stub of the tallow on her nightstand. I said nothing.

We had done this before. Many times.

The London coach was late. This was not unusual -- the London coach was late four days in five, and on the fifth it was early enough to catch the yard unprepared, which was worse -- but today it was late and short. Two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them cross about it.

The first was a woman of perhaps fifty with a trunk that had split its hinge on the road and opinions about this that she wished to share at some length. I directed her to the dining room, directed Jem to the trunk -- he had materialised in the yard without being called, the way he always did when a coach pulled in -- and told the woman that the hinge could be mended by noon if she would leave the trunk in the passage. She opened her mouth to object. I was already turning away.

The coachman caught my arm. Not roughly -- he was not that sort -- but with the grip of a man who had been sitting on a box for five hours and had no other person to talk to about what was bothering him.

"Road's gone to pieces above Hatfield," he said. "Ruts deep enough to break an axle. Someone ought to tell the turnpike trust."

"Someone has," I said. "Twice. They've promised repairs before the frost sets in."

He snorted. "They promised that last year."

"They did. And the frost came first. I suggest you keep to the left verge past the Hatfield toll -- the ground's firmer there, and the ruts are shallower." I took my arm back. "Will you want dinner, or are you pressing on?"

He pressed on. They usually did. The coachman's world was the road, and the road did not wait for him to finish his beef.

The passengers went upstairs. Jem took the trunk. The yard cleared, and the particular quiet that followed a coach's departure settled over the inn like dust after a cart has passed. Through the doorway I could see Nan behind the bar, cloth in hand, one eye on the front door. She had been watching the whole time. I noted it. Said nothing.

Mr Fenton, the chandler, arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his newly adjusted price.

"Tenpence ha'penny the dozen," he said, setting the first crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table with the care of a man presenting crown jewels.

I did not pick up a candle. I did not need to inspect them. I knew what he sold and what it was worth.

"You charged me ninepence three months ago, Mr Fenton."

"Tallow's gone up."

"Tallow has gone up a farthing the pound since June. Your price has gone up three ha'pence the dozen in the same period. That is not the same arithmetic."

He shifted his weight. He was a large man, red-faced, not unkind, but possessed of the unshakeable conviction that a woman with a ledger was a woman who could be outmanoeuvred. He had been labouring under this conviction for seven months, and it had cost him nothing so far only because I had allowed the first two increases and contested the third.

"I've got costs, Miss Dale. The cart, the wick cotton, the--"

"You pay fourpence ha'penny a pound for your tallow from Jessop's in Welwyn. I know because Mrs Jessop told me when I asked, which I did, because I do not enjoy being overcharged. You sell to the Red Lion at ninepence the dozen, which I also know, because their cook and I have a professional acquaintance. Ninepence, Mr Fenton."

Silence. He looked at the crate. He looked at me. He performed the brief, unhappy calculation of a man who has just discovered that the woman with the ledger has also been talking to the woman at the tallow works.

"Nine and a half," he said.

"Ninepence. And I'll take an extra dozen this month, which you have on the cart and would otherwise haul back to Welwyn unsold."

He took ninepence. He left the candles. He said good morning with the stiff politeness of a man who has been bested and cannot quite locate the moment it happened, and I closed the kitchen door behind him and stood there for a moment with my hand on the latch.

I was good at this. I had always been good at this -- the margins, the arithmetic, the particular satisfaction of knowing exactly what a thing cost and refusing to pay more. My mother had taught me, and her mother had taught her, and between the three of us we had kept this inn's accounts honest for forty years.

It was a small victory. It would not save me. But it was mine.

I carried the candles to the storeroom and put them on the shelf beside last month's supply, and somewhere in the crossing back -- between the storeroom door and the kitchen table, between one task and the next -- the thought arrived, as it always did, without invitation.

The ledger. The rent. The chandler's price edging up. The coal merchant who would try the same trick come November. All of it added against the same immovable fact: that the law of England would hand every farthing of this business to a husband the moment I acquired one. The lease, the income, the reputation my grandmother had built and my mother had kept and I had fought for -- all of it, transferred like a parcel at a coaching stop, into hands that might or might not know what to do with it. I had refused two proposals already for exactly this reason, and I would refuse twenty more if it came to it. The thought was not new. It did not require examination. I set it down and went back to work.

I saw the soldier through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the sign on the gate asked. Young -- twenty at most, though the war aged them and I could never be certain. Thin in the particular way of men who have been hungry for weeks rather than days, the flesh gone first from the face so that the bones showed too clearly. His coat was filthy and too large for him. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the sole of the left one separating from the upper so that I could see the grey wool of his stocking through the gap.

He stood outside the kitchen door without knocking. I watched him through the glass, and what I saw was not a man deciding whether to beg. It was a man deciding whether he was welcome enough to try. His hand came up once toward the door and dropped back to his side. He looked at the ground. He looked at the door. He was not sure.

I opened it before he could decide.

He startled, then steadied himself. Up close he was younger than I had thought. His eyes were a washed-out blue that might have been bright once, before whatever had happened to him happened.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He met my gaze for half a second, then looked away. "Not since yesterday, ma'am. The day before, properly."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He understood. He sat without protest, without the performance of gratitude that some of them offered and that I neither wanted nor had patience for. I cut bread from the loaf on the table, ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since morning, and set both in front of him. He ate steadily, not fast, with the deliberate care of a man who knew that eating too quickly on an empty stomach would make him sick. He held the spoon the way my mother had held her pen -- close to the working end, with his fingers wrapped tight, as though someone might take it from him.

When he had finished, he set the spoon down and said, "Thank you, ma'am," and the words had the shape of something he had been taught to say as a child and had not forgotten, even after everything.

"Where are you headed?" I asked.

"North," he said. "Biggleswade way. I've a sister."

I nodded. He stood, and I saw him brace himself against the table edge as his weight settled on those ruined boots. He touched his cap and went out through the kitchen door, and I watched him cross the yard and pass through the gate and turn north on the road, and then he was gone.

I stood at the window for a moment longer. His hands, when he had held the spoon -- the knuckles had been swollen and red with cold, and he had gripped it so carefully, as though the act of eating were something that required his whole attention. He was somebody's brother. He was walking to her on boots that would not last ten more miles.

I wiped the bowl and put it back on the shelf.

The afternoon passed the way afternoons at the White Hound always passed: in the long, unremarkable stretch between the morning coach and the evening trade, when the inn sat half-empty and the work was not urgent but endless. Two men in the taproom fell into a dispute over a chair, or a remark, or some small grievance that had been growing all day like a blister. I heard the voices rise from the kitchen and came through.

"Gentlemen," I said. "The next round is yours to buy or mine to refuse. Choose quietly."

They chose quietly. Nan, behind the bar, set down the cloth she had not needed to use and went back to wiping glasses.

The hours between two and five I spent on accounts, on resetting the room that the morning's passenger had vacated, on checking the kitchen stores for the evening meal and finding them adequate but not generous. Nan worked the bar. Jem was in the yard. The inn turned over its slow, familiar rhythm, and I moved inside it the way I had moved inside it every day for two years, and every day before that for as long as I could remember: not thinking about the work, exactly, but not thinking about anything else, either. The work was the thinking. The work was everything.

Evening came the way it always did -- not all at once, but by degrees, the light draining out of the windows and the candles taking over and the inn shifting from the bright, practical place it was in daylight to the close, amber-lit place it became at night. I settled the last guests, checked the last fire, tallied the bar with Nan and sent her to bed. I closed down the kitchen. Banked the range. Wiped the table. Hung the cloth on its hook.

Then I made my circuit.

Taproom fire down to coals, the embers giving off just enough heat to warm the stones. Cellar hatch locked, the bolt seated firm. Front door bolted, top and bottom. I checked the latches on the ground-floor windows and found them sound. Stable yard quiet -- I could hear Jem moving in the loft above the stable, settling for the night, and below him the soft sounds of horses shifting in their boxes.

I looked up. Second floor, the window at the far end of the corridor: a lit shape behind the glass, the outline of a woman sitting in a chair. Bridget, watching the yard. I did not look long. I had seen her there too many times for it to require acknowledgment. She was there. She was watching.

I came back inside and locked the rear door and set the bolt. My hands, when I held them up in the last of the firelight, were reddened from the cold water and the cleaning, and there was a thin cut on my index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught the skin. I had not noticed it at the time. I noticed it now, along with the ache in my shoulders and the particular heaviness in my feet that meant I had been standing for fourteen hours.

The inn was quiet. The specific, weighted quiet of a place that has been used hard and is now resting, every beam and board settling into the patience of a building that has done this ten thousand times before.

I was alone. I let myself know it for the space of a breath -- the fact of it, the plain ordinary fact that there was no one to say good night to, no voice at the other end of the kitchen, no presence in the chair by the fire except the one I did not put there -- and then I put the thought away, because I had done so before and because there was nothing useful to be done with it.

The letter was on the table.

I had not noticed it when I came through earlier, or perhaps I had noticed it and set it aside the way I set aside things that could wait until the day's work was done. It was there now, square and cream-coloured, the paper heavier than anything I received in the usual course of business. The handwriting on the direction was small, precise, and immediately familiar.

Stainforth's seal on the back. The dark red wax pressed with his signet, the mark I had seen on every lease document, every formal correspondence, every communication from the man who owned the ground beneath my feet.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy had a different quality to it -- the assurance of a man who expected to be obeyed and therefore saw no need to persuade.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable merit for the purchase of the freehold of the White Hound and its associated properties. The prospective buyer intends to take possession at the expiry of your current lease. I am prepared to meet with you at your earliest convenience to discuss the terms of your departure. I would suggest no later than the fourteenth of November, as matters must be settled before the quarter.*

The fourteenth of November. Three weeks. He was giving me three weeks to agree to the dismantling of everything my family had built, and he was calling it a meeting, as though there were something to discuss, as though my earliest convenience were a phrase that meant anything other than *now, Miss Dale, because I have decided.*

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed my fingertips against the wood, and I felt the grain of it under my skin, the same scarred oak surface where my mother had rolled pastry and my grandmother had counted coins and I had closed the ledger this morning with six weeks of solvency and the stubborn belief that six weeks was enough.

He had a buyer. He had a deadline. He had the freehold and the law and the patient, structural certainty that a woman holding a lease was a temporary condition the world would eventually correct.

I looked at the letter once more. I read the line again -- *the terms of your departure* -- and I heard it in his voice, that dry, measured voice that never rose because it never needed to.

"You'll get your meeting, Mr Stainforth," I said to the empty kitchen. "And I will bring my terms."
