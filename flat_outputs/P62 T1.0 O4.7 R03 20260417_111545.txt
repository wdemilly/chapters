Chapter One

The ledger said six weeks. I closed it anyway, because a number does not improve by being stared at, and because the kitchen fire wanted another log before the kettle went on properly. Six weeks until the next quarter was due. I could manage that. I had managed worse, and in thinner shoes.

The kitchen was cold in the way kitchens are when the fire has only just been fed and has not yet decided to oblige. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and set the kettle on the hook. My mother used to say that a woman who waited for the kitchen to warm itself would wait her whole life, and then someone else would eat her breakfast. I had not been able to disprove her on this point, and had long since given up trying.

The rime was set at the edges of the windows in a white fringe. It would be gone by ten. The taproom still smelled of last night's ale and last night's men, which was not unpleasant if you were used to it, and I was. I crossed to the hearth there and laid the fire fresh. Three faggots, two logs, the good dry kindling from the basket that Jem kept topped up without being told. The flint did its work on the second strike. I straightened, pressed my palm to the small of my back, and went to check the bar.

Six barrels, of which four were sound and two were nearly done. The cellar book told me we had another three brought up on Thursday, which meant Thursday was a negotiation I had already won and did not need to revisit. I wiped down the counter because Nan would wipe it again when she came in, and it was a small kindness to give her a counter that was only dusty rather than sticky. I wanted a cup of tea. I wanted, more than that, a morning in which no one required anything of me at all. I had wanted that particular morning for about twelve years now.

Out through the back passage, into the yard. Jem was already at the tack room, straightening the bridles with the small precise efficiency he brought to everything. He had taught the stump to do the work of the other hand, and I had stopped remarking on it years ago because remarking on it embarrassed him and slowed him down. He did not look up. I did not speak. We had arrived at a working understanding that mornings were for doing, and that greetings were a species of interruption best reserved for Sundays.

I went back in. Nan came through the kitchen door a minute later with pins in one hand and her apron already tied. She put the pins between her teeth, fixed her hair at the little glass by the pantry, took the pins out, and went to the bar. None of this required comment. If she had been late, she would have said so; if I had been angry, I would have said so; neither circumstance obtained. There was bread to slice and coffee to put on, and we had done this often enough that it had the shape of a dance neither of us would ever describe as one.

The London coach was half an hour late. I heard it before I saw it: a short team, light in the traces, not four horses but a pair and a lead, and the driver going easier on the gravel than he usually did. I was at the door before it had stopped.

"Morning, Miss Dale."

"Morning, Higgs. Where are the other two?"

"Thrown a shoe at Hatfield. Other one's lame. Picked up Mrs Varley here, and that's the lot."

A stout woman was already in the yard, standing beside a leather trunk with its hinge half-split. She had the look of a woman whose morning had not met her expectations and who intended to redistribute the deficit.

"This trunk," she said to me, "is ruined."

"It's damaged," I said. "Jem will have a look. If the hinge can be set, he'll set it. If it can't, I've a strap we can lend you to the next stage. Nan will show you to the dining room. There's coffee on."

She opened her mouth. She closed it. She went with Nan. The trunk went with Jem, who had come into the yard at no summons and carried it off by one good handle with the same absence of ceremony he brought to a bucket.

Higgs cleared his throat. "I'll have a word about the north road, if you've a minute."

"One minute. The coffee's nearly poured."

"Above Hatfield it's gone to pieces. There's a rut by the milestone that'll break an axle before Christmas if no one puts stone to it. And the inn at Welwyn's turning people off. I've had to bring two on I shouldn't have."

"I'll write to the surveyor. Stone's cheaper than coroners."

"That's what I said."

"Then we agree, Higgs. Go and have your coffee. You'll want to be on before noon if you mean to make Baldock."

He tipped his hat and went. I watched him go because that was what I did with every coach, every driver, every morning, and then I turned back to the inn and walked the taproom corner while the kettle began to sing.

By the time the chandler came, it was midday, and my hands smelled of beeswax from rubbing the long table in the dining room, and I had decided in advance that I was not going to smile at him. Partlet was a thin man with a thin mouth who said "Miss Dale" as if it cost him something. He had been raising his prices by small increments for seven months on the assumption, I believed, that I was adding them up without really looking.

I had looked.

"Miss Dale." He laid the invoice on the bar. "Tallow's gone up again. I've put the price at seven and threepence the dozen this quarter."

"Good morning, Mr Partlet. You'd like a cup of something."

"I'm pressed."

"Then I'll be brief." I took the invoice. I ran my finger down the column without hurry. "Seven and threepence. You're charging Whitmore's at Baldock six and eightpence."

"Whitmore's take three gross."

"I take two and a half, every quarter, on the day. No disputes, no delays, no sending back for the wicks to be retrimmed. Whitmore's sent back a whole gross in July. I remember because you told me about it yourself."

"Tallow's tallow, Miss Dale. The price is the market."

"The market is what two people settle on. I'll give you six and tenpence, and you'll thank me for it before Christmas, because the Swan at Welwyn has shut its doors this week, and there's a lot of tallow in Hertfordshire looking for a roof to burn under."

His mouth thinned further, which I had not thought possible. "Six and elevenpence."

"Six and tenpence, and I take three gross instead of two and a half, and you walk out of here with a standing order I'll honour in writing."

He took a breath. He let it out. He wrote the new figure on the invoice and initialled it, and I countersigned, and he picked up his hat and said, "Good day, Miss Dale," in exactly the voice he had used on coming in, as though the last two minutes had happened to someone else entirely.

"Good day, Mr Partlet."

The door shut behind him. I looked at the ceiling beam, which had a small spider living in the corner of it, and I permitted myself the thought: *well done, you*. The spider, being a spider, declined to comment.

I carried the invoice through to the little room off the kitchen that had been my mother's office and was now mine, and I filed it, and I stood for a moment with my hand on the open drawer. The rent. The chandler. The ironmonger, next week. The brewer, the week after. All of it sat on the same shelf in my head, and the shelf was not infinite, and the shelf belonged, under the law that governed such things, to any man I might marry the morning after I married him. Mr Ashton had asked, the spring before last, and Mr Horsfall before that, and I had said no to both without pausing long over it, and I did not pause long now. The inn was mine, and mine meant mine. That was the arithmetic. I closed the drawer.

The taproom took me back through one, and then a pie needed pulling from the oven, and then a guest in room four wanted hot water, and the morning folded itself into the afternoon in the unremarkable way of mornings, and I did not think of the rent again until the soldier came to the back door.

I saw him before he knocked. He had come round the side of the kitchen, as the painted board at the gate asked men like him to do, and he stood a few feet from the step with his hands at his sides. He was young. He was very thin. His coat was a regimental red so bleached by rain that it had gone the colour of brick dust, and his boots were parting at the welts, and he was not quite looking at the door. He was deciding whether he was allowed to knock.

I wiped my hands on my apron and opened it before he decided against.

"You've come round the right side," I said. "That's half the battle."

He tried to straighten. The straightening cost him.

"When did you last eat?"

"Yesterday, ma'am. Morning."

"It's kitchen, not taproom. You sit on that stool and you eat what I give you and you don't come through to the front. If you agree to that, there's broth and bread."

"Yes, ma'am."

He sat on the stool by the scullery door. I gave him a bowl of yesterday's broth warmed with a fresh onion in it, and half a loaf, and a mug of small beer, and I went back to the range because watching a hungry man eat was not a courtesy he had asked for. He ate the way a man eats when he is afraid the bowl will be taken. He held the spoon with both hands, as my father had the winter he was ill.

When he had finished he set the mug down carefully and said, "Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"Lincoln, ma'am. I've a sister there."

"Does she know you're coming?"

"Not yet, ma'am. I thought I'd be most of the way before I wrote."

"Write from here. There's paper and a pen in the passage. It costs you nothing, and it will cost her a good deal less than a surprise."

He blinked at me. "Yes, ma'am."

He wrote the letter at the passage table in a hand that was better than I had expected, and I took it for the Thursday bag, and I gave him a piece of hard cheese wrapped in a cloth because the broth would not carry him past Biggleswade. He went out the way he had come in, and the door closed on its hinges, and I stood at the range with my hand on the ladle and did not move for a moment.

His boots. He would not reach Lincoln in those boots. He was someone's brother. I had fed him and he had gone, and that was the transaction, and the transaction did not account for the boots. It did not have to. I went back to the stockpot.

Through the afternoon a pair of drovers in the taproom began to lean towards each other in the manner of men who had remembered an old grievance over a new pint. I was passing with a cloth and did not stop, exactly.

"Gentlemen," I said, "you'll settle it outside or you'll settle it silent. If you settle it loud in my taproom, you'll settle it in the road, and neither of you is sober enough to find it."

They looked at me. They looked at each other. They went back to their pints. Nan, behind the bar, did not lift her cloth from the counter. That was the end of that.

The inn was quiet between coaches. It was always quiet between coaches, in the thin bright hour after dinner when the afternoon had not yet decided what to be. I did accounts in my mother's little office. I put the evening's loaves into the oven. I checked on Bridget, whose door I tapped once, and who called "I'm alive, go and bully somebody else" through the wood, which was her usual mode of welcome. I went back down. The ordinary grind. The grind is what keeps the roof on; the grind is what I was for.

By eight the last of the supper was cleared and the last of the guests were settled, and by ten the taproom was down to its coals, and by eleven I had the inn to myself and the routine I had walked a thousand times.

Taproom fire: banked. Cellar hatch: locked, key on the hook. Kitchen range: damped. Back door: bolted. I went out through the front with the lantern and crossed the yard to check the stable. The horses were settled; Jem had been through. The yard smelled of clean straw and cold. I came back across the cobbles and looked up without meaning to, and there was the light at Bridget's window, and the shape of her in it. She did not see me. She saw the yard, which was what she always saw, and I went in.

My hands at the sink were red from the day. There was a thin cut at the edge of my index finger from the corner of Partlet's invoice, which had bled a little into my apron pocket before I had noticed it. I ran the finger under the cold pump a moment and wrapped it in a scrap of linen. A paper cut. I had been wounded, in this long campaign, by a chandler.

I locked the front door. I set the bolt. The sound of the bolt was the sound it had always been, and the house behind me was warm and quiet and empty of anyone who would speak to me until morning. I stood with my hand on the iron a breath longer than I needed to. I was alone, and I let myself know it, which was a thing I did not often permit, and then I turned away.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

It had come up with the afternoon post, and Nan had put it where I would see it, which meant she had seen the seal, and the seal was Stainforth's. I stood over it and did not sit down. I did not want to sit down to Stainforth's handwriting. I picked it up. The paper was heavy and the direction was in his own hand, which was careful and narrow and slightly backward-sloping, a hand that had been taught to write by a man who valued evenness above all other virtues.

I broke the seal.

*Miss Dale,* it began. *I write on a matter that requires your attention before the quarter day. It has come to my consideration that the lease of the White Hound, held at present under terms agreed with my late father, is approaching the end of its current term, and I am in a position to tell you that an offer has been made for the property by a party prepared to take up the lease on substantially improved terms. I write to you first, in courtesy, to invite you to call at Stainforth House on Wednesday fortnight, where we may discuss whether an accommodation can be reached which will satisfy all parties. I need hardly add that any arrangement agreeable to yourself would need to take account of the several irregularities which have attached to the house of late, in particular the entertaining of vagrants, which has occasioned comment in the parish. I am, &c., P. Stainforth.*

I read it once. I read it again, more slowly, because the second reading was where the knives were. *The end of its current term.* *An offer has been made.* *A party prepared.* *Wednesday fortnight.* *Vagrants.*

Wednesday fortnight was a deadline dressed up as a courtesy. The buyer had already been found. The terms had already been discussed. The meeting was the place where I was to be told what had been decided about me, and the word *vagrants* was there so I would know he had a second blade if the first failed.

I set the letter on the table. I laid the flat of my hand on it as if it were a cat that might otherwise get off. My finger had begun to bleed again through the linen, a small dark fleck on the paper where my palm rested.

Fourteen days. He thought I would spend them being frightened.

"Mr Stainforth," I said to the empty kitchen, and I heard my own voice quite clearly in the room, dry and steady and entirely awake. "You have given me a fortnight. I will use it."
