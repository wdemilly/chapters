The ink had frozen in the ledger's gutter overnight, a thin black rime along the spine where I'd left the pen uncapped like a fool. I breathed on it, worked the nib between my fingers until the last clot dissolved, and wrote the figure I'd been carrying in my head since before I opened my eyes: four pounds, eleven shillings, threepence. That was the distance between what the White Hound held in ready coin and what the quarter's rent would demand in six weeks' time.

Not a disaster. Not yet. But the kind of number that sits on your chest at four in the morning and will not be reasoned with.

I closed the ledger and set the pen in its rest, properly this time. The kitchen was dark except for the glow from the range, where last night's banked coals had done their duty and held. I knelt and fed the fire -- first the kindling I'd split the evening before, then two good logs from the stack Jem kept just inside the scullery door. The flue drew well. It always drew well; my grandmother had insisted on the chimney being rebuilt in 1779, and it was the soundest piece of masonry in the building.

The rest of the inn was cold and silent, which was how I preferred it at this hour. Guests would not stir for another forty minutes. The London coach was due at half seven, assuming it was not late, which it usually was. I had perhaps an hour of solitude, and I meant to spend it usefully.

I carried a candle through to the taproom and knelt at the hearth there, building a second fire. The windows were rimed at their edges, October frost tracing its lace along the glass. I checked the bar stock by touch as much as sight: the ale cask was a third full, which would see us through the day; the gin was adequate; the brandy was low, but brandy was always low, and replacing it at current prices required the kind of optimism I could not presently afford. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and tucked the cuffs into my palms.

Last night's accounts were already tallied -- I'd done them before bed, because a woman who leaves her accounts until morning is a woman who has already begun to lose her grip on the numbers. The totals were honest and unspectacular. We had taken enough to cover provisions and wages. We had not taken enough to build the margin I needed against that figure in the ledger.

I wanted -- and this was the kind of wanting I permitted myself only in the early dark, when no one could see it in my face -- I wanted one month without a crisis. One month in which the road was good, the coaches full, the suppliers honest, and nothing broke that could not be mended with what we had. I wanted to open the ledger and see a number that did not make my throat tighten. Just once.

The fire caught in the taproom grate, and I stood and wiped my hands on my apron and went to unlock the back door.

Jem was already in the stable yard.

I could see him through the grey half-light, moving between the stalls with his particular economy -- one arm doing the work of two, his system of hooks and loops so practiced that the effort was invisible unless you knew what you were looking at. He was straightening tack on the wall pegs, working left to right, the empty sleeve pinned neat at the elbow. He did not look up. He did not need to. We had done this six hundred mornings and would do it six hundred more, God willing and the lease permitting.

I went back inside. A few minutes later Nan came through the kitchen door with her pins half-set and her apron already tied, which told me she'd dressed in the dark and decided the pins could wait. She passed me without a word, took her place behind the bar, and began wiping down the counter with the cloth she kept folded on the shelf below. I heard the faint, rhythmic sound of it as I returned to the kitchen to set the water on for tea.

We had done this before. Many times.

The London coach arrived at a quarter to eight, which was late but not remarkably so. It came in short -- two passengers instead of the expected four -- and both of them were in foul temper, which I attributed to the cold and the road and the general condition of October. The first was a stout woman of middle years with a trunk whose hinge had split clean through, spilling the corner of a petticoat onto the cobbles. She had opinions about this, and she shared them with me, with Jem, and with the coachman, who was not listening.

I directed Jem to the trunk with a look. He appeared at the woman's side before she'd finished her third sentence, took the handle in his one good hand, braced the split corner against his hip, and carried it inside with no more ceremony than if it had been a sack of oats. The woman stopped mid-complaint and stared after him. I settled her with a room number, a promise that the fire would be lit within ten minutes, and a tone of voice that left no space for further grievance. She went.

The coachman wanted a word.

"North road's gone to pieces above Hatfield," he said, stamping his boots on the cobbles. He was a red-faced man with hands like hams and the permanently aggrieved expression of someone who spends his life arguing with horses. "Ruts deep enough to snap an axle. I've reported it twice and nothing's done."

"The parish surveyor was out a fortnight ago," I said. "They've patched the worst of it between the Hatfield turn and Welwyn."

"Patched it with what? Prayers?"

"Gravel and clay. It won't last the winter, but it'll hold until the frost sets in properly. After that you'll want to swing wide at the dip past Coleman's farm -- the drainage ditch has silted up and the road floods there first."

He looked at me with the particular expression men reserve for women who know things about roads. I did not have time to enjoy it.

"I'll need the horses changed inside twenty minutes," I said. "Nan will have bread and beer for you at the bar if you're quick."

He went. The second passenger -- a thin, silent man who had not troubled anyone -- went upstairs without requiring direction. Jem took the luggage. The yard cleared. Through the doorway I could see Nan behind the bar, her cloth moving in steady passes across the counter, one eye on the front door. She'd been watching the whole exchange.

I said nothing. There was nothing to say.

The chandler arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly delivery and his air of mild apology, which I had learned to distrust. Mr Fenton was a lean, sandy-haired man who smiled too much and kept his prices written on slips of paper in his waistcoat pocket, as though the numbers might change if exposed to open air.

"Morning, Miss Dale." He set the first crate of tallow candles on the kitchen table. "Sixteen dozen, as agreed. I've had to adjust the price a touch -- tenpence ha'penny the dozen."

"It was ninepence three weeks ago."

"Tallow's gone up. The renderers in Hitchin have raised their price to the whole trade."

"The renderers in Hitchin are charging you fourpence a pound, same as they charged you in July. I buy soap from the same tallow, Mr Fenton. I know what you pay for it."

The smile held, but the eyes behind it recalculated. "There's cartage to consider. And the cost of wicking has --"

"Ninepence ha'penny," I said. "Which is more than the Bull at Hatfield pays you per dozen, and they order half what I do."

A pause. He reached into his waistcoat pocket and studied one of his slips of paper as though it contained information he had not previously noticed. "I suppose I could manage ninepence ha'penny. As a courtesy."

"As a courtesy," I agreed.

He unloaded the rest of the crate, counted the candles twice -- he always counted twice, which I respected, since I would have done the same -- and departed with his cart and his smile and his slips of paper, leaving sixteen dozen tallow candles on my kitchen table at a price I could absorb.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and allowed myself one moment of private satisfaction. It was a small victory -- pennies, not pounds -- but it was mine, and I had earned it by knowing his margins better than he'd have liked. My mother would have done it faster and with less preamble. But my mother had had thirty years of practice, and I had had two, and the candles were on the table at the right price, and that was enough.

I put the candles away and returned to the ledger, not to write in it but to look at the page. The number had not changed. Four pounds, eleven shillings, threepence -- and now the chandler's bill settled, which was something, but not the something I needed. The rent, the suppliers, the coal, the wages: every shilling accounted for, every margin defended, and all of it resting on a lease that was mine only so long as I remained precisely what I was. Unmarried. Unpossessed. Alone in the eyes of the law and therefore permitted to possess things in my own name.

I had refused two proposals already for this exact, practical reason, and I did not regret either refusal. What I regretted -- if regret was the word -- was that the calculation had to be made at all.

I closed the ledger and went to check on the dining room.

I saw the soldier through the kitchen window before he knocked. He'd come around the back, as the signs asked, and he was standing outside the door with the particular stillness of a man deciding whether he was welcome enough to announce himself. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-used eighteen. Thin in the way that means weeks, not days. His coat had been military once; it was not military now. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the sole of the left one separating from the upper in a gap wide enough to let in every puddle between here and London.

He stood there for perhaps ten seconds, looking at the door. Then he raised his hand and knocked, twice, quietly.

I opened the door.

He took his hat off. His hair was cropped close, still growing out from the Army cut, and his face had the raw, wind-chapped look of someone who'd been sleeping rough.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

He met my eyes, then looked away. "Not recently, ma'am. No."

"Kitchen, not taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the range.

He understood. He sat where I directed and put his hat on his knee and said nothing further while I cut bread and ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since morning. I set it in front of him with a spoon and a cup of small beer and went back to my work at the counter, because watching a hungry man eat is a kind of theft -- you take his dignity along with his privacy.

He ate steadily, not fast. He held the spoon the way men hold things when they are trying not to show how much they need them -- carefully, with the grip too tight and the movements too controlled.

When he'd finished, he stood. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. "My mother's in Huntingdon."

I nodded. He put his hat back on and went out through the kitchen door and across the yard and through the gate, and I watched him go until he turned onto the road and the hedgerow took him.

His boots would not last to Huntingdon. I knew that, and he knew that, and neither of us had mentioned it because there was nothing either of us could do about boots. He was somebody's son, walking north on shoes held together by habit, and I had fed him because a hungry man near a coaching inn is a practical problem, and because I could.

I wiped the bowl and the spoon and put them back, and I went on with the day.

In the taproom, just after noon, two men who'd been sitting too long over too little ale found something to disagree about -- the particulars escaped me, though it seemed to involve a wager and a horse and someone's disputed recollection of the Newmarket results. The dispute was edging toward the kind of volume that unsettles other guests.

"Gentlemen," I said from the doorway. "You'll settle it outside or you'll settle your bill. I don't mind which."

They settled it by going quiet, which was the third option and the one I'd counted on. Nan, behind the bar, caught my eye and returned to her work without comment.

The afternoon stretched out in the way coaching-inn afternoons do when the road is quiet and the weather discourages casual travel. The coach had gone. The two morning passengers were settled -- the woman with the damaged trunk had come down for tea and found nothing further to complain about, which I considered an achievement. Jem was in the yard, doing whatever Jem did in the hours between coaches, which was everything that needed doing and several things that didn't but would need doing eventually. Nan polished glasses. I worked through the kitchen accounts, reset the upstairs rooms, checked the stores against the evening's requirements.

The ordinary grind. No incidents, no revelations. The work, which is its own argument and its own reward, and which does not leave you, and which does not die.

Evening came early, as it does in late October, and with it the particular heaviness of an inn preparing to close. The last guests were settled by eight. I checked the dining room -- cleared, the cloth shaken out and folded, the candles trimmed for morning. The taproom fire was down to coals, and I left it; it would burn itself out safely. The cellar hatch was locked. I tested it, as I tested it every night, because my mother had tested it every night, and her mother before her, and a woman who assumes a lock is set because she set it yesterday is a woman who will find her cellar open in the morning.

The stable yard was quiet. Jem had settled the horses; I could hear nothing from the stalls, which meant they were fed and watered and content, because Jem's horses were always fed and watered and content, and this was one of the things I did not need to worry about and was grateful for.

I looked up at the second floor. There was a lit shape at Bridget's window -- the amber glow of her candle, and behind it, the dark outline of her figure in the chair where she always sat. She was watching the yard, or the road, or the particular middle distance that old women watch when the day is finished and the night has not yet offered them sleep. I did not look long.

Back inside. The kitchen, last of all. I checked the range, banked the fire for morning, hung the kettle where it would be ready. My hands at the end of the day were what they always were: reddened from cold water, roughened from work, with a small cut on my right index finger where the edge of Fenton's delivery note had caught the skin when I'd unfolded it. I hadn't noticed it at the time. I noticed it now, in the way you notice things when the work stops and the body remembers it has been used.

I turned the key in the back door. I set the bolt. The inn was locked, the fires tended, the accounts as balanced as they were going to be, and I was standing in my kitchen in the quiet that comes after a house has been used hard and is now resting.

I was alone. I noticed it the way I noticed the cut -- only when I was still enough for it to register, and too tired to look away from it.

The letter was on the table where Nan must have put it sometime during the afternoon, half-hidden beneath the chandler's receipt. I had not seen it before. I saw it now.

I knew the handwriting on the direction, and I knew the seal -- dark red wax, stamped with the Stainforth crest, pressed down with the particular firmness of a man who considers his correspondence a form of authority. I picked it up. I turned it over once. The paper was heavy, the kind a man buys to remind you that he can afford good paper and you cannot.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though for different reasons. He wrote that he had received an enquiry from a gentleman -- he named no name -- regarding the purchase of the White Hound's lease. He wrote that the terms discussed were favourable and that he was, as the freeholder, disposed to entertain the offer. He wrote that he wished to extend to me, as the current leaseholder, the courtesy of a meeting to discuss the matter, and that he expected my reply within a fortnight.

*The courtesy of a meeting.* As though he were offering me a chair at his table rather than pulling the ground from beneath my feet.

A fortnight. He had given me a fortnight to respond to the possibility of losing everything my grandmother had built, everything my mother had held, everything I had kept alive with my own hands for two years of early mornings and late nights and ledger pages that would not add up to enough.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed my palm flat against it, feeling the weight of the paper under my skin, the cut on my finger stinging where it met the edge.

Then I said, to the empty kitchen, to the banked fire and the clean counter and the locked doors and the silence:

"A fortnight. Right. Then I'd best start tomorrow."

I left the letter on the table where I could see it first thing, and I blew out the candle, and I went upstairs to sleep -- because a woman who means to fight needs her rest, and I had a great deal of fighting to do.
