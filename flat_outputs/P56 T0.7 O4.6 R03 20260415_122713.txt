The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the page to stop it spreading before I remembered that blotting paper existed for precisely this purpose and my thumb did not.

Six weeks. That was what the numbers said when I added them honestly, which was the only way I knew how to add them. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the margin between what the White Hound earned and what it owed had narrowed to something I could feel in my chest each morning when I opened the ledger. Not panic -- I would not call it panic. More the particular tightness of a woman who has counted every farthing twice and knows that counting a third time will not produce a new one.

I closed the book and set it on the shelf above the kitchen range, spine out, where it lived between its two consultations each day -- morning and evening, like a prayer I had less and less faith in.

The kitchen fire needed building up. I knelt on the stone floor and raked the coals forward, feeding kindling until the flames caught and held. The cold had come early this year. The windows were rimed at their edges, fine white lines that would not burn off until midmorning, and my breath hung visible when I crossed to the taproom to start the second fire. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists as I went. The wool was pilling at the cuffs. I had noticed this three days ago and done nothing about it, which was unlike me, and I noticed that it was unlike me, which was worse.

The taproom fire took on the first attempt. I checked the bar stock -- ale casks, the gin, the brandy that moved slowly and cost more than it earned but which the coach passengers expected to find. Last night's accounts were already tallied; I had done them before bed because I could not sleep, and doing accounts was more useful than lying in the dark wanting things I had no business wanting. A life, perhaps, that did not smell perpetually of tallow and wood ash. A morning where the first thing I reached for was not a ledger.

I shook that thought off like water and went to check the cellar hatch.

The bolt was stiff. I worked it with both hands until it gave, checked the barrels below by the light of a tallow stub, and climbed back up satisfied that nothing had leaked or been got at in the night. The dining room wanted airing. I cracked the window an inch, no more -- enough to shift the staleness, not enough to freeze the room -- and straightened the chairs that last night's guests had pushed back from the table at careless angles. One had left a heel of bread on the plate. I ate it standing up, because waste is a sin and breakfast was an hour away.

Outside, in the grey yard, Jem was already at the stable. I could see him through the kitchen window as I came back through -- not because I had gone looking for him, but because Jem at this hour was as reliable as the cold. He was straightening tack on the wall hooks, working the leather with his one hand and the stump of the other in a rhythm so practised it looked effortless, which I knew it was not. He did not look toward the house. He did not need to be told what the morning required.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar without a word and took her position behind it, running a cloth along the counter in a single sweep. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times. The efficiency was the only language required, and we spoke it fluently.

I was in the yard checking the road when I heard the coach. Late -- not unusually so, but enough that I had already adjusted the morning's schedule twice in my head. The London coach came around the bend at a pace that suggested the horses were as tired of the journey as the passengers, and when it pulled into the yard, I counted two faces at the windows instead of the four I had expected.

Both passengers were in a foul temper. The first, a woman of perhaps fifty in a travelling cloak that had seen better decades, descended with the assistance of the coachman and immediately directed my attention to her trunk, which had a split hinge and a grievance she wished to share at length. I let her get three sentences into it before I raised my hand.

"Jem," I called, without turning. He was already crossing the yard. "The trunk to room four, and see if the hinge will take a nail or wants replacing."

Jem took the trunk. The woman stared at me as though I had performed a conjuring trick, which I suppose I had, if you considered competence a form of magic. She went inside without further complaint.

The coachman wanted a word. He was a broad man with a red face and the particular exhaustion of someone who has been driving since before dawn and will be driving again within the hour.

"Road's gone to ruin above Hatfield," he said, climbing down. "Ruts deep enough to break an axle. Someone ought to tell the turnpike trust."

"Someone has," I said. "Twice. They've promised gravel before the frost sets in, which means we'll see it by Easter if we're fortunate. Keep to the left side past the Lemsford turning -- it's firmer there."

He grunted. It was the grunt of a man who had received useful information and had no time to be grateful for it. He touched his hat and went to see about the horses.

The second passenger had taken himself inside without requiring anything from me, which was the best kind of passenger. The yard cleared. Jem led the coach horses toward the stable. Nan, I noticed through the open door, had been wiping down the bar with one eye on the entrance through all of this. I noted it. Said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past ten, as he did on the first Tuesday of every month, with his cart of tallow candles and his face arranged into an expression of professional regret that I had come to recognize the way a sailor recognizes weather.

"Miss Dale." He set the crate on the kitchen table with a thump that was meant to suggest the weight of his burden. "I'm afraid I've had to adjust the price again. Tallow's dear this season. Eightpence the dozen."

"It was sevenpence last month," I said. "And sixpence ha'penny in April."

"The cost of rendering has gone up. I can't help what the market does."

"Mr. Fenton." I folded my arms. "You buy your tallow from Jessop's in Hatfield at threepence the stone. You sell to the Red Lion at sixpence the dozen and to Mrs. Cathcart's lodging house at sixpence ha'penny, and you have done so all year without adjustment. The market has not moved. Your prices have."

He opened his mouth. Closed it.

"I'll pay sevenpence," I said. "Which is more than you're getting from anyone else on this road, and you know it."

A silence. The particular silence of a man recalculating whether the woman in front of him is bluffing and arriving at the correct conclusion that she is not.

"Sevenpence," he said.

"Leave the crate."

He left. The candles stayed. I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and allowed myself one moment -- brief, private, and entirely earned -- of satisfaction. I was good at this. It was a small skill, perhaps, and not the sort that anyone wrote ballads about, but it was mine, and on mornings when the ledger bled through and the margin narrowed and the windows rimed with cold, it was enough to know that I could still hold a line.

The thought arrived, as it always did when I had been counting: that everything I held, I held alone, and that this was the price of holding it at all. If I married, the lease would pass to my husband. The inn, the income, the name my grandmother had built -- all of it, transferred by law to a man the moment I stood before a vicar. I had done this calculation before. I had refused two proposals because of it. The arithmetic had not changed, and neither had my answer.

I picked up the chandler's invoice and went to file it in the office.

I saw the soldier through the kitchen window before he knocked. He had come around the back, as the sign on the gate asked, and he stood outside the door with the particular stillness of a man deciding whether he was welcome enough to announce himself. Young -- not yet five-and-twenty. Thin in the way that meant weeks, not days, without enough food. His coat had been military once. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather separating from the sole in a way that could not be mended, only replaced, and he did not have the means to replace them.

I opened the door before he knocked.

He looked up. Brown eyes, wary, set deep in a face that the weather had aged beyond its years.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

A pause. He had the pride of a man deciding whether honesty or dignity would serve him better. Honesty won, which told me something about how hungry he was.

"Not since yesterday, ma'am."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the range.

He sat. I cut bread from the morning's loaf, ladled broth from the pot that had been simmering since dawn, and set both in front of him without ceremony. He ate the way starving men eat when they are trying not to look starving -- steadily, carefully, holding the spoon as though it might be taken from him.

I busied myself with the kitchen while he finished, because watching a man eat when he is that hungry is an intimacy neither of us had asked for.

When the bowl was empty, he stood. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

He named a direction -- north, toward the manufacturing towns -- in the vague way of a man who did not have a destination so much as a hope that one existed.

"The road is better past Baldock," I said. "Keep to the turnpike."

He nodded. He thanked me again. He went.

I stood at the kitchen door after he had gone and watched him cross the yard, and what I felt was not pity, because pity is cheap and he had not asked for it. It was something smaller and more specific: the sight of his boots, held together by nothing but habit, and the careful way he had gripped the spoon, and the knowledge -- plain and factual and lodged beneath my breastbone like a stone -- that he was somebody's son, and that somebody did not know where he was.

I cleared the bowl and wiped the table and went back to work.

The taproom dispute happened at half past two. Two men who had been sitting too long over too little ale, arguing about something I did not bother to identify because it did not matter. The volume was rising. Nan glanced at me from behind the bar.

"Gentlemen." I did not raise my voice. "You'll settle it outside or you'll settle your accounts and leave. I don't mind which."

They settled it by going quiet, which was the third option and the one I preferred. Nan turned back to her work.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- the long gap between coaches, when the inn exhaled and the work became invisible. Nan behind the bar. Jem in the yard. Rooms to be reset, the kitchen to be managed for the evening meal, accounts to be reviewed and reviewed again, as though the numbers might rearrange themselves into something more generous if I only looked hard enough. They did not. They never did.

Evening came the way it always came -- not all at once, but by degrees, the light pulling back from the yard and the cold pressing in to fill the space it left. The last guests settled. The last fire checked, the coals raked and banked for the morning. The bar tallied, the figures entered in the ledger in my careful hand. The kitchen closed down: pots scoured, surfaces wiped, the morning's bread dough set to prove under a cloth near the warmth of the range.

I moved through the inn's closing routine alone, as I had done hundreds of times. The taproom fire was down to coals, the deep orange glow of it the only light in the room. Cellar hatch locked. Stable yard quiet -- Jem would have settled the horses and taken himself to his room above the stable, where Sapper, the stray he'd adopted, would be waiting with the solemn devotion that only dogs and very old servants can sustain.

I climbed the stairs to check the second floor. The corridor was dark and cold. At the far end, through the window that overlooked the yard, I could see a lit shape at Bridget's window -- the amber square of her candle, and behind it, the silhouette of her watching. I did not look long. I had seen it too many times for it to require acknowledgment. She was there. She was watching. That was enough.

Back in the kitchen, I looked at my hands under the lamplight. Reddened from cold water and cleaning, the knuckles rough, a small cut on my index finger where the edge of the chandler's invoice had caught the skin. I had not noticed it at the time. I noticed it now, at the end of the day, when there was nothing left to do and no one left to do it for, and the noticing felt like the day's final invoice -- a small cost I had not budgeted for.

I set the bolt on the kitchen door. The sound of it sliding home was the sound the inn made when it was finished with the world for the night, and I was the one who made it, and I was alone.

I let myself know that for exactly as long as it took to cross back to the table, which was long enough and too long at once.

The letter was where Nan had left it that afternoon, on the shelf beside the door where the post collected -- I had seen it and set it aside because the day had not allowed for letters, and because I had recognized the handwriting and the seal and had not wanted to open it while there was still work to be done. There was no work now. There was only the letter, and the kitchen, and the silence.

I picked it up. Heavy paper, cream-coloured, the seal pressed deep in dark red wax. Stainforth's hand on the direction, precise and unhurried, the penmanship of a man who had never been rushed in his life.

I turned it over once. I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy came from a different source -- not necessity, but the confidence of a man who expected to be obeyed and saw no reason to argue the point.

He had a buyer for the property. His nephew, Mr. Edward Stainforth, wished to take possession of the White Hound's lease at the next quarter day and convert the premises to suit a more modern class of establishment. Dinah was invited -- the word was "invited," as though this were a social occasion and not a siege -- to meet with Stainforth at his offices on the fourteenth of November to discuss the terms of her departure. If she preferred to negotiate by letter, she was welcome to do so, but he wished her to understand that the decision had, in substance, already been made. He expressed his respect for her mother's memory. He hoped she would see the sense in an orderly transition.

I set the letter on the table. The paper settled flat against the wood with a sound so small it should not have registered, but it did, because the kitchen was very quiet and I was very still.

Six weeks. The same number the ledger had given me this morning, only now it was not a margin. It was a deadline.

I looked at the letter once more. The careful hand, the red seal cracked open, the reasonable tone of a man who believed he was doing me a kindness by giving me notice.

"You'll have to do better than that, Mr. Stainforth," I said to the empty room, and my voice was steady, and my hands were not, and I was already thinking about what I would need to do in the morning.
