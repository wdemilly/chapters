The ledger did not balance, and I had known it would not balance before I opened it.

I sat at the kitchen table with the book laid flat and a stub of tallow candle guttering beside my hand, and I added the column again. The number came out the same as it had on Tuesday: six weeks to quarter day, and the gap between what I held and what I owed was not closing. It sat there on the page like a mouth. I could stare at it or I could shut the book. I shut the book.

The kitchen was cold. The fire I'd built twenty minutes earlier was still finding its courage, licking at the edges of the coal without committing to the business of warmth, and my breath showed faintly when I exhaled. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and pressed my palms flat on the table, feeling the grain of the wood through the wool. My mother's table. My grandmother's table before that. It had a deep burn scar near the left edge where someone--my grandmother, I suspected--had set down a hot pan in a hurry, and I touched it now the way I touched it most mornings, without thinking, the way you touch a wall to confirm it is still standing.

I wanted the fire to catch. I wanted six more weeks in the ledger than I had. I wanted one morning, just one, to sit with a cup of tea and feel that the ground beneath me was solid and paid for and mine.

That was enough wanting for five o'clock in the morning. I stood and got to work.

The kitchen fire first: I riddled the grate, added coal, set the draught. Then the taproom, where last night's ashes were a grey ruin and the air smelled of ale and tallow and damp wool, the honest stink of a working inn. I built the fire, swept the hearth, checked the bar stock by touch in the half-dark. Two casks of ordinary nearly spent. A full cask of the better bitter, tapped yesterday. The gin was low again, which meant someone had been helping themselves or I had miscounted, and I did not miscount.

I tallied last night's accounts against the cashbox and found them correct to the ha'penny. I noted the gin discrepancy in the margin of the ledger in the shorthand my mother had taught me: a small cross, the date, no comment. If it happened again I would know where to look.

The windows at the front of the taproom were rimed at their edges, the frost tracing patterns on the glass that in another life I might have stopped to admire. I did not stop. I wiped condensation from the inside of the panes with a rag, checked the latches, and moved on. The dining room: chairs straight, cloths laid, cruets filled. The front hall: floor swept, the posting schedule chalked on the board by the door. I corrected the time for the afternoon northbound, which someone--not I--had written as half past three when it had been four o'clock since the first of October.

Every surface, every latch, every level in every bottle. Nothing wasted, nothing overlooked. If the inn was a ship, as my mother used to say, then the captain who stopped checking was the captain who drowned. She had not put it quite so neatly. What she'd actually said was, *The woman who trusts her staff to count the gin is the woman who finds her gin has walked off to Hatfield.* I missed her most at this hour, when the inn was quiet and the work was only mine, and missing her was another thing I did not have time for.

I went out the back to check the yard.

Jem was already there. He stood at the tack bench with a bridle in his hand, working the leather through his fingers and the hook he'd rigged at the stump of his left arm, checking each buckle and strap with the methodical attention of a man who had been doing this since well before I asked him to. He did not look up. The yard was clean, the muck heap squared, the water trough full. I noted it and went back inside without speaking. There was nothing to say. The work said it.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar and took her place behind it, running one hand along the counter edge the way a musician might touch an instrument before playing. She did not say good morning. I did not say it either. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

---

The London coach was late.

This was not unusual. The London coach was late more often than it was on time, owing to the state of the road above Hatfield, the temperament of the horses, the reliability of the coachman, the weather, and--I sometimes suspected--the position of the stars. I stood in the yard with my arms folded and listened for the sound of it, which on a clear morning carried half a mile: the particular rhythm of a four-horse team on packed road, the creak of springs, the coachman's voice raised to no purpose since the horses had stopped listening to him somewhere around Barnet.

When it arrived, it was running short. Two passengers instead of the expected four. One was a red-faced woman in a green travelling cloak who descended from the coach in a fury and pointed at her trunk, which had a split hinge and gaped open like a broken jaw.

"That was done on the road," she announced, to me, to the yard, to anyone who might be held responsible. "I want it seen to."

I had Jem at the trunk before she finished the sentence. He assessed the hinge, looked at me, and took the trunk to the stable without comment. The woman watched him go and seemed momentarily deprived of her grievance, which left her standing in the yard with nothing to do but be cold.

"There's a fire in the dining room," I said, "and breakfast in twenty minutes."

She went. The second passenger, a thin man in a brown coat who had the look of someone who had been listening to the woman since London, followed her without a word.

The coachman climbed down and stood by his horses with the particular exhaustion of a man who has been sitting for hours and is somehow more tired for it.

"The road above Hatfield is a disgrace," he said. "Ruts the size of graves. I near lost a wheel at the Stanborough turn."

"I'll note it for the next post," I said. "Was it the south side or the north?"

"South side. The drainage is gone entirely. There's standing water across half the road."

"I'll send word to the turnpike trust this afternoon."

He looked at me with the flat appraisal of a man who knew the turnpike trust would do nothing and knew that I knew it too. "Right," he said. "Much obliged."

"Your horses want water. Jem will see to them."

He nodded and moved off. He had twelve miles to the next stop and no more time for conversation, and neither did I.

The yard cleared. Jem took the horses. The passengers settled. Nan stood behind the bar with a cloth in one hand and her eyes on the dining room door, ready for whatever the red-faced woman might require and already, I could tell, forming a private opinion about her. I caught Nan's expression--the faint tightening at the corner of her mouth that meant she was filing someone away for future reference--and said nothing. Nan's assessments were generally accurate, and she had the good sense to keep them to herself.

---

Mr. Fenton the chandler arrived at half past ten with his cart and his monthly invoice and his fourth price increase in seven months.

"Tallow's gone up again, Miss Dale," he said, setting the crate of candles on the kitchen table. He laid the invoice beside it, face up, and stepped back as though he'd done me a kindness.

I picked up the invoice and read the figure. Then I read it again.

"Eightpence a dozen," I said. "It was sevenpence last month."

"The price of tallow, Miss Dale. I don't set the market."

"You don't. But you do set your margin, Mr. Fenton, and yours has widened three times since April. You're paying fourpence a pound for tallow from Bridger's yard--the same as you were paying in March--and you're charging me as though it's come from the West Indies on a silk cushion."

He had the grace to look uncomfortable, which was not the same as having the grace to lower his price. "I have costs, Miss Dale. Transport. Labour."

"Your transport is the same cart and the same horse you've had for six years, and your labour is yourself and your boy. Your costs have not risen by a penny since Easter." I set the invoice down. "Sevenpence ha'penny. That's fair, and you know it's fair, because the Woolpack and the Red Lion are both paying sevenpence, and I doubt you'd like me to confirm that with Mr. Harker at the Woolpack the next time he calls."

A silence. Mr. Fenton looked at his crate of candles. He looked at me. He was calculating, I knew, the cost of loading the crate back onto his cart and driving to the next customer who might accept eightpence, against the certainty that I would take every candle he had at a price that still left him a decent profit, and that I would go on taking them month after month for as long as we both drew breath.

"Sevenpence ha'penny," he said. "But I'll want payment on delivery."

"You always have payment on delivery, Mr. Fenton. That has never been in question."

I paid him from the cashbox. He took his money and his cart and left, and the candles stayed.

When the door closed behind him, I stood for a moment with my hand on the crate and allowed myself, just briefly, the private satisfaction of a woman who has done the one thing she is very good at. It was a ha'penny a dozen. It was not nothing.

I put the candles away and went back to the ledger, and the satisfaction lasted exactly as long as it took me to look at the numbers again. The ledger did not care about ha'pennies. The ledger cared about the gap between what I held and what I owed, and the gap was still there, and it would still be there in six weeks when the rent came due.

This was the calculation I had made before, the one that lived at the back of every negotiation and every sleepless morning: everything I did to hold this place--every candle counted, every gin bottle watched, every shilling stretched to cover a shilling and a half--depended on the fact that the lease was mine to defend. If I married, it would not be. If I married, a husband would stand where I stood, and the chandler would negotiate with him, and the ledger would be his to close or keep open, and the inn my grandmother built would pass out of my hands as surely as if I had sold it. I had refused two proposals for this reason. I did not regret either refusal. I did not, most days, think about them at all.

I closed the ledger and went to check the dining room.

---

I saw the soldier through the kitchen window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the signs asked. He was young--twenty, perhaps, or less--and thin in the particular way of men who have been thin for a long time, not starved but whittled, the body pared down to what it needed and no more. His coat was too large for him. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather cracked and separating from the sole on the left foot so that I could see a stripe of dirty stocking beneath.

He stood outside the kitchen door for several seconds without raising his hand. He was looking at the door, then at the yard, then at the door again. He was deciding whether to knock. He was not sure he would be welcome enough to try.

I opened the door before he had to find out.

He startled--just slightly, a quick tightening of the shoulders--and then stood straight and looked at me with the careful blankness of a man who has learned not to expect much.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

A pause. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there."

He understood. He sat at the table where I pointed, near the fire but away from the door that led to the public rooms, and he put his hands in his lap and waited while I cut bread and ladled broth from the pot that had been on since morning. I set it before him with a spoon and a cup of small beer and stepped back.

He ate carefully. Not slowly--he was too hungry for that--but carefully, the way a man eats when he is conscious of being watched and does not want to seem desperate. He held the spoon with a steadiness that cost him something. I could see the effort in his wrist.

When he finished, he looked up. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. "There's family in Nottingham. Or there was."

He rose, and I did not ask him anything else, and he did not offer anything more. He touched his cap and went out through the kitchen door, and I watched him cross the yard and pass through the gate, and then he was on the road and walking north, and I stood with my hand on the edge of the table and thought about the boots. The left boot, the sole peeling away from the leather, and the ground already freezing at night. He was somebody's son, and he was walking to Nottingham in boots that would not last the week.

I cleared the bowl and the cup and wiped the table where he had sat, and then I went back to work, because the work was always there and the feeling was not something I could do anything with.

---

In the taproom, late in the afternoon, two men who had been sitting too long with too much ale and too little purpose began arguing about the proper way to shoe a horse. It was the kind of dispute that starts in boredom, escalates through pride, and ends, if you let it, with a broken chair and a bloody nose.

I did not let it.

"Gentlemen," I said, from the doorway. "If you wish to continue this conversation, you may do so in the yard. If you wish to remain inside, you will do so quietly."

They looked at me. They looked at each other. The larger one opened his mouth. I waited. He closed it. They settled. Nan, behind the bar, did not look up from the glass she was polishing, but I saw her shoulders ease.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way. The coach gone, the guests settled or departed, the inn in its midday pause. Nan at the bar. Jem in the yard. I did the accounts for the second time, reset two of the upstairs rooms, supervised the kitchen for the evening, and checked the coal store, which was lower than I liked and would need replenishing before the week was out. The work was not dramatic. It was not interesting. It was the work, and it was mine, and I did it the way I did it every day, which was thoroughly and without complaint, because complaining required an audience and I did not have one.

---

Evening came on early, the way it does in late October, the light draining out of the sky by half past four and the yard falling into shadow while the kitchen was still busy. I served dinner. I settled the last guests. I banked the taproom fire and tallied the bar and closed the kitchen down, and then I moved through the inn the way I moved through it every night, alone, checking every door and every window and every fire.

The taproom hearth was down to coals, pulsing faintly in the dark. I checked the draught and closed the fender. The cellar hatch: locked, the bolt firm under my hand. The front door: bolted, the chain set. The stable yard: quiet. I could hear Jem's boots on the cobbles somewhere near the far stall, the last sounds of a man finishing his own closing routine. The horses were settled. I did not need to check. Jem had it in hand.

I looked up at the second floor. There was a lit shape at Bridget's window--the soft amber glow of a candle behind glass, and the dark outline of a figure sitting where she always sat, watching the yard. I did not look long. She was there. She was watching. She was always watching.

I came back inside and stood in the kitchen. My hands, in the candlelight, were reddened from cold water and from wringing out cloths, and there was a small cut on my right index finger where the edge of Fenton's invoice had caught me when I unfolded it. I had not noticed it at the time. I noticed it now. The blood had dried to a thin dark line. I should have been more careful, but there it was--the chandler's parting gift, delivered free of charge.

I bolted the kitchen door. I set the latch. The inn was closed, and the inn was quiet in the way that only a place used hard all day can be quiet: not peaceful but spent, the silence of exhaustion rather than calm. I stood in it for a moment, and I let myself notice--just for the span of one breath--that I was alone. That the last voice I had heard was my own. That the kitchen was warm now and the fire was banked and the day was done, and there was no one to say so to.

Then I saw the letter.

It was on the table, near the ledger, where someone--Nan, probably--had placed it during the afternoon. I had not seen it, or I had seen it and not registered it, which was unlike me. It was a folded sheet sealed with wax, and the handwriting on the direction was precise and slanted and immediately recognizable.

Stainforth.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The seal was dark red, pressed with an initial I did not need to examine. I knew whose seal it was. I had seen it on the lease itself, and on every communication his office had sent my mother for the last decade of her life.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, and I might have respected this quality in him if he had not been using the economy to dismantle my life.

*Miss Dale,* it began. *I write to inform you that I have received a serious offer of purchase for the property currently held under lease by yourself, comprising the inn known as the White Hound and its associated buildings and grounds. The prospective buyer is my nephew, Mr. Harold Stainforth, who intends to undertake a considerable programme of improvement to the premises.*

I read on. There was a meeting proposed--his office, a date twelve days hence. There was a figure named, though it was not an offer to me; it was the price his nephew was willing to pay for the freehold, mentioned, I understood, so that I could appreciate the seriousness of the interest and the futility of my position. There was a sentence near the close that I read twice: *I should be glad to discuss the terms of a new arrangement, should you wish to remain in some capacity, though I must be candid that the present lease, held as it is on terms that I believe were unusually generous, is unlikely to be renewed on the same basis when it falls due.*

When it falls due. He did not need to name the date. I knew the date. My mother had known the date. My grandmother had known the date. It was the fixed point around which three generations of women had built their lives, and Stainforth was now informing me, in his careful slanting hand, that he intended to let it pass without renewal and sell the ground from under my feet.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed my hands flat on either side of it. I looked at the words again, and the cut on my finger throbbed dully against the wood.

Then I said, to the empty kitchen, to no one, to the quiet and the dark and the walls my grandmother had scrubbed:

"Twelve days, Mr. Stainforth. I'll see you in eight."
