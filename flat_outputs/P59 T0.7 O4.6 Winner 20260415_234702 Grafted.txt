The ink had frozen in the well overnight.

I discovered this with my pen already in hand, the ledger open to October's accounts, the kitchen fire not yet giving enough heat to reach the table by the window. I breathed on the ink, set the well closer to the grate, and counted what I knew without needing to write it down. Forty-three days until the quarter's rent came due. Enough in the strongbox to cover it, but only if nothing broke, no deliveries went short, and every coach between now and December stopped where it was supposed to stop.

Forty-three days was not comfort. It was arithmetic.

I wanted more than arithmetic. I wanted sixty days, eighty, a full quarter's breathing room where I did not have to hold every shilling up to the light and ask it to stretch. I had wanted this for two years, since my mother dropped dead between the soup and the joint, and I had not got it yet. Wanting, in my experience, was not a strategy. Work was a strategy. So I closed the ledger, because the ink was still too thick to write, and I started the day.

Kitchen fire first. It had gone to coals in the night and needed building up from the ash, which I did with kindling from the box Jem kept stocked by the back door. The wood was dry. It caught fast. I hung the stockpot on its hook over the flame and filled it from the pail, then checked what remained of yesterday's bread. Half a loaf. Enough for the morning, not enough for the afternoon. I made a note in my head, which was where most of my notes lived, the ledger being largely ceremonial.

Taproom next. I crossed the passage in the dark, feeling for the doorframe with my hand. The windows here faced east and the first grey light was just reaching the glass, though it did little more than show me the shapes of the furniture. Tables wiped down last night. Chairs pushed in. The floor wanted sweeping, but it always wanted sweeping. I checked the bar: three casks of ale, one nearly spent. Two bottles of brandy, the good one and the one I served to people who wouldn't know the difference, because a coach party shorted on brandy is a coach party with opinions. The gin was low. I would need to speak to Harker about the gin.

I crouched at the taproom grate and laid a fire there too, smaller than the kitchen one, because the room would not fill for hours yet and fuel cost money. The cold was serious this morning. Not the clean cold of a hard frost but the damp kind that crept through wool and settled in the joints of your fingers. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and held my hands near the new flame until the feeling came back into them.

The accounts from last night were on the shelf behind the bar where I'd left them. I tallied them standing up, in my head. Fourteen shillings, eightpence. A decent night, not a good one. Three rooms occupied, two of them singles. The Moretons in the large front room were staying through Wednesday. Mrs Moreton had complained about the draught from the window, and she was right to complain, because the sash cord had gone and I hadn't replaced it yet. I added this to the list in my head, which was growing longer than the list in the ledger.

The inn was quiet. Twenty rooms, most of them empty. The staircase settled and creaked in the cold the way old timber does, as if the building were having its own conversation about the temperature. I stood in the passage between the taproom and the kitchen and listened to it. I knew every sound this place made. I knew which floorboard groaned on the first-floor landing and which door stuck in wet weather and which window rattled when the wind came from the north. My grandmother had built this knowledge. My mother had inherited it. I maintained it the way you maintain a wall: daily, attentively, because the moment you stopped paying attention was the moment something gave way.

Outside, the yard was still dark. I could hear Jem moving in the stable block, the particular sound of tack being handled by a man who did not need to be told what the morning required. A bridle ring clinked against a hook. A horse blew softly. Jem had been here before me, which was not unusual. He slept lightly and started early, and he did not discuss either habit.

Nan came through the kitchen door a few minutes later, pins half-set in her hair, apron already tied. She crossed to the bar and took up her position without a word. I heard her checking the glasses, the quiet clink of one being held up to the window light to see if it was clean. It was. She checked anyway.

We had done this before. Many times.

The London coach was late by twenty minutes, which was not unusual for October, when the roads north of Hatfield turned to soup after a week of rain. I heard it before I saw it: the particular grinding note of iron-rimmed wheels on gravel, the driver's shout to the horses, the heavier thud of a coach that was carrying less weight than it should.

Two passengers instead of four. The first was a stout woman in a brown travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the rigid dignity of someone who had been personally offended by every mile of the journey. The second was a thin man who said nothing and went directly upstairs. The woman had a trunk, and the trunk had a split hinge, and she had opinions about this.

I directed Jem, who had appeared in the yard without being called. He took the trunk. The woman watched him manage it one-handed and opened her mouth, then closed it. People generally did, with Jem.

I settled her into the dining room with tea and the last of the morning bread, and by the time I returned to the yard the coachman was waiting for me. He was a big man named Fenton whom I had known for three years, and he had the look of someone who wanted to say his piece and get back on the road.

"North side of Hatfield, the ruts are two feet deep," he said. "We nearly lost a wheel."

"Take the Essendon fork. It adds ten minutes but the surface holds."

"It's not my road to choose, Miss Dale. I go where the company tells me."

"Then tell the company. I'll write to Chaplin's office myself if it helps."

He looked at me for a moment, calculating whether this was worth his time, and decided it was not. He touched his hat and climbed back up to the box. The coach pulled out of the yard, and the gravel settled behind it, and the morning went on.

The chandler arrived at half past ten.

His name was Pollard, and he was a lean, careful man who smelled permanently of tallow and conducted his business with the manner of someone doing you a tremendous personal favour. He brought the candles in himself, two crates of them, and set them on the kitchen table as though presenting a gift.

"Tenpence the dozen," he said.

"It was ninepence last month."

"Tallow's gone up. The renderers in Hitchin have raised their price."

"The renderers in Hitchin are charging you fourpence a pound, same as they charged you in March. I buy soap from the same tallow, Mr Pollard. Mrs Carswell at the Woolpack has no gift for discretion. I know what it costs."

He looked at me. I looked at him. The kitchen fire crackled between us.

"Ninepence ha'penny," he said.

"Ninepence, and I'll take an extra two dozen at the same price, which saves you a delivery next month. Seven and a half leaves you a margin any honest man would be comfortable explaining to his wife."

"You'll bankrupt me, Miss Dale."

"You'll survive."

He considered this. He had no other coaching inn on this stretch of road that bought in the quantity I did, and we both knew it. Walking away with his candles would cost him more than the ha'penny he wanted, and he was not a man who enjoyed losing money on principle.

"Ninepence," he said. "And I'll want payment on delivery next time, not net thirty."

"Agreed."

He left. The candles stayed.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and allowed myself one moment of satisfaction. It was a small victory, half a penny on a dozen candles, the kind of thing no one would ever notice or remark upon. I noticed it. I remarked upon it to myself, silently, and then I put the candles away.

The ledger, the rent, the chandler's price. All of it weighed against the same calculation I had made before and would make again: that everything I held, I held because I was unmarried. The lease was in my name because there was no husband's name to supersede it. If I married, the inn passed into his control. His debts could swallow it. His judgment could ruin it. His death could leave me with nothing. I had refused two proposals already, for exactly this reason, and I did not expect to be troubled by a third.

I put the thought away with the candles. There was work to do.

He came around the back, as the signs asked. I saw him through the kitchen window before he knocked: young, thin, standing outside the door with his arms at his sides, looking at it. Not knocking. Deciding whether to knock. His coat was too large for him, Army issue, worn through at the elbows. His boots were coming apart where the sole met the upper, the stitching gone white with strain.

He could not have been older than twenty.

I opened the door before he raised his hand.

"Have you eaten today?"

He looked at me. His eyes were brown, and there was nothing in them that frightened me. "Not since yesterday, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not the taproom. Sit there."

He sat. I gave him bread and broth from the stockpot, which was not much but was hot, and he ate with the careful steadiness of someone who had learned not to eat too fast on an empty stomach. He held the spoon the way my mother used to hold a pen, close to the bowl of it, as though it might be taken from him.

When he had finished, he stood. "Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. "There's a cousin in Biggleswade. Or there was."

He went out the way he had come, around the back, past the stable block, and out through the gate to the road. I watched him go. His boots left uneven prints in the mud of the yard, deeper on the left side where the sole was pulling away.

He was somebody's son. The thought arrived in my own voice, plain and unwelcome, and I turned back to the stockpot before it could become anything more.

The afternoon was the kind that passed without incident and was better for it. Two men in the taproom got loud over a question of whose coach had the right of way at the Welwyn crossroads, and I put an end to it with six words.

"Gentlemen. Not in my house."

They went quiet. Nan, behind the bar, did not look up from the glass she was polishing.

The hours between the morning coach and the evening trade were the hours I used for everything that could not be done when the house was full. Accounts. Rooms turned and aired. The stockpot replenished. A list made for the butcher. The sash cord in the Moretons' room replaced, which took longer than it should have because the frame had swollen with damp and I had to plane it down with a knife before the new cord would seat. I did this kneeling on the floor of the room while Mrs Moreton was at her dinner, and I did it myself because there was no one else to do it.

The inn between rushes was the inn I knew best. Nan at the bar, reading something below the counter when she thought I wasn't looking. Jem in the yard, the rhythmic knock of his mallet on a fence post that had worked loose. The kitchen ticking along on its own, the stockpot doing its slow work, the bread for tomorrow already set to prove. The ordinary machinery of a day. No drama. No disaster. Only the work, and the work was enough, and if I said it often enough I would go on believing it.

I locked up alone, as I always did.

The last guests were settled by nine. The taproom fire was down to coals. I raked them flat and set the guard. The bar was tallied, the figures in my head matching the figures Nan had scratched on the slate before she went up. The cellar hatch: locked. The front door: bolted. The kitchen: banked and closed, the stockpot moved to the side of the range where it would stay warm but not boil dry.

I made my circuit. Taproom, passage, dining room, front hall. Upstairs: the first-floor corridor, every door shut, no light beneath any of them except the Moretons', where a candle still burned. Second floor: empty. Back down, through the kitchen, out to the yard.

The air was cold and sharp and smelled of horse and hay and the faint mineral tang of the well. The stable block was dark. Jem had finished for the night. Across the yard, on the second floor of the east wing, a light burned in Bridget's window. I could see the shape of her, sitting in her chair, watching the yard below. She was always there. She was always watching. I did not look long.

I bolted the yard gate. I came back inside and locked the kitchen door behind me. My hands, in the lamplight, were red from the cold water and the day's work. There was a thin cut on my index finger, a paper cut from the edge of Pollard's delivery note, which I had not felt when it happened and now could not stop feeling. I pressed my thumb against it and the small sting grounded me in my own skin.

The inn was quiet. I was alone in it. I let myself know this for the space of a breath, the way you let yourself look at a bruise, and then I turned to put out the lamp.

The letter was on the kitchen table.

It had not been there an hour ago. Nan must have taken it from the afternoon post and left it for me, not thinking it urgent, or thinking it exactly urgent enough to leave where I would find it last. The seal was red wax, pressed with an insignia I knew. The paper was heavy, the kind a man used when he wanted the weight of the letter to do some of the work before the words began. The handwriting on the direction was small and precise and slanted hard to the right.

Stainforth.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. He did not waste words any more than I did, which was one of several things I would have preferred not to have in common with him. He had a buyer for the property, he wrote, a gentleman of means who intended to modernize the premises. He was prepared to offer me reasonable terms for the surrender of the lease. He proposed a meeting at his offices on the fourth of November to discuss the matter. If I did not attend, he would take it as an indication that I did not wish to negotiate, and he would proceed accordingly.

Proceed accordingly. I read the phrase twice. It was the kind of language that sounded civil and meant ruin.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed both palms flat against the wood, feeling the grain of it, the scars and burns and ring-marks of a hundred years of use. My mother's table. My grandmother's table.

"The fourth of November," I said to the empty kitchen. "I'll be there at eight."
