The ink had bled through the third column again, and I pressed my thumb against the smudge before it could ruin the page beneath.

Six weeks. That was what the ledger said when I stripped away the small comforts I told myself at night -- that the autumn trade would hold, that the London coaches would keep running full, that the price of tallow would not climb again. Six weeks until the quarterly rent came due, and the numbers in front of me did not lie, however much I might have preferred them to.

I closed the ledger and set my hands flat on the kitchen table. The wood was cold. The whole inn was cold -- that particular October cold that crept in through stone walls and settled into the bones of a building as though it meant to stay. The fire I'd built twenty minutes ago had not yet taken proper hold, and my breath made a faint ghost in the air above the page.

I wanted, just for one morning, to sit here a moment longer. To drink my tea while it was still hot and think about nothing at all.

Instead I pushed back from the table and went to work.

The kitchen fire first -- I riddled the grate, added coal, adjusted the damper until the draw was right. Then the taproom, where last night's ashes needed clearing and the hearth resetting. I knelt on the stone floor and laid the fire the way my mother had taught me: paper, kindling, two splits of oak crossed at the centre, coal on top only when the wood had caught. Patience Dale had opinions about fire-building the way some women had opinions about embroidery, and I had absorbed every one of them before I was ten.

The bar stock next. I counted bottles, checked the cask levels by feel -- a tap and a tilt told me more than any gauge -- and noted what needed ordering. Two barrels of ale would see us through the week if trade held. If. I pulled my sleeves down over my wrists and moved on.

Last night's accounts were still on the bar where Nan had left them, the figures neat in her careful hand. I checked them against the cashbox. Correct to the farthing, as usual. Nan made errors so rarely that when she did, I suspected she'd done it on purpose to see if I was paying attention.

The windows at the front of the taproom were rimed at their edges, a thin crust of ice that caught the early light and turned it silver. I wiped the nearest pane with my sleeve and looked out at the yard. Empty. The first coach was not due for another hour, and the road beyond the gate was a grey ribbon of nothing, stretching north toward Baldock and south toward Hatfield and London.

I adjusted a chair that had been left crooked, straightened the cloth on the nearest table, and went out through the back.

Jem was in the stable yard. He had a halter over his shoulder and was checking the tack hung along the wall, running his hand -- his one hand -- along each piece with the unhurried attention of a man who understood that a frayed girth strap was not a minor inconvenience but a broken passenger and a lawsuit. He did not look up when I crossed the yard. He did not need to. The tack would be right because Jem was the one looking at it, and we both knew this, and there was nothing further to discuss.

I went back inside.

Nan arrived a few minutes later through the kitchen door, pins not yet set in her hair, apron already tied. She took her place behind the bar without a word, and I heard the soft thump of the cloth as she began wiping down the counter. We had done this before. Hundreds of times. The rhythm of it was as familiar to me as breathing, and just as invisible -- until the morning it stopped, which I refused to think about at half past six with the fires barely lit.

The London coach came in forty minutes late, which was not unusual for October, when the roads north of Hatfield turned to a sort of malicious pudding that punished wheels and horses alike. What was unusual was the number: two passengers instead of the expected four, and both of them in the kind of temper that comes from being rattled over bad road for an hour longer than promised.

The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended from the coach with the air of someone who had been personally insulted by Hertfordshire. She had a trunk, and the trunk had a split hinge, and she had opinions about this that she began expressing before her second foot touched the ground.

"The clasp has been wrenched clean off. I shall require it repaired before I continue north, and I shall require an explanation from whoever loaded it, and I shall require --"

"Jem," I said.

He was already there. He took the trunk from the coachman's hands, assessed the hinge with a glance, and carried it toward the stable block where he kept his tools. The woman watched him go with her mouth still open around the next requirement.

"He'll have it mended within the hour," I told her. "Breakfast is laid in the dining room. The fire's been lit since six."

She looked at me. I looked at her. Whatever she saw in my face -- and I have been told my face does not invite extended negotiation -- persuaded her to close her mouth, gather her skirts, and go inside.

The coachman wanted a word. He was a thick-necked man called Burrell whom I'd known for three years, and he did not waste time on pleasantries.

"Road's gone above Hatfield," he said. "Whole stretch past the Lemsford turn is near impassable. Lost half an hour and damn near lost an axle."

"The parish was meant to mend it after Michaelmas."

"Well, they haven't. I'll not run this route after dark until it's done. You might tell whoever you tell about such things."

"I'll write to the surveyor this afternoon," I said. "You'll want water for the horses before you go on."

He nodded, and that was the end of it. Burrell did not require sympathy. He required someone to hear the complaint and act on it, and I was that person, and he would be on the road again in twenty minutes.

The second passenger had already gone inside without fuss. Jem took the horses. The yard cleared. Through the window I could see Nan behind the bar, cloth in hand, watching the dining room door with the particular expression she wore when she was assessing whether a guest would be trouble. I noted it. Said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past ten.

His name was Fenton, and he was a lean, dry man who smelled perpetually of tallow and self-satisfaction. He came monthly with his cart of candles and his invoice, and for the past seven months he had been edging his price upward with the careful patience of a man who believed I would eventually stop noticing.

He set the crate on the kitchen table and handed me the bill.

I looked at it. "Fourpence ha'penny the dozen."

"Tallow's gone up," he said. "I'm sure you've heard."

"Tallow's gone up a farthing the pound since March. You've raised your price three ha'pence the dozen in the same period. That's a generous interpretation of your costs, Mr Fenton."

He shifted his weight. "I've other customers on this road who pay without complaint."

"You've four customers on this road, and the Bull at Hatfield has been making their own dips since July. Which leaves you three, and I am the one who orders in the largest quantity. Fourpence the dozen, same as last month."

A pause. Fenton looked at the crate. He looked at me. He looked at the crate again, as though the candles themselves might offer a more sympathetic audience.

"Fourpence farthing," he said.

"Fourpence. And I'll take an extra two dozen at the same rate, which saves you a journey next month."

He worked this over in his mind. I could see the arithmetic moving behind his eyes -- the cost of the extra journey weighed against the quarter-farthing he wanted, the knowledge that I knew his margins as well as he did, the grudging recognition that walking away from my custom would cost him more than the concession.

"Done," he said.

He unloaded the candles. He took his money. He left.

I stood in the kitchen after the door closed and allowed myself one moment -- just the one -- of private, unsentimental satisfaction. I was good at this. It was not a grand talent. No one would write a ballad about a woman who could negotiate the price of tallow candles. But it was mine, and it kept the lights burning, and today that was enough.

The thought came, as it always did, hard on the heels of any small triumph: that everything I had just done -- the ledger, the negotiation, the daily machinery of keeping this place alive -- all of it existed only because I had not married. The inn was mine by lease, and the lease was mine by my mother's name, and both would pass out of my hands the moment I took a husband. That was the law. Not opinion, not custom -- the law, as fixed and indifferent as the road outside.

I had refused two proposals on the strength of that calculation, and I did not regret either one.

I wiped the table where Fenton's crate had been and went to see about the midday bread.

I saw him through the window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the sign on the gate directed, and he stood outside the kitchen door with the particular stillness of a man who was deciding whether he was welcome enough to ask. Young -- not much past twenty, if that. Thin in the way of someone who had been thin for a while, not merely since breakfast. His coat was too large for him, as though he had been a bigger man when he acquired it, or it had belonged to a bigger man entirely. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the leather cracked and separating from the sole on the left foot.

He looked at the door. He looked at his hands. He raised one fist, hesitated, and then he knocked.

I opened it.

He took a half-step back when he saw me, as though he had expected the door to remain shut and had not prepared for the alternative.

"Have you eaten?" I asked.

He met my eyes. He was fair-haired, with a face that would have been pleasant if there had been more flesh on it. "Not since yesterday, ma'am. I don't mean to be a trouble."

"Kitchen," I said. "Not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the hearth.

He understood. He came in and sat where I told him, and he did not ask for more than what was offered, and I respected him for that. I cut bread and ladled broth from the pot that had been on the range since morning. I set it in front of him with a spoon and a cup of small beer and went back to my work at the table.

He ate carefully. Not slowly -- he was too hungry for slow -- but carefully, as though he remembered that there were manners and was determined to observe them even now. He held the spoon the way a child holds a pencil, with a grip that was too tight and slightly wrong, and I thought: he has not held a spoon in a while. He has been eating with his hands, or not eating at all.

When the bowl was empty he set the spoon down and looked at me.

"Thank you, ma'am. I'm grateful."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. "There's family in Nottinghamshire. Or there was."

Neither of us said anything about the war. There was nothing to say about it that his boots did not already tell.

He thanked me again at the door. I watched him cross the yard and go out through the back gate, and then I stood there a moment longer than I needed to, looking at the place where he had been.

He was somebody's son. He was twenty years old and walking north in boots that would not last another week, and he was somebody's son, and I could not do a single thing about it beyond the bread and broth I had already done.

I turned back to the kitchen and washed his bowl.

The afternoon brought the usual small crises. Two men in the taproom -- commercial travellers, both, who had been sitting too long over their ale and had found something to disagree about. The volume was climbing toward the register that precedes broken furniture.

I walked over. "Gentlemen. The next round is on the house if you can manage it quietly. Otherwise you'll settle your bills now and find another establishment."

They managed it quietly.

Nan watched from behind the bar with one eyebrow slightly raised, which was her version of applause.

The hours between the afternoon coach and the evening trade were the inn's held breath -- the gap when the work did not stop but changed its nature, from the urgent to the necessary. I tallied accounts. I reset the rooms the morning guests had vacated, stripping beds and checking for anything left behind. I managed the kitchen for the evening meal, inventoried what we had, planned what we could serve. Nan restocked the bar. Jem was in the yard. The ordinary grind, unremarkable, unending, and as essential as the walls themselves.

By the time I locked the front door that evening, my hands had been in cold water four times and I had stopped feeling the tips of my fingers an hour ago. The last guests were settled upstairs. The last fire had been checked -- taproom coals banked, kitchen range damped down, the hearth in the dining room cold. I moved through the closing routine the way I always did, alone, because this was the part of the day that belonged to no one but me.

The taproom fire was down to its last orange glow. I stood and looked at it for a moment -- the particular way embers hold their heat in the dark, patient and purposeful, giving off light for no audience at all. I checked the cellar hatch: locked. The bar: tallied and shut. I went out the back to the stable yard, where the air hit my face like a slap and the sky was clear and thick with stars.

The yard was quiet. Jem would have settled the horses -- I could hear the soft shift of a hoof on straw from inside the stable, and nothing else. On the second floor, above the kitchen, a candle burned behind Bridget's window. I could see the shape of her there, sitting in her chair, watching the yard below as she always did. I did not look long. She was there. She was watching. That was all.

I went back inside and locked the kitchen door. I set the bolt. The inn was sealed, and I was alone in it, and for one breath I let myself feel the weight of that -- the silence, the absence of any voice but my own, the fact that when the last door closed there was no one on the other side of it waiting for me.

One breath. Then I turned to bank the kitchen fire, and I saw the letter.

It was on the table beside the ledger, where Nan must have placed it during the afternoon -- I had not noticed it beneath the invoices and the chandler's receipt. The seal was dark red, pressed with an impression I knew without needing to look closely. Stainforth's seal. His handwriting on the direction, small and precise and perfectly legible: Miss D. Dale, The White Hound, Great North Road, Hertfordshire.

I picked it up. I turned it over once. The paper was good -- heavier than anything I used for my own correspondence, cream-coloured, the kind of paper that announces its sender's position before a single word is read.

I broke the seal.

The letter was brief. Stainforth did not waste words any more than I did, though his economy had a different quality -- not efficiency but compression, as though he wished to make clear that the matter required very little of his attention.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer for the property and lease of the White Hound from a party whose intentions for the premises I regard as sound and advantageous to the parish. I propose a meeting at my offices on the 14th of November to discuss the terms of transfer. You will appreciate that the current arrangement, while it has served adequately, cannot be considered permanent, and I trust you will approach this conversation with the practicality for which your family has always been known.*

The fourteenth of November. Three weeks.

I set the letter down on the table. I looked at it there beside my ledger, beside the accounts I had spent the morning over, beside the numbers that said six weeks and the candles I had fought for and the bowl I had washed after feeding a boy who could not feed himself.

My hands were reddened from the day's cold water. There was a small cut on my right index finger where the edge of Fenton's invoice had caught the skin -- I had not noticed it until now, and it stung with the belated sharpness of a wound that has been waiting for attention.

Three weeks. He had a buyer. He wanted the lease.

I stood in my kitchen, in my inn, in the silence I had made by locking every door myself, and I said aloud to the empty room:

"Then you had better bring more than a letter, Mr Stainforth, because I am not going anywhere."
