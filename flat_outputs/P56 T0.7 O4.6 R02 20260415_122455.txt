The ink had bled through to the second column again, and I pressed the heel of my hand against the ledger page as though I could push the numbers back into order by force of will.

Six weeks. Six weeks until the quarter's rent came due, and the figures would not rearrange themselves into comfort no matter how many times I tallied them. I knew this because I had tallied them four times since October began, and the answer was always the same: we would meet it, but only just, and only if nothing broke, no deliveries went short, and every coach between here and York stopped to change horses and buy ale. I wanted, very badly, for the month of November to be a month in which nothing broke. I suspected November had other plans.

I closed the ledger and set it on the shelf above the kitchen range, spine out, where I could reach it without looking. The kitchen was cold. The fire I had built twenty minutes ago was still finding its courage, licking at the edges of the coal without committing to proper heat. I crouched and adjusted the damper, waited for the draw to strengthen, and pulled my sleeves down over my wrists while I waited.

The rest of the inn was dark and still. No guests had risen yet. The two in room six would want coffee when they did, and the commercial traveller in room nine would want it before them and complain that it was late regardless. I had set the kettle on already. The bread from yesterday would do for toast if I cut the dry edges off, and there were eggs enough for four, which was two more than I expected to need.

I moved through the ground floor the way I did every morning, in the order my mother had taught me and her mother had taught her. Kitchen fire first, then the taproom grate, where last night's coals had gone to ash and needed clearing. I knelt on the hearthstone and raked them out, the iron scraping loud in the empty room. Built the new fire. Checked the bar: three barrels on the stillage, the nearest two-thirds empty, the others full. We were adequate for the week if trade held steady. I wiped down the taps and ran a cloth along the counter where someone had left a sticky ring from a glass of something sweet. The windows were rimed at their edges with the season's first real frost, and the draught that crept beneath the taproom door had a blade to it that said winter was done being patient.

I pulled my shawl tighter and went to check the yard.

Jem was already there. He had the big bay's halter in his one hand and was running a brush down its neck with the practised economy of a man who had learned to make half the usual number of limbs do the full work. The tack on the near wall had been straightened -- not because I had asked, but because the morning required it. He did not look up when I appeared in the doorway, and I did not interrupt him. We had done this before. Many hundreds of times.

Back inside, Nan came through the kitchen door as I was measuring coffee. Her cap pins were not yet set and her hair was escaping at the temples, but her apron was already tied and she moved past me to the bar without a word, taking up the cloth I had left on the counter and continuing where I had stopped. She did not need to be told what the morning looked like. She had eyes.

I returned to the kitchen and waited for the coach.

It was late, which surprised no one. The London coach had not arrived on time since the third week of September, when the driver had been a man named Pollard who kept a schedule the way a parson keeps Lent -- with sincere intention and imperfect execution. Pollard had since been replaced, and his replacement was worse.

When the horn sounded at last, I was in the yard with Jem, and the coach swung through the gate at a speed that suggested the driver was making up lost minutes by endangering his passengers. Two climbed down instead of the expected four. The first was a stout woman in a travelling cloak who descended the step with the compressed fury of someone who had been composing complaints for the last twelve miles. The second was a thin gentleman who looked as though he wished to be anywhere else on earth.

The woman's trunk had a split hinge. She pointed this out to me as though I had personally taken a chisel to it.

"Jem," I said, and he was already there, lifting the trunk with his good arm and bracing the broken hinge with his stump so the lid would not fly open. He carried it inside without comment. The woman watched him go, her next objection dissolving on her lips, and allowed Nan to steer her toward the dining room.

The coachman climbed down last. He was a big man, red-faced, with the particular grievance of someone who has been sitting in weather for six hours and wants the world to know it.

"The road above Hatfield is a disgrace," he said, pulling off his gloves. "Ruts a foot deep past the Lemsford turning. I near lost a wheel."

"You'll want to take the lower fork after the bridge," I said. "The parish crew patched it last week. Adds ten minutes but saves your axle."

"Ten minutes I haven't got."

"You haven't got a spare axle either. The fork after the bridge."

He looked at me the way coachmen often did when I gave them practical information they had not requested -- with the brief, irritated surprise of a man who has been outmanoeuvred by someone he did not consider a combatant. Then he nodded, took the ale Nan had brought to the door, and went to see to his horses.

The yard cleared. The passengers went upstairs or to the dining room. Jem took the gentleman's bag. The coachman drank his ale in the stable and spoke to no one. Nan was behind the bar again, wiping down the counter with one eye on the door through all of it. I noted this. Said nothing.

The chandler arrived at half past eleven, as he did on the last Tuesday of every month, with his cart of tallow candles and his invoice and the expression of a man who believed himself to be engaged in an act of charity by supplying me at all.

"Morning, Miss Dale." He set the crate on the kitchen table and produced his bill with a flourish, as though presenting a gift. "Three dozen at fourpence ha'penny the dozen. Same quality as last month."

I looked at the bill. Then I looked at him.

"It was fourpence the dozen last month, Mr Harkett. And threepence three-farthings the month before that. And threepence ha'penny the month before that."

"Tallow's gone up. The renderers in Hatfield--"

"The renderers in Hatfield are charging you twopence farthing, same as they've charged you all year. I know because Mrs Greaves at the Red Lion told me what she pays you, and she pays fourpence, which means your margin is comfortable enough without squeezing an extra ha'penny out of me every time the wind changes."

Harkett's face did something complicated. "Mrs Greaves ought to mind her own accounts."

"Mrs Greaves minds them very well, which is how I know yours. Fourpence the dozen, Mr Harkett. Same as last month."

He held the silence for a moment -- a negotiator's pause, meant to suggest he was weighing the grave possibility of taking his candles elsewhere. I let him weigh it. He had six customers on this stretch of road, and I was the only one who paid on delivery instead of running a tab he had to chase for months. He knew this. I knew he knew it.

"Fourpence," he said, and set the invoice down.

"Thank you. Nan will bring you a cup of something before you go."

He took his cup. He left. The candles stayed.

I allowed myself one moment after the kitchen door closed -- a single breath in which I acknowledged, privately and without undue satisfaction, that I was very good at this. Then I stacked the candles in the store cupboard and went back to work.

The thought arrived, as it sometimes did, between one task and the next. The ledger, the rent, the chandler's creeping prices -- all of it balanced on the same slender fact: the lease was in my name, and it stayed in my name only so long as I remained unmarried. The law was not complicated. A wife's property became her husband's property. The inn, the income, the right to negotiate with men like Harkett over a ha'penny on tallow -- all of it would pass to whatever man I married, as surely as water runs downhill. I had refused two proposals on this precise calculation, and I had not regretted either refusal, and I did not intend to revisit the arithmetic now. I picked up the broom and swept the kitchen floor.

I saw him through the window before he knocked.

He had come around the back, as the sign on the gate asked, and he stood outside the kitchen door with the particular stillness of a man who was not sure he was welcome enough to announce himself. Young -- twenty, perhaps, or a hard-worn eighteen. Thin in the way of someone who had been thin for a long time, not merely since breakfast. His coat was army issue, or had been once; it was too large for him now and the colour had faded to something between brown and nothing. His boots were coming apart at the welt, the sole of the left one separating from the upper in a way that meant he had been walking on wet ground for days.

He looked at the door. He looked at his boots. He raised his hand, hesitated, and let it fall.

I opened the door before he could decide against it.

He startled -- a quick, whole-body flinch that he mastered almost immediately, squaring his shoulders as though someone had called him to attention.

"Have you eaten?" I said.

He met my eyes. He was deciding, I think, whether to lie. "Not recently, ma'am."

"Kitchen, not the taproom. Sit there." I pointed to the bench by the range. "I'll bring you something."

He sat. I cut bread -- two thick slices from yesterday's loaf -- and ladled broth from the pot I kept on the back of the range for exactly this purpose. Not charity. Soup costs less than trouble, and a hungry man standing in a coaching-inn yard is trouble waiting to choose its moment. I set the bowl and bread in front of him and went back to my work.

He ate carefully, without rushing, holding the spoon the way a man holds a tool he is afraid of breaking. When he had finished, he stood and brought the bowl to the basin without being asked.

"Thank you, ma'am."

"Where are you headed?"

"North," he said. Then, as though he owed me something more specific for the meal: "There's family in Huntingdon. Or there was."

I nodded. He went out through the kitchen door and crossed the yard without looking back, and I watched him go -- the broken boots, the too-large coat, the careful way he held himself upright as though dignity were the last thing he owned and he was not ready to set it down.

He was somebody's son. The thought came before I could stop it, plain and sharp as a pin, and I stood with my hands in the washing-up water and felt it land. Then I wrung out the cloth and wiped down the table where he had sat.

The afternoon passed in the ordinary way of afternoons at the White Hound -- which is to say, in work. The coach had gone, the guests were settled or departed, and the inn entered the slack hours between midday and evening when there was nothing dramatic to manage and everything mundane to do. Nan restocked the bar. Jem was in the yard. I did accounts, reset the upstairs rooms, and began the evening's kitchen preparations, which today meant a mutton stew that would feed whoever arrived and keep without spoiling if no one did.

In the taproom, two men who had been sitting too long over too little ale began to disagree about something -- I heard the pitch of it change through the kitchen wall, the particular upward slide that means a conversation has stopped being a conversation and started being a problem.

I went through.

"Gentlemen. If you'd like to continue your discussion, the yard is available and the evening is fine. If you'd prefer to stay warm, you'll do it quietly."

They looked at me. They looked at each other. They returned to their ale. Nan, behind the bar, had not moved. She did not need to.

The light went early, as it does in late October, and by five o'clock the windows were dark and the inn had drawn in on itself like a creature settling for the night. I lit the lamps in the taproom and the dining room, checked the fires, served the evening meal to the three guests who wanted it, and cleared the plates after. Nan tallied the bar. Jem came in from the yard to report that the horses were settled and the gate was latched.

By eight o'clock the last guest had gone upstairs, and I began the closing circuit. Taproom fire raked down to coals and the guard set in front. Cellar hatch locked -- I checked the bolt twice, as I always did, because my mother had checked it twice and her mother before her. The kitchen closed down: pots scoured, surfaces wiped, the morning's bread dough set to prove by the range where the last heat would do its work overnight.

I stepped into the yard for the final check. The air was sharp enough to make my eyes water. The stable was dark and quiet -- Jem had done his work and gone to his room above the tack store. The gate was latched. The second-floor window at the far end of the building showed a faint glow behind the glass, and in it, the shape of Bridget watching. I did not look long. I had seen it too many times for it to require acknowledgment. She was there. She was watching.

I went back inside and locked the kitchen door. The bolt slid home with the sound it always made -- a heavy, certain thud that meant the day was done and the inn was mine again, mine alone, as it was every night when the work was finished and the people were settled and there was no one left who needed anything from me.

My hands, resting on the bolt, were reddened from cold water and cleaning. There was a small cut on my index finger -- the edge of Harkett's invoice, I thought, though I could not remember the moment it happened. I noticed it now the way one notices such things only when the body is finally still: a thin line of dried blood, insignificant, the kind of wound that does not merit attention and receives it anyway, at the end of a long day, when there is no one to show it to.

The kitchen was quiet. I was alone in it. I let myself notice this -- the aloneness, the particular quality of silence in a room that has been full of people and is now empty -- and then I turned to put the kettle on, because noticing was enough and there was no profit in lingering.

The letter was on the table.

It had not been there at supper. Nan must have taken it from the afternoon post and set it down while I was doing the rounds, and I had walked past it twice without seeing it. But I saw it now. The heavy cream paper. The precise, slanting hand on the direction. The seal -- dark red wax, pressed with an impression I knew before I picked it up.

Stainforth.

I stood with the letter in my hand and felt the weight of it, which was only paper and wax and nothing at all, and which was also everything.

I turned it over once. Then I broke the seal.

The hand was not Stainforth's own -- he had a clerk for correspondence -- but the signature at the bottom was his, and the language had his particular quality of courtesy that was not courteous at all.

*Miss Dale -- I write to inform you that I have received an offer of considerable interest for the property and grounds presently held under lease by yourself, including the inn and its associated buildings. The prospective purchaser wishes to take possession no later than Lady Day next. I am prepared to discuss terms for the surrender of your lease at a meeting at my offices on the 14th of November. I trust you will see the prudence of attending.*

Lady Day. The twenty-fifth of March. Five months.

He had a buyer. He had a date. He was not asking whether I wished to leave. He was informing me of the schedule on which I would do so.

I set the letter down on the table. I pressed my palms flat against the wood on either side of it and stood there, breathing, while the fire ticked in the grate and the bread dough proved silently by the range and the inn settled around me in all its ordinary, fragile, irreplaceable solidity.

Five months to Lady Day. Six weeks to quarter rent. A buyer whose name Stainforth had not troubled to provide, because he did not consider it any of my business who would be taking my home.

I picked up the letter and read it once more, carefully, the way I read supplier invoices -- looking for the gap, the margin, the place where the numbers did not add up and a woman who knew what things cost could find her footing.

"The fourteenth of November," I said to the empty kitchen. "I'll be there, Mr Stainforth. And I'll be bringing my ledger."
